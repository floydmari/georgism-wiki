## Overview

The **Fairhope Single Tax Corporation** was established in **1894** (as the Fairhope Industrial Association, taking its present name on incorporation in Alabama in 1904) to found and govern Fairhope, Alabama as a demonstration of [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) ideas in practice.[1][2] Rather than selling land outright, the Corporation retains ownership of the land and leases it to residents on 99-year renewable leases, charging an annual rent in lieu of private land ownership. The proceeds are used for community purposes.[1][2] Fairhope grew from this experiment into a thriving town, and the Corporation still operates today, making it one of the longest-running practical applications of Georgist land policy anywhere.[1]

## The Operating Lease Model

The Corporation's core mechanism is a **community land-ownership and leasing system**. Land is held by the Corporation rather than divided into private freehold parcels. Residents lease sites from the Corporation and pay an annual rent — a practical application of the [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) principle, though implemented through leasehold rather than taxation.[1] This design means rising land values — driven by community growth, infrastructure, and economic activity — accrue to the Corporation rather than to individual landowners, capturing the [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/) for community benefit. In practice the capture has always been partial: by the Corporation's own account, "in theory, the lessees would be charged an amount equal to the full rental value of the land as defined by Henry George, although this never actually occurred," and today only the land-value-based "demonstration fee" component of the rent reflects the Georgist assessment principle (see [Survival and Continuing Operation](#survival-and-continuing-operation) below).[2]

This model is conceptually related to [public land leasing](/wiki/public-land-leasing/) as practiced in places like [Hong Kong](/wiki/hong-kong/) and [Singapore](/wiki/singapore/), where the state retains land ownership and captures value through lease payments rather than recurrent taxation. The Fairhope model, however, predates those systems and operates at the scale of a single town through a private corporation rather than a sovereign government.

## Founding and Early History

Fairhope was founded in **1894** as part of the broader single-tax colony movement of the late nineteenth century.[1] The [single-tax colonies](/wiki/single-tax-colonies/) — intentional communities organized to demonstrate Henry George's ideas in miniature — represented a practical strategy: rather than waiting for national legislative reform, Georgist reformers established communities that implemented land-rent principles directly.

The founders were members of a single-tax club in **Des Moines, Iowa**, led by **Ernest B. Gaston**, who "wanted to do more than just discuss the single tax theory."[2] Still meeting in Iowa in 1894, they wrote a constitution and formed the **Fairhope Industrial Association**; the name Fairhope records a founding member's remark that there was "a fair hope that the experiment would succeed."[2] A site committee of James Bellangee and Shuah S. Mann examined prospective locations in Arkansas, Alabama, Louisiana, Mississippi, Tennessee, and Texas before selecting the eastern shore of Mobile Bay, and in November 1894 a group of 28 single-taxers from Des Moines and other cities assembled there to begin the colony.[2] The Association bought land with the members' pooled resources — only what happened to be for sale, which is why Colony holdings form a patchwork rather than a contiguous block — and leased it on 99-year renewable terms, with lessees owning all improvements.[2] The Association was incorporated in Alabama as the Fairhope Single Tax Corporation in **1904**.[2] Henry George himself never endorsed the Fairhope experiment and declined all invitations to visit the community.[2]

Fairhope's sister colony, **Arden, Delaware**, was founded in **1900** by Frank Stephens and Will Price as an arts-and-crafts single-tax community; it too retains its land-rent system and remains an incorporated village today.[1]

## Survival and Continuing Operation

The Fairhope Single Tax Corporation is notable among Georgist experiments for its longevity. While many single-tax colonies and Georgist political campaigns of the era did not survive, the Corporation has continued to own and lease land in Fairhope for over a century.[1] This makes it a living demonstration that [community land ownership](/wiki/community-land-trust/) with a land-value charge is workable over the long term — the most tangible institutional legacy of the single-tax movement's community-building phase.[1]

Today the Corporation — locally "the Colony" — is a **501(c)(4) not-for-profit corporation** (status granted November 7, 2003) that still owns about **4,500 acres** in and around Fairhope, roughly **25% of the city**, concentrated downtown and eastward along Fairhope Avenue.[2] Lessees hold 99-year renewable leases (a new lease is issued at each transfer) and own all improvements outright.[2] The annual rent bundles three elements: the state and local ad valorem taxes due on land and improvements (which the Corporation pays to the county on lessees' behalf), an administration fee for the office, and a **"demonstration fee"** calculated from the appraised value of the land, spent on community projects — bayfront parks, the public library, the history museum, school and hospital facilities, sidewalks, and similar works.[2] Governance is by **Members**, who must complete a course on Henry George and the single tax, pay a $100 membership fee, and be approved; the Members elect five Directors (the Executive Council, which holds voting power on corporate matters, subject to Member referendum), four Officers, and three Trustees who audit the accounts and oversee elections, with day-to-day operations run by an Executive Director.[2]

The Corporation is candid that the model has drifted from the theory: full rental value of the land was never actually charged (early on, pier fees covered much of the budget), collection standards were relaxed during the Great Depression, and today's rent "in no way reflects the annual return on the value of the land" — only the demonstration-fee component is still assessed on land value.[2] In the 1930s the Colony deeded its parks (with parks-only reservations), the Fairhope Pier, the original utilities, and the original streets and sidewalks to the City of Fairhope, which had incorporated as a separate governmental entity in 1908.[2]

## Significance for the Georgist Tradition

The Corporation's significance is primarily **demonstrative** rather than statistical. It does not constitute a controlled experiment or a large-scale policy implementation, and Fairhope's experience cannot be straightforwardly generalized to national tax policy. What it does provide is evidence that the core Georgist institutional design — community ownership of land with rent paid to the community — can be sustained over generations within a real town, under American legal and political conditions, without the community collapsing or abandoning the model.

For the broader Georgist case, Fairhope stands alongside other long-running land-rent institutions — including [Singapore](/wiki/singapore/)'s state land leasing, [Hong Kong](/wiki/hong-kong/)'s leasehold system, and [Taiwan](/wiki/taiwan/)'s land value increment tax — as evidence that capturing land value for public benefit is not merely theoretical.
