## Overview

The **Land Tenure Reform Association (LTRA)** was a British land-reform pressure group
founded in **1869** at the initiative of [John Stuart Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/), who
became its most prominent figure and wrote its *Programme, with an Explanatory Statement*
in **1871**. Its purpose was to carry Mill's land programme — above all the
**taxation of the future [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/)** of land values —
out of his *Principles of Political Economy* and into organised politics. It was Mill's last
major public campaign before his death in 1873.

## Programme

The LTRA's platform combined the fiscal proposal with a broader attack on the landed
settlement:

- **Tax the future unearned increment.** The state would value land at present levels and
  claim, through taxation, the *future* rise in that value arising from the growth and
  effort of society rather than from the owner — capturing socially-created value
  prospectively, without confiscating past gains. This was the association's defining plank.
- **Free trade in land.** Abolish **primogeniture** and **entail**, the legal devices that
  kept great estates intact and land off the market, so that land could be bought and sold
  more freely.
- **Cooperative agriculture and smallholdings.** Promote cooperative farming and the
  creation of a class of small proprietors.
- **Reclaim the commons and waste lands** for public benefit rather than private
  enclosure.

The through-line was Mill's conviction, shared with the classical tradition, that land is
categorically different from produced wealth: its rent is a fit and peculiar subject for the
state because taxing it discourages no production.

## History

The LTRA was a small association of radical-Liberal notables rather than a mass movement —
its supporters included the economist **Henry Fawcett** and the radical MP **Sir Charles
Dilke**, alongside sympathetic trade unionists. It was active from 1869 into the mid-1870s
but never became a broad campaign, and Mill's death in 1873 removed its intellectual centre
of gravity; it faded soon after. Its direct legislative achievements in its own lifetime were
essentially nil.

## Why It Matters

The LTRA is the **institutional hinge** between classical rent theory and the practical
land-tax politics of the twentieth century. Two lineages run through it:

- **Forward to the statute book.** [Blaug's history](/wiki/economic-theory-in-retrospect/)
  observes that it was Mill's "milder proposal" — tax the future increment, compensate the
  present — rather than George's full rent capture that eventually reached British law: in
  the land-value duties of [David Lloyd George](/wiki/david-lloyd-george/)'s
  [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/) and, later, the development charge of the
  [1947 Town and Country Planning Act](/wiki/town-and-country-planning-act-1947/).
- **Sideways to Georgism.** The LTRA and [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s
  [single-tax](/wiki/single-tax/) movement represent the two forms the same idea took: a
  reform association of respectable notables proposing to tax the *future* increment, versus
  a mass electoral movement demanding capture of the *whole* rent. The wiki's
  [Mill on Land and Taxation](/wiki/mill-principles-land/) page carries the programme and the
  contrast in detail.
