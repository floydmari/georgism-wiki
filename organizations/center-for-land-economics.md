## Overview

The **Center for Land Economics** (CLE) is a US nonprofit research and education organisation launched in **February 2025** by **Greg Miller** and [Lars Doucet](/wiki/lars-doucet/), which "conducts research and provides education to promote equitable assessments and foster sustainable development for the benefit of communities."[1][2] Its core thesis is "the value of land returned to the community that makes it valuable."[1] The Center represents the modern, empirically minded wing of the Georgist revival, focusing on the practical questions that determine whether land value taxation can work in the real world. Co-founder Greg Miller previously worked as a Program Analyst in the Office of Policy Development and Research at the US Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD); Doucet is the author of the *Progress and Poverty* Substack and a longtime writer on Georgist economics.[3]

## Focus

The Center's central concern is **land assessment** — building better methods and data to value land separately from improvements, directly tackling the most common [objection to LVT](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/), that land value cannot be assessed accurately. Its flagship tool, **LVTShift**, is an open-source modelling platform that lets a city estimate the effect of a land-value-tax shift on property tax bills by property type and neighbourhood, drawing on parcel-level data about vacant and underutilised land and land-to-improvement ratios.[4] The Center has also published empirical studies of assessment failures — including analyses of the systematic undervaluation of vacant land in Baltimore and across Maryland (2024–2026), arguing that such undervaluation shifts tax burdens and rewards speculative blight.[5]

## Significance

Where older organisations preserve and teach the tradition, the Center for Land Economics aims to **operationalise** it — turning Georgist theory into deployable assessment technology and policy guidance for the 2020s. Its work was recognised with a 2025 J.M.K. Innovation Prize.[1]
