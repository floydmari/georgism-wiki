## Overview

The **School of Cooperative Individualism** maintains one of the largest online archives of Georgist and land-economics literature: papers, essays, biographies, historical documents, and periodicals from across the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) movement's history. It is directed by **Edward J. Dodson**, who states that "The School was established in January 1997 as a place where thoughtful persons would find meaningful — and sometimes new — insights into the long and tireless search for a socio-political philosophy that, if implemented, would nurture the development of just societies."[2] (Founding date is per the organisation — a C-claim.) The name refers to a school of thought "derived from … the objective search for truth" that the site associates especially with the emergence of Henry George; the site is organised around a large library, a Single Tax / Henry George section, and a Movement Periodicals Archive.[1]

## Role

Rather than an advocacy body, it functions as a **library and research resource** — preserving and making freely available primary and secondary sources that would otherwise be hard to find, including out-of-print works and movement periodicals. It hosts free copies of important papers (such as [Arnott & Stiglitz 1979](/wiki/arnott-stiglitz-henry-george-theorem/) and Oates & Schwab) that this wiki cites. Together with [Wealth and Want](https://www.wealthandwant.com/) and [masongaffney.org](/wiki/mason-gaffney/), it is part of the open documentary backbone of Georgist scholarship.

Its practical importance to this wiki is hard to overstate: **cooperative-individualism.org is one of the most frequently cited domains in the corpus**, appearing 19 times in this wiki's source registry and referenced across dozens of articles — from [Arnott & Stiglitz](/wiki/arnott-stiglitz-henry-george-theorem/) and the [Henry George Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/) to Mill, Rothbard, Churchill, and the Pennsylvania two-rate-tax history. Its hosted PDFs and HTML transcriptions of movement periodicals (*Henry George News*, *Fragments*, and others) supply primary-source text that would otherwise be inaccessible.
