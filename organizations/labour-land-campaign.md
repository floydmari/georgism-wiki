## Overview

The **Labour Land Campaign** (LLC) is a broad-left, voluntary organisation that "campaigns within the UK labour movement for the collection of land rent for public benefit."[1][2] It was founded in **1983** by a group drawn from architecture, land economics, farming, town planning, trade unions, and academia, brought together by a shared concern for the use of land, and it advocates [land value taxation](/wiki/land-value-tax/) as the means to "a fairer distribution of the wealth derived from land."[1][3] The Campaign carries forward a tradition in British politics — running through the [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/) and back to Henry George's influence on the early British left — that treats taxing the unearned value of land as a progressive cause.

## Rationale

The LLC's core argument rests on land's fixed supply: unlike labour and capital, "the supply of land is fixed," so rising demand raises its value and rent as an unearned gain to owners rather than a return to production — a windfall the Campaign argues should be collected for the community.[3] It frames LVT as a tool against land monopoly, housing unaffordability, and inequality, noting that UK land ownership is highly concentrated and that a large share of national wealth is held as land value.[2]

## Work

The Campaign produces briefings and policy arguments aimed at trade unionists, Labour members, and policymakers; it responds to government and Labour Party consultations and submits resolutions to Labour Party and trade-union conferences.[4] Recent activity includes a 2024 submission to the All-Party Parliamentary Group on Council Housing arguing that the housing question "is all about land."[2] Prominent supporters have included Labour transport figure [Dave Wetzel](/wiki/dave-wetzel/), and the Campaign has publicised endorsements of LVT by senior Labour politicians.[4]
