## Overview

The **Henry George Foundation of Canada** is Canada's long-standing Georgist organization, based in Ottawa and operating online as **earthsharing.ca**. It describes itself as "part of an international network of organizations that work to popularize the thought of 19th century American economist, Henry George," with three stated goals — "a just society, a green society and a prosperous society" — and frames that network as "an interconnected think tank advocating financing governments by capturing 'economic rent' in lieu of taxes on jobs, business and goods and services."[1]

Corporations Canada records show **The Henry George Foundation of Canada** federally incorporated on **December 1, 2014** (corporation #9104593), registered in Ottawa, with directors listed as Glenn Grignon, Francis K. Peddle, and Brendan Hennigan.[3] The 2014 federal incorporation formalized an advocacy operation that had already existed for several years: the organization's current website, earthsharing.ca, was online as early as August 2011 under the name **Earthsharing Canada** — a grassroots tax-shift project whose spokesperson was Frank de Jong, former leader of the Green Party of Ontario — and the site continued to carry the "Earthsharing Canada" name through at least January 2016, more than a year after the federal entity was registered.[7]

## Work

- **Economic-rent education.** Its concise "Economic Rent" article defines rent as "unearned income, windfall profit, or … revenue without a corresponding cost of production" that "exists because of the existence of the community, and should accrue to that community," and argues rent recovery "does not inhibit extraction, use or economic activity."[2]
- **Earthsharing Economics Primer** and explainer pages on Henry George, [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) mechanics (worked household, business, and farm scenarios), and the [18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/), which features on its homepage.[1]
- **Reviews and resources.** It publishes book reviews and commentary (e.g., Francis K. Peddle's November 2024 review of Patrick Condon's *Broken City*) and hosts movement documents such as the Green Party of Ontario's 2010 "Proposed Government of Canada Budget Based on Rent Recovery."[1][4]

## Significance

The Foundation's practical importance to recent Canadian rent-capture research is as a **source of definitions and benchmarks**: [Common Wealth Canada](/wiki/common-wealth-canada/)'s *[Natural Common Wealth and Economic Rent in Canada](/wiki/natural-common-wealth-economic-rent-canada/)* (2023) cites the Foundation's economic-rent article for its working definition of rent and for the argument that rent recovery does not deter extraction (fns 21, 27), and adopts "the proposed royalty rate proposed by the Henry George Foundation of Canada" — **rent at 24% of the total value of mineral production**, yielding the paper's $11.3 billion/year mining-rent scenario (fn 32, Table 5).[5] [VERIFY — UNRESOLVED: no standalone Foundation document stating a "24% of value of mineral production" royalty could be located. Searches of earthsharing.ca (the site and a domain-scoped web search), the Foundation-hosted *Modernizing Henry George* paper, and the 2010 Green Party of Ontario "Budget Based on Rent Recovery" report written for the Foundation — which proposes minerals-and-aggregates rent of $10 billion, not a percentage rate — turned up no such figure. The Common Wealth paper attributes the rate to the Foundation in fn 32 but its Table 5 row for the Foundation carries no footnote, and the 24% equals the paper's own $11.3B ÷ $47B total-mineral-value figures, suggesting the rate may be Common Wealth's back-calculation rather than a separately published Foundation proposal.] The Foundation has in turn covered Common Wealth Canada's proposal on its news pages.[6]
