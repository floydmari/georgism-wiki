## Overview

The **Robert Schalkenbach Foundation** (RSF), founded in 1925, is the oldest organisation dedicated to advancing the ideas of Henry George. Established under the will of New York printer Robert Schalkenbach, its founding purpose was to keep George's works in print and promote the study of his economics.

## Role in the Field

For nearly a century RSF was the custodian of George's intellectual legacy: it published authoritative editions of *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* — including the 75th-anniversary (1954) edition used as the standard modern text of George's work — funded academic work (including an endowed chair), and supported the *American Journal of Economics and Sociology*.[2] Its most significant single publishing project was *[Critics of Henry George: A Centenary Appraisal of Their Strictures on Progress and Poverty](/wiki/critics-of-henry-george/)* (1979), a 27-chapter edited volume assembled by [Robert V. Andelson](/wiki/robert-v-andelson/) to mark *Progress and Poverty*'s centenary — RSF both published and, jointly with Basic Economic Education, Inc. of San Diego, funded the project, which brought together contributors including [Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) to give George's academic critics a fair hearing.[3] RSF was also a member organization of the [Council of Georgist Organizations](/wiki/council-of-georgist-organizations/), the movement's coordinating umbrella body alongside the [Henry George School](/wiki/henry-george-school/).[4]

In **2025**, marking its centennial, the Foundation renamed itself the **[Progress and Poverty Institute](/wiki/progress-and-poverty-institute/)** — presented as "a new name, a continuing mission" — repositioning the organization as a research-forward institute with a published LVT research-priorities agenda, a researcher registry, and grant programs, while continuing the publishing and stewardship role it built over its first century.[5]

## Significance

RSF is the institutional through-line of the Georgist movement — the body that carried George's texts and ideas across the 20th century, from the decline of the single-tax movement to the modern revival, and whose renamed successor now sets much of the field's contemporary research agenda.
