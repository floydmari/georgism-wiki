## Summary

*Social Problems* (1883) is Henry George's shorter, more accessible sequel to *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*. Written for a popular audience and originally serialised, it applies George's land-and-rent analysis to the concrete social and political ills of the Gilded Age.

## Themes

George argues that the concentration of wealth, the corruption of democratic politics, urban squalor, and the displacement of workers by machinery all trace back to the same root: private monopolisation of land and the resulting capture of the [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/). He warns that material progress without addressing [land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) breeds inequality and threatens republican government itself. The book is often recommended as a gentler entry point to George's thought than the more rigorous *Progress and Poverty*.

## Significance

*Social Problems* broadened George's readership and influence, framing the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) not just as an economic fix but as a defense of democracy and social cohesion. It directly influenced reformers such as [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/).
