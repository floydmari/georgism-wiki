## Summary

"Valuing Rail Access Using Transport Innovations" (*Journal of Urban Economics* 57(1), 2005) by
**Stephen Gibbons** (London School of Economics) and **Stephen Machin** (then Professor of
Economics at University College London, also a research associate of LSE's Centre for Economic
Performance, where the paper first circulated as CEP Discussion Paper No. 0611) is among the most
widely cited causal studies of transport capitalization. The paper's abstract frames its
contribution directly: it sets out "to ascertain how much consumers value rail access" by studying
"the effects on house prices of a transport innovation that altered the distance to the nearest
station for some households, but left others unaffected"; "[u]sing the innovation to implement a
quasi-experimental approach studying house price changes in affected versus unaffected areas allows
us to avoid the biases inherent in cross-sectional valuation work." Where earlier work simply correlated
house prices with distance to stations — leaving open the objection that stations are *built* in
already-valuable places — Gibbons and Machin exploit the timing of two late-1990s London rail
expansions, the **Jubilee Line Extension** and the **Docklands Light Railway** extension, as
quasi-experiments: they compare price changes in areas that *gained* station access against
otherwise similar areas whose access did not change.

## Findings

The study finds that properties in areas gaining rail access appreciated relative to comparison
areas, with the effect confined to homes within about 2 km of a station (the paper's walking-distance
cutoff) and increasing with the size of the station-distance reduction. The data are Nationwide
Building Society housing transactions for 1997–2001, aggregated to postcode units and split before
(1997–1999) and after (2000–2001) the 1999 Jubilee Line Extension and DLR extension openings; the
treatment group is postcode units whose distance to the nearest station fell and ended up within
2 km. In the headline difference-in-difference comparison, log prices rose by 0.45 in treated areas
against 0.37 in control areas — in the authors' words, "showing house price growth to be roughly
9.3 percentage points (= [exp(.089) – 1]×100) higher in areas affected by the transport innovation"
(t-ratio 3.46). This is the source of the ~9% headline figure commonly quoted for the study. In
regression form, "prices rose in areas affected by the Jubilee Line and Docklands Light Railway by
around 5.5% for each kilometre reduction in station distance close to the new stations (within
2km)," falling to "around 2.1% of property prices" per kilometre once full property and
neighbourhood controls and differential spatial time trends are included, and to a most-conservative
1.5% per kilometre in the South East London subsample; "[t]here is no statistically significant
impact from distance reductions to postcodes that are beyond 2km from the new stations." The paper
also runs conventional cross-sectional specifications on the same data as a benchmark — there
"[p]rices fall at the rate of 8.9% per kilometre up to 2km," estimates "substantially higher than
those from the innovation models" — direct evidence that correlational designs overstate the rail
premium, the bias the quasi-experiment exists to avoid. The valuations are sizable relative to other
local amenities studied in the same hedonic literature: the most conservative estimates "indicate
that prices go up by 1.5% for a one-quarter standard deviation reduction in station distance,"
versus "under 0.75%" for a one-quarter standard deviation improvement in local primary school
performance. The authors also translate the estimated price effects into an implied value of travel
time savings (roughly £1.60 to £6 per hour across specifications, bracketing the £4/hour then
recommended to the UK Department for Transport), a step relevant to appraising how much of a
transit project's cost could in principle be recovered from the value it creates nearby.

## Relation to the Georgist Case

This is direct, credibly identified evidence for the mechanism at the heart of
[land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/): public transport investment creates location
value that accrues to nearby landowners who did nothing to earn it — the
[unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment-narrative/) in its cleanest modern form. It supports
the outcome that [public investment capitalizes into nearby land values](/wiki/public-investment-capitalizes-into-land/),
and implies that a land value tax or capture instrument could recover part of the cost of transit
from the value it creates, as the [Henry George Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/) suggests in
the limit.

## Nuances and Limits

- The estimates are for **property** values (land + structure bundled); the land-value share of
  the uplift is not separately identified — a general limitation of capitalization studies.
- Results are London-specific; the [Mohammad et al. meta-analysis](/wiki/mohammad-rail-meta-analysis/)
  shows uplift estimates vary widely across cities, rail types, and distances.
- Capitalization measures *local* value creation; it does not by itself establish the net social
  return of the investment (some uplift may be displaced from elsewhere).
- **Magnitudes on this page are verified against the authors' full working-paper text (CEP
  Discussion Paper No. 0611, January 2004), not the published journal tables**, which remained
  paywalled at ScienceDirect when this page was reviewed; the published *JUE* version is the same
  study, but readers quoting exact point estimates should note the working-paper provenance. Note
  also that secondary summaries disagree on "the" effect size largely because the paper reports
  several magnitudes in different units — a ~9.3 percentage-point relative price rise for treated
  areas overall, versus per-kilometre gradients of 1.5% to 5.5% depending on controls and sample —
  so any single quoted number should specify which estimate it is.

## Bears On

- **Outcome:** [Public investment capitalizes into nearby land values](/wiki/public-investment-capitalizes-into-land/)
  — one of the cleanest quasi-experimental designs in that literature.
