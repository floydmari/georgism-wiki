## Summary

"Valuing Rail Access Using Transport Innovations" (*Journal of Urban Economics* 57(1), 2005) by
**Stephen Gibbons** and **Stephen Machin** (both London School of Economics) is among the most
widely cited causal studies of transport capitalization. Where earlier work simply correlated
house prices with distance to stations — leaving open the objection that stations are *built* in
already-valuable places — Gibbons and Machin exploit the timing of two late-1990s London rail
expansions, the **Jubilee Line Extension** and the **Docklands Light Railway** extension, as
quasi-experiments: they compare price changes in areas that *gained* station access against
otherwise similar areas whose access did not change.

## Findings

The study finds that properties in areas gaining rail access appreciated relative to comparison
areas, with the premium concentrated near the new stations and fading with distance. The headline
estimate is an appreciation on the order of **around 9% for areas gaining nearby station access**
[VERIFY: exact point estimate, distance bands, and specification — the published tables could not
be fetched first-hand in this session; the ~9% figure is the commonly cited summary of the paper's
main result]. The authors also translate the estimates into an implied willingness to pay for
transport access, showing values large enough to bear on the financing of transit investment
(B-claims, reported at the precision verifiable this session).

## Relation to the Georgist Case

This is direct, credibly identified evidence for the mechanism at the heart of
[land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/): public transport investment creates location
value that accrues to nearby landowners who did nothing to earn it — the
[unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment-narrative/) in its cleanest modern form. It supports
the outcome that [public investment capitalizes into nearby land values](/wiki/public-investment-capitalizes-into-land/),
and implies that a land value tax or capture instrument could recover part of the cost of transit
from the value it creates, as the [Henry George Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/) suggests in
the limit.

## Nuances and Limits

- The estimates are for **property** values (land + structure bundled); the land-value share of
  the uplift is not separately identified — a general limitation of capitalization studies.
- Results are London-specific; the [Mohammad et al. meta-analysis](/wiki/mohammad-rail-meta-analysis/)
  shows uplift estimates vary widely across cities, rail types, and distances.
- Capitalization measures *local* value creation; it does not by itself establish the net social
  return of the investment (some uplift may be displaced from elsewhere).

## Bears On

- **Outcome:** [Public investment capitalizes into nearby land values](/wiki/public-investment-capitalizes-into-land/)
  — one of the cleanest quasi-experimental designs in that literature.
- **Concept:** [Land Value Capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/) — the financing policy this
  evidence underwrites; [Tax Capitalization](/wiki/tax-capitalization/) — the general mechanism.
