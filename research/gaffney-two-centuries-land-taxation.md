## Summary

"Two Centuries of Economic Thought on Taxation of Land Rents" is [Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/)'s attempt to answer, name by name, a question [Harry Gunnison Brown](/wiki/harry-gunnison-brown/) posed as a complaint: is there really a "conspiracy of silence" against the land tax idea among economists?[1] Gaffney's answer is no — silence is not what the record shows. Surveying roughly sixty economists across two centuries, he finds "a topic on which so many economists have rendered opinions and taken positions" that the problem is comprehensiveness, not silence.[1] He sorts them into five camps by their "apparent sympathies towards the policy": (1) mainly negative, (2) mixed or shifting, (3) noncommittal, detached, or supercilious, (4) positive but tentative/limited/partial/remote, and (5) mainly positive — and states up front that "many, indeed most" contributors to the debate had to be omitted to save space, naming roughly forty of them in a footnote he does not otherwise discuss.[1]

The essay is a **historiographical map**, not an empirical study: its evidentiary content is Gaffney's characterization of what named economists wrote, with page citations, not a new dataset or model. Its wiring value to this wiki is correspondingly broad rather than deep — it is the single richest source the wiki holds for cross-linking `people/` pages to a documented textual position on land taxation, and for showing that the "land question" was never confined to Georgist advocates: Ricardo, Marshall, Walras, Mill, Wicksell, and Adam Smith himself all appear with citable positions.

## Provenance, Publication, and How to Read This Page

The chapter appears in Richard W. Lindholm and Arthur D. Lynn, Jr. (eds.), *Land Value Taxation: The Progress and Poverty Centenary* (Madison: University of Wisconsin Press, for the Committee on Taxation, Resources and Economic Development, 1982), pp. 151–196 — Gaffney's own citation abbreviates the range "151–96."[2] **This is not a Lincoln Institute of Land Policy volume**; TRED (the Committee on Taxation, Resources and Economic Development) was a University of Wisconsin–based land-economics research group distinct from the Lincoln Institute, which publishes a separate, better-known line of LVT scholarship this wiki also carries. Internal evidence dates the writing to around 1980–81: Gaffney discusses California's Proposition 13 (June 1978) as recent history, and remarks that the centennial of a nationalization timeline Alfred Marshall floated in an 1883 lecture "is now only two years away" — placing composition close to 1981.[1]

The source text mined for this page is an OCR transcription (`sources/gaffney/text/K142_Centuries_Thought_Land_Taxation.CV.txt`) of the scanned PDF held at `scratchpad/cache/gaffney-mirror/publications/K142_Centuries_Thought_Land_Taxation.CV.pdf`. The OCR carries the usual scan artifacts (the author's own byline renders as "GarFney"/"GaFENEY" throughout; stray characters and misrecognized ligatures elsewhere). Every direct quotation used on this page was independently re-extracted from the source PDF with `pdftotext -layout` and checked against the OCR text before use; no quotation here rests on OCR output alone.

**Honest grading.** Gaffney was a committed, lifelong Georgist economist, and this essay is explicitly organized by "apparent sympathies" — a classification scheme that is itself an interpretive act, not a neutral inventory. Where Gaffney characterizes a rival's motive rather than just their stated position (e.g., reading J. B. Clark's and Frank Knight's theoretical moves as serving to "block the mental pathway" toward land taxation), the wiki reports this as Gaffney's attributed interpretation — the same D-claim caveat carried on [Gaffney's *Neo-classical Economics as a Stratagem*](/wiki/gaffney-neoclassical-stratagem/), which covers overlapping ground on Clark from the sharper, more polemical angle. Where Gaffney simply reports and cites a position with a page number (Ricardo's rent-incidence chapter, Smith's Book V argument, Walras's *Études*), the wiki treats that as a checkable factual claim, subject to the verification above.

## The Five Camps

### 1. Mainly Negative

The largest camp, subdivided by *how* each economist tried to defeat the case. **[J. B. Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)**, in *The Distribution of Wealth* (1899), gets the fullest treatment: Gaffney credits Frank A. Fetter's and Charles Collier's readings that Clark's marginal-productivity theory was developed partly in reaction to George's wage doctrine, then documents Clark's "two strokes" — treating all factors as symmetrically productive (so rent is no longer a residual "surplus") and recasting capital as a permanent, non-depreciating fund indistinguishable from land ("Clark's ectoplasm is permanent... a mystic who creates ectoplasm in lieu of material capital") (p. 152–154).[1] **[Philip Wicksteed](/wiki/philip-wicksteed/)** is grouped with Clark on the theory, not against him: "All resources are fixed. 'There is no such thing' as a supply curve... Rent is erased. 'There is no surplus at all'" (quoting Wicksteed 1914, p. 15) (p. 154).[1] Vilfredo Pareto's "transfer rent" and **[Frank Knight](/wiki/frank-knight/)**'s total fusion of individual and social cost ("There is no basis for distinguishing land from capital") round out the theoretical wing; Gaffney also traces Knight's "innocent buyer" doctrine of vested land titles to earlier statements by **[Francis A. Walker](/wiki/francis-a-walker/)** and **[J. S. Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/)**, and calls the reversed logic of Prop. 13 — which protects long-tenured owners and taxes recent buyers — an "ironic" mirror of Knight's own argument (p. 154–157).[1]

A separate strand fuses land and capital from the political left: **Karl Marx**, in the posthumous Vol. III of *Capital*, "generally merges [rent] with capital and its 'mode of production'" and, Gaffney argues, shares Clark's erasure of the land/capital line for opposite political ends — "Clark simply stir[s] them up, Marx subordinates land to capital" (p. 157–158).[1] Other named critics include E. R. A. Seligman and H. J. Davenport (the "fixed supply of capital" argument against Fillebrown's Georgist tax-capitalization ploy, anticipating the Harberger thesis), Richard T. Ely (the "ripening costs" theory that land speculation is socially useful), F. Y. Edgeworth (credit-market objections), and **Edwin Cannan**, whose "tragedy of the commons" argument — that untaxed buildings free-ride on services paid for by land taxes, subsidizing central-city congestion — Gaffney calls "a more challenging negative" that "currently is sweeping the field" against purely local land-tax proposals (p. 163–164).[1]

### 2. Mixed, or Shifting and Changeable

**Francis A. Walker** attacked George's data on landownership concentration but still held that "property in land stands lower, much lower in the hierarchy than property in capital" (1883), and by 1888 accepted land taxation as "reasonable in principle but impracticable in implementation" (p. 165–166).[1] **[Alfred Marshall](/wiki/alfred-marshall/)** gets an extended, ambivalent treatment: Gaffney credits him with a genuine theoretical advance over Ricardo — the concept of the "public value of land," extending rent theory from farm fertility to urban location, community synergy, and public works (Marshall 1920, Ch. XI and Appendix G, on land as "everywhere and always... a thing by itself") — while noting Marshall's 1883 anti-George campaigning was personal and stylistic ("no doubt George seemed a cocky intruder"), that his mature *Principles* never republished those polemics, and that Marshall concretely "did win Marshall's support for a British land tax" when [David Lloyd George](/wiki/david-lloyd-george/) proposed one in 1909 — citing Terence Hutchison's 1969 account (p. 166–168).[1] Marshall also distinguished "onerous" local taxes (funding services to *people*) from "beneficial" ones (funding services to *property*) — a framing Gaffney calls the ancestor of the modern "property should pay only for services to property" slogan (p. 167).[1] **[A. C. Pigou](/wiki/arthur-cecil-pigou/)** favored Marshall's public-value base for its "announcement effect" — unavoidability and unshiftability — while rating it poorly on the horizontal-equity grounds Pigou himself preferred (p. 168).[1] Marx's *Manifesto* demanded rents "applied to public purposes" (Plank 1) even as his *Poverty of Philosophy* attacked Proudhon's rent tax on different grounds — Gaffney reads this as genuine wavering, mostly toward hostility (p. 168–169).[1]

### 3. Noncommittal, Detached, or Supercilious

**[David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/)** heads this camp, notably: Gaffney credits him with the foundational technical point — "he merely explains in short and simple terms that a tax on rent is not shifted" (1817, Ch. X) — while insisting Ricardo "does not take an advocate's role" and guesses Ricardo "doubted the adequacy of rent for taxes" since his treatment of every other tax has it shifted forward, never back into rent (p. 169).[1] Heinrich von Thünen and Martin Faustmann get credit for sophisticated rent mathematics (location theory, forestry) with no recorded interest in tax policy; **[Paul Samuelson](/wiki/paul-samuelson/)**'s textbook states land rent's taxable-surplus character "as lucid as any heart could desire... and there they rest" — detachment, Gaffney says, not reticence (p. 170).[1] Friedrich Hayek is noted for insisting on the land/capital distinction on Austrian grounds while never becoming a land taxer himself (p. 171).[1]

### 4. Positive but Tentative, Limited, Partial, and Remote

This is the largest catch-all: development economists writing on land taxation in the colonial and postcolonial world (Haskell Wald, John Due's East/West Africa comparison, Robert Haig on Western Canada, Harold Groves on Canadian vs. American property taxation), and — most relevant to this wiki's `people/` roster — **[J. S. Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/)**, whose gradualism Gaffney treats with more skepticism than the wiki's own Mill page currently carries: Mill "recognizes fleetingly that present land values include discounted future increments, but dances calisthenically around this," yet still concludes "among the very few kinds of income which are fit subjects for peculiar taxation, these (urban) ground-rents hold the principal place," and that a house-tax falling on the ground-landlord "is liable to no valid objection" (Mill 1872, Book V, Ch. II) (p. 174).[1] Gaffney also notes, pointedly, that Mill's activism through the [Land Tenure Reform Association](/wiki/land-tenure-reform-association/) came only "after his autobiography is finished and his main energies spent... almost a posthumous activity" (p. 174–175).[1] A separate subsection covers advocates for taxing specific rent categories — timber site rents (Ellis Williams), Harold Hotelling's marginal-cost-pricing case for financing utility deficits from land rent (a case [William Vickrey](/wiki/william-vickrey/) "carries much further to the logical conclusion of a thoroughgoing general shift to the land value tax base"), and mineral rents (Lewis C. Gray, John Ise, Mason Gaffney's own 1967 work) — plus, notably for the wiki's rent-gradient scope, brief mentions of active contemporary literatures on **radio-spectrum rent** and **water rent** as underexploited public-revenue bases (p. 175–179).[1]

### 5. Mainly Positive

Gaffney explicitly sets aside Henry George and Harry Gunnison Brown here as "so well known, so total, and so easily referenced" that they need no fresh treatment, and instead profiles the "eccentric band" beyond them (p. 179).[1] The standout is **[Léon Walras](/wiki/leon-walras/)**, whom Gaffney — working from his own then-unpublished 1967 translation of Walras's *Études d'économie sociale* (1896) — calls "a thoroughgoing land taxer, who writes with the passion of a Gallic Henry George," quoting: "the State must own the land and find in its rent the means to subsist... The assignment of land to the State solves the question of taxation by erasing it," and "Lands do not belong to all the men of one generation; they belong to humanity" (p. 180).[1] **John R. Commons** is profiled as a politically engaged land taxer behind Wisconsin's 1921 Grimstad Bill, proposing a three-tier income-tax rate (low on wages, medium on capital, highest on land income) grounded in an "ability to pay, inversely to service to the community" doctrine (p. 180–182).[1] **[François Quesnay](/wiki/francois-quesnay/)** and the Physiocrats (including **[Turgot](/wiki/anne-robert-jacques-turgot/)** as their "political spokesman") get credit for the founding *produit net* concept and an early, if overstated, grasp of excess burden (p. 182–183).[1] **Knut Wicksell**'s 1896 benefit-theory-of-taxation argument is traced step by step to a Georgist conclusion — "I agree... wholeheartedly with the special taxation, or better confiscation... of the increase in land value... society has both the right and the duty to revise the existing property structure" (Wicksell 1896, p. 7) (p. 183–184).[1] Ralph Turvey, Lowell Harriss, Colin Clark (a scheme to equalize per-capita land-value tax burdens across jurisdictions), and Dick Netzer (who "systematically disposes of the various cases against land taxation" surveyed in Part 1) close the roster (p. 184–185).[1]

The essay's **conclusion returns to [Adam Smith](/wiki/adam-smith/)** as the frame for the whole two-century survey: Smith's ground-rents-as-fittest-tax argument (*Wealth of Nations* Book V, "Nothing can be more reasonable... that a fund which owes its existence to the good government of the state, should be taxed peculiarly"), his preference for periodic reassessment on the Venetian model over the frozen English land tax, and his warning that taxing capital ("stock") instead would diminish "not only the profits of stock, but the rent of land and the wages of labor" (Smith 1776, pp. 796, 800) (p. 185–186).[1] Gaffney closes: "May economics progress faster in the next two hundred years than in the last... or at least stop retrogressing from the necessary and sufficient wisdom of the great Adam Smith" (p. 186).[1]

## Standing and Limits

- **What kind of source this is.** A synthesis and finding-aid, not primary data — its factual claims are almost entirely "economist X wrote Y, citation Z," independently checkable against the cited primary texts, several of which (Ricardo, Smith, Mill) this wiki already holds in full or in dedicated book/text pages. Its interpretive claims (motive-reading of Clark, Knight, Fetter) are Gaffney's own and are the weaker, D-claim layer.
- **Selection bias is explicit, not hidden.** Gaffney names roughly forty additional economists he chose to omit "to save space" (p. 151) — a reminder that "who counts as mainstream" in this survey is Gaffney's editorial judgment, not a systematic literature review.
- **Overlaps with, and is distinct from, other Gaffney sources the wiki already carries.** [*Neo-classical Economics as a Stratagem*](/wiki/gaffney-neoclassical-stratagem/) covers Clark's motive in more polemical depth from the same 1994 *Corruption of Economics* volume; this 1982 essay is broader (sixty names vs. one central figure) and noticeably less accusatory in tone toward Clark specifically, though it shares the underlying "blocked mental pathway" reading.
- **A genuinely useful complication, not just corroboration:** Blaug's history (cited on [the land-is-just-capital objection page](/wiki/land-is-just-capital/)) groups Wicksteed with Walras as marginalists who "kept land distinct enough to advocate its nationalization" rather than merging it into capital. Gaffney's own primary reading here cuts the other way for Wicksteed's *theory*, even while agreeing on his *politics*: Wicksteed's 1914 apparatus is grouped explicitly with Clark's as erasing rent as a distinct surplus ("There is no surplus at all"). The two are reconcilable — Wicksteed's Fabian, pro-land-tax politics coexisted with a marginalist technical framework that, like Clark's, gave land no privileged analytical status — but a reader relying on Blaug alone would miss the tension. See [Philip Wicksteed](/wiki/philip-wicksteed/) for both sides, held together.

## Bears On

- **Objection:** [Marshall's Objection to the Single Tax](/wiki/marshall-single-tax-objection/) — undercuts the objection's force by documenting Marshall's concrete 1909 support for Lloyd George's land tax and his own "public value" concept, alongside the ambivalence the objection page already carries
- **Objection:** [Progress and Poverty Was Thirty Years Out of Date](/wiki/progress-and-poverty-outdated/) — undercuts the "relic" framing: contemporaries of the marginal revolution (Walras, Wicksell) independently reached George's own policy conclusion using the new marginalist apparatus, rather than George's work going unengaged by the generation that superseded classical value theory
- **Objection:** ["Land Is Just a Form of Capital"](/wiki/land-is-just-capital/) — complicates the page's citation of Wicksteed as a nationalization-minded marginalist who "kept land distinct": Gaffney's primary reading groups Wicksteed's *theoretical* apparatus with Clark's land-erasing move, even as his politics diverged
- **Concept:** [Quasi-Rent](/wiki/quasi-rent/) — Marshall's "onerous"/"beneficial" tax distinction and public-value-of-land concept, surveyed here, are adjacent to his quasi-rent theory
- **Research:** [The Modern Georgism of Respected Economists](/wiki/modern-georgism-respected-economists/) — the same "roll call" project for a later era; this page is its 1982, more rigorously cited predecessor
