## Overview

"New Life in Old Cities: Georgist Policies and Population Growth in New York City, San
Francisco, Chicago, Cleveland, Toledo, Detroit, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh, and Other Cities,
1890-1930" is a historical-quantitative essay by **Mason Gaffney**, last emended
10-22-2006 and published on masongaffney.org.[1] Its abstract states the thesis
plainly: "the Georgist movement inspired a large number of civic leaders — mayors,
assessors, governors, congressmen and others — to implement Georgist policies in a
number of US and Canadian cities," reducing or removing assessments on buildings while
raising them on land and financing "low-cost, high-quality public services" from the
land-value base; "where implemented, these policies resulted in rapid population
growth."[1] Gaffney uses **decennial US Census population data, 1890–2000**, as his
comparison metric — building-permit records, he notes, "do not go back to the 1920s"
in usable, uniform form across cities — and tabulates growth rates for New York City
against three comparison groups (upstate New York cities, mid-Atlantic cities, and
NYC's New Jersey neighbors) plus roughly a dozen other US and Canadian cities.

## The New York City Case

The paper's most developed episode is **New York City under Governor Al Smith's 1920
Tax Reform Act** ("the Al Smith Act"), which exempted new residential construction —
but not the underlying land — from local property taxation from 1921 through 1931,
capped per room and per building.[1] Gaffney draws on a contemporaneous chronicle
(Pleydell & Wood, 1960) and the pamphlet literature (C.J. Post 1984; Polak 1924; Geiger
1933) to document an "extraordinary boom in both building and population," and adds
his own population comparison: from 1920–40 New York City grew **32.7%**, versus
**13.8%** for four other major New York State cities, **7.3%** for five mid-Atlantic
cities, and **2.8%** for its New Jersey neighbors — continuing to grow through the
Great Depression while every comparison group stalled or shrank.[1] He is explicit
about the limits of the inference: "a sequence is not always a consequence, and in the multivariate world of economics, 'proofs' are always subject to doubt and open to challenge."[1] Gaffney also credits assessor **Lawson Purdy**, a former Manhattan
Single Tax Club officer, with simultaneously shifting NYC's assessment practice toward
valuing land first at full market value and building value as a residual — reinforcing
the exemption's tax-base effect — and quotes a 1924 Stein Committee report: "There has
been a tremendous increase in land assessments since 1920 in all the boroughs...Tax
exemption is creating aggregate taxable values to an extent heretofore unknown in the
history of any municipality," which he reads as evidence for the Physiocratic "all
taxes come out of rent" (ATCOR) thesis.[1]

*This episode is covered independently and in greater depth, including its causal
caveats (the concurrent national 1920s building boom, permissive 1916 zoning, the
absence of rent control), on the wiki's [New York City's 1921–1931 tax
exemption](/wiki/nyc-1920s-tax-exemption/) page, which draws on Gaffney's companion
paper "The Resurgence of New York City After 1920" and Mary Conyngton's 1922 Bureau of
Labor Statistics report. This page does not duplicate that treatment; it draws only on
"New Life in Old Cities" itself, which frames NYC as one of nine parallel cases rather
than the sole subject.*

## The Other Cities

Gaffney identifies Georgist-aligned political leadership behind growth spurts in eight
more cities, arguing the NYC pattern was not unique:

- **Cleveland, 1900–20** (+109%): Mayors Tom L. Johnson and Newton D. Baker raised
  land assessments citywide (from $180m to $500m) using W.A. Somers's "standard unit"
  land-value mapping system, funding a 3-cent trolley fare from land taxation.[1]
- **Toledo, 1890–1920** (population tripled): single-tax Mayors Samuel "Golden Rule"
  Jones and Brand Whitlock.[1]
- **Detroit, 1890–1930**: growth under Mayor/Governor Hazen Pingree, who brought Tom
  Johnson to Detroit to build cheap public transit; Gaffney separately notes Detroit's
  post-Pingree annexation-driven sprawl produced "one of the worst cases of excess
  subdivision in the U.S.A."[1]
- **Milwaukee, 1916–40**: Socialist Mayors Emil Seidel and Daniel Hoan pursued
  "sewer socialism" — low transit and utility rates funded by land-focused property
  taxation, plus Somers-system assessment maps distributed to citizens.[1]
- **Chicago, 1890–1930**: no single leader but a dense network of Georgist-aligned
  figures (Governor John Peter Altgeld, Mayor Edward Dunne, Clarence Darrow, Jane
  Addams, Louis F. Post) and the 1894 Illinois Bureau of Labor Statistics report on
  landownership concentration in the Loop.[1]
- **San Francisco, 1907–30** (+22% each decade, 1900–1930): recovering from the 1906
  earthquake and fire with **75% of its real-estate tax base already in land value**
  before the disaster, under Mayor Edward Robeson Taylor — who had helped Henry George
  edit *Progress and Poverty* — and later "Sunny Jim" Rolph.[1] Gaffney also reports
  that statewide California land assessments reached **72% of the property tax roll
  in 1917**, during the peak years of Luke North's statewide single-tax initiative
  campaigns (which won 31% of the vote in 1916) — the high-land-tax-reliance baseline
  that predates, and stands in contrast with, the post-1978 Prop. 13 regime Gaffney
  discusses separately (see [A Severance Tax on California
  Oil?](/wiki/gaffney-california-severance-tax/)).[1]
- **Houston, 1911–15** (+25%): single-tax assessor J.J. Pastoriza, until a court order
  reversed his practice.[1]
- **San Diego, 1920–26**: assessor Harris Moody converted the property tax toward a
  land-value basis until a court stopped him — after which, per Gaffney (citing Robert
  Andelson), "the city skyline froze for the next 75 years."[1] **[VERIFY: this specific
  claim is Gaffney's citation of an unpublished Andelson conference address; the wiki
  has not independently traced San Diego building data to confirm the 75-year figure.]**
- **Vancouver, 1895–1909** (population quintupled): 8-term Mayor Louis Denison
  "Single-tax" Taylor progressively exempted first half, then all, of building value
  from the property tax — which Gaffney calls "the fastest growth rate on record."[1]
  **[VERIFY: superlative claim, not benchmarked against an independent global
  cross-city dataset in this pass.]**

## The Pittsburgh Anomaly

Gaffney treats **Pittsburgh** as a deliberate counter-case. Pittsburgh's "two-rate"
(split-rate) property tax plan is, he notes, the most-cited example in the tax
literature of a land-tax-induced building effect — citing the same Cord, Oates &
Schwab, and Tideman & Plassmann studies this wiki's [split-rate taxation increases
construction](/wiki/split-rate-increases-construction/) page relies on — yet
Pittsburgh's **population fell** sharply after 1980, "steeper than most cities" in his
dataset, and "no one publishing on Pittsburgh's Plan, pro or con," had addressed the
population trend.[1] He offers four explanations, none of which involve the graded
tax's construction effect being false: (1) the plan taxed only the *city's* levy, not
the overlapping school-district or Allegheny County property tax, diluting its
citywide effect; (2) Pittsburgh imposed a 4% wage tax in the 1980s that survey
respondents identified as a driver of out-migration; (3) unlike Cleveland, Milwaukee,
or NYC, the city did not control its own assessments — the Allegheny County assessor
"systematically" undervalued land, which by 2000 was reportedly only **10% of
Pittsburgh's property tax base**, a figure Gaffney attributes to a contract assessor
(George Donatello of Sabre Systems) and a city councilman (Daniel Cohen) quoted in
local news coverage.[1] **[VERIFY: the 10%-of-base figure and the assessor-undervaluation
claim rest on Gaffney's citation of 1999–2000 Pittsburgh newspaper reporting, not
independently re-fetched here.]** (4) Pittsburgh's construction gains were
commercial/industrial, which can compete with residential land use rather than
complement it. Gaffney's conclusion is that construction growth and population growth
are *distinct* outcomes that a partial, poorly-assessed graded tax can decouple.

## The "Pro-Labor Mayors" Argument

Gaffney closes the city survey with a provocative reversal of the conventional
"pro-business governance drives growth" narrative: across his cases, the fastest-growing
cities (New York, San Francisco, Cleveland, Toledo, Milwaukee, Detroit under Pingree)
were led by left-leaning, labor-aligned, or explicitly Socialist administrations, while
"business-friendly" Cincinnati shrank over the same century (363,000 in 1910 to
331,000 in 2000).[1] This is offered as correlational political history, not as a
controlled comparison; Gaffney's own causal mechanism is that these administrations
happened to combine land-value taxation, low transit/utility fares, and heavy public
investment — not that left-wing governance is generically pro-growth.

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** This is a historical-quantitative synthesis by a committed Georgist
economist, not a peer-reviewed paper: it was self-published on masongaffney.org, not
submitted to a journal, and Gaffney states plainly that "published literature on this
episode, either popular or scholarly, is sparse."[1] The **population figures
themselves are A-claims** — they are US Census data, checkable in principle — but the
**causal attribution to Georgist tax policy is a D-claim** (interpretive/argumentative):
no city-pair in the essay is matched on other growth determinants the way, say, Oates &
Schwab's 15-city Pittsburgh panel is, and Gaffney's own hedge ("a sequence is not always a consequence") should travel with every city case cited from this source. The essay's
strongest evidentiary contribution is not any single city, but the **breadth of the
pattern** — nine independent cities, three countries' worth of comparison groups,
spanning four decades — which is suggestive corroboration for the historical prologue
to modern split-rate research, not a substitute for it. The **Pittsburgh anomaly is the
paper's most methodologically valuable contribution**: it is Gaffney, a Georgist
advocate, actively complicating his own thesis rather than cherry-picking confirming
cases, and it supplies a genuine caveat — a well-evidenced *construction* effect
(Pittsburgh) does not guarantee a *population* effect if the tax is narrow in scope,
undermined by other policies (a wage tax), or subverted by assessment practice.

## Bears On

- **Benefit:** [Split-rate taxation increases urban construction](/wiki/split-rate-increases-construction/)
  — this paper supplies a wider historical prologue than the NYC case alone (nine
  cities, 1890–1930) supporting the general pattern that shifting taxation from
  buildings to land coincided with growth, while its Pittsburgh section adds an
  explicit **counter-nuance**: the same graded-tax city that anchors the page's
  Pennsylvania construction evidence saw population *decline*, for reasons (partial tax
  scope, a wage tax, county-level assessment capture) distinct from the construction
  question itself.
- **Problem:** [Land underuse and speculative vacancy persist in high-demand
  cities](/wiki/speculative-vacancy-wastes-cities/) — the San Diego and Houston
  episodes, where courts halted a land-value assessment shift and (per Gaffney's
  secondary citation) building activity subsequently froze, are a historical
  illustration of the reverse mechanism this page documents: removing land-tax
  pressure removes the holding-cost incentive to develop or sell, consistent with the
  page's real-option account of why land sits idle.
