## Overview

"Identifying Berlin's Land Value Map Using Adaptive Weights Smoothing," by Jens Kolbe, Rainer Schulz, Martin Wersing, and Axel Werwatz (Technische Universität Berlin and Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin), was published in *Computational Statistics* 30(3), pp. 767–790, in 2015.[1] The paper applies **adaptive weights smoothing (AWS)** — an iterative, nonparametric statistical technique — to sparse transaction records of vacant land sales in Berlin, producing a continuous, city-wide land-value surface without relying on an expert appraiser's manual judgment or zone boundaries.[1] The authors report that their AWS-derived values reach roughly 85% agreement with Berlin's official expert-committee land values, which they characterize as "fairly good" concordance for a purely data-driven method.[1]

Lars Doucet's *Land Is a Big Deal* cites this paper (alongside a related 2019 Kolbe et al. paper testing nonparametric kernel regression and semiparametric regression on the same Berlin data) as part of a broader survey of statistical land-valuation methods, using it as city-scale evidence that unimproved land value can be estimated accurately from data alone — a direct response to the objection that land value cannot be assessed without abundant vacant-land sales.[2]
