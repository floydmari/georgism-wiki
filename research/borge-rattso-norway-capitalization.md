## Overview

"Capitalization of Property Taxes in Norway" is a peer-reviewed article by economists **Lars-Erik Borge** and **Jørn Rattsø**, both of the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU), published in *Public Finance Review*, volume 42, issue 5 (2014), pages 635–661.[1] Norway is a useful test case for [tax capitalization](/wiki/tax-capitalization/) because, unlike most developed countries, a large share of Norwegian municipalities historically chose **not** to levy a local property tax at all, creating cross-municipality variation the authors could exploit.[1]

Using housing-transaction data from 1997–1999 combined with municipal-level data on property taxation, local public services, and community characteristics, and instrumenting for the endogeneity of local tax choices, Borge and Rattsø find that **housing prices respond to property taxation with close to full capitalization at realistic discount rates** — i.e., a euro of expected future property-tax liability lowers the sale price of a home by close to a euro of present value.[1] The finding sits alongside similar capitalization results from Denmark, the United States, and elsewhere in the wiki's incidence literature, reinforcing that the capitalization mechanism central to the land-value-tax case is not a single-country artifact.
