## Summary

*Who Owns the Sky? Our Common Assets and the Future of Capitalism* (Island Press,
2001) by entrepreneur and writer **Peter Barnes** proposes treating the atmosphere's
capacity to absorb carbon as a **common asset owned equally by all citizens** — and
charging for its use. Barnes' "Sky Trust" is the acknowledged origin of the
**cap-and-dividend** policy family, and it is the most direct modern application of
the Georgist rent principle to an ecological commons: what the
[citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) does with land and resource rent, the
Sky Trust does with the scarcity rent of a capped atmosphere.

The book carries no `supports_outcomes` wiring: it is a **policy proposal**, not
empirical evidence. Its evidential relatives — what the proposal would look like in
operation — are the [Alaska Permanent Fund](/wiki/alaska-permanent-fund/) record
collected on [resource-rent dividends work](/wiki/resource-rent-dividends-work/).

## The Sky Trust Mechanism

Barnes proposes:

1. **Cap** total US carbon emissions at a declining level.
2. **Sell emission permits "upstream"** — auctioned to the first sellers of fossil
   fuels (producers and importers), so the price propagates through the economy
   without regulating end users.
3. **Route the revenue to a trust** — a non-governmental institution holding the
   atmosphere in common for all Americans, including future generations.
4. **Pay equal per-capita dividends** — every American receives an identical annual
   cash payment, on the model of Alaska's oil dividend.

The design is explicitly patterned on the [Alaska Permanent Fund](/wiki/alaska-permanent-fund/),
whose dividend has paid every Alaskan annually since 1982.[1][2] In Georgist terms:
the cap creates a measurable scarcity rent in a previously unpriced commons; the
auction collects that rent; the dividend distributes it to its owners.

## The Policy Lineage It Started

- **Cap-and-dividend legislation.** Rep. (later Sen.) Chris Van Hollen introduced the
  *Cap and Dividend Act* in 2009 (H.R. 1862) and the *Healthy Climate and Family
  Security Act* repeatedly thereafter (2014, 2015, 2018, 2019, 2022, 2024) — each
  auctioning 100% of carbon permits to first sellers of fossil fuels and returning
  the proceeds as equal quarterly dividends to every American with a Social Security
  number. None received a floor vote.[3]
- **The Economists' Statement on Carbon Dividends (2019).** Organized by the Climate
  Leadership Council and published in the *Wall Street Journal*, signed by more than
  3,500 US economists including 27 Nobel laureates (28 by later counts) and all four
  living former Fed chairs: a carbon tax is "the most cost-effective lever" for
  emissions reduction, and "all the revenue should be returned directly to U.S.
  citizens through equal lump-sum rebates."[4] The dividend design Barnes proposed
  in 2001 had become, by 2019, the revenue recommendation of the largest public
  statement of economists on record (the scale claim is the organizers' own).
- **Barnes' later books** extend the argument: *Capitalism 3.0* (2006, released as a
  free PDF) generalizes the trust model to other commons; *With Liberty and
  Dividends for All* (2014) traces the dividend lineage from Thomas Paine's
  *Agrarian Justice* (1797) to Alaska.[5]

## Relation to the Georgist Case

Barnes supplies the bridge between classical Georgism and climate policy that the
[ecological Georgism](/wiki/ecological-georgism/) concept describes: the atmosphere
is "land" in the classical sense — a scarce, unproduced natural opportunity — and a
carbon price under a cap is rent, not a tax on production. The equal dividend answers
the standard regressivity objection to carbon pricing the same way
[land-value-tax progressivity](/wiki/land-value-tax-can-be-progressive/) arguments
run: the charge falls on use of a commons, and the rebate returns it per capita.

## Nuances and Limits

- **A proposal, not a program.** No jurisdiction has implemented a full Sky Trust;
  the nearest operating relatives are Alaska's (oil-funded) dividend and
  cap-and-trade systems whose revenues mostly do *not* go to per-capita dividends.
- **The trust-vs-treasury choice is load-bearing.** Barnes insists on a trust outside
  annual appropriations precisely because windfalls routed through ordinary budgets
  behave differently — the concern [Martinez documents for Colombia](/wiki/martinez-colombia-resource-rents/).
