## Overview

"Rent-Seeking and Global Conflict" is a short (roughly 1,500-word) summary Mason
Gaffney prepared for a UC seminar on Global Conflict and Cooperation, held in
Laguna Beach, February 26–27, 1988.[1] It states, in compressed conference-talk
form, the same core thesis that [Gaffney and John B. Cobb Jr. would develop into a
62-page, heavily sourced treatise thirty years later](/wiki/gaffney-corporate-power-military/):
that national governments originate historically to seize, hold, and police land
rent, and that modern military conflict — despite its abstraction into "rent-seeking"
game theory in academic economics — remains substantially the same land-grabbing
phenomenon at a larger scale.[1]

## The Argument

Gaffney opens by criticizing how economists use "rent-seeking" — typically modeling
"dribs and drabs of transfer rent derived from contracting advantages" while
"ignoring the basic" fact that the largest rent-seeking prize in history is simply
land itself: "When William of Normandy conquered England the prize was land rent,
all of it."[1] He states the land-tenure-first theory of the state directly: "every
chain of title goes back to a grantor who originally seized the land," and
therefore "[e]very land title in the world goes back to a taking by force" —
a claim he extends to the "public good" defense of military spending, arguing that
"[w]hat is defended behind the defense wall is land previously seized," so "[r]ent
is what is being defended," not an evenly shared public benefit.[1]

He introduces the **"cacique"** terminology later expanded in the 2018 paper — a
generic term (borrowed from the Spanish colonial administrative vocabulary) for
local rulers who cooperate with an imperial or metropolitan power in exchange for
continued tenure — and distinguishes caciques from a second class of offshore
rent-seeker, "U.S. or allied multinational interests," which extract concessions
(minerals, transportation routes, spectrum, bank charters) through caciques rather
than direct rule.[1] The paper's single concrete case study is **Aramco**: founded
in 1933 with $100,000 in capital, valued at "well over $5 billion" by 1970 — a
gain Gaffney attributes overwhelmingly to "appropriations[] and appreciation," not
"capital flows" or "plowbacks," i.e., to statecraft and force rather than
productive capital investment.[1] The paper closes with a policy prescription
(recouping rents from beneficiaries of state protection via taxation, rather than
letting rent-seeking incentives drive foreign policy) and a specific, disavowed
anticipation of the "Marxist" label — Gaffney distinguishes his land/rent-based
class analysis from Marx's underconsumptionist theory of imperialism and from
Marx's own failure to distinguish land from capital.[1]

## Standing and Limits

This is a brief, informal conference summary — not a developed argument, and
Gaffney does not return to or expand it anywhere else in the wiki's corpus except
via the much fuller 2018 treatise. Its claims (land seizure as the origin of the
state, the "capitalized value" logic later applied to Aramco) are D-claims:
theoretical and historical framing from a committed Georgist advocate, offered
without the extensive sourcing the 2018 paper later supplies. This page exists
primarily to establish **priority** — the caciques framework and the
land-tenure-first theory of military spending were both already substantially
formed by 1988 — and to preserve the Aramco data point, which the 2018 paper
repeats without adding further detail. Readers wanting the developed, fully cited
version of this argument should consult [the 2018
paper](/wiki/gaffney-corporate-power-military/) directly.
