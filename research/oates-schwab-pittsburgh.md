## Summary

Wallace Oates and Robert Schwab's 1997 *National Tax Journal* study is the most-cited empirical examination of the effects of land value taxation in a US city. It analyses Pittsburgh's experience after the city sharply increased the rate at which it taxed land relative to buildings around 1979–80, raising the land-tax rate to roughly five times the rate on structures.

## Method

The authors compared building activity in Pittsburgh against fifteen other eastern US cities of comparable size over the period surrounding the reform. By using a control group of cities that did **not** adopt split-rate taxation, they sought to separate the effect of the tax change from the regional and national construction trends of the 1980s.

## Key Finding

Pittsburgh experienced a building boom in the 1980s — a roughly 70% increase in the value of building permits — substantially larger than any of the comparison cities, several of which saw construction decline. Oates and Schwab argue the surge cannot be explained by a general demand boom (Pittsburgh's economy was not unusually strong) and is consistent with the incentive created by taxing land more heavily than improvements: holding land idle becomes costly, while building on it is no longer penalised.

## Caveats

The authors are careful that Pittsburgh's reform coincided with other downtown development efforts, and a single-city case cannot definitively isolate the tax effect. They frame the result as strong suggestive evidence rather than proof — a finding later reinforced by larger multi-municipality studies (see [Plassmann & Tideman, 2000](/wiki/plassmann-tideman-construction/)).
