## Overview

"Toward Full Employment with Limited Land and Capital" is a 68-page chapter by
[Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) in Arthur D. Lynn Jr. (ed.), *Property Taxes,
Land Use and Public Policy* (Madison: University of Wisconsin Press, 1976),
pp. 99–166 — confirmed from the chapter's own running head ("LAND USE EFFECTS," the
volume's section title) and its explicit self-identification as work for "the
Committee on Taxation, Resources and Economic Development" (TRED), the same
research committee whose conference proceedings Gaffney edited elsewhere in the
wiki's corpus (see [Extractive Resources and Taxation](/wiki/gaffney-extractive-resources-taxation/)
and [Two Centuries of Economic Thought on Taxation of Land Rents](/wiki/gaffney-two-centuries-land-taxation/)).[1]
It carries an epigraph from J.S. Mill — "Capital is kept in existence from age to
age not by preservation, but by perpetual reproduction" — that recurs, attributed
to Mill, on Gaffney's shorter [land-boom capital-destruction notes](/wiki/gaffney-land-booms-destroy-capital/)
decades later. A companion piece, Chapter 6 of an edited volume on environmental
policy ("Environmental Policies and Full Employment," c. mid-1970s, 24pp), explicitly
calls itself a "condensed, non-technical version" of this chapter and is not
separately mined here (see "I16: A Condensed Restatement," below).[2]

This is, on current evidence, **the fullest and most technically developed
statement of Gaffney's employment theory in the wiki's corpus** — fuller than the
1993 "True Fiscal Stimulus" keynote already covered on
[research/gaffney-full-employment-tax-reform](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-tax-reform/),
which asserts the same underlying mechanism rhetorically to a Georgist advocacy
audience without deriving it, and earlier and more mathematically explicit than the
capital-turnover identity Gaffney uses without full derivation in his 2015 *AJES*
paper on [China's real-estate cycle](/wiki/gaffney-real-assets-model-china/).

## The Core Argument

Gaffney's thesis is that chronic unemployment alongside inflation — the
"stagflation" of the mid-1970s — is not, as both Keynesian and monetarist
economists of the day argued, an inescapable trade-off on a Phillips curve. It is
the predictable result of a tax system that makes capital and land artificially
cheap relative to labor, pushing investment toward long-lived, slow-turnover,
land-substituting forms that "combine" poorly with labor. Gaffney states the paper
in four parts:[1]

- **(A) Factor mixes are sensitive to relative factor prices** — demonstrated with
  California farm-crop data (Table 6.1: the same land yields $15/acre in grazing or
  $1,500/acre in tomatoes, depending only on labor intensity) and Fortune 500
  profits-per-employee data showing large, capital-heavy firms use markedly less
  labor per dollar of property than small ones.
- **(B) Tax bias systematically favors land and capital over labor** — an extensive
  catalogue of income-tax, property-tax, and payroll-tax provisions (expensing,
  capital-gains treatment, timber and mineral preferences, homeowner imputed-income
  exemption, the corporate income tax's bias toward internalizing capital) that
  together make hiring labor expensive relative to holding land or capital.
- **(C) Demand for capital is not sufficient — or even necessary — to make jobs**,
  because capital substitutes for labor as readily as it complements it; the
  "Keynesians say investment creates jobs, Georgists say labor produces capital"
  consensus is, Gaffney argues, a shared half-truth that both traditions have
  failed to examine.
- **(D) Factor substitution and replacement demand** — the paper's macroeconomic
  payoff: turning capital over faster (shortening its "life") is both how to
  combine more labor with a fixed capital stock *and* how to raise aggregate
  demand for labor without inflation, because faster turnover means faster
  delivery of real goods to match the income it generates.

## The Wicksell "Great Revolving Fund" Model

The paper's central macroeconomic device, developed at length in section D, is an
explicit revival of a "wages-fund" argument from Adam Smith and Mill — which
Gaffney says they left mathematically incoherent — corrected, he argues, by Knut
Wicksell: "It is only the part of capital annually set free which can purchase
labor (or land)."[1] Gaffney restates this as a national accounting identity: the
flow of income an economy's capital stock can sustain (F) equals capital (K) times
its rate of turnover (T) — "the flow (F) you can handle equals your capital (K)
times turnover" — illustrated with a worked numerical example of a baker whose
$200 of working capital, turned over daily, finances an annual payroll of $36,500,
versus the same capital tied up for a seven-year production period, where
compound interest alone consumes half of cash flow and capital "has virtually
stopped sustaining any payroll."[1] He extends the model to real institutions: the
Bell Telephone Company's 1971 capital-market call for $4.5 billion (roughly 20
percent of all new capital raised that year by American industry) to fund
electronics switching equipment that would take "another 30 years" to fully
displace older gear, and the U.S. Bureau of Reclamation's Revolving Fund, chartered
in 1902 to recoup capital every ten years, which "by now" — 1976 — "was to have
completed eight cycles... except for one problem: it has yet to complete the
first."[1]

Gaffney calls the aggregate version of this fund the economy's "**Great Revolving
Fund**": a closed system in which "the flow of income cannot exceed K·T (plus
direct services)," so that turnover — not the total stock of capital, and not the
volume of money "recycled" by fiscal or monetary policy — is what determines how
much labor a given capital stock can employ. His central critique of both
Keynesian and monetarist macroeconomics is that both treat "consumption" as an
income-creating expenditure while burying and ignoring turnover, when in fact
"replacement anticipates liquidation" and it is *replacement* spending, not gross
"investment" as conventionally measured, that generates most income: "Turnover has
its own set of determinants, including the tax biases we have surveyed... It is
the pacer, not the paced."[1] (C-claim; Gaffney's own theoretical framework,
attributed — not an empirical test of the K·T identity against national accounts
data.)

**Priority note.** This same identity — capital times turnover equals income flow
— is the technical core of Gaffney's 2015 *AJES* paper on the China property
crisis, which states it (as **K·T = F**) without full derivation and attributes
the underlying insight, without claiming novelty, to "Smith, Ricardo, Wicksell,
and Turgot."[3] This 1976 chapter is the fuller, earlier, and mathematically
worked-out statement of the identical mechanism in the wiki's corpus — see
"Bears On," below.

## The Mathematical Appendices: Factor Shares as a Function of Capital Life

Unique among the wiki's Gaffney holdings, this chapter carries four formal
appendices deriving, with calculus, how the shares of labor, land, and capital in
an investment's total return change as a function of the capital's "life" (the
holding period before it is recovered and reinvested).[1]

- **Appendix 1** derives that for both "flowing" (depreciating) and "growing"
  (appreciating) capital, labor's share of the value produced is a decreasing
  function of capital life *n* — for flowing capital, labor's share is exactly
  `I/n = (1 − e^(−in))/(in)` where *i* is the interest rate; for growing capital
  (e.g., timber), labor's share is `e^(−in)`, falling off faster. Gaffney derives
  that this holds identically whether analyzing a single investment cycle or a
  normalized "going concern" with one unit of every age in continuous production —
  a result he attributes to Wicksell's *Lectures on Political Economy* (1938 trans.,
  pp. 194–96). A worked numerical table (Table 6.5, *i* = 5%, land rent S = 800)
  shows labor's share of a tree-growing operation's receipts falling from 80% at a
  1-year cycle to under 1% at an 84-year cycle, while land's share peaks at an
  intermediate cycle length and capital's share rises monotonically.
- **Appendix 2** derives that the after-tax rate of return on growing capital is an
  increasing function of its life — the formal basis for the paper's claim that
  capital gains taxation and other preferences for long-held assets are
  systematically biased toward longer investment cycles.
- **Appendix 3** derives the key comparative-statics result behind the paper's
  policy claims: differentiating the tree's growth-value function shows a
  parcel is financially mature — ready for harvest and reinvestment — in the year
  when its marginal growth rate equals the sum of interest on its achieved value
  *and* rent on the land beneath it (`dR/dn = i(R + S)`). **Raising land rent, like
  raising interest rates, therefore shortens the optimal capital cycle** — the
  paper's most policy-relevant derived result, since it means a land tax (which
  raises the effective holding cost of land, S, in the formula) works in the same
  direction as tight money to accelerate capital turnover, without the
  contractionary side effects of tight money on capital formation itself.
- **Appendix 4** shows algebraically that inflation combined with historical-cost
  depreciation taxes short-cycle investments more heavily in real terms than
  long-cycle ones — a corrective to what Gaffney calls "an unbalanced argument"
  common at the time (1976, amid double-digit U.S. inflation) that inflation
  burdens long investments more.

(C-claim throughout; original theoretical derivations, illustrated with assumed
numerical parameters rather than estimated from data — Gaffney does not fit the
model to observed factor shares in this paper.)

## Monuments, Frontiers, and War: Three Sinks of Capital

The paper's concluding policy section names three "sinks" into which
tax-subsidized capital is diverted away from job-creating turnover: **monuments**
(public works and corporate headquarters "built with one eye on eternity," Gaffney's
examples running from the pyramids and Angkor Wat to the California Water Plan and
the Interstate Highway System, with the 1845–49 Irish famine relief roads — 570,000
men set to building roads while "half the people died of starvation" — as the
starkest historical case); **frontiers** (territorial and continental expansion, a
"reversal" of George's own logic, which Gaffney argues nineteenth-century economic
historians have shown attracted disproportionate, often premature, capital); and
**war**, which "combines the frontier fallacy and the public works syndrome and the
waste-makes-jobs doctrine into a claim on the national treasure" far exceeding the
simple cost of protection.[1] This three-way taxonomy is framed at the scale of
whole-economy capital allocation and is distinct from — not a restatement of — the
five-fold micro-level taxonomy of land-substituting capital (land-saving,
land-enhancing, land-linking, land-capturing, rent-leading) in Gaffney's later,
undated working paper already covered on
[research/gaffney-land-market-distortions](/wiki/gaffney-land-market-distortions/);
"frontiers" overlaps loosely with that paper's "land-linking" category but the 1976
"monuments" and "war" categories have no counterpart there.

## A Critique of Neo-Georgists

Gaffney directs part of the paper's argument at his own tradition: "Keynesians and
Georgists have this in common. Keynesians say investment creates jobs and Georgists
say labor produces capital... Both schools share the idea that benefits to capital
are benefits to labor." He argues this is a half-truth neo-Georgists have
"overlooked... in their zeal to untax capital" — because a great deal of the cash
flow from long-lived capital "imputes to capital as such, as interest," not labor,
so untaxing capital while continuing to tax payrolls "stacks the cards against
labor."[1] (D-claim; Gaffney's own polemical framing, attributed — a position
within Georgist debate, not adjudicated here against contemporary Georgist replies.)

## Standing and Limits

- **Genre.** A book chapter in an edited TRED conference volume, not a
  peer-reviewed journal article — the volume's other chapters were conference
  papers subject to committee discussion rather than blind referee review.
  Gaffney's own citations elsewhere describe the TRED committee's proceedings
  volumes as edited, not refereed, publications.
- **1976 macro data — dated.** The paper's real-world illustrations (Bell
  Telephone's 1971 capital raise, Kendrick's 1899–1953 capital/labor ratios,
  Commerce Department 1948–1971 corporate data, the double-digit-inflation
  backdrop) are all a half-century old; they illustrate the mechanism, not current
  conditions, and should not be read as evidence about the present-day capital/labor
  ratio or tax structure.
- **No empirical test of the K·T = F identity or the derived comparative-statics
  results.** The mathematical appendices are original derivations from assumed
  parameters (e.g., *i* = 5%, S = 800 in Table 6.5), not fits to observed data —
  the paper's own numbers illustrate the shape of the relationships, not their
  magnitude in any real economy. Gaffney's policy conclusions (tax capital
  and land more, payrolls less, to raise turnover) follow from the model given its
  assumptions; the paper offers no test of whether observed unemployment in the
  1970s U.S. economy was in fact driven by the tax-bias mechanism described, as
  opposed to the demand-management failures conventional macroeconomics blamed at
  the time.
- **Native text, no OCR.** The masongaffney.org copy (recovered this wave from a
  previously 404 stub in the mirror cache) carries a clean, `pdftotext`-extractable
  text layer (CVISION-processed scan, 2004/2005); no re-OCR was needed. Read in
  full, all 68 pages, this wave.

## I2012: Fold Check — Reverberations Between Land-Price and Banking Cycles

A separate 2012 Gaffney lecture, "Reverberations Between Immoderate Land-Price
Cycles and Banking Cycles" (AFEE, Chicago, Jan. 8, 2012, heavily footnoted by Polly
Cleveland and John Tepper Marlin), was read in full for this wave's fold-check
against [concepts/18-year-land-cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/). Its content —
Pecora-hearings history, a peace-treaty-driven "overlay" cycle distinct from
Hoyt's endogenous 18-year rhythm, and an explicit forecast of a crash "in about
2026" — is genuinely new to that page and has been folded there directly (see the
concept page's new "Gaffney (2012)" subsection), not duplicated here.[4] It shares
no content with the present 1976 paper beyond the general Georgist land-cycle
premise.

## I16: A Condensed Restatement

"Environmental Policies and Full Employment," Chapter 6 of an edited volume on the
income-distribution and allocative effects of environmental policy (undated in the
text itself; Gaffney's byline credits him as "Executive Director at the British
Columbia Institute for Economic Policy Analysis," consistent with the mid-1970s),
was skimmed in full for this fold-check.[2] Its own opening footnote identifies it
as explicitly derivative: "A more technical expansion of the ideas in this chapter
is in the writer's 'Toward Full Employment with Limited Land and Capital'... in
Arthur Lynn, Jr. (ed) *Property Taxes, Land Use and Public Policy*."[2] A
section-by-section comparison confirms the claim: I16's argument, examples (the
same California crop table, the same Fortune 500 profits-per-employee table and
appendix, the same Wicksell "Great Revolving Fund" passage, the same Mill epigraph)
and even its closing paragraph are close paraphrases or verbatim reproductions of
material in this 1976 chapter, with a light environmental-policy gloss added (e.g.
recasting "the compulsion to grow and expand" as "the compulsion to grow and expand
and pollute") and the mathematical appendices, the tax-code itemization of section
B, and most of the historical illustration cut for a popular audience. **Verdict:
declined, no fold** — I16 adds nothing this page or the wiki does not already carry
from the fuller 1976 original.

## Bears On

  — read and mined in full on a later wave (2026-07-18) after being flagged here as a
  precursor and left unchased. That five-part AJES series works the same Faustmann-
  maturity machinery this chapter's Appendix 3 formalizes as `dR/dn = i(R+S)`, but
  applied specifically to how a *tax*-driven change in site value shifts optimal
  harvest timing — six years earlier, and itself building on the 1957 Financial
  Maturity of Timber monograph. Read together, the three works form one 19-year line
  of development on a single mechanism rather than three independent treatments.
- **Research:** [Gaffney (1993), Full Employment through Total Tax Reform](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-tax-reform/)
  — this 1976 chapter is the technical foundation the 1993 keynote's "True Fiscal
  Stimulus" argument asserts rhetorically without deriving; a reader wanting the
  worked mechanism behind the later keynote's claims should start here.
- **Research:** [Gaffney (2015), A Real-Assets Model of Economic Crises](/wiki/gaffney-real-assets-model-china/)
  — priority correction: the K·T = F capital-turnover identity central to the 2015
  China paper is stated there without full derivation; this 1976 chapter is the
  earlier, fuller mathematical working of the same mechanism in Gaffney's own
  corpus (Appendices 1–3 above), decades before the China application.
- **Problem:** [High land rents suppress productivity](/wiki/high-land-rents-suppress-productivity/)
  — a further, earlier theoretical precursor (alongside the WP041/WP042 working
  papers and the 1961 time-indivisibility paper already cited there) to the
  privately-captured-rent misallocation channel: this page's Appendix 3 result
  (`dR/dn = i(R+S)`) is a formal derivation of how land rent redirects capital into
  slower-turnover, less labor-intensive forms. Cited in prose only, not added to
  that page's `supported_by` list — this is theoretical framework, not empirical
  evidence, consistent with the wiki's convention of keeping theory out of
  evidence-tier citation arrays.
