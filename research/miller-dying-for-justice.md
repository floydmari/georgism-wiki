## Overview

*Dying for Justice*, by epidemiologist George J. Miller, was published by the Centre for Land Policy Studies (London) in **2003** (ISBN 978-1-901202-04-5).[1] Fred Harrison's *[Ricardo's Law](/wiki/harrison-ricardos-law/)* (2006) describes the study as calculating that tax-induced economic inequality is associated with roughly **50,000 premature deaths per year in England and Wales**, and states the analysis was submitted to the UK Department of Health and the Treasury.[2] The 2003 date is now well-supported by three convergent sources — Miller's British Medical Journal obituary (as reproduced by the Royal College of Physicians) and both the bibliography and a footnote of *Ricardo's Law* itself — which makes the 1999 date in the AbeBooks bookseller catalogue the likely outlier/error, and dissolves the apparent mismatch between the book's date and Harrison's "around 2003" submission account.[1][2][3] The book itself is not digitized (confirmed absent from the Internet Archive and from Google Books preview) and is known here only through secondary accounts; the 50,000-deaths figure, the underlying calculation, and the departmental-submission detail all rest on Harrison's account in *Ricardo's Law* and have not been checked against a primary copy of *Dying for Justice* or the submission documents themselves.

Two further honesty points from the 2026-07-17 verification pass. First, Harrison's own text concedes the analysis was never vetted: *Ricardo's Law* states that "None of Miller's peers, some of whom were consultants to the Department of Health, considered his analysis worthy of critical evaluation" — so by the citing author's own admission, the 50,000 figure never received critical peer evaluation.[2] Second, the one fully independent account of Miller's work located — his BMJ obituary — confirms his standing as a cardiovascular epidemiologist (professor of epidemiology at Bart's and the London School of Medicine and Dentistry; co-author of a landmark 1975 *Lancet* paper on HDL cholesterol and heart disease) and confirms both land-policy books' existence and dates, but conspicuously does not mention the 50,000 figure or any government submission; his land-policy work receives a single sentence.[3]

Miller is also the author of a related work, *[On Fairness and Efficiency](/wiki/miller-on-fairness-and-efficiency/): The Privatisation of the Public Income Over the Past Millennium* (Policy Press, 2000), which Harrison cites for the same broader thesis — that privatising publicly-created land rent produces class-based health inequalities via a spatial "north-south" mortality gradient.[2] The two Miller studies together form the empirical core of Harrison's Chapter 8 argument that the distribution of rent, not merely income, drives inequalities in life expectancy.
