## Summary

*Making the Difference! The BIG in Namibia* is the assessment report of the **Otjivero-Omitara Basic Income Grant (BIG) pilot**, published in April 2009 by the Namibian **BIG Coalition** — a partnership of the Council of Churches in Namibia, the Namibian Union of Namibian Workers, the Namibian NGO Forum, and the Namibian Network of AIDS Service Organisations, with the Evangelical Lutheran Church in the Republic of Namibia's Desk for Social Development (DfSD) as legal and financial administrator, and research conducted jointly with the Labour Resource and Research Institute (LaRRI). Starting in January 2008, every registered resident of Otjivero-Omitara (a settlement roughly 100 km east of Windhoek) under age 60 received an unconditional monthly cash grant of N$100 (about US$12–13 at the time), described by the Coalition as "the first universal cash-transfer pilot project in the world." This entry reads the report's Executive Summary, Section 1 (project design and methodology), and Section 2 (impact assessment) in full.

The pilot originated from the Namibian government's own 2002 Tax Commission (NAMTAX) recommendation for a national BIG financed by a progressive expenditure tax; the Coalition organized the pilot to demonstrate that recommendation in practice, explicitly as advocacy for a national rollout. The report was assembled from four data sources: a November 2007 baseline survey (before the first payout), panel re-surveys in July and November 2008, key-informant interviews, and individual case studies.

## The Core Argument / Findings

The report's headline claims, drawn from its Executive Summary, are extensive. Using the food poverty line, the share of residents below it fell from 76% (November 2007) to 37% one year into the grant, and to 16% among households not affected by in-migration. The share of adults engaged in income-generating activity rose from 44% to 55%, which the report frames as direct evidence against the prediction that unconditional cash induces "laziness and dependency." Using a WHO weight-for-age measure, child underweight prevalence fell from 42% (November 2007) to 17% (June 2008) to 10% (November 2008). School drop-out rates fell from nearly 40% to about 5% and then near 0%, and the share of parents paying school fees rose from roughly 40% to 90%. Clinic usage rose enough that the settlement clinic's fee income increased fivefold. Overall crime reported to the local police fell 42%, with stock theft down 43% and other theft down nearly 20%. Household debt fell (from a mean N$1,215 to N$772) while savings, proxied by livestock ownership, rose. The report also reports qualitative findings on reduced transactional sex and no measured increase in alcoholism, and estimates a national BIG would cost N$1.2–1.6 billion/year (2.2–3% of GDP), which it argues is affordable given Namibia's tax-collection rate (below 25%) sits well under its estimated 30%-of-GDP tax capacity.

## Relation to the Georgist Case

The Namibian BIG is not rent-funded: it was funded by voluntary donor and church contributions during the pilot, and the report's own national-rollout financing proposal centers on VAT and income-tax adjustments, with "a special levy on natural resources" mentioned only as one option among several for a hypothetical national program — a passing gesture toward resource-rent financing, not a worked design. Its relevance to the [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) lane is therefore as a **transfer-design and delivery-mechanics case study** — what an unconditional, universal, small cash grant does to poverty, health, and local economic activity at community scale — not as evidence that rent capture specifically can fund such a grant. Georgist advocates citing this pilot should be explicit that it demonstrates (contestedly, see below) the behavioral case for universality and unconditionality, while the funding-source case for a rent-financed dividend rests on separate evidence (Alaska's Permanent Fund, resource-royalty literature).

## Nuances and Limits — Steelmanning Both Sides

**The pilot's own admitted limitation.** The report states plainly in its methodology section that "ideally, a survey of other areas at the same time should have been conducted ('control group'). However, this is not only statistically very difficult, given the particular features of Otjivero-Omitara, but also ethically problematic." No control village was surveyed; before/after comparison within Otjivero-Omitara is the report's only design.

**In-migration confounds the denominators.** The report itself documents this: a 27% in-migration rate into Otjivero-Omitara during the pilot period (against 16% out-migration), driven by people moving to the settlement specifically to access the grant even though migrants themselves were not eligible. Per-capita grant income fell from N$89/month to N$67/month as household sizes grew from in-migration. The report attempts to control for this by separately reporting figures for "households without substantial migration" (e.g., the 16% food-poverty figure above), but this splits an already-small sample (roughly 1,000 registered residents) into smaller, self-selected subgroups, and the report does not fully resolve how comparable migrant and non-migrant households were on other dimensions.

**Osterkamp's published critique.** Economist **Rigmar Osterkamp** (who was based at the University of Namibia, 2007–2011) published "The Basic Income Grant Pilot Project in Namibia: A Critical Assessment" (*Basic Income Studies*, 2013) and an earlier popular-press version ("Lessons from Failure," *D+C – Development and Cooperation*, 2013). Osterkamp's critique targets both the pilot's methodology and its use in policy advocacy: beyond the missing control group (which he calls a departure from "the international norm"), he flags specific figures as implausible on their face — a reported ~29% per-capita income increase that would exceed China's growth rate several times over against Namibia's actual 4.4% average GDP growth (2000–2008); a reported 38% household savings rate that he judges "hard to believe" for households this poor; and rapid malnutrition improvements over six months that he calls "astonishing" relative to the roughly N$15/month of additional food spending the grant implies per malnourished child. He also criticizes the Coalition for declining "to make its data publicly available" for independent re-analysis, a departure he says from international research-transparency norms, and for not engaging Namibian academic economists or holding academic conferences on the project over its multi-year run. His overall assessment: "the methodological shortcomings mean that the project does not provide any serious information on what the scheme actually achieved" [VERIFY: exact wording — quoted from a secondary summary of the paywalled *Basic Income Studies* article, not the original text directly]. Independent commentary responding to Osterkamp has also pushed back on parts of his own analysis — noting, for instance, that an arithmetic-mean poverty calculation he used has been argued to bias his comparison — so the exchange itself remains contested rather than a clean rebuttal in either direction.

**Both readings have force.** The BIG Coalition's design choices (no control group, no independent data release) are defensible on the ethical and practical grounds the report states — a single-settlement advocacy pilot with limited funds could not ethically withhold a poverty-alleviating grant from a matched control village nearby, and doing so raises its own contamination risks in a small, mobile rural population. But those same choices are exactly what make the headline percentage changes unsuitable as a rigorous causal estimate: before/after comparison in a single, migration-affected community, run and reported by the grant's own advocates, cannot rule out concurrent trends, migration-driven composition change, or reporting bias, especially given the report was produced to build the case for a national rollout the Coalition supported.

## Bears On

This is advocacy-run pilot evidence and should be weighted accordingly wherever cited. It provides descriptive, contested support for the general behavioral case that unconditional cash transfers can reduce poverty and improve child health and school attendance without inducing "dependency" — the same claim [Forget's Mincome study](/wiki/forget-mincome-town-with-no-poverty/) examines with a stronger (though still imperfect) quasi-experimental design in a different context. It does not bear on the funding-source question central to [Rent dividends reduce poverty and inequality](/wiki/rent-dividends-reduce-poverty/), since the pilot was donor-funded, not rent-funded.
