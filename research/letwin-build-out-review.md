## Summary

Commissioned at Budget 2017 and presented to Parliament in October 2018 (Cm 9720),
the **Independent Review of Build Out** by the Rt Hon Sir **Oliver Letwin** MP was
tasked, in its terms of reference, to "explain the significant gap between housing
completions and the amount of land allocated or permissioned in areas of high housing
demand, and make recommendations for closing it." It is the UK government's most
authoritative investigation of *why permissioned developable land in high-demand areas
is built out slowly* — the mainstream counterpart to the Georgist land-banking
critique, reaching a partly different diagnosis.

The review focused on the largest sites, because "the largest sites are dominated by
the major house builders... and it is in relation to these major firms that concern has
been expressed in some quarters about 'land banking' and 'intentional delay'."

## Findings

**Build out is strikingly slow on large sites.** Letwin reports that "the median build
out period on the large sites I investigated was 15.5 years," equivalent to a median of
just **6.5% of the site built out each year**. Cross-checked against a Molior dataset
for London supplied by the Mayor, he found the sample was, if anything, fast: "The
median percentage annual build out rate for London sites of over 1,000 homes in the
Molior data-set was 3.2%." Very large, fully permissioned sites in the highest-demand
market in Britain thus release only a few percent of their homes per year.

**The cause is the market absorption rate, not speculation on land price.** Letwin's
central conclusion: "the homogeneity of the types and tenures of the homes on offer on
these sites, and the limits on the rate at which the market will absorb such homogenous
products, are the fundamental drivers of the slow rate of build out." Builders release
homes at the rate the local market will buy them without a fall in price; selling faster
would require cutting prices, which Letwin judged would "create very serious problems
not only for the major house builders but also, potentially, for prices and financing in
the housing market." His prescription is therefore diversity of product and tenure on
large sites (new planning rules for sites above a size threshold), so that complementary
absorption rates across housing categories raise the overall build-out rate — not price
controls and not, primarily, anti-land-banking enforcement.

**Other constraints are secondary.** Reviewing transport, remediation, utilities,
logistics, capital, materials and labour, Letwin concluded none would bind if the
absorption-rate constraint were removed — "with one exception... namely, the
availability of skilled labour" (notably bricklayers).

## Why It Matters

The review documents, with government authority, that a large stock of fully
permissioned developable land in the highest-demand English markets is deliberately held
in a state of slow build-out — the "significant gap" between permissions and completions.
That supports the claim that
[valuable land is used well below its potential](/wiki/speculative-vacancy-wastes-cities/)
even where demand is most acute, and it corroborates
[Prosper Australia's](/wiki/prosper-speculative-vacancies/) land-banking argument that
"developers strategically delay financially feasible developments" to protect
staged-release revenue.

Crucially, Letwin refines the *mechanism*. He did not find that builders withhold land
betting on future price appreciation (the crude land-banking thesis); he found they
meter out completions to avoid depressing the price of a homogenous product. That is a
form of strategic supply restriction driven by market power over local price — closer to
the [option-value](/wiki/cunningham-seattle-options/) and
[speculative-vacancy](/wiki/speculative-vacancy/) logic than to simple hoarding, and it
sits alongside, not against, the zoning-constraint reading of urban underuse.

## Limits

- Letwin explicitly rejects the simplest "land banking for capital gain" story; citing
  this review as proof of *speculative* withholding overstates it. Its finding is that
  build-out is metered to the absorption rate — a price-protection behaviour, not a bet
  on appreciation.
- Scope is England's *large permissioned sites*; it does not measure vacant dwellings or
  price the underuse of already-developed parcels (see
  [Gyourko & Krimmel](/wiki/gyourko-krimmel-zoning-tax/) for the latter).
- It is a policy review, not a peer-reviewed causal study, though its build-out data are
  quantitative and cross-checked.
