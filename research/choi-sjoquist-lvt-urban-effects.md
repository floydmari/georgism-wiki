## Summary

Ki-Whan Choi and **David L. Sjoquist** (Andrew Young School of Policy Studies, Georgia State University) published "Economic and Spatial Effects of Land Value Taxation in an Urban Area: An Urban Computable General Equilibrium Approach" in *Land Economics*, Vol. 91, No. 3 (August 2015), pp. 536–555 (DOI: [10.3368/le.91.3.536](https://doi.org/10.3368/le.91.3.536)).[1] The paper builds an urban computable general equilibrium (CGE) model calibrated to the Atlanta, Georgia metropolitan area to simulate the effects of switching from a conventional capital (improvements-inclusive) property tax to a pure land value tax.[1]

The authors describe their model as extending prior LVT simulation studies in four ways: residents are modeled as owning a fixed amount of land themselves, rather than assuming an absentee landowner class; three income groups are considered rather than one; cases where housing capital is not perfectly mobile are modeled explicitly; and households are given a labor-leisure choice rather than a fixed labor supply.[1]

## Relation to the Georgist Case

Jacob Doucet's survey book *[Land Is a Big Deal](/wiki/land-is-a-big-deal/)* (Ch. 21) — the discovery source for this page — lists Choi & Sjoquist (2015) among more than a dozen empirical and simulation studies it characterizes as supporting **full capitalization** of a land value tax into land prices, i.e., evidence for the claim that [landlords cannot pass a land value tax on to tenants](/wiki/landlords-cannot-pass-lvt-to-tenants/).[2] This session did not obtain the paywalled full text of the paper directly, so the specific quantitative capitalization and spatial (density/welfare) results are not reproduced here. ## Significance

As a CGE-based simulation study (rather than a natural-experiment or panel-regression design like the Pennsylvania split-rate literature), this paper adds a different methodological angle to the incidence and welfare literature on land value taxation, and its Atlanta calibration extends the geographic range of that literature beyond the Pennsylvania and Pittsburgh cases that dominate US empirical LVT research.
