## Overview

"Soil Depletion and Land Rent" is a January 1965 paper by Mason Gaffney,
published in the *Natural Resources Journal* (University of New Mexico School of
Law), volume 4, issue 3, pages 537-557, while Gaffney was Chairman of the
Department of Economics at the University of Wisconsin, Milwaukee.[1] Gaffney
describes it as "an outgrowth of a larger study of the meaning and function of
land rent" — a 1962 chapter, "Ground Rent and the Allocation of Land among
Firms" — and explicitly scopes it as "not represented as more than a
subspecialized monograph focused on its fragment of the wider topic."[1] The
paper opens by noting a mid-century reversal in farmland economics: after decades
of decline, US farm real-estate values had risen from $97 billion to $155 billion
since 1953 (a 58% increase), the land share of farm real estate had risen to 74%,
and the annual increment to farm land values alone equaled 30-40% of net farm
operating income — evidence, Gaffney argues, that agricultural land rent was
again a serious and topical subject, not a residual curiosity.[1]

The paper's central question: **when farm soil fertility is being depleted (used
up, not merely used), how much of the landholder's income is still properly
"rent" — and how much is really depletion of capital, disguised as income?**
This is the same question the [land-is-just-capital
objection](/wiki/land-is-just-capital/) raises in its "Limits and Caveats"
section — that depletable natural resources "genuinely are 'used up,' in tension
with the 'permanent, non-depreciating' description" of land — and Gaffney's
paper is a sophisticated, worked-out economic answer to exactly that tension,
eight years before this wiki's other Gaffney forestry material and a decade
before his major severance-tax writing.

## Four Economic Categories of Soil Fertility

Gaffney rejects the binary "fund vs. flow" distinction economists had inherited
as too coarse for soil, and proposes four categories, ranked by permanence:[1]

1. **The matrix** — "the enduring matrix of the ground, with its location,
   subsoil and drainage, macro-relief, climate," plus nutrients so abundant
   relative to withdrawal as to be effectively inexhaustible. The matrix
   "maintains itself without human aid, and despite human neglect and abuse
   incident to farm operations" — it is humanly indestructible in ordinary use
   (though not against deliberate destruction like strip mining). **All matrix
   income is rent.**
2. **The conservable flow** — soil elements it is *possible* but not
   *economical* to destroy: elements whose "emplaced value exceeds their
   liquidation value," so a rational owner conserves them. Virgin humus is
   Gaffney's example. **Income from conservable-flow elements is rent, net of
   conservation costs.**
3. **The revolving fund** — nutrients (nitrogen, potassium, phosphorus, calcium)
   that leave the soil embodied in crops and are economically replaced from
   off-site — "best likened to an inventory." **Income imputable to revolving
   funds is *not* rent** — it is "the return to an improvement to the site,
   quite analogous to a return on the capital tied up in stores of silage."[1]
4. **The expendable surplus** — finite fund resources not economical to renew
   (e.g., topsoil depth beyond root reach). Gaffney's key technical result: the
   correct depletion charge on an expendable surplus is **not** its current
   liquidation value, but the *discounted value of the most remote future
   liquidation receipt* — because withdrawing a unit this year sacrifices only
   the terminal (far-future) unit, not next year's, the way a squeeze of
   toothpaste "does not constitute a sacrifice of tomorrow night's supply."[1]
   Consequently, **most of an expendable surplus's liquidation value is still
   properly rent**, with only a small, heavily discounted depletion allowance
   subtracted.

## The Central Finding: Depletion Rarely Displaces Much Rent

The paper's conclusion states the upshot plainly: "depletion of virgin
fertility, or the possibility of it, does not ordinarily detract much from
ground rent. Matrix income is all rent. So is conservable flow income, only net
of conservation costs... Expendable surpluses yield liquidation values from
which only a small depletion charge... is properly deductable, so that their
liquidation yields are largely a species of rent income."[1] Only revolving-fund
income — the genuinely capital-like, replenished-by-investment portion of soil
income — is not rent at all. "Only when the economic exhaustion of a geologic
fund is imminent does the depletion element loom larger than the income
element," at which point Gaffney recommends "the gradual or partial replacement
of ad valorem by severance taxes" as exhaustion approaches — a specific,
early (1965) design principle anticipating his later severance-tax writing on
California oil.[1][2]

**Extension to oil and minerals.** Gaffney explicitly flags, without developing,
the implication of his depletion-allowance logic for extractive industries:
"the extended implications of that simple conclusion in relation to mines and
oil reserves should be exciting. If the conclusion is valid, oil depletion
allowances as presently reckoned are even more of an outrage than we already
know them to be for other reasons... Oil and mines are able to bear much higher
taxes, both income and ad valorem, than is currently believed" — before
explicitly declining to pursue the point further ("we are overstepping the
bounds of this brief paper, and will close that toothpaste tube until a
sequel").[1] This is a direct theoretical anticipation of the argument Gaffney
would make in detail decades later in his California severance-tax essay: that
generous depletion allowances understate the true rent extractive industries
can bear.[2]

## Conservation vs. Maintenance: Land Is Not Depreciating Capital

The paper's most direct bearing on the land-is-just-capital debate is Gaffney's
extended contrast between **soil conservation** and **building maintenance** —
two activities that superficially look alike (both involve spending to preserve
an asset's value against decay) but that Gaffney argues are economically
opposite in structure:

> "Thus structures deteriorate with time, which nature uses to undermine the
> work of man; soils deteriorate with use, whereby man undermines the work of
> nature. Maintenance is man's struggle against time and nature; conservation
> is his struggle against use, and himself."[1]

Buildings decay simply by existing — "all structures, all human products, are
something of an affront to the elements, which gnaw at them continually" —
whereas virgin soil, being "already the product of long decomposition," has
"reached the angle of repose" and will sustain itself in perpetuity *unless*
actively used up; "time works toward conservation, since time lets Nature work
to restore the status quo ante hominem."[1] Buildings also obsolesce — they
compete with "an endless outpouring of more modern ones" — while soils, absent
deliberate destruction, are "not generally subject to Schumpeter's 'gale of
creative destruction.'"[1] Gaffney's practical test for whether soil income is
genuinely rent: would the landholder still supply the resource, in the long run,
if the full imputed rent were charged as an uncontingent periodic tax? For
matrix and conservable-flow elements, yes — a rent-style tax "would, if
anything, sharpen his incentives to adopt optimal conservation practices,"
because it does not decline as depletion occurs, so the owner's income falls
faster if he liquidates than if he conserves.[1]

**On Ricardo's "indestructible" soil.** Gaffney directly defends Ricardo against
the charge of naivety on this point: "Ricardo is often accused of fundamentalism
in describing soil as 'indestructible.' He may be more the victim of a hasty
turn of phrase than a true extremist" — the four-category taxonomy is offered as
a rigorous cash-value replacement for Ricardo's loose shorthand, not a
repudiation of it.[1] This bears directly on the [land-is-just-capital
objection](/wiki/land-is-just-capital/)'s citation of Ricardo's "nonreproducible...
fixed in supply" formulation: Gaffney's paper shows the formulation survives
close economic scrutiny of the hardest case (exhaustible agricultural fertility)
rather than merely asserting it does.

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** This is a **peer-reviewed law-journal article** (*Natural
Resources Journal*, a University of New Mexico School of Law publication) —
higher in the source-quality hierarchy than most of Gaffney's other essays and
addresses this wiki covers, though a law review rather than an economics
journal, and its theoretical apparatus (the four-category taxonomy, the
discounted-remote-liquidation depletion rule) is Gaffney's own proposed
framework, not an empirically estimated or independently replicated result — a
C-claim (theoretical), not a B-claim (empirical). **Citation count established
(2026-07-18):** Semantic Scholar's paper-search API records **21 citations**
for Gaffney's "Soil Depletion and Land Rent" — a modest but real independent
reception history for a 1965 law-journal paper, corroborating (not merely
inferring from venue) the standing already noted from its explicit citation in
later Georgist-adjacent scholarship (Dwyer 2014, AJES).[3][4]

## Bears On

- **Objection:** [\"Land Is Just a Form of Capital\"](/wiki/land-is-just-capital/)
  — strengthens the Response side of the objection with a worked economic
  answer to the depletable-resources caveat in the page's Limits and Caveats
  section: even exhaustible soil fertility remains overwhelmingly rent, not
  capital return, once "conservable flow" and "expendable surplus" income are
  correctly separated from genuinely capital-like "revolving fund" income, and
  Gaffney's conservation-vs-maintenance contrast gives an independent economic
  argument (not just Ricardo's assertion) for why land's depreciation profile
  differs categorically from a building's.
- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — supplies the
  theoretical groundwork, a decade before Gaffney's severance-tax writing, for
  why extractive resources (oil, minerals) can bear substantially higher
  ad valorem and income taxation than conventional depletion-allowance
  practice assumes, and a specific design principle (shift from ad valorem
  toward severance taxation as exhaustion becomes imminent) the concept page's
  existing Gaffney material does not carry.
