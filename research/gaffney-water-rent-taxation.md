## Overview

This page covers two Gaffney works on water, read together because they form
a matched pair spanning his career, applying the same rent-capture logic
already documented on the wiki for land, minerals, oil and gas, and timber to
a resource domain the wiki has not yet covered at all: **water rights**.
[Concepts/resource-rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) names water in its opening
definition of resource types but, before this page, developed none of it —
this page closes that gap.

**"Diseconomies Inherent in Western Water Laws: A California Case Study"**
(read before the Western Agricultural Economics Research Council, Tucson,
Arizona, January 23, 1961; published in *Economic Analysis of Multiple Use*,
Report No. 9 in the Water and Range Resources series, Western Agricultural
Economics Research Council, pp. 55–82) is an early, intensely empirical case
study — written while Gaffney was on the University of Missouri faculty, nine
months **before** "The Unwieldy Time-Dimension of Space" (October 1961),
making it the earliest-dated professional/academic paper so far identified
in the wiki's Gaffney corpus after the 1957 timber monograph and the 1958
*Yearbook of Agriculture* essay (see [priority note](#priority-note-earliest-dated-items-in-the-corpus)
below and the cross-reference on
[people/mason-gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/)). `[VERIFY: OCR quality —

**"The Taxable Surplus in Water Resources"** (*Contemporary Policy Issues*
10, October 1992, pp. 74–82, Western Economic Association International — a
revised version of a paper delivered at the WEAI's 66th annual conference,
Seattle, July 1, 1991) is Gaffney's mature synthesis, written thirty-one
years later while he was at UC Riverside: a systematic proposal to tax water
withdrawals, structured as a rebuttal of six named fallacies obstructing the
idea. Native, cleanly `pdftotext`-extractable text — no OCR needed.

Between them, the two essays cover the full arc: an empirical diagnosis of
how legal doctrine (not scarcity or technology) misallocates a rent-bearing
resource, and a mature policy proposal for how to fix it.

## Part I: Diseconomies Inherent in Western Water Laws (1961)

### The Kaweah River as a Natural Experiment

Gaffney studies the Kaweah River distributary system in the southeastern San
Joaquin Valley (largely Tulare County, California) — over twenty separate
water-user organizations sharing water from a single alluvial-cone system,
with unusually complete daily-diversion records (California Division of
Water Resources Bulletins 49, 49A, 49B, 1920–55) and low transportation and
water-quality complications, making it, in his framing, close to a
controlled test of whether water law allocates a shared resource
efficiently.[1]

### Wide Dispersion in the Marginal Value of Water

Gaffney assembles several independent lines of evidence to show the marginal
productivity of water varies enormously *between adjacent, physically
connected users on the same system* — a direct sign of misallocation, since
an efficient market would equalize marginal returns:

- **Conservation effort as a revealed-value proxy.** The Lindsay-Strathmore
  Irrigation District (citrus, in the "thermal zone") pumped water over 200
  feet, distributed it under pressure through steel pipe for sprinklers, and
  paid $8.14 per delivered acre-foot in tolls and taxes (1949) — one of the
  most elaborate conservation systems of any California irrigation district.
  The Tulare Irrigation District, a few miles away in the delta, lost roughly
  50% of diverted water through a long unlined ditch, with an "irrigation
  efficiency" independently estimated at .39 by state engineers — Gaffney is
  explicit this reflects rational behavior *given* the legal framework, not
  incompetence: "It is rather that framework itself which is under
  examination here."[1]
- **Value of output per acre-foot.** Navel-orange land in the thermal zone
  grossed roughly $300 per acre-foot of water versus roughly $30 for the
  Consolidated Peoples' Ditch Company's delta service area (barley,
  pasture) — a better than tenfold gap on adjacent, physically shiftable
  water. Net of variable costs, marginal water value ran $200–$500/acre-foot
  for navels versus roughly $10–$40 for cotton, alfalfa, barley, and
  pasture.[1]
- **Land-value response to water access.** Reliable water access in the
  thermal citrus zone added roughly $500–$800 an acre to raw land otherwise
  worth $50–$100 (dry, grazing value) — a direct land-value capitalization
  of water rent, the same mechanism the wiki documents for land access and
  transit access elsewhere, here applied to water specifically.[1]
- **A physically costless transfer blocked by law alone.** Lindsay-Strathmore
  had bought shares in the water-rich Consolidated Peoples' Ditch Company and
  an easement to shift water to itself at zero physical transfer cost in
  1928 — and was stopped by a court injunction (*Consolidated Peoples' Ditch
  Co. v. Foothill Ditch Co.*, 1928).[1] Gaffney's point: the barrier was
  **only** legal, not physical or economic.

### Water Rights Are Assigned by Doctrine, Not Productivity

Gaffney's structural diagnosis is that none of California's three water-right
doctrines allocates water toward its highest-value use:

- **Riparian rights**, tied to land fronting a natural channel, happened to
  attach the Kaweah's surface water to lands with the *shallowest* pump
  lifts and *least* need for it — "riparian rights attach surface waters to
  the lands that need them least."[1]
- **Appropriative rights** ("first in time, first in right") reward early
  diversion regardless of soil quality: fast-developing, low-capital uses
  (hay, grain) staked early claims on good water, while high-value,
  high-capital uses (citrus orchards) that took longer to develop — and
  ultimately generated far higher marginal productivity — arrived too late
  to get it. Worse, the "doctrine of relation" (priority dated to *first
  claim*, not first *use*) rewards premature, inflated claims: "a cost to
  society — withdrawing water — is made a revenue to the appropriator,"
  which Gaffney calls perverse once water is scarce rather than
  superabundant.
- **Correlative rights** (percolating groundwater) similarly tie use to
  overlying land rather than value, and on the Kaweah the courts had denied
  permission to export groundwater from low- to high-marginal-productivity
  parcels even where the law in principle allowed "surplus" transfers.

### The Dynamic Pattern: Monumental Projects, the Price Umbrella, and Logrolling

Gaffney's second major argument is that water law does not merely freeze a
bad initial allocation — it actively **biases new development toward
large, remote, publicly financed projects** over cheap local/marginal
adjustment, because courts will not sanction small local reallocations
("Justice… is more to be likened to the near-sighted Mr. Magoo") but will
recognize "surplus" once a project reaches far enough afield. He traces a
self-reinforcing cycle distinct from anything already on the wiki's Gaffney
corpus:

1. **Indivisibility of scale.** A remote import must be large to justify
   canal-capacity economies, to muster the political support needed for
   water rights and financing, and because service areas typically include
   dry land that must be served too — producing, on the Kaweah's own case,
   a Central Valley Project increment several times the *entire* pre-existing
   local water supply.
2. **Slow response, long lags.** Lindsay-Strathmore's wells first struck
   toxic boron brine in 1913; Central Valley Project water did not reach
   them until 1951 — a 38-year lag, during which the area's citrus
   development was "arrested completely" and competing regions filled the
   gap.
3. **The price umbrella.** Because private land development lags decades
   behind the public works meant to serve it, land prices in a newly
   watered area stay artificially high relative to the abundance of raw
   land the new water supply implies — enticing *more* competing project
   starts than markets can ultimately absorb.
4. **Racing for water rights.** Any region seeking "surplus" water elsewhere
   provokes defensive claim-staking by others, incentivizing premature
   development purely to establish a use history — "the surest means… is to
   begin developing waters to establish a history of use."
5. **Logrolling.** Because monumental interregional transfers exceed local
   finances, they require state/federal subsidy, which is secured through
   political bargaining — "there must be something for everyone" — and, to
   preserve bargaining power, projects are deliberately started before
   completion of earlier ones, suppressing the visible impact of the first
   until the next is already underway. Gaffney explicitly likens this
   mechanism to Indiana's 1836 canal-boom bankruptcy — a distinct
   historical land/infrastructure-boom-and-bust parallel from the ones
   already on [gaffney-land-booms-destroy-capital](/wiki/gaffney-land-booms-destroy-capital/),
   applied here to water infrastructure rather than land credit.

Gaffney documents seven concurrent, mutually reinforcing California
subsidy/financing channels feeding this overexpansion circa 1960 (rising
value of tax-exempt local-bond status; new state Small Projects Act and
Davis-Grunsky Act subsidies; power companies trading free water for
Irrigation Districts' property-tax immunity; Army Engineers water projects
under flood-control cover; the Bureau of Reclamation's Central Valley
Project; the pending $1.75 billion Feather River Project; and a 1961
Interior Department pledge of still more federal funding) — while noting no
comparable stimulus existed for private on-farm improvement, so public
capacity chronically outran private demand for it, a mirror image of the
usual "underinvestment in public goods" framing (he cites Galbraith by name
to distinguish his argument from it). He closes by naming 1893 and 1929 as
the two prior US land collapses in which excessive water-supply overbuilding
for undeveloped land played a leading role, warning that by 1961 the same
pattern was recurring a third time.

## Part II: The Taxable Surplus in Water Resources (1992)

### The Proposal

Gaffney proposes a package of severance, net-proceeds, property, transfer,
and gains taxes and/or rental charges on water withdrawals and power drops,
paralleling the resource-rent instruments already documented for oil, gas,
minerals, and timber elsewhere on the wiki. He frames the tax as a price
"charged by the owner of water (the state) for using its property" — the
same public-landlord logic as [Alaska's ad valorem
charge](/wiki/gaffney-alaska-oil-leasing/) and the [mineral-leasing
essays](/wiki/gaffney-mineral-leasing-tax-reform/), applied to a renewable
rather than exhaustible resource — and argues it would simultaneously (i)
spur holders of surplus water to sell, (ii) foster conservation, (iii)
convert water from a fiscal "sink" to a source of state revenue, (iv) offset
concentrated water holdings, and (v) integrate ground- and surface-water
economies under one uniform incentive.

### Six Fallacies Dispelled

1. **"Water rights are real property."** Most state constitutions (Gaffney
   quotes California Water Code §102: "All water within the State is the
   property of the people of the State") hold water in public trust; a
   license is a revocable privilege — analogous to an airline's time slot or
   a broadcaster's frequency — not a deed. He traces the false equation
   partly to the "'Forty-niner fallacy": conflating today's large,
   perpetual appropriative claims with the historically narrow, time-limited
   ("ten feet square") placer-mining claims that originated the
   first-in-time doctrine.
2. **"A water charge would be passed on to consumers."** Standard
   tax-shifting doctrine holds for price-takers in competitive markets, but
   water bears **economic rent** — it "arises naturally where demand
   exceeds supply" — so a tax on the rent component is non-shiftable in the
   same way a land-value tax is.
3. **"Water is worth what it costs to develop."** Gaffney contrasts the
   $20/acre-foot cost of Gage Canal water (Santa Ana River to Riverside)
   against $240/acre-foot the State charges for imported water at the same
   city line, and up to roughly $2,000/acre-foot as the true opportunity
   cost at the system's outer margin — a **100-fold** gap between price and
   social cost he attributes to hidden and cross-subsidies ("the books are
   cooked").
4. **"Firm up property rights and the market will provide."** Gaffney names
   four specific reasons water markets fail on faith alone: some access is
   effectively an unalienable birthright best handled as a state revenue
   duty rather than private sale; water distribution is a natural monopoly
   requiring centralized, regulated integration (the same argument applied
   to power, gas, and telecoms); sellers of water are chronically
   **undermotivated** because water permits carry no debt and no property
   tax, so holders face no cash-drain pressure to sell (unlike the recorded,
   debt-financed real-estate market); and the prior-appropriation doctrine
   is "the locus classicus of rent-seeking," incentivizing premature,
   half-used diversion purely to establish a claim to future rents — a
   distinct instance of the wiki's general rent-seeking mechanism, applied
   here to water-claim staking rather than to land, farm-subsidy, or
   resource-leasing rent-seeking. He also critiques 1980s–90s water
   marketing as typically "win-win-lose" (the public loses) rather than
   genuinely win-win, since sold permits retroactively validate what he
   frames as an original private seizure of public domain, subsidies
   attached.
5. **"Only net consumptive use is a social cost."** Using an entropy
   framing, Gaffney argues that elevation and quality losses are real social
   costs even where water is technically "returned" to a stream — a point
   with a formal water-specific content (loss of hydropower/gravity-delivery
   potential) not analogous to anything already on the wiki's land or
   mineral rent-taxation pages.
6. **"Common rights must spell tragedy."** Gaffney separates the empirical
   fact of aquifer overdraft from the standard "tragedy of the commons"
   causal story, arguing that asserting a common right need not mean open,
   unrestricted access — it can instead mean a state revenue-collection duty
   (a withdrawal tax being one of five examples he gives, alongside bag
   limits, effluent charges, timber-harvest regulation, and boating
   restrictions) — a position that reframes Hardin's tragedy as a
   **property-design failure**, not an inherent flaw of common ownership,
   consistent with the Georgist reading of the commons elsewhere on the wiki.

## Priority Note: Earliest-Dated Items in the Corpus

With H3 now read in full, the wiki's Gaffney corpus contains four
chronologically distinct "earliest" claims that must be kept separate,
extending the disambiguation already made for E1 on
[people/mason-gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/):

- **Earliest work overall:** the 1957 timber/Faustmann-formula monograph —
  see [gaffney-financial-maturity-timber](/wiki/gaffney-financial-maturity-timber/)
  — a technical capital-theory question, not land or water policy.
- **Earliest land-policy item:** "Urban Expansion — Will It Ever Stop?"
  (1958) — see the note on [people/mason-gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/).
- **Earliest professional/academic conference paper, and earliest water
  economics item:** "Diseconomies Inherent in Western Water Laws" (delivered
  January 23, 1961) — this page.
- **Earliest theoretical land-economics AJES paper**, and the earliest
  known statement of the credit-concentration "strong hands" mechanism
  specifically: "The Unwieldy Time-Dimension of Space" (October 1961) — see
  [gaffney-time-dimension-of-space](/wiki/gaffney-time-dimension-of-space/).
  H3 does **not** state the strong-hands mechanism (its concentration
  argument runs through legal-doctrine misallocation, not differential
  credit access), so this priority attribution is unaffected.

## Standing and Limits

Both essays are Gaffney's own advocacy/analytical work, not third-party
peer review of his claims. **H3 (1961)** is a single-river-system case study
(the Kaweah) generalized by assertion ("diseconomies of kinds and degrees
that in my observation prevail throughout the Valley and the State") rather
than by a multi-system statistical sample; its dollar figures come from a
mix of Gaffney's own fieldwork, state engineering bulletins, and other
economists' contemporary studies (Adams, Horn, Brewer), and — per the
`[VERIFY]` flag above — its text was recovered via fresh OCR of a poor
legacy scan rather than a native text layer, so exact figures should be
treated as approximate. **H21 (1992)** is a synthesis/advocacy essay for a
policy-issues journal, not an econometric study of California's water
market; its price comparisons ($20 vs. $240 vs. ~$2,000 per acre-foot) rest
on Gaffney's own estimate and a single cited Alan Post 1982 state task-force
effort, and Gaffney is candid that "no one is able to figure the cost to the
penny because the books are cooked" — an admission of estimation
uncertainty the wiki preserves rather than smooths over. Neither essay
offers a modern (post-1990s) empirical test of water-market performance
under the marketing reforms California adopted after 1992; that update is
outside this page's scope.

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — develops water for
  the first time as a resource-rent domain, adding an empirical
  misallocation case study and a six-fallacy taxation proposal structurally
  parallel to the wiki's existing oil/gas, mineral, and timber
  rent-capture material.
- **Benefit:** [Capturing resource rent works — where institutions are
  strong](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/) — an eighth institutional-
  failure mode, distinct from the seven already documented: water rights
  assigned by legal doctrine (riparian/appropriative/correlative priority)
  rather than by price or productivity, with a specific mechanism (the
  "price umbrella"/racing/logrolling cycle) for how that doctrine actively
  drives systematic public overinvestment in remote water-supply projects.
