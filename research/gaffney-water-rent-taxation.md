## Overview

This page covers Gaffney's water corpus, read together because it spans his
entire career and applies the same rent-capture logic already documented on
the wiki for land, minerals, oil and gas, and timber to a resource domain the
wiki had not covered at all before this page: **water rights**.
[Concepts/resource-rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) names water in its opening
definition of resource types but, before this page, developed none of it —
this page closes that gap. The page's core is the matched 1961/1992 pair
below (Parts I–II); a second triage pass the same session read the rest of
the H-series water cluster and folded in five more works that survived a
strict-delta read against that core — a direct scholarly debate over the
1961 case study (H4), a systematic 1968 doctrine comparison and
irrigation-district theory (H8), a 1973 congressional critique of the water
giveaway (H18, light addition), a 1977 water-market design note (H19), a
1989/91 conference paper contributing two distinct facts (H20), and — as
the largest single addition — a 1997 mature synthesis on why voluntary
water marketing has kept failing to clear (H22). One more H-series item was
read and declined as redundant (H5, plus most of H20's eighteen-fallacy
list beyond the two facts folded in); see [Standing and
Limits](#standing-and-limits) below and the internal triage ledger
(`sources/gaffney-corpus-triage.md`) for the full accounting.

**"The Taxable Surplus in Water Resources"** (*Contemporary Policy Issues*
10, October 1992, pp. 74–82, Western Economic Association International — a
revised version of a paper delivered at the WEAI's 66th annual conference,
Seattle, July 1, 1991) is Gaffney's mature synthesis, written thirty-one
years later while he was at UC Riverside: a systematic proposal to tax water
withdrawals, structured as a rebuttal of six named fallacies obstructing the
idea. Native, cleanly `pdftotext`-extractable text — no OCR needed.

Between them, the two essays cover the full arc: an empirical diagnosis of
how legal doctrine (not scarcity or technology) misallocates a rent-bearing
resource, and a mature policy proposal for how to fix it.

## Part I: Diseconomies Inherent in Western Water Laws (1961)

### The Kaweah River as a Natural Experiment

Gaffney studies the Kaweah River distributary system in the southeastern San
Joaquin Valley (largely Tulare County, California) — over twenty separate
water-user organizations sharing water from a single alluvial-cone system,
with unusually complete daily-diversion records (California Division of
Water Resources Bulletins 49, 49A, 49B, 1920–55) and low transportation and
water-quality complications, making it, in his framing, close to a
controlled test of whether water law allocates a shared resource
efficiently.[1]

### Wide Dispersion in the Marginal Value of Water

Gaffney assembles several independent lines of evidence to show the marginal
productivity of water varies enormously *between adjacent, physically
connected users on the same system* — a direct sign of misallocation, since
an efficient market would equalize marginal returns:

- **Conservation effort as a revealed-value proxy.** The Lindsay-Strathmore
  Irrigation District (citrus, in the "thermal zone") pumped water over 200
  feet, distributed it under pressure through steel pipe for sprinklers, and
  paid $8.14 per delivered acre-foot in tolls and taxes (1949) — one of the
  most elaborate conservation systems of any California irrigation district.
  The Tulare Irrigation District, a few miles away in the delta, lost roughly
  50% of diverted water through a long unlined ditch, with an "irrigation
  efficiency" independently estimated at .39 by state engineers — Gaffney is
  explicit this reflects rational behavior *given* the legal framework, not
  incompetence: "It is rather that framework itself which is under
  examination here."[1]
- **Value of output per acre-foot.** Navel-orange land in the thermal zone
  grossed roughly $300 per acre-foot of water versus roughly $30 for the
  Consolidated Peoples' Ditch Company's delta service area (barley,
  pasture) — a better than tenfold gap on adjacent, physically shiftable
  water. Net of variable costs, marginal water value ran $200–$500/acre-foot
  for navels versus roughly $10–$40 for cotton, alfalfa, barley, and
  pasture.[1]
- **Land-value response to water access.** Reliable water access in the
  thermal citrus zone added roughly $500–$800 an acre to raw land otherwise
  worth $50–$100 (dry, grazing value) — a direct land-value capitalization
  of water rent, the same mechanism the wiki documents for land access and
  transit access elsewhere, here applied to water specifically.[1]
- **A physically costless transfer blocked by law alone.** Lindsay-Strathmore
  had bought shares in the water-rich Consolidated Peoples' Ditch Company and
  an easement to shift water to itself at zero physical transfer cost in
  1928 — and was stopped by a court injunction (*Consolidated Peoples' Ditch
  Co. v. Foothill Ditch Co.*, 1928).[1] Gaffney's point: the barrier was
  **only** legal, not physical or economic.

### Water Rights Are Assigned by Doctrine, Not Productivity

Gaffney's structural diagnosis is that none of California's three water-right
doctrines allocates water toward its highest-value use:

- **Riparian rights**, tied to land fronting a natural channel, happened to
  attach the Kaweah's surface water to lands with the *shallowest* pump
  lifts and *least* need for it — "riparian rights attach surface waters to
  the lands that need them least."[1]
- **Appropriative rights** ("first in time, first in right") reward early
  diversion regardless of soil quality: fast-developing, low-capital uses
  (hay, grain) staked early claims on good water, while high-value,
  high-capital uses (citrus orchards) that took longer to develop — and
  ultimately generated far higher marginal productivity — arrived too late
  to get it. Worse, the "doctrine of relation" (priority dated to *first
  claim*, not first *use*) rewards premature, inflated claims: "a cost to
  society — withdrawing water — is made a revenue to the appropriator,"
  which Gaffney calls perverse once water is scarce rather than
  superabundant.
- **Correlative rights** (percolating groundwater) similarly tie use to
  overlying land rather than value, and on the Kaweah the courts had denied
  permission to export groundwater from low- to high-marginal-productivity
  parcels even where the law in principle allowed "surplus" transfers.

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` A later, more systematic Gaffney paper —
**"Economic Aspects of Water Resource Policy"** (*American Journal of
Economics and Sociology* 28(2), April 1969, pp. 131–44; a paper delivered at
a Pennsylvania State University colloquium, June 4, 1968; hereafter **H8**)
— scores riparian and appropriative doctrine against the same nine criteria
(initial allocation, motive to develop, constraint on uneconomic use,
rationing under scarcity, transferability, pooling for cooperative action,
aquifer management, containment of service area, and distributive equity)
and finds both wanting on nearly every one, generalizing H3's single-river
Kaweah diagnosis into a structural argument: riparian rights fail because
they attach water to land "regardless of productivity" and play a
"dog-in-the-manger" role (the riparian can enjoin others' use of water he
neither needs nor develops himself); appropriative rights fail because
"first in time, first in right" converts a social cost (withdrawal) into a
private claim on all of a stream's future rents, and because seniority
rationing under scarcity pools no risk — a single senior appropriator holds
a 100%-firm supply while the most junior holds one "so uncertain it is
unusable," discarding water's marginal productivity entirely in favor of
priority date.[4] H8 also supplies an institutional answer largely absent
from H3/H21: California's **irrigation districts**, which solved the
water-supply/farm-scale mismatch (large optimal scale for conveyance versus
small optimal scale for the farm unit) through three legally granted
powers — taxation, bonding, and forced inclusion of all serviceable land —
financed by land-value assessment rather than a per-acre-foot toll, letting
districts capture the "pecuniary spillover" (rising land and town values)
their own water supply created to retire the bonds that built it. This is
the same Wright Act financing mechanism the wiki documents in more
historical/political detail on [California Irrigation Districts and the
Wright Act](/wiki/california-irrigation-districts/); H8 supplies Gaffney's
own primary-source economic-theory account of *why* that financing
structure works (natural-monopoly economies of scale in conveyance,
synchronization of interdependent settler investment, land-tax-financed
credit passed to incoming settlers as unappreciated purchase prices) to
complement that page's largely secondary-sourced (Scrofani/Georgist-movement)
account — a cross-reference rather than a duplicated pass, since this page's
subject is rent taxation on withdrawals and that page's is infrastructure
finance.[4]

### The Dynamic Pattern: Monumental Projects, the Price Umbrella, and Logrolling

Gaffney's second major argument is that water law does not merely freeze a
bad initial allocation — it actively **biases new development toward
large, remote, publicly financed projects** over cheap local/marginal
adjustment, because courts will not sanction small local reallocations
("Justice… is more to be likened to the near-sighted Mr. Magoo") but will
recognize "surplus" once a project reaches far enough afield. He traces a
self-reinforcing cycle distinct from anything already on the wiki's Gaffney
corpus:

1. **Indivisibility of scale.** A remote import must be large to justify
   canal-capacity economies, to muster the political support needed for
   water rights and financing, and because service areas typically include
   dry land that must be served too — producing, on the Kaweah's own case,
   a Central Valley Project increment several times the *entire* pre-existing
   local water supply.
2. **Slow response, long lags.** Lindsay-Strathmore's wells first struck
   toxic boron brine in 1913; Central Valley Project water did not reach
   them until 1951 — a 38-year lag, during which the area's citrus
   development was "arrested completely" and competing regions filled the
   gap.
3. **The price umbrella.** Because private land development lags decades
   behind the public works meant to serve it, land prices in a newly
   watered area stay artificially high relative to the abundance of raw
   land the new water supply implies — enticing *more* competing project
   starts than markets can ultimately absorb.
4. **Racing for water rights.** Any region seeking "surplus" water elsewhere
   provokes defensive claim-staking by others, incentivizing premature
   development purely to establish a use history — "the surest means… is to
   begin developing waters to establish a history of use."
5. **Logrolling.** Because monumental interregional transfers exceed local
   finances, they require state/federal subsidy, which is secured through
   political bargaining — "there must be something for everyone" — and, to
   preserve bargaining power, projects are deliberately started before
   completion of earlier ones, suppressing the visible impact of the first
   until the next is already underway. Gaffney explicitly likens this
   mechanism to Indiana's 1836 canal-boom bankruptcy — a distinct
   historical land/infrastructure-boom-and-bust parallel from the ones
   already on [gaffney-land-booms-destroy-capital](/wiki/gaffney-land-booms-destroy-capital/),
   applied here to water infrastructure rather than land credit.

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` **6. A federal fiscal channel compounding the
race.** A later Gaffney piece — **"The Water Giveaway: A Critique of Federal
Water Policy"** (in Robert Haveman and Robert Hamrin, eds., *The Political
Economy of Federal Policy*, Harper & Row, 1973; excerpted from Gaffney's
1969 testimony to the Joint Economic Committee's Subcommittee on Economy in
Government; hereafter **H18**) — largely restates the racing/logrolling
dynamic above for a congressional audience, but adds one distinct
mechanism this page did not yet document: the pre-1986 U.S. income tax let
developers **expense** early operating losses on submarginal water
projects against ordinary income while the resulting land-value
appreciation was taxed, if at all, at preferential capital-gains rates —
"the taxpayer can expense early operating losses which he invests to
capture a resource, while he pays no tax on the resource appreciation which
he captures." This is a *fiscal* driver of premature racing, distinct from
the *legal-doctrine* driver above and from the general land-gains-shelter
argument the wiki already documents in [gaffney-capital-gains-free-enterprise](/wiki/gaffney-capital-gains-free-enterprise/),
here applied specifically to water-project racing rather than land holding
generally. H18 also names two concrete illustrations not elsewhere on this
page: the Colorado River Compact, which Gaffney calls an "economic
monstrosity" for dividing water among political states "regardless of
demand" rather than productivity — a specific interstate instance of the
doctrine-not-productivity diagnosis above — and the Bureau of
Reclamation's 1902 "revolving fund," designed to recover and reinvest its
capital every ten years but which, by 1969, "has yet to complete the first
revolution," a concrete illustration of the slow-payout problem in item 2.[5]

Gaffney documents seven concurrent, mutually reinforcing California
subsidy/financing channels feeding this overexpansion circa 1960 (rising
value of tax-exempt local-bond status; new state Small Projects Act and
Davis-Grunsky Act subsidies; power companies trading free water for
Irrigation Districts' property-tax immunity; Army Engineers water projects
under flood-control cover; the Bureau of Reclamation's Central Valley
Project; the pending $1.75 billion Feather River Project; and a 1961
Interior Department pledge of still more federal funding) — while noting no
comparable stimulus existed for private on-farm improvement, so public
capacity chronically outran private demand for it, a mirror image of the
usual "underinvestment in public goods" framing (he cites Galbraith by name
to distinguish his argument from it). He closes by naming 1893 and 1929 as
the two prior US land collapses in which excessive water-supply overbuilding
for undeveloped land played a leading role, warning that by 1961 the same
pattern was recurring a third time.

### The Groundwater Treadmill: A Parallel Dynamic for Aquifers

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` The surface-water dynamic pattern above has
a groundwater counterpart Gaffney names **"The Great Water Treadmill"** in
two later pieces — **"Whose Water? Ours? Clearing Fallacies about
Implementing Common Rights"** (paper presented at the public conference
"Whose Water?", Institute for Environmental Studies, University of
Washington, September 29–30, 1989; revised for publication April 1991;
hereafter **H20**) and, in nearly identical language, the more polished
**"What Price Water Marketing? California's New Frontier"** (*American
Journal of Economics and Sociology* 56(4), October 1997, pp. 475–520;
hereafter **H22**, cited more fully below): subsidized surface-water supply
lets landowners overdraft the aquifer beneath their land essentially for
free (correlative rights notwithstanding — Gaffney notes pumping *can*
legally be constrained, citing California's coastal saltwater-intrusion
limits and the Bunker Hill aquifer restrictions under the Santa Ana River),
provoking a state "emergency" rescue project once the overdraft threatens
the water table, which resets the subsidy and restarts the cycle —
"subsidized water supply followed by overdraft followed by State rescue
projects followed by new overdrafts, etc. ad bankruptcy." Both pieces trace
the treadmill's origin to a single, named historical case not previously on
this page: Los Angeles's 1913 diversion of the Owens Valley's water to the
San Fernando Valley, where the served lands "were timely prepurchased by
insiders" ahead of annexation — the episode popularly known, after the 1974
film, as the **"Chinatown Syndrome."**[7][8] `[VERIFY: the "Chinatown
Syndrome" attribution and the specific insider-prepurchase claim rest on
Gaffney's own summary in both pieces; this page does not independently
verify the Owens Valley land-purchase history against primary sources.]`

A second, more institutional H20 finding: California's water-district
governance is frequently disenfranchised from the residents it serves,
because voting is typically apportioned by land ownership rather than
residency — Gaffney cites the Irvine Water District in Orange County, where
of "50,000 registered voters... only four (4) could vote in Water District
elections," because the Irvine Company owned essentially all the land
titles and merely leased land to residents. He identifies the Wright Act
irrigation districts — the same institution documented on [California
Irrigation Districts and the Wright Act](/wiki/california-irrigation-districts/)
— as "a notable exception," since Wright Act districts extend the vote to
all resident registrants rather than to landowners alone, a distinct
institutional-design point neither page previously made explicitly.[7]

## Part II: The Taxable Surplus in Water Resources (1992)

### The Proposal

Gaffney proposes a package of severance, net-proceeds, property, transfer,
and gains taxes and/or rental charges on water withdrawals and power drops,
paralleling the resource-rent instruments already documented for oil, gas,
minerals, and timber elsewhere on the wiki. He frames the tax as a price
"charged by the owner of water (the state) for using its property" — the
same public-landlord logic as [Alaska's ad valorem
charge](/wiki/gaffney-alaska-oil-leasing/) and the [mineral-leasing
essays](/wiki/gaffney-mineral-leasing-tax-reform/), applied to a renewable
rather than exhaustible resource — and argues it would simultaneously (i)
spur holders of surplus water to sell, (ii) foster conservation, (iii)
convert water from a fiscal "sink" to a source of state revenue, (iv) offset
concentrated water holdings, and (v) integrate ground- and surface-water
economies under one uniform incentive.

### Six Fallacies Dispelled

1. **"Water rights are real property."** Most state constitutions (Gaffney
   quotes California Water Code §102: "All water within the State is the
   property of the people of the State") hold water in public trust; a
   license is a revocable privilege — analogous to an airline's time slot or
   a broadcaster's frequency — not a deed. He traces the false equation
   partly to the "'Forty-niner fallacy": conflating today's large,
   perpetual appropriative claims with the historically narrow, time-limited
   ("ten feet square") placer-mining claims that originated the
   first-in-time doctrine.
2. **"A water charge would be passed on to consumers."** Standard
   tax-shifting doctrine holds for price-takers in competitive markets, but
   water bears **economic rent** — it "arises naturally where demand
   exceeds supply" — so a tax on the rent component is non-shiftable in the
   same way a land-value tax is.
3. **"Water is worth what it costs to develop."** Gaffney contrasts the
   $20/acre-foot cost of Gage Canal water (Santa Ana River to Riverside)
   against $240/acre-foot the State charges for imported water at the same
   city line, and up to roughly $2,000/acre-foot as the true opportunity
   cost at the system's outer margin — a **100-fold** gap between price and
   social cost he attributes to hidden and cross-subsidies ("the books are
   cooked").
4. **"Firm up property rights and the market will provide."** Gaffney names
   four specific reasons water markets fail on faith alone: some access is
   effectively an unalienable birthright best handled as a state revenue
   duty rather than private sale; water distribution is a natural monopoly
   requiring centralized, regulated integration (the same argument applied
   to power, gas, and telecoms); sellers of water are chronically
   **undermotivated** because water permits carry no debt and no property
   tax, so holders face no cash-drain pressure to sell (unlike the recorded,
   debt-financed real-estate market); and the prior-appropriation doctrine
   is "the locus classicus of rent-seeking," incentivizing premature,
   half-used diversion purely to establish a claim to future rents — a
   distinct instance of the wiki's general rent-seeking mechanism, applied
   here to water-claim staking rather than to land, farm-subsidy, or
   resource-leasing rent-seeking. He also critiques 1980s–90s water
   marketing as typically "win-win-lose" (the public loses) rather than
   genuinely win-win, since sold permits retroactively validate what he
   frames as an original private seizure of public domain, subsidies
   attached.
5. **"Only net consumptive use is a social cost."** Using an entropy
   framing, Gaffney argues that elevation and quality losses are real social
   costs even where water is technically "returned" to a stream — a point
   with a formal water-specific content (loss of hydropower/gravity-delivery
   potential) not analogous to anything already on the wiki's land or
   mineral rent-taxation pages.
6. **"Common rights must spell tragedy."** Gaffney separates the empirical
   fact of aquifer overdraft from the standard "tragedy of the commons"
   causal story, arguing that asserting a common right need not mean open,
   unrestricted access — it can instead mean a state revenue-collection duty
   (a withdrawal tax being one of five examples he gives, alongside bag
   limits, effluent charges, timber-harvest regulation, and boating
   restrictions) — a position that reframes Hardin's tragedy as a
   **property-design failure**, not an inherent flaw of common ownership,
   consistent with the Georgist reading of the commons elsewhere on the wiki.

### Designing the Price Mechanism (1977)

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` H21 argues *that* withdrawals should be
priced but says little about *how* a price would actually be struck
period to period. A short, earlier Gaffney memo — **"How a Water Market
Might Work"** (notes submitted to California's Governor's Commission on
Water Rights Law Reform, July 27, 1977; hereafter **H19**) — sketches a
concrete mechanism: for an unstorable stream with unpredictable flow, each
user submits a demand schedule (desired withdrawal at each of a range of
prices) to a state "watermaster," who cumulates these schedules across
users into a single market demand curve and, for whatever flow actually
arrives, reads off the market-clearing price and the resulting allocation
by interpolation — functioning like a real-time commodity auction (Gaffney
compares it to the coffee market) rather than a fixed-tariff utility
charge. The watermaster's statutory mandate is to maximize net revenue
subject to *not* withholding supply to raise price — a competitive-market
constraint imposed on a legal monopolist — and, for any capital works
(storage, conveyance), to maximize discounted cash flow. Gaffney is candid
the scheme is imperfect (a single seller facing an organized buyers'
association) but frames "maximum feasible improvement," not a textbook
perfect market, as the realistic goal — the same standard implicit in
H21's and H22's (below) case for withdrawal pricing generally.[6]

## Part III: What Price Water Marketing? — Why Voluntary Trading Kept Failing to Clear (1997)

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` **"What Price Water Marketing? California's
New Frontier"** (*American Journal of Economics and Sociology* 56(4),
October 1997, pp. 475–520; hereafter **H22**) is Gaffney's most mature and
comprehensive single treatment of water marketing specifically — five
years after H21 argued for taxing withdrawals, this AJES article
diagnoses in detail *why*, despite two decades of legal reform (Assemblyman
Richard Katz's 1982, 1986, and failed 1991 bills removing transfer
barriers), California's water market had remained "only as fluid as glue."
It substantially supersedes and updates the fallacy-clearing material in
the related but less formal 1989/91 conference paper H20 (above) — roughly
a third of H20's eighteen numbered fallacies reappear here in expanded,
citation-backed form (real property, cost pass-through, development cost,
consumptive use, tragedy of the commons, and undermotivated sellers — all
already documented on this page from H20/H21) — but adds substantial content
genuinely new to this page:

- **The "yield-cutting substitute."** Gaffney coins this term for a
  counterintuitive relationship: because water is such a potent substitute
  for labor and capital, *more* water per acre can mean *lower* yield per
  acre, as farmers substitute cheap water for costlier inputs rather than
  intensify. Water's productivity shows up not in higher yields but in
  lower labor/capital costs that raise net rent despite lower yields —
  meaning *less* water per acre, priced correctly, can raise *both* output
  per acre and net rent. He illustrates with Fresno, Kings, and Tulare
  counties, whose population grew 32% in the 1940s under expensive water,
  then stagnated after two federally subsidized projects (Pine Flat Dam,
  Friant-Kern Canal) delivered cheap water.
- **Entitlements, not just efficiency, determine outcomes.** Gaffney
  invokes the willingness-to-pay/willingness-to-accept (WTP/WTA) asymmetry
  from environmental economics to argue that *who starts out owning* water
  is not a distributional afterthought Coasian bargaining can costlessly
  undo — "distribution dominates allocation." His sharpest illustration:
  survey researchers routinely discard Native American tribes' WTA
  responses for treaty fishing rights as statistical "outliers" because
  tribes will not name *any* sale price, when the correct reading is simply
  that ownership (in this case, treaty rights with constitutional
  supremacy) lets a holder be "as unreasonable as you please" — a
  methodological critique of applying market-clearing logic to holders who
  were never asked whether to sell in the first place.
- **A modernized, named account of rent-seeking speculation.** Updating
  H21's more abstract "locus classicus of rent-seeking" fallacy, H22 names
  specific 1990s buyers acquiring stationary water permits from existing
  holders to commercialize for distant cities — PG&E Properties, Cadiz
  Land Company (Riverside County), Tenneco (Kern County), and Sid and Lee
  Bass (oil billionaires who had assembled 45,000 acres in the Imperial
  Valley) — as a "new, modified, more sophisticated form of rent-seeking,
  blended with old-fashioned land speculation," concentrating irrigated-land
  ownership further in the process.
- **"Win-win-lose" made concrete.** H21 already flags 1980s–90s water
  marketing as typically "win-win-lose," but H22 supplies the mechanism and
  a dated example: a December 1988 U.S. Department of the Interior policy
  letting recipients of *subsidized* federal project water resell it and
  keep the profit, which Gaffney reads through Henry George's line on
  stolen property — "because I was robbed yesterday... is it any reason...
  that the robber has acquired a vested right to rob me?" — arguing that
  each resale manufactures a new "innocent purchaser" who retroactively
  legitimizes the original underpriced giveaway. His proposed fix is the
  same withdrawal charge as H21 (a "win-win-win": seller, buyer, and public
  all gain), not a ban on trading.
- **"Avoided cost" traced to Henry George.** Gaffney links the Federal
  Energy Regulatory Commission's "avoided cost" doctrine (requiring
  utilities to pay independent power producers the cost of the generation
  their output displaces) to Henry George's *The Science of Political
  Economy*: that the value of a thing is not the past labor that produced
  it but the future labor its possession avoids — applied here to argue
  that naturally occurring water at its source is worth its *opportunity*
  cost (up to roughly $3,000/acre-foot at the margin for imported Southern
  California supplies) rather than its historical development cost (as low
  as $20–30/acre-foot for local sources like the Gage Canal), a sharper,
  differently sourced restatement of the $20/$240/$2,000 comparison already
  on this page from H21.
- **A political-economy postscript.** H22's closing section documents that
  mid-century federal officials who tried to pool and price California
  water flexibly (Bureau of Agricultural Economics staff including Marion
  Clawson and Walter Goldschmidt) were publicly denounced as "Communist" by
  landowner lobbies and Senator Sheridan Downey during the McCarthy era,
  and had their budgets and careers cut for it — offered as one concrete
  historical answer to why water-institution reform has proven so durably
  resistant, complementing the "line-up of vested interests" Gaffney
  catalogues from a landowner's-eye view in H18 (above).[8]

## Priority Note: Earliest-Dated Items in the Corpus

With H3 now read in full, the wiki's Gaffney corpus contains four
chronologically distinct "earliest" claims that must be kept separate,
extending the disambiguation already made for E1 on
[people/mason-gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/):

- **Earliest work overall:** the 1957 timber/Faustmann-formula monograph —
  see [gaffney-financial-maturity-timber](/wiki/gaffney-financial-maturity-timber/)
  — a technical capital-theory question, not land or water policy.
- **Earliest land-policy item:** "Urban Expansion — Will It Ever Stop?"
  (1958) — see the note on [people/mason-gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/).
- **Earliest professional/academic conference paper, and earliest water
  economics item:** "Diseconomies Inherent in Western Water Laws" (delivered
  January 23, 1961) — this page.
- **Earliest theoretical land-economics AJES paper**, and the earliest
  known statement of the credit-concentration "strong hands" mechanism
  specifically: "The Unwieldy Time-Dimension of Space" (October 1961) — see
  [gaffney-time-dimension-of-space](/wiki/gaffney-time-dimension-of-space/).
  H3 does **not** state the strong-hands mechanism (its concentration
  argument runs through legal-doctrine misallocation, not differential
  credit access), so this priority attribution is unaffected.

## Standing and Limits

Most of this page's sources are Gaffney's own advocacy/analytical work, not
third-party peer review of his claims. **H3 (1961)** is a single-river-system
case study (the Kaweah) generalized by assertion ("diseconomies of kinds and
degrees that in my observation prevail throughout the Valley and the State")
rather than by a multi-system statistical sample; its dollar figures come
from a mix of Gaffney's own fieldwork, state engineering bulletins, and
other economists' contemporary studies (Adams, Horn, Brewer), and — per the
`[VERIFY]` flag above — its text was recovered via fresh OCR of a poor
legacy scan rather than a native text layer, so exact figures should be
treated as approximate. **H21 (1992)** is a synthesis/advocacy essay for a
policy-issues journal, not an econometric study of California's water
market; its price comparisons ($20 vs. $240 vs. ~$2,000 per acre-foot) rest
on Gaffney's own estimate and a single cited Alan Post 1982 state task-force
effort, and Gaffney is candid that "no one is able to figure the cost to the
penny because the books are cooked" — an admission of estimation
uncertainty the wiki preserves rather than smooths over. Neither essay
offers a modern (post-1990s) empirical test of water-market performance
under the marketing reforms California adopted after 1992; H22 (1997)
partially closes that gap for the 1982–1997 window specifically, but no
post-1997 update was located this session.

`[EDITORIAL: added 2026-07-18]` **One correction to the "no third-party peer
review" characterization:** H3's Kaweah case study *was* publicly
challenged in print, and Gaffney's reply survives — **"Water Law and
Economic Transfers of Water: A Reply"** (*Journal of Farm Economics* 44(2),
May 1962, pp. 427–34; hereafter **H4**), responding to a comment by Dean
Frank Trelease of the University of Wyoming College of Law (*JFE* 43(5),
Proceedings issue, December 1961, pp. 1147–52). Trelease argued California
law already made appropriative water rights transferable in principle, so
Gaffney's blame belonged not on the doctrine but on (a) unaccounted-for
spillover benefits from existing uses and (b) licensees' "irrational"
attachment to rights they could legally sell. Gaffney's reply — the
strongest empirical point in it — states that **no appropriative right had
transferred on the Kaweah system for decades**, and argues the "heirloom
attitude" Trelease treats as an exogenous cultural quirk is instead
*produced by* the law's own insecure, conditional framing of water
licenses: because holders sense their claims could be challenged or
declared surplus, they cling to them, which starves the market of sellers
and reinforces the very insecurity that caused the clinging — a feedback
loop distinct from, though compatible with, H21's later "undermotivated
sellers" argument and H22's parallel treatment (below). Gaffney also
proposes, in passing, an early version of a mechanism this page documents
more fully elsewhere on the wiki: relying on **ad valorem land taxation**
to automatically capture and redistribute the spillover gains and losses of
water-rights transfers, rather than litigating each transfer's externalities
case by case. This does not overturn H3's diagnosis — Gaffney's factual
rebuttal (zero transfers for decades) went unanswered in print — but it
means the wiki's characterization should be that H3's central claim
survived a specific, professionally credentialed (a law school dean's)
public challenge, not that no such challenge existed.[3]

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — develops water for
  the first time as a resource-rent domain, adding an empirical
  misallocation case study, a six-fallacy taxation proposal structurally
  parallel to the wiki's existing oil/gas, mineral, and timber
  rent-capture material, and (from H22) a detailed diagnosis of why
  voluntary market trading alone has not delivered that rent capture in
  practice.
- **Benefit:** [Capturing resource rent works — where institutions are
  strong](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/) — an eighth institutional-
  failure mode, distinct from the seven already documented: water rights
  assigned by legal doctrine (riparian/appropriative/correlative priority)
  rather than by price or productivity, with a specific mechanism (the
  "price umbrella"/racing/logrolling cycle, and its groundwater-specific
  counterpart "the Great Water Treadmill") for how that doctrine actively
  drives systematic public overinvestment in remote water-supply projects.
