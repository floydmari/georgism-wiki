## Overview

"George's Economics of Abundance: Replacing Dismal Choices with Practical Resolutions
and Synergies" is a workpaper-length essay (masongaffney.org workpaper WP018) by
**Mason Gaffney**.[1] The source carries no publication date; internal references to
"California Governor Pete Wilson" (in office 1991–99) and to "President and Mrs.
Clinton" then proposing "the new health plan" (1993–94) place its composition in that
window — this page dates it **1994** as an approximation, not a stated fact. **[VERIFY:
exact publication year not given by the source; inferred from internal topical
references only.]**

The essay's organizing conceit, stated in its introduction, is that "it is part of
George's genius that his proposals solve one problem by resolving it with another,
turning two problems into one solution" rather than offering the "dismal" trade-offs
Gaffney says characterize most economic policy debate.[1] Gaffney catalogues
**nineteen** such "resolutions" a Georgist land-tax shift achieves, summarized in a
closing list, and closes with a political epilogue contrasting George's program with
Keynesianism, Reagan-era supply-side economics, and New Deal planning as rival
"panaceas" that voters embraced in turn.

## The Argument

The essay is not an empirical study; it is a rhetorical synthesis that cites Gaffney's
own other technical papers (repeatedly marked "bound herein" or "on reserve," indicating
the piece was written for an anthology or course reader) as the evidentiary backing for
each claim, rather than presenting new data itself. Its recurring devices are
deliberately playful: "TAANSTAAFL" ("There Ain't No Such Thing As A Free Lunch," the
economists' cliché he is arguing against) and his own coinage "TITSTAAFL" ("There Is
Too Such a Thing As A Free Lunch"), plus "WIGGI?" ("Who Is Going to Get It?") as his
reframe of the real policy question.[1] Selected resolutions, condensed:

- **Equity and efficiency.** A land tax "combines the magic of justice with the magic
  of incentive" — it is redistributive because land ownership concentrates with wealth
  ("the great cause of the concentration of wealth is concentration of the ownership of
  land," quoting George), without the incentive-warping of progressive income taxation
  or price controls.[1]
- **Demand-side and supply-side.** Because a land tax is a fixed charge that "cannot be
  avoided by underusing land, and it is not increased by using it," Gaffney argues it
  applies a "leverage effect" pushing landowners to raise output — stimulating
  production (the supply side) at the same time untaxing labor stimulates hiring and
  spending (the demand side), which he calls "true fiscal stimulus" that avoids the
  inflation risk of pure demand-side Keynesian stimulus.[1] He is explicitly dismissive
  of the rival supply-side claim of the era: on the Reagan/Laffer tax-cut program, "this
  one proved to be a fraud."[1]
- **Structural and macro reform.** Land taxation, Gaffney argues, "lubricates the land
  market in the very process of extracting more tax revenues from it," discouraging
  land-withholding and the self-reinforcing spiral in which firms hoard land against
  future expansion, forcing competitors to hoard defensively in turn.[1]
- **Urban form and civic participation.** An anecdotal aside: asked why a Riverside,
  California pedestrian mall failed, a local developer answered "absentee ownership" —
  Gaffney generalizes this into a claim that Georgist taxation favors engaged owners
  ("who 'apply their shadows' daily") over passive rentier ownership.[1]
- **Common rights and private tenure.** Gaffney frames the program as reconciling
  collective and individual claims to land — licensing/leasing common-pool resources to
  the highest bidder rather than giving them away, and taxation asserting a public claim
  on land income "without impairing private tenure rights," so an owner can say "This is
  my land, I pay the taxes on it."[1]
- Further claimed resolutions (population/capital growth without diluting the resource
  base — his "Accommodating Lifeboat Theorem"; paying down public debt while still
  stimulating employment; environmental protection alongside job creation) extend the
  same "resolution, not trade-off" framing across macro, fiscal, and environmental
  policy.[1]

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** This is a **D-claim (interpretive/argumentative) essay**, and should
be presented on the wiki as "Gaffney argues," never as settled research. It is
essay-register advocacy writing — informal, aphoristic, self-citing — not a
peer-reviewed or data-driven paper; its empirical backing lives in the separate Gaffney
papers it repeatedly cites as "bound herein" (works such as "The Taxable Capacity of
Land" and "The Synergistic City," both independently covered on this wiki at
[gaffney-hidden-taxable-capacity](/wiki/gaffney-hidden-taxable-capacity/) and
[gaffney-synergistic-city](/wiki/gaffney-synergistic-city/)), not in this essay itself.
Its historical-political claims — that Georgism's "political crest" ran 1901–20, or
that its later eclipse owed to the loss of "young leaders" in World War I and the
1919–89 "Red Scare" — are Gaffney's own historical interpretation, offered without
citation to independent historiography, and should be flagged as such if used
elsewhere on the wiki. The essay's core theoretical claim — that a tax on a
perfectly-inelastic-supply base is simultaneously efficient and progressive — is not
new to this piece; it restates the argument documented with primary sources on
[Tax Land, Not Labor](/wiki/tax-land-not-labor/), which this essay is cited to
supplement, not to establish independently.

## Bears On

This essay does not carry independent empirical evidence for any `benefits/` or
`problems/` claim page; its contribution is rhetorical and belongs in the narratives
lane. See [Tax Land, Not Labor](/wiki/tax-land-not-labor/), which cites it as an
additional, distinctively-voiced articulation of the efficiency-and-equity reconciliation
