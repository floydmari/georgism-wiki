## Overview

"Containment Policies for Urban Sprawl" is Chapter X of *Approaches to the
Study of Urbanization*, edited by Richard L. Stauber (Governmental Research
Center, University of Kansas, 1964), pp. 115–133 — reprinted separately as
a Robert Schalkenbach Foundation pamphlet.[1] Gaffney was then at the
University of Missouri. The chapter is a personal-essay-style argument (no
regressions, no data tables) addressed to a mixed audience of planners,
agricultural economists, and Malthusian conservationists gathered at a
University of Kansas symposium on urbanization.

**Priority note.** This is the earliest work in the wiki's Gaffney corpus to
make the land-value-tax anti-sprawl argument — predating [Gaffney's 1969 *AIP
Journal* article](/wiki/gaffney-land-planning-property-tax/) by five years and
[his 1972–73 *AJES* essay](/wiki/gaffney-urban-land-rent/) by eight to nine.
Both of those later pieces are already cited on
[benefits/lvt-reduces-sprawl](/wiki/lvt-reduces-sprawl/) as the historical
priority statement of Gaffney's theoretical case; this 1964 chapter is the
actual origin and predates them, and the postage-stamp-pricing-subsidizes-sprawl
argument it develops recurs, in more abstract form, in [Gaffney's 1978
"Synergistic City"](/wiki/gaffney-synergistic-city/) essay's "Cross-Subsidy in
Mass Systems" section.

## The Argument

**Rejecting soil-scarcity containment.** Gaffney opens by conceding
agricultural economists' complaint that sprawl destroys cropland (citing the
loss of southern California citrus and Santa Clara County orchards to Los
Angeles and San Francisco sprawl) but rejects their reasoning: America has
vast reserves of substitute cropland and "no danger of running out of
cropland" — the real damage of sprawl to agriculture is that it sends "shock
waves into the countryside" of premature, capital-taxed intensification driven
by speculative capital-gains-motivated buying, not soil loss as such. (C-claim;
Gaffney's own diagnostic argument, illustrated with contemporary examples, not
tested econometrically.)

**Distinguishing "artificial scarcity" from "efficiency" containment.**
Gaffney's central methodological move is to separate two things planners
routinely conflate: containment that **redistributes** value to existing urban
landowners by restricting the land supply (which he opposes as inequitable),
and containment that **increases efficiency** by compacting settlement to
economize on area-sensitive costs (which he supports). He argues cities show
"increasing returns to scale, but negative returns to more space" — the same
increasing-returns-to-access framing later developed at length in [the
Synergistic City essay](/wiki/gaffney-synergistic-city/).

**"Negative containment" fails.** Zoning, greenbelts, and agricultural
exclusion zones don't contain sprawl, they relocate and worsen it: builders
leapfrog the restricted zone, county zoning boards succumb to logrolling and
overzoning, and jurisdictions compete for growth. Gaffney draws a deliberately
provocative historical parallel: greenbelts and low-density zones "bear a
family resemblance to King George's Proclamation Line" restricting colonial
settlement west of the Appalachians — a fence the American frontier simply
went around, leaving "an uneconomic scatter and sprawl" rather than order. (D-claim;
rhetorical/historical analogy, not an empirical test of zoning outcomes.)

**"Neutral containment" — stop subsidizing sprawl.** The chapter's most
concrete and quantifiable argument is that utility and street financing
systematically subsidizes low-density fringe development at the expense of
the dense center through **postage-stamp pricing**: utilities charge uniform
rates regardless of distance from the load center, even though "distribution
costs more than production" and outlying customers require proportionally
far more pipe, pressure, and capacity per customer served. Gaffney proposes
**distance-graduated utility rates** and requiring street extensions to "pay
for themselves... from increased tax revenues" (via prompt reassessment of
the land value the extension creates) as a "neutral" fix requiring no
new regulation — just removing an existing subsidy. This is the same
cross-subsidy mechanism ("postage-stamp pricing," "the center is sapped to
serve the fringes") the wiki's [lvt-reduces-sprawl](/wiki/lvt-reduces-sprawl/)
page currently documents only from the later, more abstract 1978 Synergistic
City restatement — this 1964 chapter is the earlier, more concretely
worked-out version, with the water-pipe engineering example spelled out.
(C-claim; Gaffney's own theoretical/engineering argument, not independently
tested.)

**"Positive containment" — the site-value tax.** Gaffney's constructive
proposal is a heavy tax on land value alone: "we can keep down the selling
price of land titles, and still keep up the yearly cost of holding land, by
levying heavy taxes on the base of site value. Thus land is made cheap to
buy, but dear to hold, an optimal arrangement in my opinion." He argues the
conventional property tax on buildings amounts to "taxing vertical
transportation" (the wires, ducts, elevators, and stairs inside a skyscraper)
while subsidizing horizontal transportation (utility lines extended to
sprawl), closing with a parody of Omar Khayyam ("Thou wilt not with
predestined choice propel / Me outwards, then impute my sprawl to
'Taste'!") aimed at economists who attribute sprawl to consumer preference
rather than fiscal bias. A **site-capacity tax** would hit the city center
hardest, he argues, "because that is where the land values are" — the same
logic, he notes, that exclusive agricultural zoning already applies to force
farmers to intensify rather than sprawl. (C-claim; Gaffney's own theoretical
mechanism and normative proposal, not independently tested in this chapter.)

## Standing and Limits

- **Genre.** A symposium book chapter for a mixed academic/planning audience,
  written in a discursive, occasionally polemical essay style typical of
  Gaffney's earlier work — no data, no citations to his own later empirical
  studies (which postdate it), no peer review in the economics-journal sense.
  All of its central claims are Gaffney's own theoretical and normative
  argument, not independently tested findings; graded C/D-claim throughout per
  the wiki's convention and cited here for historical priority and mechanism
  detail, not as additional empirical evidence.
- **Dated context.** Written against a specific 1960s backdrop (postwar
  suburbanization, the "urban renewal" era, and Malthusian farmland-loss
  advocacy of the period) — some framing (e.g., the confident dismissal of
  cropland scarcity) reads as period-specific and should not be read as a
  general claim about agricultural land economics today.
- **Provenance.** Native, `pdftotext -layout`-extractable text (no OCR
  needed). Local mirror: `sources/gaffney/text/E3Containment_policies.CV.txt`.
