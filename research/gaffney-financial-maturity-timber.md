## Overview

"Concepts of Financial Maturity of Timber and Other Assets" is a September 1957
monograph by Mason Gaffney, published as **A.E. Information Series No. 62** of the
Department of Agricultural Economics, North Carolina State College, Raleigh, NC,
where Gaffney was then junior faculty in the Department of Economics.[1] `[VERIFY:
this page corrects a common characterization — the monograph is sometimes loosely
described as Gaffney's "Duke dissertation-era" work; the document itself states no
Duke affiliation, and Gaffney's actual PhD dissertation, "Land Speculation as an
Obstacle to Ideal Allocation of Land" (UC Berkeley, 1956), is a separate work. This
monograph was written the year after that dissertation, while Gaffney held his
first faculty post at NC State — "dissertation-era" in the loose sense of timing,
not in the sense of being the dissertation itself or a Duke production.]`[2] It
carries no explicit Georgist framing and does not mention Henry George — it is a
technical contribution to forest economics and capital theory, addressed to
economists and foresters, not a piece of Georgist advocacy.[1]

The monograph's project: derive, from first marginalist principles, the correct
rule for when to harvest an appreciating asset (timber, above all, but by
extension any appreciating or depreciating capital). Gaffney shows this rule is
identical to the **Faustmann formula** — a result published by German forester
Martin Faustmann in 1849 but, per Gaffney's own later account, essentially unknown
to economists until this monograph and the "flurry of interest" it triggered.[3]

## The Core Problem: Two Elements of Urgency

Gaffney frames the question with an analogy: deciding when to harvest timber is
like deciding when to adjourn a convention — there are two costs to prolonging the
meeting, releasing the delegates and releasing the hotel space they occupy, and
"the problem is to balance these costs, at the margin of decision, against the
benefits of prolonging the meetings."[1] Applied to timber: the naive rule is to
harvest when annual growth (Δg) falls to interest on the standing timber's value
(gi) — but Gaffney argues this omits a second, land-specific cost: **the annual
value of the site itself**, which is not an externally given number but must be
found *simultaneously* with the harvest decision, since the best alternative use
of forestland is ordinarily to harvest the present stand and start the next.[1]
Solving for both site rent and financial maturity together, Gaffney shows,
reproduces Faustmann's classical formula — and that every rival "concept of
financial maturity" then current among economists and foresters (R.G.D. Allen and
Irving Fisher's "maximum discounted yield"; Kenneth Boulding's "maximum internal
rate of return"; the zero-interest *Waldrente* doctrines popular among foresters;
the US Forest Service's own "culmination of mean annual increment" standard) fails
because it omits one or the other element of urgency.[1] Applied to standard
European larch yield data, the different criteria produce wildly different
"optimal" rotations — from 33 years (Boulding) to over 100 (pure growth
maximization) — for the *same* stand.[1]

## Contribution to Rent Theory: The Site as Residual Claimant

Although the monograph never cites Henry George, its analytical core is a
technical elaboration of Ricardian site-rent theory applied to time. Gaffney
argues it is "generally advantageous to treat the site as the residual claimant on
the product; not for any traditional or mystical reason but because it is harder
to put an external value on the site than on other inputs" — a rent-theoretic
move (site value as the *undetermined* residual, discovered only by solving the
whole allocation problem) that Gaffney would make explicit fifty years later, in
his 2008 *American Journal of Economics and Sociology* retrospective "Keeping Land
in Capital Theory," as a direct link between Faustmann's formula and Henry
George's land-rent theory: Faustmann's site-value measure, Gaffney there writes,
"provid[es] a mathematical basis for George's later observations on the damage
done by land speculation."[4][1]

## How Taxes Affect Optimal Rotations

Chapter V works out the tax implications of the Faustmann framework in terms
directly relevant to forest and resource tax design:

- **A general property tax on standing timber** (an ad valorem levy on the growing
  stock) "tends obviously to hasten harvests," discourages regeneration, and
  pushes owners toward less capital-intensive land uses, because the owner
  minimizes the tax burden by keeping as little forest capital tied up as
  possible.[1]
- **A constant annual tax on site capacity** (i.e., a pure site-rent/land-value
  tax, independent of the standing timber's value) "do[es] not affect optimum
  rotations in the least" — its effect on the incremental cost of time is exactly
  offset by an equal effect on site rent — while still pressuring inefficient
  holders to sell to operators who can impute a higher rent, since "taxes higher
  than site rents imputable by the most efficient users of land would eventually
  prove uncollectible and the lands revert for taxes."[1] This is Gaffney's
  earliest worked demonstration of the land-value-tax neutrality result the wiki
  documents elsewhere (see [ATCOR](/wiki/atcor/)) — stated here specifically for
  a *time-allocation* decision (harvest timing) rather than a static-allocation
  one.
- **Severance taxes** — levies contingent on harvest, like royalties — "tend to
  lengthen rotations," the opposite effect of a standing-timber property tax.[1]
- **Income taxes** have a "capricious" effect depending on the individual
  taxpayer's situation, but Gaffney notes that timber's federal capital-gains
  treatment (50% of gain non-taxable, a 25% maximum rate at the time) "doubtless
  tends to lengthen rotations" for high-bracket owners, since the tax preference
  "magnifies several times the low percentage gains of postmature timber."[1]
- **A forest's tax-paying capacity**: Gaffney defines this precisely as "the
  amount of its maximum site rent" — a constant annual tax up to that ceiling is
  sustainable; above it, "eventually prove[s] uncollectible."[1]

## Are US Timber Rotations Too Long?

Chapter V also applies the analysis to a live policy question. Gaffney assembles
seven arguments that prevailing US forest rotations run **uneconomically long** —
including the dominance of doctrines (the Forest Service's "culmination of mean
annual increment," a zero-interest standard) that prescribe excessive rotations;
the systematic gap between Faustmann-optimal rotations computed from standard
yield tables and rotations actually practiced; landholders who hold timber for
"ulterior" motives (mineral rights, water rights, speculative price appreciation,
political control) rather than active timber management; and the federal
capital-gains preference.[1] He weighs three counter-arguments (that local
property taxes already push rotations too short; that expected price appreciation
justifies longer holding; that faster harvest would "flood the market") and finds
each weak — on price appreciation specifically, citing W.E. Hiley's finding that
English sawn-wood prices rose only 0.5%/year in real terms over 1857-1937, too
little to justify substantially longer rotations, and noting that 1907-era
predictions of imminent "timber famine" were followed by a price collapse.[1] His
summary diagnosis: "the most glaring diseconomy in forestry is the extreme
contrast between credit-starved small holders who harvest young trees while they
are still growing very rapidly, and large public and corporate holders who keep
stagnant decaying virgin timber off the market" — an institutional-failure
argument (asymmetric access to capital, not efficient allocation, determines who
holds timber how long) rather than a purely technical one.[1]

## Macroeconomic Implications

Gaffney closes by flagging, as the monograph's most important unexplored
implication, the possibility that Faustmann's formula — if adopted broadly,
outside forestry — would **speed the turnover of the economy's whole capital
stock**, with macroeconomic effects on income and employment: "of all the things
this study has had to neglect, the most important by far are the macroeconomic
implications... Should economists find Faustmann's formula acceptable and devise
public policies to prompt its practical application, this benefit would, from the
standpoint of human welfare, probably outweigh all the other benefits."[1] He
flags this as a direction for future research rather than developing it further
here; it foreshadows Gaffney's later working papers connecting land-holding
patterns to capital turnover, employment, and macroeconomic stagnation (see
[How Land Markets Misallocate Capital](/wiki/gaffney-land-market-distortions/)).

## Standing and Reception — Why This Page Grades "Important"

Unlike most of Gaffney's other essays and working papers on this wiki, which are
advocacy pieces written for a legislative, movement, or popular audience, this
monograph is a work of **mainstream, mathematically developed forest economics**,
and its academic standing is independently documented, not merely asserted by
Gaffney or this wiki:

- **Citation record.** Semantic Scholar records **81 citations** for the
  monograph — a substantial count for a 1957 state-college agricultural-economics
  information series bulletin, not a journal article.[5]
- **Contemporary academic review.** The monograph was reviewed in *Forest
  Science* (the flagship US forest-economics journal) by Robert O. McMahon and
  Donald F. Flora in 1963.[6]
- **Credited with reviving the Faustmann formula.** Gaffney's own 2008
  retrospective states plainly: "There was a flurry of interest in reviving
  Faustmann, ca. 1957-76... Jack Hirshleifer circulated it among the economic
  'elite,' while many forest economists saw it as a useful tool. Samuelson
  endorsed it."[4] This is corroborated independently: D.H. Newman's 2002
  historiographical review in the *Journal of Forest Economics* treats Gaffney
  (1957) as one of the field's foundational assessments of the optimal-rotation
  literature, in the same lineage as Samuelson (1976), Hyde (1980), and Reed
  (1986).[7]
- **Paul Samuelson's 1976 endorsement.** Samuelson's "Economics of Forestry in an
  Evolving Society" (*Economic Inquiry*, 1976) is, per later retrospectives
  (Amacher 2012), the single most influential article in modern forest
  economics; it takes up and validates the Faustmann/site-rent solution Gaffney's
  1957 monograph had already worked out independently, citing Gaffney among the
  economists who anticipated the correct formulation.[8][3] `[VERIFY: this page
  was unable to access the full text of Samuelson (1976) directly — paywalled —
  to quote his citation of Gaffney verbatim; the claim rests on secondary sources
  (Newman 2002, Amacher 2012 abstracts, and Gaffney's own 2008 account) rather
  than a direct read of Samuelson's text.]`

**Honest caveat on scope.** The monograph's academic importance is squarely
within forest/capital economics, not within the Georgist policy literature per se
— it contains no explicit land-tax advocacy and is cited overwhelmingly by
forestry economists working on rotation theory, not by Georgist writers. Its
relevance to this wiki is (a) the land-value-tax-neutrality result it derives
independently for a dynamic, time-allocation problem, and (b) Gaffney's own later
explicit linkage of the Faustmann site-rent logic to George's rent theory. Graded
**Important** rather than **Core**: it is Gaffney's most externally, academically
cited single work, but its direct contribution to the Georgist land-value-tax
argument is a single neutrality result and a historical footnote to Faustmann,
not a flagship statement of the rent-capture case the way *The Corruption of
Economics*, *Two Centuries of Economic Thought on Taxation of Land Rents*, or
*The Hidden Taxable Capacity of Land* are.

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — supplies an early,
  independently derived instance of the land-value-tax neutrality result (ATCOR's
  logic) applied to a *dynamic* allocation problem (optimal harvest timing) rather
  than the static allocation problem the concept page otherwise documents, and
  distinguishes it sharply from output-contingent levies (severance taxes,
  property taxes on standing timber) which do distort timing.
