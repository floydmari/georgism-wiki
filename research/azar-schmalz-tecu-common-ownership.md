## Overview

"Anticompetitive Effects of Common Ownership" is a paper by José Azar, Martin C. Schmalz, and Isabel Tecu, published in *The Journal of Finance*, volume 73, issue 4 (2018), pp. 1513–1565.[1] Using panel data on the US airline industry, the authors construct a Modified Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (MHHI) that accounts for cross-ownership by large diversified institutional investors (chiefly index funds), and find that route-level increases in this common-ownership-adjusted concentration measure are associated with statistically significant increases in ticket prices — reported across the paper and subsequent summaries of it as being in the range of roughly 3% to 7%, with some estimates and route subsets as high as around 12%.[2] The paper argues these effects are an order of magnitude larger than the concentration increases antitrust regulators normally treat as competitively significant.[1]

This is the empirical anchor for [Eric Posner and Glen Weyl](/wiki/posner-weyl-radical-markets/)'s treatment of [horizontal shareholding](/wiki/horizontal-shareholding/) in *[Radical Markets](/wiki/radical-markets/)* (2018, Ch. 4), which cites the study's airline findings as the leading evidence that diversified common ownership functions as a rent-extraction mechanism warranting an antitrust-style remedy.[3]

## Significance and Contestation

On the wiki's rent gradient this paper sits at the **contested monopoly/financialization frontier**, well outside the airtight land case. The finding has been actively disputed in the literature that followed: several later papers, using different identification strategies or additional years of data, report smaller or statistically insignificant price effects, and the mechanism by which passive index-fund managers would translate diversified stakes into coordinated route-level pricing remains disputed among economists — a point the wiki's [horizontal-shareholding](/wiki/horizontal-shareholding/) page discusses at length. This paper should be read as the originating, most-cited study in an ongoing empirical debate, not as a settled result.
