## Overview

"The Surprising Incidence of a Tax on Pure Rent: A New Answer to an Old Question" is a paper by Harvard economist **Martin Feldstein**, published in the *Journal of Political Economy*, vol. 85, no. 2 (April 1977), pp. 349–360.[1][2] It is widely cited as the strongest mainstream theoretical challenge to the textbook Georgist claim that a tax on the rent of a fixed factor (land) is simply capitalized onto the landowner with no further effects on the rest of the economy.

Feldstein's model is a one-sector, closed-economy neoclassical growth framework in which land and capital are substitutable in producing output. In the static, partial-equilibrium textbook case, a tax on pure land rent falls entirely on landowners because land supply is fixed. Feldstein shows that in a dynamic setting this result does not hold: taxing land rent lowers the price of land, which frees up saving that gets redirected into physical capital investment; the resulting rise in the capital stock changes the economy's long-run factor returns, so the tax's ultimate incidence is partly shifted away from the original landowners rather than falling on them alone.[2] This is offered as a direct complication of the "old" answer — that a land tax's burden stays put on land value — that Georgist and public-finance textbooks typically give.
