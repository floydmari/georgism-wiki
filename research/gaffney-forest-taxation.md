## Overview

This page combines two of Gaffney's forest-policy pieces, read together because
they cover the two halves of the same problem — how forest *rent* should be
taxed or captured — from opposite institutional settings: **private** forest
taxation and **public** (National Forest) management.

**"Alternative Ways of Taxing Forests"** (March 1980) is a systematic tax-design
survey. **Resolved 2026-07-18:** "Number 43" is Gaffney's UC Riverside working-paper
number, not a lecture/proceedings series — the document itself carries no venue
statement, but masongaffney.org's own publication catalog identifies the print
venue as Will Knedlick (ed.), *State Taxation of Forest and Land Resources*,
Lincoln Institute of Land Policy, 1980, pp. 5–16.[1] Gaffney opens by noting that Henry Aaron's and Dick Netzer's
Brookings Institution books on property taxation — "establishmentarian" surveys
that "would appear to constitute definitive works on the subject" — say nothing
about forest taxation at all, and frames his own essay as a corrective: a general
tax economist's systematic weighing of the alternatives, "as a citizen and an
economist," not a forestry-industry partisan.[1]

**"Greater Social Benefits From our National Forests"** (March 4, 1977) is an
after-dinner-style address to the **Western Timber Association** in San
Francisco, reproduced by the Association that year.[2] It applies the same
site-rent logic to a different target: not how private forest owners should be
taxed, but how badly the US Forest Service manages the National Forests as an
economic asset. Both pieces are Gaffney working in the same idiom as
[his 1957 Faustmann monograph](/wiki/gaffney-financial-maturity-timber/) —
explicit annual site rent as the correct opportunity-cost benchmark against
which to judge tax design and management practice — applied seventeen and
twenty years later to concrete policy fights.

## Taxing Private Forests: Weighing Four Bases

Gaffney's 1980 survey works through four candidate tax bases for private
forestland, giving both sides of each case before ranking them.

**Property tax on standing timber.** The standard complaint — that annual ad
valorem taxation on a crop that "ripens only periodically" compounds to a large
share of harvest value and drives less-capitalized owners out of forestry — is
real, Gaffney concedes, but he finds the case for the tax stronger than critics
allow: capital in timber should "earn the same return as other capital has to
earn to justify its keep," so exempting it is a **capital-intensity subsidy**,
and the doctrine that Gaffney elsewhere attacks — *culmination of mean annual
increment* (CMAI), which "is optimal only when the rate of interest equals
zero" — is itself an artifact of ignoring the tax and interest cost of
capital.[1] Historically, he notes, small owners subject to real property
taxation have simply practiced "leaner, more economical forest management,"
not gone bankrupt as the tax's critics predicted.[1]

**Property tax on site value alone** (standing timber exempt) is Gaffney's
preferred base, "on all counts": because bare cutover land is taxed at the same
rate as land under mature timber, the tax "promotes land improvement, rather
than suppressing it" — pressure falls entirely on restocking cutover land
quickly, with no penalty on full or early stocking.[1] It also, he argues,
concentrates pressure on the *better* sites (where site value is high) and
leaves marginal, high-cost land essentially untaxed — the opposite of a uniform
ad valorem levy, and, he suggests, a design von Thünen "would presumably have
loved" for forcing higher-value sites to their highest use.[1] The chief
practical drawback: because the tax base (bare site value only) is far smaller
than a full property-tax base including timber, the rate must run roughly
**ten times higher** to raise equivalent revenue — a figure Gaffney flags as
illustrative, not exact, varying with rotation length and site productivity.[1]

**Yield tax** (levied only at harvest, on stumpage value) Gaffney rejects
outright: "in my opinion is no choice at all. The cumulative weight of the case
against it is overwhelming."[1] His central objection is a worked calculation:
to compensate for a 1-percentage-point cut in the standing-timber property tax
rate, a yield tax would need a rate of **38% on stumpage value** (at a 50-year
rotation and 7% discount rate) — a rate so front-loaded onto the harvest
transaction that it creates a severe, uniform bias against short investment
cycles: a $100 timber-stand-improvement outlay expected to return $200 ten
years later (a 7%/year pre-tax return) nets only $122 after a 38% yield tax,
a post-tax return of about 2%/year — "much too low to interest any
investor."[1] This intertemporal bias, he argues, penalizes intensive
management on good sites (the "intensive margin") more than it penalizes
extensive, low-effort use of marginal land — the opposite of the site-value
tax's incentive structure.

**Income tax**, Gaffney's second choice if a pure site-value base is politically
unavailable, at least allows cost deductibility, which "removes the worst bite"
of the yield tax's short-cycle bias, though he flags that the actual US tax
code's asymmetric treatment — property costs (interest, taxes) currently
expensable while planting/labor costs must be capitalized — perversely biases
owners toward letting nature reforest cutover land (deductible carrying costs)
rather than paying to replant it promptly (a barely-deductible capital
outlay).[1] His preferred reform, if some preference for timber must be kept
politically, would flip this: expense restocking costs, capitalize property
costs — "the reverse of current practice."[1]

**Gaffney's stated ranking**: site productivity tax first ("I would generalize
that case as far beyond forestry as possible"); income tax with restocking
costs expensable second; a general uniform property tax on all land and capital
third; yield tax last, rejected entirely.[1]

## The National Forests as an Undercapitalized-Return Asset

The 1977 Western Timber Association address turns the same analytical apparatus
on the US Forest Service. Gaffney's central move is to cost the Forest Service's
capital the way a private firm's balance sheet would: citing economist **Marion
Clawson's** accounting, he notes the Service's National Forest holdings were
worth an estimated **$42 billion**, and that "as of 1974, cash receipts of $486
million dollars including income from mineral leases and forage were just offset
by cash outlays of the same amount" before a further $80 million in
payments-to-states brought the current account to an $80 million deficit — then
applies a conservative 5% imputed interest charge to the $42 billion asset base,
yielding an implicit annual carrying cost of **$2.1 billion** that dwarfs the
Service's own accounting of water-regulation ($50 million) and recreation ($440
million) benefits.[2] "This figure towers above all the others," Gaffney says,
"and serves as a constant reminder that the Forest Service is not earning any
return of consequence on the colossal investment of public values placed at its
disposal."[2]

He identifies the Forest Service's central management fallacy as the
**"allowable cut effect" (ACE)** — crediting a *new* investment with the value of
a mature-timber harvest arbitrarily tied to it, which he calls "a fraud on the
National Treasury" and predicts "will go down in history as the Watergate of the
Forest Service" — and a companion fallacy, that a "normalized" (balanced-age)
forest makes standing inventory economically disappear because harvest and
planting occur simultaneously, which he likens to "saying that a bathtub becomes
empty at the moment when the inflow equals the outflow."[2] Structurally,
Gaffney's critique of CMAI here restates the 1957 monograph's technical finding
in plain language for a lay industry audience: "if the forest manager aims for
that cutting cycle which will maximize the [Faustmann] expectation value at a
given rate of interest, and if that interest rate falls to zero, then what you
get is maximum mean annual increment" — i.e., CMAI is the correct rule only
under the counterfactual assumption that capital is free.[2]

**The property-tax analogy, applied to public land.** Gaffney grounds the whole
argument in the same rationale he gives for taxing private forest owners: "The
rationale for property taxation is that the tax compensates those who are
excluded from owning real estate for the benefits enjoyed by others who do. The
tax puts pressure on owners to provide social benefits" by forcing them to
finance government, render usable product, and provide employment.[2] Since the
National Forests are *already* publicly owned, he argues they should deliver
even more social benefit than privately taxed land — instead, "far from
yielding government revenues, the Forest Service absorbs them... far from
providing employment, the Forest Service manages land in such a way as to
minimize it," tying up scarce capital in "sleepy," return-free standing
inventory rather than releasing it (via more active, shorter-rotation
management on the best sites) to sectors — Gaffney specifically names housing
construction — that were, in 1977, paying roughly 10% to borrow while the
Service's timber grew at only 2-3% a year.[2]

**Policy recommendation.** Gaffney proposes the Forest Service be required to
"act as though it were saddled with a $42 billion dollar mortgage" and pay
several billion dollars a year in imputed interest to the Treasury until it
sells off timber and land holdings earning no return — with Congress, not the
Service itself, as the necessary actor, since "we cannot realistically expect
Forest Service people" to advocate divesting their own agency's assets.[2] He
also calls for concentrating management on "GOOD-site" forestry (his rephrasing
of "high-site," to avoid confusion with elevation) rather than the Service's "a
little of everything everywhere" spread of resources across marginal, high-cost
terrain — citing William Hyde's contemporaneous Resources for the Future
research on the same overdecentralization problem.[2]

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** Both pieces are **advocacy essays/addresses by a credentialed
economist for live policy debates**, not peer-reviewed papers. "Alternative Ways
of Taxing Forests" is the more balanced of the two, structured explicitly as
pro/con survey before Gaffney states his own ranking; "Greater Social Benefits"
is an industry-association speech with sharper, more polemical language ("fraud,"
"Watergate of the Forest Service") aimed at a receptive timber-industry
audience — the wiki should not present its rhetorical framing as neutral
description. The **tax-incidence and design reasoning** (yield-tax bias against
short cycles, site-value-tax neutrality toward stocking decisions) is standard
public-finance analysis and can be read as C-claims (theoretical); the specific
**empirical figures** (the 38% equivalent yield-tax rate, the $42 billion Forest
Service asset value, the $2.1 billion imputed carrying cost) are Gaffney's own
calculations or his citation of Marion Clawson's contemporaneous accounting,
not independently re-verified by this page — Gaffney's 38% figure explicitly
depends on assumed 50-year rotations and a 7% discount rate that he states are
illustrative, not universal.[1] **Clawson source identified (2026-07-18):** the
$42-billion figure traces to Marion Clawson, "The National Forests," *Science*
191(4228): 762–767 (1976), whose own abstract independently states the Forest
Service held "approximately $42 billion in assets" generating "$400–500
million annually" — corroborating Gaffney's headline figure and revenue range
via the abstract alone (the article itself is paywalled, not read in full this
session, so Gaffney's more granular $486-million 1974 cash-receipts/outlay
split is still his own citation of Clawson, not independently re-derived).[3]

**A5's title is a claim, not a finding.** "Greater Social Benefits from our
National Forests" describes the *goal* Gaffney argues policy reform would
achieve (releasing capital to higher-return uses while National Forest revenue
and employment rise), not a measured outcome — the address is prescriptive, and
no follow-up evaluation of whether the Forest Service actually adopted anything
resembling Gaffney's proposed reforms is covered by this page.

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — extends the concept
  page's severance-tax and leasing-design material (from the California and
  Alaska oil-and-gas pages) to a third rent-bearing resource, standing timber,
  and adds the site-value-tax-vs-yield-tax comparison in numerically worked
  form (the 38%-equivalent-rate calculation) that the concept page's existing
  Gaffney material does not carry.
- **Benefit:** [Capturing resource rent works — where institutions are
  strong](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/) — adds a third institutional
  failure mode alongside Norway/Botswana (design succeeding) and California
  (capture built, then lost): the US Forest Service shows rent that was
  **never captured at all** in an ongoing public asset, not because of
  resource-curse corruption or a political shock, but because of the
  agency's own accounting doctrines (the "allowable cut effect," culmination
  of mean annual increment) — and extends the pattern to a renewable, rather
  than extractive, resource.
