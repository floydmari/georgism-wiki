## Overview

"A Severance Tax on California Oil?" is a July 2006 policy essay by **Mason Gaffney**,
written, he states, for the "California State Assembly Committee on Revenue and
Taxation," in the run-up to that November's **Proposition 87** ballot measure.[1] Prop.
87 would have imposed a severance tax on oil production (a graduated 1.5%–6% of gross
wellhead value depending on the oil price, topping out at 6% above $60/barrel) to fund
alternative-energy programs; Gaffney's essay, describing it in simplified form as a flat
6% levy, was written before the final measure text and campaign were fully settled.[1][2]
**Prop. 87 was defeated at the ballot**, 54.7% to 45.3%, after a then-record $154
million in campaign spending, most of the opposition funded by Chevron and Aera
Energy.[2] The essay is a work of applied public-finance advocacy — Gaffney frames it
explicitly as an attempt to referee an internal Georgist dispute: "Georgists are of
mixed minds on severance taxation. Some oppose it as being a tax on production...
Others say that extracting a natural deposit is not the same as production... There
are elements of truth in both views."[1]

## California's Property-Tax Alternative and Proposition 13

Gaffney's starting point is that California **already had** a working instrument for
capturing oil and gas rent before 1978: an *in-situ* property tax on mineral reserves,
administered by the State Board of Equalization, which he says produced substantial
county revenue (Los Angeles County above all) and was, per BOE specialist Robert
Paschall's testimony to the Alaska legislature, no harder to assess accurately than
ordinary real estate.[1] **Proposition 13 (1978)** — aimed, per Gaffney, entirely at
residential property taxes, with "no voter intent to untax oil and gas" — cut the
property tax rate to roughly a third of its prior level and froze/rolled back assessed
values, which by Gaffney's own arithmetic ("1/3 the original rate times 1/2 the
original base equals 1/6 the original tax revenue") eliminated most of that
resource-rent revenue as an unintended side effect.[1] California, he notes, "is the
only state, major or minor, with no severance tax" — a state that once captured
mineral rent through property assessment lost that capacity through a tax reform aimed
at an entirely different target, then had no substitute mechanism in place.[1]

## The Constitutional and Legal Foundation

Gaffney treats the severance tax's constitutionality as settled: **Commonwealth Edison
Co. v. Montana**, 453 U.S. 609 (1981), upheld Montana's 30% coal severance tax against
a Commerce Clause challenge from an out-of-state utility buyer, reasoning that in-state
buyers were taxed identically and that much of the tax burden fell on Montana
coal-land owners rather than being exported to consumers.[1]

## The Case That There Is a Large Taxable Rent

Gaffney assembles six converging indicators that California oil and gas carries
substantial economic rent (defined as "fat in the private sector" — private income in
excess of what is needed to induce production): most fields were developed and remain
profitable at prices far below current levels; major-oil-company acquisitions of
California fields at high valuations are themselves evidence of high rent; the state
already taxes no severance at all, unlike every other oil-producing state; publicly
owned California "tidelands" and federal Outer Continental Shelf leases fetch high
competitive-auction prices, implying comparable private-land rents; oil and gas
extraction earns roughly 40% of Fortune 500 profits with only about 10% of Fortune 500
employment — a high profit-per-employee ratio Gaffney reads as a rent signature; and
the state gasoline tax fails to capture rent because it is a flat per-gallon (not ad
valorem) charge that erodes with inflation, taxes low-cost and high-cost wells
identically, and does not even cover the state's highway-building and maintenance
costs.[1]

## Incidence: Why the Tax Would Not Simply Pass Through to Consumers

The essay's most theoretically developed section addresses the standard objection that
a severance tax would be "shifted forward to California consumers, and therefore be
regressive, like a retail sales tax."[1] Gaffney sets out **four conditions** under
which a production tax passes to consumers — inelastic demand, elastic supply, a high
world market share for the taxed jurisdiction, and a tax structured to hit marginal
production — and argues California meets, at most, one of them partially:

- **World demand for oil is price-sensitive**, evidenced by the demand collapse and
  price crash that followed the 1970s Arab oil embargoes despite OPEC's market
  power.[1]
- **California's supply is comparatively inelastic**: most fields were developed and
  remain productive at prices well below current levels, so — unlike, in his example,
  the high-price-dependent Overthrust Belt — most of the state's production is not on
  the margin the tax would discourage; heavy, low-quality oil (often cited against
  severance taxes) is taxed on wellhead value before refining costs are incurred, so
  the tax does not touch the added refining burden.[1]
- **California is a small share of world oil supply**, so even a fully inelastic
  domestic demand curve would face a highly elastic supply of substitutes (Alberta's
  oil sands, Rockies shale, Middle Eastern and Latin American imports).[1]
- On the fourth condition, Gaffney argues the tax's bite on marginal production would
  be **largely offset by lower royalties**: because royalty payments and a severance
  tax are both variable, unit-of-production charges, lessees negotiating new leases
  would rationally offer landowners lower royalty rates once a severance tax is
  anticipated, shifting the tax's incidence toward existing rent recipients rather than
  new production decisions. Gaffney frames royalty owners' incentive relevance as
  minimal in the standard lease structure, since lessees (not royalty owners) control
  extraction rates.[1]

He separately notes that oil and gas already carry standard royalty rates far above
the proposed severance rate (minimum 12.5%, and above 50% in Saudi Arabia) "with no
noticeable disincentive effects," offered as evidence that a single-digit severance
rate is unlikely to be the disincentive critics warn of.[1]

## Design: Toward a "Net Proceeds" Tax Base

Gaffney's policy recommendation is that a severance tax be redefined, where
administratively feasible, from a **gross wellhead value** base toward a **net
proceeds** (post-cost) base — closer to the "well-foot" value before extraction costs
are incurred — citing this design as already used in Nevada, Idaho, Utah, and South
Dakota, and developed theoretically by Australian economists Ross Garnaut and Anthony
Clunies-Ross for Papua New Guinea's mining tax regime.[1] He argues this design change
would let the state raise the *rate* (recovering revenue lost to the narrower base)
while more precisely targeting pure rent, sparing genuinely marginal or high-cost
fields (steam-injection recovery, for instance) from bearing a tax that would abort
production rather than merely capture surplus. He closes: "Oil and gas are indeed a
source of rent. The severance tax would abort only a minor share of it, and this flaw
may be remedied by modifying the tax to allow reasonable cost deductions."[1]

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** This is advocacy testimony/essay writing by a credentialed economist
for a live legislative and ballot-measure debate, not a peer-reviewed paper — Gaffney
says so implicitly by describing the piece as written "for" a legislative committee
and pegged to a specific initiative. The **incidence theory (who bears a severance
tax)** is standard public-finance reasoning and can be read as a C-claim (theoretical,
citing accepted tax-incidence conditions); the **empirical claims about California's
supply elasticity and the profit-per-employee rent signature** are asserted rather than
formally estimated in the essay — Gaffney himself flags uncertainty in a footnote on
the Fortune 500 profit-share figures ("these figures are only roughly correct; check
specific source before publishing"), a caution this page inherits.[1] The essay
predates, but is broadly consistent with, the design principles this wiki documents
from the IMF (Baunsgaard & Vernon) and Canadian public finance (Mintz & Chen) on the
[resource-rent-capture-works](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/) page — that
rent-targeting, cost-deductible tax bases outperform blunt gross-value levies — though
those sources were not available to, or cited by, Gaffney in 2006.

## Bears On

- **Benefit:** [Capturing resource rent works — where institutions are strong](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/)
  — adds a US state-level historical case distinct from Norway and Botswana: California
  once captured oil-and-gas rent effectively through an in-situ property tax, then lost
  that capacity as an unintended side effect of Proposition 13 — illustrating that
  capture can be lost through an unrelated political shock, not only through weak
  institutions or a resource curse, and that Gaffney's proposed remedy (a net-proceeds
  severance tax) is a design response to that specific loss.
- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — supplies a detailed
  incidence argument (the four pass-through conditions, and the royalty-offset
  mechanism) and a concrete design alternative (net-proceeds taxation) that the concept
  page's one-line mention of "severance and royalty taxes" did not previously carry.
