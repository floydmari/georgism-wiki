## Overview

"Tax-Induced Slow Turnover of Capital" is a five-part series Mason Gaffney published
in the *American Journal of Economics and Sociology* running from vol. 29 (1970)
through vol. 30, no. 1 (January 1971), expanding a shorter 1967 article of the same
title in the *Western Economic Journal* 5(4): 308–23.[1] Gaffney's own footnote in
his later ["Toward Full Employment with Limited Land and Capital"](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-limited-land/)
(1976) describes the relationship directly: the AJES series is "much more fully
treated than in the [1967] WEJ" version, with the land-income material specifically
at AJES 29(3): 409–24.[2] This page reads and mines the full unabridged series —
obtained as Gaffney's own posted copy at masongaffney.org, which runs continuously
through all five installments in one PDF — rather than relying on the two second-hand
routes by which the wiki previously touched it: I1's 1976 footnote, and a partial
reprint of the series' Appendix II inside Gaffney's 1977 [Alaska oil-leasing
report](/wiki/gaffney-alaska-oil-leasing/).[1]

The series carries mathematical appendices credited jointly to Gaffney and several
collaborators (Michèle Consigny, William Vickrey, Matthew P. Gaffney Jr., Ralph
Krause, and Joseph Holzinger), reflecting seminar circulation at RFF, UCLA, UC Santa
Barbara, and UC Berkeley in the late 1960s.[1] A 1971 rejoinder, "Time, Taxes,
Turnover, and Intensity" (AJES, catalogued in the wiki's Gaffney corpus triage as
O11), responds point-by-point to a critique by Thomson and Goldstein; it was fetched
for context on this wave and confirms the triage's characterization — it is a
technical priority-and-terminology dispute (a mislabelled "income tax" that is
actually a land tax; credit for the Faustmann-formula rediscovery; "yield" vs.
"excise" vs. "gross" tax terminology) that restates rather than extends the parent
series' substantive results, so it is not separately mined here.[3]

## Part I: Four Classes of Real Assets and the Bias to Longevity

Part I sets up the taxonomy the whole series works within: **appreciating assets**
(trees, cattle — point-input, point-output, whose yield is entirely salvage value at
maturity); **"full salvage" or constant-value assets** (the "Bessie the milk cow"
model — yields a steady income and returns full initial value at the end of a fixed
life, used for its analytical simplicity); **depreciating assets** (plant and
equipment, yielding a cash flow that mixes income and capital recovery); and
**land** (infinite life, appreciates more often than it depreciates).[1] Gaffney's
central claim, worked through each class in turn, is that ordinary excise and income
taxation biases investors toward longer maturities and slower capital turnover —
because deferring tax liability to a later date is itself a form of return, so
investments structured to defer income (rather than realize it currently) gain an
implicit tax subsidy. He traces the macro stakes to Böhm-Bawerk and Wicksell's
argument that faster capital turnover expands the number of jobs a fixed capital
fund can finance — the same "wages-flow" mechanism the wiki's [marginal-productivity](/wiki/marginal-productivity/)
page carries only via Gaffney's 2008 retrospective (K2008); here it is Gaffney's own
1970 working of the same turnover-and-employment link, six years before he builds it
into the full K·T macro model on [I1](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-limited-land/).[1]

## Part II: The Excise-Tax Case and an Early ATCOR Precursor

Part II works the mathematics of excise (gross-receipts) taxation on the point-input,
point-output case, showing algebraically that any positive excise tax rate makes the
after-tax return negative for maturities below a threshold that rises with the tax
rate — a formal proof of the longevity bias sketched in Part I.[1] It then examines
**expensing** (writing off capital investment immediately, in year zero, as a current
cost) as a hypothetical fix, and derives a result with a direct bearing on the wiki's
[ATCOR](/wiki/atcor/) page: because land cannot legally be expensed (it is not a
depreciable cost) while capital investment can be, universal expensing of capital
"tends to convert the corporate income tax, which exempts wages, into a tax on land
income" — since only land income remains inside the tax base once capital's return is
fully sheltered.[1] Gaffney immediately qualifies the result (expensing has several
practical defects — it favors old, established, cash-rich taxpayers; it discriminates
against sub-annual investments; land can be gamed into the expensed category by
disguising it as an old building's purchase price) and recommends the ad valorem
land-value tax as the cleaner instrument for reaching the same base.[1] **Priority
note:** this is a formal 1970 statement of the mechanism the wiki's ATCOR page
currently traces only to 1994's "negative shifting" language in *The Corruption of
Economics* — see the cross-link added to [concepts/atcor.md](/wiki/atcor/) below.
It is narrower than the full ATCOR claim (Gaffney derives it specifically for
capital-expensing under the corporate income tax, not as a general theorem about
all taxes), so it is presented as a precursor to the mechanism, not a restatement of
ATCOR itself.

## Parts III-IV: The True-Depreciation Neutrality Theorem

Part III completes the "Bessie" constant-value case (own criteria: (a) tax income
when it accrues, whether as cash or appreciated value; (b) write off assets only
when and as they actually depreciate) and states the resulting synthesis tersely:
"tax cash receipts, deduct cash outlays; tax appreciation, deduct depreciation."[1]
It then opens the general depreciating-asset case, laying out a four-step program —
(1) modify the after-tax-return equation to include a depreciation write-off term,
(2) define "true" yearly depreciation as the actual drop in an asset's present value,
(3) substitute true depreciation into the equation, (4) show the after-tax return
equals `i(1-t)` for every asset life once true depreciation is allowed — which Part
IV/V carries to completion in the body text.[1] This body-text derivation is the
general theorem that the series' Appendix II (credited to Vickrey and Consigny, and
already noted on the [Alaska report page](/wiki/gaffney-alaska-oil-leasing/) as
reprinted there) proves formally with continuous-time calculus: **an income tax
using true economic depreciation is intertemporally neutral.** The wiki's prior
touch on this result via the Alaska report captured only the formal proof; this page
is the first to carry the verbal derivation and worked numerical example (Table 2,
showing after-tax return turning negative for maturities under 19 years at a 50%
excise rate and 8% pre-tax return) that motivate it.[1]

Part IV turns to land specifically. Beyond the theoretical case (land income taxed
at accrual is neutral, per the same logic as the excise-tax analysis), Gaffney
catalogues institutional mechanisms by which land in practice escapes even that:
taxpayers routinely allocate purchase price for land-under-an-old-building
disproportionately to the (depreciable) building, writing off land's cost — "not
just once, but several times" as buildings are replaced — despite the letter of the
law forbidding land depreciation; percentage depletion allowances let mineral-rights
value (a pure rent) go untaxed even before extraction; and "implicit expensing" (an
owner foregoing current land income to capture a larger, later, tax-deferred gain) is
available to landowners more than to holders of other assets because land's life is
infinite.[1] The section closes on a Faustmann-formula application — reprising the
same maturity condition worked in Gaffney's 1957 [Financial Maturity of Timber](/wiki/gaffney-financial-maturity-timber/)
monograph — to show that when a tax on land income is shifted (reducing site value
S), the lower S delays a timber stand's optimal harvest date, because the tree's
required carrying-cost return (interest on accumulated stumpage value *plus* interest
on the now-lower S) is easier to clear.[1] This is the same "financial maturity"
machinery that becomes the formal `dR/dn = i(R+S)` result in Appendix 3 of the 1976
[Full Employment with Limited Land and Capital](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-limited-land/)
chapter — read together, the three works (1957 pure Faustmann economics → 1970
Faustmann-plus-taxation → 1976 full K·T macro model) form a single 19-year line of
development on one mechanism, not three independent treatments.

## Part V: Summary, a Constitutional Aside, and the Two Mathematical Appendices

Part V's own summary states the series' upshot plainly: the only part of the income
tax that can be made "perfectly neutral" is the part that falls on land income,
because land rent is a residual that cannot be shifted away by the owner the way
capital and labor returns can.[1] Gaffney adds a legal aside — since his
true-depreciation definition of taxable income makes an income tax "much like the
property tax," he suggests the 16th Amendment's authorization of taxes on "incomes
from whatever source derived" might let Congress tax property directly, free of the
apportionment-among-states rule, if the *Eisner v. Macomber* (1920) realization
doctrine were revisited — a speculative legal argument (a D-claim, Gaffney's own,
not independently checked here) rather than an economic result.[1]

The series closes with two distinct mathematical proofs, both already partly on the
wiki's radar via the Alaska report, now placed in their original context:

- **Appendix I**, credited to William Vickrey, Matthew Gaffney Jr., and Joseph
  Holzinger, proves that the effective after-tax rate of return on a point-input,
  point-output investment *rises* with the investment's life, for any tax rate on
  the maturity gain strictly between 0 and 1 — the formal version of the longevity-
  bias claim worked more roughly with Table 2 in Part III.[1] This proof is
  **not** the one reprinted in the Alaska report; it has no prior wiki citation.
- **Appendix II**, credited to Michèle Consigny (original formulation) and William
  Vickrey (the shortened, generalized form Gaffney published), proves the general
  intertemporal-neutrality theorem stated in Part III: using continuous-time present-
  value calculus, it shows that taxing net income after true depreciation leaves the
  private and public discount functions related by an identity that makes the
  after-tax present value of any asset's cash-flow stream equal its pre-tax cost —
  i.e., the tax collects revenue without distorting the timing decision at all.[1]
  This is the proof already reprinted, word for word modulo scan noise, as Appendix
  J of Gaffney's 1977 Alaska oil-leasing report, where it backs the report's claim
  that an ad valorem charge on oil-in-place does not bias extraction timing the way
  a royalty does; see that page's Part II discussion for the applied argument.[4]

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [ATCOR](/wiki/atcor/) — priority-note cross-link added: Part II's
  expensing-converts-the-income-tax-to-a-land-tax derivation is a 1970 formal
  precursor to the shifting-onto-rent mechanism, predating the 1994 "negative
  shifting" language in *The Corruption of Economics* by 24 years. Narrower in scope
  (capital-expensing specifically, not a general theorem about all taxes), so framed
  as a precursor mechanism, not an earlier coining of ATCOR itself.
- **Research:** [Gaffney (1976), Toward Full Employment with Limited Land and
  Capital](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-limited-land/) — this series is the
  derivational link between Gaffney's 1957 Faustmann-formula monograph and the 1976
  chapter's K·T=F macro model; see Part IV above.
- **Research:** [Gaffney (1957), Concepts of Financial Maturity of Timber](/wiki/gaffney-financial-maturity-timber/)
  — the pure Faustmann-maturity machinery this series applies, for the first time in
  Gaffney's corpus, to the question of how taxation (not just interest rates) shifts
  harvest timing.
- **Research:** [Gaffney: Oil and Gas Leasing Policy for Alaska (1977)](/wiki/gaffney-alaska-oil-leasing/)
  — reprints this series' Appendix II verbatim as Appendix J; that page's note now
  points back here for the appendix's original context and for the previously
  uncited Appendix I.
- **Concept:** [marginal-productivity](/wiki/marginal-productivity/) — the
  Wicksell wages-flow/turnover material that page carries only via Gaffney's 2008
  retrospective (K2008) is a theme Gaffney was already developing in his own voice
  in Part I of this 1970 series; cited in prose only, not added to that page's
  citation apparatus, since the 2008 paper remains the fuller treatment of Wicksell
  specifically.

## Standing and Limits

This is Type-C theoretical material — formal derivations from stated assumptions,
not empirical findings — and is kept out of every `supported_by` array on
problems/benefits pages, consistent with the wiki's convention for Gaffney's other
capital-theory derivations (see the [Full Employment](/wiki/gaffney-full-employment-limited-land/)
page's Bears On section for the same discipline applied to its Appendix 3 result).
None of the series' claims are tested against data anywhere in the five parts; the
numerical tables (e.g. Table 2) use assumed interest and tax rates to illustrate the
algebra, not estimated parameters. The series is still under U.S. copyright (1970-71,
well inside the wiki's public-domain cutoff), so this page paraphrases with only
short quotations rather than reproducing text; the full unabridged series was OCR'd
from Gaffney's own posted PDF and is not currently mirrored to the wiki as a `texts/`
page. O11, the 1971 rejoinder to Thomson and Goldstein, was fetched and read for
context only (confirming the triage's "technical rejoinder" characterization) and is
not separately mined; if it later proves to contain content beyond restating the
parent series, that is future-wave work, not resolved here.
