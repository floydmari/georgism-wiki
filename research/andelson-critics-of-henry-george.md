## Overview

*Critics of Henry George: A Centenary Appraisal of Their Strictures on Progress
and Poverty* is an edited scholarly anthology, first published in 1979 by
Fairleigh Dickinson University Press (Rutherford, NJ) and Associated University
Presses (London), edited by philosopher **Robert V. Andelson** (Auburn
University).[1] The book gathers and critically appraises, chapter by chapter,
the objections leveled against [Henry George's](/wiki/henry-george/)
*[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* by figures ranging from
[Alfred Marshall](/wiki/alfred-marshall/) and [John Bates Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)
to twentieth-century critics such as [Murray Rothbard](/wiki/murray-rothbard/)
and [Mark Blaug](/wiki/blaug-henry-george-rebel/). It has been described as the
most comprehensive review and analysis of the strictures against George's
single-tax argument.[1] Andelson substantially revised and expanded the work
into a two-volume second edition, published by Blackwell in 2004 under the
title *Critics of Henry George: An Appraisal of Their Strictures on Progress
and Poverty*.[2]

Because the book's purpose is to compile the opposing case against the single
tax essay by essay, it functions on this wiki as a reference index into the
critical literature rather than as a source for any single claim — later pages
on named critics and objections may cite specific chapters once read in full.
