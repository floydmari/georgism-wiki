## Summary

Power and Frerick, "Have Excess Returns to Corporations Been Increasing Over
Time?" (*National Tax Journal*; also Treasury Office of Tax Analysis Working
Paper 111, 2016), measure what share of the US corporate tax base represents the
**normal return to capital** versus **excess returns** — returns above what a
risk-free benchmark would generate, the tax-data operationalization of
[economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) plus risk premia.[1]

## Findings

Using C-corporation tax-return microdata for 1992–2013, the normal
(risk-free-rate) return share of the corporate tax base **averaged about 40%
in 1992–2002 and about 25% in 2003–2013** — equivalently, excess returns rose
from roughly 60% to roughly 75% of the base.[1] [VERIFY: exact figures against
OTA WP 111 — snippet-corroborated across multiple independent sources this
session, but the paper itself was proxy-blocked.]

This is the actual source of the widely circulated claim that "60–75% of the US
corporate tax base is excess returns." It sits in a family of Treasury-data
work: Cronin, Lin, Power & Cooper (2013) had already attributed 63% of
corporate taxable income to supernormal returns in Treasury's distributional
methodology (assigning that share to shareholders), and Fox (2020) later showed
that for 1995–2013 the actual corporate tax raised roughly what a pure
cash-flow tax would have — implying the existing tax already falls
predominantly on above-normal returns.[2]

## Interpretation — and Its Limits

The finding cuts two ways, per the wiki's [rent gradient](/wiki/geoism/):

- **For the Geoist reading:** a corporate tax base that is 60–75% excess
  returns means rent-targeting designs like the
  [allowance for corporate equity](/wiki/allowance-for-corporate-equity/) or a
  [cash-flow tax](/wiki/cash-flow-tax/) would exempt only a minority of the
  base — rate-for-rate, most corporate revenue survives the switch while the
  distortion falls on the exempted normal-return margin.
- **The honest caveat:** "excess over the risk-free rate" bundles pure rent
  with risk premia and [quasi-rents](/wiki/quasi-rent/) on innovation — the
  benchmark choice drives the number (Reynolds & Neubig 2016), and the
  [Schumpeterian objection](/wiki/taxing-quasi-rents-kills-innovation/) turns
  on exactly that bundling.[3]
