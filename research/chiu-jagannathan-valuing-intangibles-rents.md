## Summary

"A Simple Approach to Valuing Intangibles and Rents" (NBER Working Paper 30829,
January 2023, revised February 2025) by **Wan-Chien Chiu** (National Tsing Hua
University), **Ravi Jagannathan** (Kellogg/Northwestern and NBER), **Yo-Lan Lin**
(Yuan Ze), and **Kevin Tseng** (CUHK) proposes a way to
measure how much of a public company's stock-market value is *not* explained by its
tangible assets or its investment in intangible capital — and argues that this
leftover is, for the most part, **economic rent**.[1] It is directly relevant to
this wiki because it puts a large, rising, dollar-denominated number on the same
market-power surplus the [corporate profits increasingly reflect economic
rents](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/) page tracks — while using the
*same* intangibles-capitalization method as [Crouzet &
Eberly](/wiki/crouzet-eberly-intangibles/), the page's leading counter-argument.

One caution frames everything below: the paper's headline quantity is a **residual**,
not a direct measurement of rent, and its authors are careful (and hedged) about the
inference from one to the other. The wiki carries the finding on that basis.

## What the paper measures

The method decomposes a firm's market value into four parts (eq. 1, p. 6):

> Market value of assets = Tangible assets + Book intangible assets + Capitalized
> intangible assets + Valuation residual

The third term, **capitalized intangible assets**, is the estimated value of the
firm's own investment in knowledge capital (from R&D) and organization capital (from
a portion of SG&A), built up by the standard perpetual-inventory method — this is the
"it's really investment, not rent" component. The fourth term, the **valuation residual
(VR)**, is what remains, and the authors state plainly that it "consists of economic
rents, capacity adjustment costs, and potential mispricing" (p. 1).

Their headline aggregate result (p. 3):

> "the share of valuation residuals as a percentage of the market value of firms has
> increased from almost nothing in 1976 to 45% in 2020, with capitalized intangible
> assets contributing consistently around 10%."

In dollars, the 2020 aggregate market value of the sample (~16,500 US public firms,
excluding financials) is about **$45 trillion**, of which the residual is about **$21
trillion**, tangible assets about $14 trillion, book intangibles about $5.5 trillion,
and capitalized intangibles about $4 trillion (p. 18). The residual was actually
*negative* until 1984 (the authors attribute the 1970s–80s gap to stock-market
undervaluation), then climbed steadily. The rise "stems entirely from firms in the
top half of the distribution" (pp. 16–17): the largest 10% of firms account for roughly
$20 trillion of the $21 trillion. At the firm level the residual is extremely
right-skewed — a median of about $5 million but a mean of $671 million — and **Apple**
tops the ranking with an average residual near **$367 billion** over 2016–2020, ahead
of Amazon (~$279bn) and Microsoft (~$270bn) (p. 16, Appendix B). By industry the
residual is largest in **high-tech and healthcare** (Online Appendix Table OA5).

## Why the authors read the residual as (mostly) rent

This is the load-bearing inference, and it is explicitly an *inference*, not a
measurement. The argument (p. 3):

> "The extensive finance literature suggests that VRs are unlikely to have significant
> persistent mispricing components... Moreover, Crouzet and Eberly (2023) demonstrate
> that capacity adjustment costs are small relative to economic rents. Hence, VRs are
> likely to have significant ex-post economic rents components."

That is: of the residual's three possible sources — rents, capacity-adjustment costs,
and mispricing — the authors argue the other two are small (adjustment costs from
Crouzet & Eberly, whose own decomposition attributes ~85% of the relevant gap to
rents; mispricing from the efficient-markets literature), leaving rent as the
dominant remainder. Their estimation is even built on this as a stated *prior* — that
"product markets are competitive and economic rents are difficult to obtain and
maintain... and capital markets are on average informationally efficient" (p. 10) —
which the empirical sections then test for consistency rather than prove from scratch.
The rent concept is explicitly **ex-post** (realized): in their patent-race example,
"ex ante, there are no economic rents, but the winning firm will earn huge economic
rents ex post" (p. 2, fn. 3).

## The market-power evidence

The paper's strongest contribution is a battery of correlations showing that
high-residual firms behave like firms with market power. Comparing a firm at the 75th
versus 25th percentile of size-and-industry-adjusted residual, the authors find it
has: markups higher by **15.5% above the median** (using the [De Loecker–Eeckhout–Unger](/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/)
measure, p. 22); profitability higher by **6–12.5%** (p. 4); a labor share **7.1%
lower** despite paying higher absolute wages (echoing [Autor et al.](/wiki/autor-superstar-firms/)
and [Barkai](/wiki/barkai-declining-shares/), p. 23); **3.4% fewer** product-market
threats (p. 23); and lower sensitivity of investment to Tobin's Q (p. 27). High-residual
firms with strong pre-existing competitive advantages also benefited
disproportionately from the China trade shock after 2000 (p. 28). Each of these is what
the market-power reading predicts and the pure-mispricing reading does not — which is
the sense in which the correlations support the rent interpretation.

## Relation to the Georgist case

This belongs on the **contested frontier** of the wiki's [rent
gradient](/wiki/geoism/), well away from the clean land case, and the honest framing
matters more here than the headline number. Three points:

- **It is market-power / franchise rent on corporate intangibles, not land rent.** The
  paper's "rents" are the returns to patents, brands, network effects, scale, and
  superstar-firm position among US public companies. The word "land" does not appear as
  an economic category anywhere in it. Its value to the Georgist argument is as evidence
  that the rent phenomenon extends far beyond land into modern corporate positions — the
  same move the [corporate-profits](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/),
  [data-rents](/wiki/data-rents/), and [taxing tech rents](/wiki/taxing-tech-rents/)
  pages already make — not as a measurement of land rent.
- **It sharpens, rather than settles, the contested question.** Because it uses the same
  intangibles-capitalization method as [Crouzet & Eberly](/wiki/crouzet-eberly-intangibles/)
  yet separates capitalized intangibles (~10%, the "return to real investment" story)
  from the residual (~45%, the rent story), it is a genuine attempt to draw the line
  between the two readings the corporate-profits page holds in tension — and it lands on
  the rent side, while conceding the residual is not *purely* rent.
- **What the paper does not do — and cannot be cited for.** It contains no tax-policy
  analysis, no rent-capture proposal, and no [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/)
  arithmetic. Its sample is US *public* firms only, so it says nothing directly about
  private companies (which hold much corporate value) or the whole-economy rent base.
  Extending the $21 trillion figure to private firms, or converting it into potential
  tax revenue or per-capita dividends, is a further argument the paper itself does not
  make; such extensions are advocacy extrapolation, not findings of this study, and
  should be labeled as such wherever they appear.

## Nuances and Limits

- **Working paper, not peer-reviewed.** NBER WP 30829 carries the standard notice that
  it has "not been peer-reviewed." Treat its estimates as a serious but unrefereed
  result.
- **The residual is an upper bound on rent, not rent itself.** By construction VR also
  contains capacity-adjustment costs, labor-force-change costs, and mispricing. The
  authors argue rent dominates, but that is an inference resting on external evidence and
  a stated prior — not a decomposition that isolates rent within the residual.
- **Mispricing is real for a defined subset.** The paper's own asset-pricing test finds
  significantly negative risk-adjusted returns (about −10.7%/year) concentrated in
  **small-cap, low-profitability, high-residual** portfolios — i.e., for those firms the
  residual reflects overpricing, not rent. The authors concede this: other firms "are on
  average fairly priced" (p. 33), but this subset is the exception.
- **Within-industry uniformity assumption.** The method assumes the same intangible
  depreciation rates and SG&A investment share for all firms in an industry, so genuine
  firm-level differences in intangible-building efficiency get pushed into the residual
  (p. 2, fn. 4) — a channel by which some non-rent value could be mislabeled.
- **Ends in 2020.** Despite the 2025 revision, the data stop in 2020; there is no
  post-pandemic or recent-AI-era figure.
