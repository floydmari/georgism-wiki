## Overview

Jason Barr, Fred Smith, and Sayali Kulkarni's "What's Manhattan Worth? A Land
Values Index from 1950 to 2014," published in *Regional Science and Urban
Economics* (2018), constructs an annual land-value index for Manhattan using
vacant-lot sale transactions rather than the more common (and less reliable)
cost-approach method of subtracting estimated structure value from total
property sale prices.[1] The authors identify three land-value cycles between
1950 and 2014, with a trough around 1977 following New York City's fiscal
crisis, and find an average annual real growth rate of 5.5% over the full
period — accelerating to roughly 15.8% per year after 1993, which they
attribute to development barriers pushing prices up faster than other
measures of local economic well-being.[1] By their estimate, all developable
land on Manhattan was worth approximately **$1.74 trillion** in 2014.[1]

Lars Doucet's *Land Is a Big Deal* cites this figure as part of a broader
case that most of the value of urban real estate is, in fact, land rather
than structures — a premise underlying arguments that land value taxation
could capture a very large revenue base.[2] Doucet also uses the paper's
vacant-land-sales methodology as a model for why direct market transactions
in unimproved land are a more defensible way to estimate land value than
residual cost-approach methods, which he argues systematically understate
land's share.
