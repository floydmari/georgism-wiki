## Summary

"A Short History of Enclosure in Britain" (*The Land Magazine*, Issue 7, Summer 2009)
by **Simon Fairlie** — editor of *The Land* and a former editor of *The Ecologist* —
is the most widely-circulated modern survey of British enclosure: the multi-century
process by which common land was "taken out of some form of collective ownership and
management and handed over to individuals."[1] It is the standard accessible source
for the historical backbone of the Georgist claim that existing land titles descend
from acts of appropriation rather than production (see
[land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) and
[The Land Question](/wiki/the-land-question/)).

This page also carries the **revisionist counter-evidence** — the wiki's honesty rule
for contested history — because the scale of enclosure's harm is disputed among
economic historians.

## Fairlie's Account

- **Scale of parliamentary enclosure.** Between 1760 and 1870, about **7 million
  acres — roughly one sixth of England** — were converted from common to enclosed
  land by some **4,000 Acts of Parliament**.[1] (The UK Parliament's own heritage
  account counts over 5,200 enclosure bills from 1604 to 1914, covering just over a
  fifth of England's area — about 6.8 million acres; the two figures use different
  periods and definitions.[2])
- **A longer arc than the Acts.** Parliamentary enclosure was the *final* phase:
  Fairlie surveys Tudor sheep enclosures, seventeenth-century drainage and
  "improvement," and the informal engrossment that preceded the legislative wave.
- **Who lost.** Commoners lost grazing, fuel, and gleaning rights that had made
  landless subsistence viable; the process, in Fairlie's reading, created the
  landless labour force of industrialization and severed most Britons' connection to
  agricultural land.
- The account sits in the tradition of **E.P. Thompson**, whose *Making of the
  English Working Class* (1963) called enclosure "a plain enough case of class
  robbery, played according to fair rules of property and law laid down by a
  parliament of property-owners and lawyers,"[3] and of **J.M. Neeson's**
  *Commoners* (1993), the leading archival study of what common right was worth to
  those who held it.[4]

## The Revisionist Counter-Evidence

Honest use of this history requires the other side. **Gregory Clark and Anthony
Clark (2001)**, in the *Journal of Economic History*, argue from land-rent charity
records that:

- common land was only about **27% of England by 1600** (and perhaps only a third
  even in 1500) — "private property was thus the norm in England by 1600";
- most common land was **"stinted"** — usable only by holders of formal property
  rights, not by the landless;
- common waste genuinely open to the landless poor was only around **4% of land**,
  much of it marginal;
- consequently **both** the efficiency gains claimed for enclosure **and** the
  welfare losses to the landless poor were much smaller than the
  Hammond–Thompson–Neeson tradition holds.[5]

The disagreement is about *magnitude and incidence*, not about whether enclosure
happened or whether Parliament's process favoured those who controlled it. Any wiki
page deploying enclosure as a moral datum should state the dispute.

## Relation to the Georgist Case

Enclosure is the concrete British instance of the appropriation story Henry George
argued in general form in [*The Land Question*](/wiki/the-land-question/) (1881) and
[*A Perplexed Philosopher*](/wiki/a-perplexed-philosopher/): trace any land title
back far enough and one finds not production but taking. The Georgist inference —
that rent capture going forward is the practical remedy — is a separate, attributed
argument (see [Narrative: The Great Land Robbery](/wiki/the-great-land-robbery/)),
not a fact this history establishes by itself.

## Nuances and Limits

- **Fairlie is an advocate-editor, not an academic historian**; *The Land* is a
  land-rights magazine. His survey is well-sourced and widely cited, but tier and
  use should reflect an advocacy-adjacent venue (EDITORIAL source hierarchy level 6
  for representing the position; the underlying facts corroborated to Parliament,
