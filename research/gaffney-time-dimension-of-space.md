## Overview

"The Unwieldy Time-Dimension of Space" is a short (seven-page) theoretical
article Mason Gaffney published in *The American Journal of Economics and
Sociology* 20(5): 465–481 (October 1961), while at the University of
Missouri — confirmed directly from the journal's masthead and the article's
closing affiliation line.[1] It is, on current evidence, **the earliest dated
Gaffney publication in the wiki's corpus** — eleven years before the
1972–73 [urban-land-rent pair](/wiki/gaffney-urban-land-rent/), eighteen
years before ["Land Rent, Taxation, and Public Policy"](/wiki/gaffney-urban-land-rent/),
and thirty-three years before the 1994 [Land as a Distinctive
Factor](/wiki/gaffney-land-distinctive-factor/) essay — and it develops, from
first principles and with an original algebraic model, the theoretical root
of a mechanism Gaffney would restate in increasingly applied form for the
rest of his career: that **financial power, not productive intent, allocates
land**.

## Time-Indivisibility: The Core Concept

Gaffney's starting point is a distinction from the credit-discrimination
literature he developed later. Most durable goods can be *rented* — bought
in small time increments matched to a user's actual need — but land cannot,
in practice: "he who would use a durable good cannot easily buy its services
from week to week or year to year as he needs them... but must buy in one
piece a claim to its services over its full span of years."[1] He calls this
**time-indivisibility**: because land is permanent, its price capitalizes an
infinite income stream, so a buyer must finance not just this year's use but
a claim running "from now to doomsday" — "longer than he is likely to need
it, and costs more money... than many hard-pressed entrepreneurs can
conveniently raise at one time."[1] (C-claim; Gaffney's own theoretical
framing.)

Gaffney then argues, at some length, that the market's usual answers to this
problem — leasing and lending — are poor substitutes for direct ownership,
not full solutions: leasing "intrudes into management an operationally
superfluous human relationship," gives short-term tenants no stake in
long-term asset care, and rarely runs long-term because landlords will not
give up the option to recapture rising rent or evict a defaulting tenant;
lending is segmented by "lenders' risk" (Keynes), credit rationing, and
collateral requirements rather than allocated to the highest marginal
product, quoting agricultural economist Rainer Schikele: "the principle of
allocation is collateral security, not marginal productivity... instead of
allocating capital to where it is scarce, our credit system allocates it to
places where it is ample."[1] This leasing/lending-as-imperfect-time-dividers
argument is genuinely new territory for the wiki's Gaffney corpus — it is not
restated in the later essays already covered, which take the credit-access
gap as a starting premise rather than deriving it from an institutional
critique of leasing and lending markets. (C-claim; theoretical/institutional
argument, attributed.)

## The Dynamic Equilibrium Equation

The paper's most original contribution is a formal model. Gaffney derives
that each individual expands landholding until marginal revenue product
equals the annual marginal cost of holding land — price times the
individual's own interest rate — so that in a market where interest rates
differ across buyers, land's marginal productivity comes to **differ across
holdings**, contrary to the "equimarginal ideal" that rent should sort land
to its most productive use.[1] He illustrates with a numerical example:
against identical land, a buyer facing a 2% opportunity cost ("Mr. 2 Per
Cent") will hold land down to a $2-per-$100 marginal return, while a buyer
facing 6% ("Mr. 6 Per Cent") economizes on land far more strictly, holding
only down to $6 per $100 — so the same land parcel earns very different
marginal returns for its neighboring owners purely as a function of their
financing costs, not their skill or effort.[1]

Extending the model to expected price changes, Gaffney derives what he calls
the "dynamic equilibrium equation" — a buyer now adds land until marginal
product *plus* the expected annual price increment, divided by price, equals
his interest rate. The key result: **appreciation widens the gap that
interest-rate differences create, while depreciation narrows it.** In his
worked numerical case, three buyers facing interest rates in ratio 1:2:3
(Messrs. 2%, 4%, 6%) end up, once a 2%-per-year price increment is expected,
holding land at marginal-return ratios of 0:2:4 — a *wider* spread than
their underlying interest-rate differences alone would produce. Because most
capital depreciates (so its holding cost is dominated by depreciation, which
does not vary by owner) while land does not, Gaffney concludes
time-indivisibility "little perverts" the allocation of ordinary capital but
"seriously perverts" the allocation of land — "immortal land, and especially
appreciating land."[1] (C-claim; original theoretical derivation.)

## Relationship to the Wiki's Existing "Strong Hands" Coverage

The wiki already carries three later, more applied statements of a closely
related mechanism — that unequal access to credit ("strong hands" vs. "weak
hands") concentrates land ownership independent of productive use — from the
1972–73 [urban-land-rent essay](/wiki/gaffney-urban-land-rent/) (with its
worked 9%/3% leverage example), the 1992 [farm-property-tax
study](/wiki/gaffney-rising-inequality-farm-property-tax/), and the 1994
[Land as a Distinctive Factor](/wiki/gaffney-land-distinctive-factor/) essay
(B-8/B-9), all synthesized on [concepts/land-monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/).
**This 1961 paper is their theoretical ancestor, not a fourth independent
data point** — its own numerical example (rates in ratio 1:2:3 producing
returns in ratio 0:2:4 under appreciation) makes the same qualitative claim
as the later "leverage" arithmetic (a ratio of 1.8 widening to 3.0) using
different numbers and a more general, formally derived model. Per the delta
rule already applied when this same recurring mechanism turned up a third
time in the B1 (1967) essay, **the leverage/concentration conclusion itself
is corroboration, not new evidentiary weight** — what this page adds is (1)
the priority correction that the mechanism's earliest known statement is
1961, not 1972–73, (2) the formally derived "dynamic equilibrium equation"
and its appreciation-vs-depreciation asymmetry result, which none of the
later essays re-derive, and (3) the leasing/lending institutional critique
(time-indivisibility proper), which is wholly new territory.

## A Land-Concentration-of-Power Closing Claim

Gaffney closes with an early, informal statement of what would become a
recurring Georgist theme: that the conventional emphasis on land's *imputed
income* undersells its importance, because land's true significance may lie
"in the distribution of land: the conclusion suggests that land may be,
contrary to a widespread impression, near the focus of the concentration of
economic power."[1] Written in 1961, this anticipates by decades the modern
capital-share-and-land literature the wiki covers on
[problems/capital-share-rise-is-land](/wiki/capital-share-rise-is-land/), though
Gaffney offers no empirical test of the claim here — it is asserted as a
theoretical implication of the model, not measured. (D-claim; interpretive,
attributed.)

## Standing and Limits

- **Genre.** A short, formally argued theoretical article in a peer-reviewed
  (though non-mainstream) journal — *AJES* published under a Robert
  Schalkenbach Foundation grant "in the interest of constructive synthesis in
  the social sciences," not a general-interest economics journal. The model
  is Gaffney's own, not independently reviewed or tested against data in this
  paper.
- **No empirical test.** The entire argument is a comparative-statics model
  with illustrative numerical examples, not an empirical study — treat its
  conclusions as theoretical predictions, consistent with (but not proof of)
  the credit-concentration mechanism documented empirically elsewhere on the
  wiki (e.g., [gaffney-rising-inequality-farm-property-tax](/wiki/gaffney-rising-inequality-farm-property-tax/)).
- **OCR provenance.** The source PDF carries a native, `pdftotext`-extractable
  text layer with scanning-era OCR noise typical of this 1961 issue (e.g.
  "GPNEY" and similar character-substitution errors do not appear here, but
  minor character-recognition errors are present throughout the equations,
  which were reconstructed from context and cross-checked against the prose
  description of each numerical example rather than trusted as rendered).

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Land Monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) — supplies the priority
  correction (1961, not 1972–73, is the earliest known Gaffney statement of
  the credit-concentration mechanism) and the formal theoretical apparatus
  (the dynamic equilibrium equation, the appreciation/depreciation asymmetry)
  underlying the "strong hands" argument already covered there.
- **Problem:** [High land rents suppress productivity](/wiki/high-land-rents-suppress-productivity/) —
  adds a second, independent weakest-tier theoretical precursor (alongside
  [gaffney-land-market-distortions](/wiki/gaffney-land-market-distortions/))
  to the privately-captured-rent misallocation channel: land gravitating to
  low-productivity, low-interest-rate holders rather than high-productivity,
  credit-constrained users.
