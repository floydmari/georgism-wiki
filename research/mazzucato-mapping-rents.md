## Summary

This 2023 *Cambridge Journal of Economics* paper by Mariana Mazzucato, Josh Ryan-Collins, and Giorgos Gouzoulis builds a modern framework for identifying **economic rent** — value extracted without corresponding value creation — across the contemporary economy.

## Key Contribution

The authors extend the classical analysis of [land rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) to three domains: **land and natural resources**, **finance**, and **digital platforms** (network and data rents). They argue that distinguishing productive returns from extractive rents is essential for designing taxes and regulation, and that much of what is recorded as "profit" or "capital income" is in fact rent. The paper connects the Georgist tradition to current debates about platform monopolies and financialisation.

## Why It Matters to the Wiki

The paper is the wiki's bridge between the classical land story and
[the modern rents debate](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/): it treats
land rent as the *template* case and asks which contemporary income flows share its
extractive structure. Its three-domain map organizes several of the wiki's clusters —
the [FIRE-sector](/wiki/fire-sector/) financial-rents analysis
([Hudson's](/wiki/michael-hudson/) territory), the
[superstar-firms](/wiki/superstar-firms/) / platform-rents dispute, and the
land-and-housing base ([Ryan-Collins](/wiki/josh-ryan-collins/)'s own
[book](/wiki/rethinking-economics-land-housing/)). The wiki carries the
market-power-vs-efficiency dispute over whether these flows ARE rents at full
strength ([Crouzet & Eberly](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/) on the
challenge side); this framework paper takes the rent reading as its premise —
report it as a framework, not a finding.
