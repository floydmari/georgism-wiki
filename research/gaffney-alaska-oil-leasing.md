## Overview

"Oil and Gas Leasing Policy: Alternatives for Alaska in 1977" is a February 1977
report Mason Gaffney wrote as a consultant to Governor Jay Hammond's Department of
Natural Resources (Commissioner Guy Martin) and the Alaska Legislature's Interim
Committee on Oil and Gas Taxation and Leasing Policy (chaired by Senator Chancy
Croft).[1] The timing matters: Alaska voters had approved the constitutional
amendment creating the **[Alaska Permanent Fund](/wiki/alaska-permanent-fund/)** only
three months earlier, in November 1976, and the Trans-Alaska Pipeline had only just
begun carrying Prudhoe Bay oil that summer — Gaffney's report is a piece of applied
rent-capture design written at the literal founding moment of the fiscal apparatus
the Fund would later feed, five years before the first Permanent Fund Dividend was
paid in 1982.[2] The report itself notes this timing directly: discussing the
state's appropriate discount rate, Gaffney observes that Alaska "is no longer
desperate for front money but on the contrary is looking for outlets for its
permanent fund," and separately warns against forcing capital into "ventures of
questionable marginal productivity merely in order to do something with the
Permanent Fund" — treating the Fund as an already-operating fact of state finance,
not yet a dividend mechanism.[1]

The document is a **commissioned government consulting report**, not a peer-reviewed
paper: Gaffney describes its tenor as "expository and not advocatory," a systematic
survey of leasing alternatives for legislative review rather than a single argued
thesis, though he offers his own "Tentative Recommendations" at the end.[1] This page
covers **Part I** (~36,600 words) — the main report. A companion **Part II**
(Appendices A–L, including contributions by Michael Crommelin, Richard Norgaard, and
Robert Rooney, and Gaffney's own numerical worked examples) exists in the same
archive but is not covered by this page; it remains a candidate for a future wave.

## The Founding Anecdote: $10 Billion of Oil for $6 Million

Gaffney frames the entire report against a single cautionary episode. At a 1965
noncompetitive lease auction, the State sold what he estimates as roughly $10
billion worth of oil at Prudhoe Bay for **$6 million** — elsewhere in the report
specified as "the 1965 sale of 90 percent of the oil in the Prudhoe Bay field for $6
million."[1] He calls the underlying dynamic close to fraud: because payment is
fixed before enough is known about actual reserves, "[t]he rule of caveat emptor is
applied, frequently intentionally by the seller. This constitutes a fraud with
malice aforethought, which would not be tolerated in private business dealings," and
questions "if the State should observe a lower level of elementary morality than is
expected of private sellers."[1] The 1965 sale is Gaffney's recurring example of
**noncompetitive and bonus-bid leasing's central flaw**: because payment is made
before reserves are proven, the State bears nearly all the risk of catastrophic
underpricing while lessees bear the (much smaller) risk of overpaying for a dry
tract. The whole report can be read as a search for lease designs that let the State
learn what it is selling *before* it commits to a price.

## The State's Objectives: Business, Equity, and Waste

Gaffney opens by trying to state Alaska's leasing objectives explicitly rather than
leave them implicit. **Business-like management** he defines narrowly and
operationally as maximizing the state's discounted cash flow (DCF) — "[t]his report
uses... Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)," net of costs and of interest on capital tied up
between commitment and recovery — while stressing the criterion is not
narrowly financial: "[i]t does not always comprehend all relevant values," but
"provides a logical analytical framework within which newly recognized goods and
bads may be added," citing environmental damage to commercial and sports fisheries
as an example of a cost the framework can absorb rather than ignore.[1]

**Equity** he ties explicitly to the classical/Georgist productivity theory of
distribution: "rewards should be shared in relation to productive contributions
rather than other factors like luck of the draw and the weight of superior wealth
accumulated in the past," quoting Jefferson's 1813 letter to Adams on the "natural
aristocracy" of "virtue and talent" against the "artificial aristocracy" of "wealth
and birth."[1] The policy implication he draws is direct: "we should seek to
identify and measure that value which belongs to the State, that is the rent of
land, and then collect it. At the same time we leave to lessees all the other
values, the ones which they create" — and he adds that a well-designed
rent-collecting instrument "will leave more incentive to lessees than a blunt
instrument such as a royalty accomplishes," because it "will take more from the
flush deposits in their flush years and less or none from the marginal deposits."[1]

**Avoiding economic waste**, his fourth objective, extends the state's existing
physical-waste rules (against flaring, against overly fast extraction) to
**economic** waste — producing at excessive cost or at the wrong time — including an
explicit warning against "the dissipation of rent by overspending, particularly on
capital," a theme that connects this 1977 policy report to the capital-misallocation
mechanism Gaffney would elaborate in later working papers.[1][3]

## The Ad Valorem Charge: Gaffney's Central Design Proposal

The report's most substantive policy contribution is the **ad valorem charge (AVC)**
— a rental "specified as a percentage of the appraised value of the unproduced
minerals in the ground," which Gaffney says "closely resembles the ad valorem
property tax in its impact and administration."[1] He positions it against the
leasing system's other elements on one key distinction: the AVC is assessed **ex
post discovery** (based on what has actually been found), while bonus bids, delay
rentals, and most royalty designs are **ex ante** (based on guesses about what might
be there) — "[t]hus AVC has some of the good and some of the bad qualities of a
royalty," collecting on what is actually proven while retaining royalty's mild
disincentive to prove up reserves in the first place, an effect he judges "much
weaker... because the ad valorem charge is based only on the value of the resource
in situ" and not, as with a royalty, on production costs as well.[1]

The AVC is explicitly the report's top recommendation: "I would place greatest
emphasis on that lease element I have called the ad valorem charge (AVC)."[1] Ranked
against the alternatives — bonus bidding, delay rentals, royalties, profit sharing —
Gaffney judges bonus bidding worst, because it screens out capital-poor but
efficient operators and gambles the State's entire return on a single, largely
uninformed guess; a high AVC, by contrast, "assures that the State will collect
[deposit] surpluses" without that front-end filter, and "at a high rate obviates
reservations of acreage for the purpose of getting top dollar at drainage
sales."[1] The design closely parallels the *in-situ* property tax on mineral
reserves that California's State Board of Equalization actually operated before
1978 — the same instrument Gaffney would later document, in his 2006 essay on
[a severance tax for California oil](/wiki/gaffney-california-severance-tax/), as
having been effectively eliminated as a side effect of Proposition 13.[4] Robert
Paschall, the California assessor whose testimony Gaffney cites in both reports for
the administrative practicality of appraising reserves in place, appears in this
1977 Alaska report nine years before the California mechanism he had helped
administer was gutted.[1][4]

## Timing Leases: The "Ripeness" Criterion

A secondary but distinctive contribution is Gaffney's answer to *when* the State
should sell leases, not just on what terms. He proposes ranking undeveloped state
lands by "ripeness" — ready for sale once the anticipated growth rate of the DCF of
the State's retained interest slows below the State's own borrowing rate — arguing
this is not, as critics might assume, simply a call to sell faster: "[i]t is often suspected that discounting the future leads us... in the direction of faster resource use. Not so. We will see that this criterion may well lead the State to retard lease sales and production, so long as the anticipated proceeds to the State are growing faster than money in the bank."[1] This is a straightforward application
of the theory of optimal-extraction timing Gaffney had developed in an earlier work,
"Extractive Resources and Taxation," attached to the report as an appendix — the
principle that a resource owner should withhold, not rush, an appreciating asset
whose value is compounding faster than the owner's cost of capital.[1]

## Standing and Limits

This is a **commissioned government consulting report**, not a peer-reviewed paper —
Gaffney states its expository intent explicitly, though it closes with his own
tentatively-worded policy recommendations. It sits at the honest boundary between
formal state economic advice and the kind of unpublished working-paper material the
wiki carries elsewhere in Gaffney's corpus: unlike a working paper, it was
commissioned and delivered to name officials for a live legislative process, but
like a working paper, it was never submitted to peer review or, so far as this
session established, formally published. `[VERIFY: publication/circulation history
of the report beyond delivery to the Alaska Legislature and DNR — not established
this session]` Its empirical claims (the 1965 Prudhoe Bay sale price, the leaseholder
concentration data, the cost-inflation figures) are Gaffney's own citations of
contemporary Alaska sources (DNR staff, industry survey data) rather than
independently verified by this page. The AVC proposal itself is a **design argument**
— Gaffney argues from administrative and incentive principles, not from an estimated
model of Alaska field values, for why an ex post reserves-in-place charge dominates
ex ante bonus bidding; the report offers no econometric test of the claim.

The report predates, and is not itself about, the **Permanent Fund Dividend**: it is
addressed to how the State should structure *leases* to capture oil rent as
landlord, not to how a fund should later distribute that rent to citizens — the
distribution question the wiki covers on
[resource-rent dividends are workable and durable](/wiki/resource-rent-dividends-work/)
via the PFD's actual operating record from 1982 onward (Guettabi, Jones &
Marinescu, Goldsmith).[5] This page is wired to the **capture** side of that split,
not the **dividend** side, and no change is made to the dividend page this session:
its supporting literature (four decades of PFD operating data) already covers the
distribution question directly, and Gaffney's 1977 report — written before any
dividend existed — has nothing to add there beyond the historical-context sentence
carried on [places/Alaska](/wiki/alaska/). A related, later Gaffney paper,
"Alaska's Proposed Tax on Oil Reserves in Situ" (1981, WP053), appears to extend the
AVC proposal specifically for Alaska's actual post-Prudhoe-Bay tax debate; it is not
yet mined by the wiki and is noted here as a discovery candidate for a future wave.

## Bears On

- **Benefit:** [Capturing resource rent works — where institutions are strong](/wiki/resource-rent-capture-works/)
  — adds a design-stage case distinct from Norway (flagship capture), Botswana
  (non-Nordic capture), and California (capture built, then lost): Alaska
  commissioned a systematic rent-capture design study at the very moment its
  constitutional Permanent Fund was created, showing capture-oriented economic
  advice shaping a resource-rich jurisdiction's leasing law before, not after, the
  windfall was fully realized.
- **Concept:** [Resource Rents](/wiki/resource-rents/) — supplies a leasing-side
  design instrument (the ad valorem charge on reserves in place) distinct from and
  complementary to the severance-tax design already covered there from Gaffney's
  2006 California essay.
