## Summary

This 2024 research note by **Liam Wilkinson** for [Common Wealth Canada](/wiki/common-wealth-canada/) models how a Canada-wide [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) would affect different households once paired with income-tax reform. It is a direct empirical test of whether the [LVT-can-be-progressive](/wiki/land-value-tax-can-be-progressive/) claim holds in Canadian data, and how the answer depends on what is done with the revenue.

## Methodology

The study models a **17% land-asset-value tax** (capturing ~75% of annual land rent) using Statistics Canada's 2019 Survey of Financial Security, which links household income to property ownership. Land value is estimated by applying a national average land-to-property ratio of ~58%, and tax burdens are computed across income deciles, owner/renter status, and retirement stage. Net new LVT revenue is estimated at ~**$194 billion**.

## Key Findings

- **LVT alone is regressive.** Lower-income households often hold substantial real estate (chiefly their home), so they pay LVT at a much higher share of income than the wealthy.
- **LVT + a higher tax-free bracket (to $88,100) stays regressive** through the eighth decile: low earners can't earn enough to use the bigger exemption. **Retired homeowners** were hit hardest — income drops of up to **43%** — the classic [asset-rich, cash-poor](/wiki/lvt-hurts-asset-rich-cash-poor/) problem. Renters gained at every income level.
- **LVT + a flat $12,700 refundable credit is broadly progressive:** about **80% of households end up better off**, with the largest proportional gains at the bottom. The fixed credit acts as a *de facto* basic income / [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/).

## Conclusion

A land tax's distributional sign is set by the **revenue recycling**, not the tax alone: a uniform per-person refund (negative income tax / basic income) makes the reform progressive, whereas a larger zero-rate bracket does not. The authors flag major data limits — land-to-property ratios vary widely and reliable land assessment exists only in [British Columbia](/wiki/british-columbia/) — and call for provincial-level study where better land data is available.
