## Summary

*Natural Common Wealth and Economic Rent in Canada* (July 2023) is the flagship report of [Common Wealth Canada](/wiki/common-wealth-canada/), authored by Ben Earle, Liam Wilkinson, Floyd Marinescu, and Ken Yang. It is a national-scale estimate of Canada's [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) — *"income from owning or controlling a limited asset or resource, without any effort or contribution of productivity"* — and a demonstration of what publicly capturing that rent could finance.

## Key Findings

- Canada generates roughly **$421 billion per year** in economic rent from its natural and publicly-created commons.
- Of this, about **$241 billion** is realistically **collectable** through policy without distorting production.
- The single largest component is **land**: a [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) capturing 75% of annual land rental value would raise around **$194 billion** per year.
- The remaining ~**$47 billion** comes from minerals, energy, forestry, fisheries, and carbon/atmospheric [resource rents](/wiki/resource-rents/).

## Policy Scenarios

The report shows that ~$241 billion is enough to fund any of several large reforms, for example:

- Raising the basic personal exemption to about **$88,100**, eliminating federal income tax for roughly **91% of Canadians**; or
- Paying every Canadian adult an annual [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) of roughly **$6,100–$7,600**; or
- Eliminating federal personal income tax entirely.

## Methodology

The authors estimate the extractable return on each asset class — the surplus above reasonable production and opportunity costs — across land, minerals, energy, forestry, fisheries, and atmospheric resources, then net out costs to arrive at the collectable figure. The work provides the empirical foundation for the organisation's [Common Wealth Fund](/wiki/common-wealth-fund/) and [distributional-impact](/wiki/cwc-distributional-impacts-lvt/) analyses.

## Significance

It is one of the few rigorous, country-wide attempts to quantify the full Georgist tax base for a modern developed economy — extending the tradition of [Gaffney's](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) "hidden taxable capacity" work to Canada and grounding the [claim that land rent could fund government](/wiki/land-rent-could-fund-government/) in national data.
