## Overview

Four short Mason Gaffney pieces, read in full for this page, form a single genre: historical
case narratives arguing that Georgist-era tax choices — untaxing buildings, taxing land, and
using the resulting revenue to fund cheap public services — shaped which cities grew and which
stagnated.

- **"Cleveland's Explosive Growth Under Mayors Tom Johnson and Newton Baker"** (workpaper
  WP089, undated, 1 page) — Cleveland's population growth, 1900–20, under single-tax Mayors
  Tom L. Johnson and Newton D. Baker.[1]
- **"Chicago's Growth Spurt, 1890-1900"** (workpaper WP091, undated, 2 pages) — Chicago's
  growth linked to Governor John Peter Altgeld's administration and a dense network of
  single-tax-adjacent reformers, with an explicit hedge about how far the case can be pushed.[2]
- **"What's the Matter with Michigan? The Rise and Collapse of an Economic Wonder"** (November
  2008, written for the "Insights" column of *Groundswell*, 12 pages) — by far the longest and
  richest of the four, tracing Detroit and Michigan from Mayor/Governor Hazen Pingree's 1890s
  reforms through the 2008 "Big Three" bankruptcy crisis.[3]
- **"Enterprising Johannesburg and Sleepy Cape Town: A Contrast"** (workpaper WP077, undated
  but internally datable to c. 1991–92, 2 pages) — a comparative case crediting Johannesburg's
  4% site-value-only property tax for its rise despite no natural site advantages, against Cape
  Town's superior natural site and lack of the same tax.[4]

All four are self-published on masongaffney.org (the Michigan essay's only other outlet was a
Georgist newsletter column), argued by a committed Georgist advocate, and none is peer-reviewed.
Two of the four (Cleveland, Chicago) are the unabridged source notes behind capsule summaries
Gaffney later condensed into his 2006 nine-city survey **[New Life in Old
Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/)**; this page draws out the fuller narrative detail,
including Gaffney's own hedges, that survey compresses away. The Michigan and Johannesburg
pieces are not summarized anywhere else on this wiki. **Per the wiki's standing historiographic
caution on Gaffney's historical-narrative papers, none of these four works is added to any
outcome page's `supported_by` list**: they are hand-picked comparative case narratives from an
advocate, not a matched-control study, and are carried here as attributed historical argument
with their confounders flagged, not as load-bearing evidence for any benefit or problem claim.

## Cleveland, 1900–20 (WP089)

Cleveland grew **109%** from 1900 to 1920, mostly under single-taxers **Tom L. Johnson**
(mayor 1901–09) and his city solicitor and successor **Newton D. Baker** (mayor 1911–16).[1]
Gaffney's workpaper gives more procedural detail than the capsule on
[New Life in Old Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/) (which already carries the
headline: $180m→$500m assessment shift, the Somers system, and the 3-cent trolley fare):

- **W.A. Somers** supplied his "standard unit" land-value mapping system to Johnson in 1901 and
  was made chief clerk of assessments in 1909, when Johnson formally reformed the city's
  assessment machinery; assessments rose from **$180 million to $500 million** with new emphasis
  on land values.[1] (The Somers system was later carried to New York City by assessor Lawson
  Purdy — already documented on this wiki's [Lawson Purdy](/wiki/lawson-purdy/) coverage via
  [New Life in Old Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/).)
- Johnson and Somers found assessors had systematically **undervalued rich neighborhoods and
  overvalued poor ones**; Johnson publicized this with large public maps and ran a city-sponsored
  "tax school" from 1901 that operated for twenty months (surviving a lawsuit from the city's
  biggest landowner) before closing, aiming to build public support for up-valuing land and
  down-valuing buildings.[1]
- Baker continued Johnsonian policy until President Wilson appointed him **Secretary of War in
  1916** — Gaffney reads the appointment as evidence of the single-tax movement's real political
  weight in that era, "a power that later historians and economists have wrongly trivialized or
  ignored or dismissed."[1] Fiery single-taxer **Peter Witt** then ran to succeed Baker and lost
  only narrowly, which Gaffney takes as evidence Johnsonian policy retained a broad
  constituency.[1]
- After 1916, per Gaffney, Cleveland "slowly fell into old-line Tory hands... and began its long
  slide into its present torpor and mediocrity."[1] He extrapolates: had Cleveland kept growing
  at the Johnson-Baker rate, its population today "would be 15 million or so, double that of NYC"
  — an explicitly counterfactual, unweighted projection, not a claim about what actually
  happened.[1]
- A small but durable biographical detail not on [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/)'s
  existing wiki page: a bronze statue of Johnson still stands in downtown Cleveland, holding a
  book with the visible title *Progress and Poverty*.[1]

**Delta assessment.** The population figure, the assessment shift, the Somers system, and the
trolley-fare financing are already on this wiki via [New Life in Old
Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/); this workpaper's genuine additions are the tax
school episode, Baker's wartime appointment as evidence of the movement's contemporary standing,
the Peter Witt near-miss, the explicit post-1916 decline narrative, and the statue detail. None
of it changes the underlying claim; it thickens the same narrative Gaffney already told more
compactly elsewhere.

## Chicago, 1890–1900 (WP091)

Chicago grew **54%** from 1890 to 1900 — a figure Gaffney immediately qualifies: "This is
complicated by annexation (Hoyt, p.153)."[2] That caveat, absent from the [New Life in Old
Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/) capsule, is Gaffney's own; he does not attempt to
strip out the annexation component before citing the growth rate.

The case is a network argument, not a single-mayor argument (Chicago had no equivalent of
Johnson or Pingree):[2]

- **Governor John Peter Altgeld** (1892–96), a Chicago lawyer who "understood" George's ideas,
  per protégé Brand Whitlock (future single-tax mayor of Toledo, already covered on [New Life in
  Old Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/)), corresponded directly with Henry George.[2]
- Under Altgeld, the **Illinois Bureau of Labor Statistics's 1894 8th Annual Report** (compiled
  by labor leader George Schilling) published Lorenz-curve data on landownership concentration
  in the Loop — which Gaffney calls unique: "There is no comparable study, to my knowledge, of
  any other American city."[2] This wiki's [Chicago](/wiki/chicago/) and [One Hundred Years of
  Land Values in Chicago](/wiki/hoyt-chicago-land-values/) pages do not currently mention this
  1894 Bureau study; it is a distinct, earlier data point than Homer Hoyt's 1933 dissertation.
- Chicago's 1890s skyscraper boom is read as capital-for-land substitution consistent with (not
  proof of) a de facto land-favoring assessment policy; the 1892 *Illinois Central Railroad v.
  Illinois* decision (146 U.S. 387), establishing the public trust doctrine over the railroad's
  claimed lakefront land, is cited as a parallel land-policy episode timed to the Columbian
  Exposition.[2]
- A long roster of Chicago-based reformers and radicals is invoked as evidence of a general
  climate — Clarence Darrow, Jane Addams, Mayor Edward F. Dunne (who later brought Tom Johnson
  in as an adviser), Louis F. Post (editor of the Georgist journal *The Public*), Thorstein
  Veblen, John Dewey, and others — plus George C. Olcott's *Blue Book of Land Values* and the
  claim that Chicago's land-value consciousness "inspired" [Homer Hoyt](/wiki/homer-hoyt/)'s 1933
  dissertation, already the subject of this wiki's dedicated [Chicago](/wiki/chicago/) and
  [Hoyt](/wiki/homer-hoyt/) pages.[2]
- **Gaffney's own hedge, quoted in full because it is the single most important sentence in the
  piece:** "That does not fully add up... to a definitive showing that a city administration
  consciously shifted taxes to land values, as in Toledo, Cleveland, New York, Detroit, and
  Milwaukee. More research into Chicago's political history is needed."[2] Gaffney himself ranks
  Chicago's evidentiary status **below** the other cities in his own survey — a caveat this wiki
  should carry forward rather than smooth over.

**Delta assessment.** The Altgeld/Dunne/Darrow/Post/1894-Bureau-report elements are already
compressed into one sentence on [New Life in Old Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/);
the workpaper's real additions are the specific 54% figure with its annexation caveat, the IC
Railroad lakefront case, the Olcott/Hoyt/Babcock lineage, and — most importantly — Gaffney's own
explicit downgrade of Chicago's case relative to his other cities.

## Detroit and Michigan, 1890–2008 ("What's the Matter with Michigan?")

This is the richest of the four pieces and the one with no prior wiki coverage — there is no
existing places/detroit.md or places/michigan.md, and the wiki's only other Detroit-adjacent
page, [events/detroit-lvt-proposal](/wiki/detroit-lvt-proposal/), covers a 21st-century LVT
policy proposal, not this history. Gaffney wrote it after a 1995 meeting at the Levy Institute
where Georgists (including Gaffney) unsuccessfully warned Governor John Engler's staff against
shifting Michigan's school funding off the property tax and onto a sales tax, by analogy with
California's post-Proposition 13 experience.[3] The essay narrates roughly a century of
Michigan's economic history as a single arc from that land-tax-funded rise to a tax-shift-driven
"senility."

**I. Pingree's era (1890s–1901).** Detroit's population grew 40% in the depression decade
1890–1900 (Gaffney says "faster than almost all other cities except Cleveland"), then another
60% by 1910 and 113% more by 1920.[3] Mayor and later Governor **Hazen Pingree** (1840–1901)
raised land assessments (1891 campaign for "higher taxes on the vast landed estates of the
city"), subsidized a competing traction firm to force fare cuts when he could not win outright
municipalization, used the crash of 1893 to inaugurate "Pingree's Potato Patches" — vacant
speculator-held lots turned over to unemployed workers to farm — and as Governor from 1897
centralized statewide property assessment, finding so much untaxed (chiefly railroad) land that
tax rates could fall even as revenue rose.[3] Gaffney frames Pingree as the direct mentor of
Cleveland's Tom Johnson and Newton Baker and of Toledo's Samuel Jones and Brand Whitlock — a
mentorship lineage this wiki's [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/) page does not currently
carry — with the further irony that the traction monopoly Pingree fought in Detroit was owned by
Johnson himself, part of what "converted" Johnson to Georgism.[3] Detroit grew from 205,000
(1890) to 1,850,000 (1950), briefly the USA's 4th-largest city; Gaffney credits Pingree's
"growth without annexation" approach as distinct from what came next.[3]

**II. The stagnant Upper Peninsula.** A contrasting internal case: forest and mineral tax
exemptions (a 15¢/acre "certified commercial forest" levy, with the state paying counties 25¢/acre
drawn from Lower Peninsula taxpayers; near-total exemption of newly discovered iron ore) left the
U.P., per Gaffney citing engineer Ben Smith's *Latifundia in Gitche Gumi* (1975), "a land of
barons and peasants" that escaped Pingree's reforms and stayed an economic desert while the L.P.
boomed — a "Dutch disease"-style internal counter-case Gaffney does not fully integrate into his
main Detroit narrative.[3]

**III. 1920s sprawl.** Detroit added 68% more people 1920–30 but under "New Era" Republican
leadership that, per Gaffney, "twisted Pingree's ideas by growing in area even more than in
people" — annexation-driven sprawl documented at the time by Business Professor Ernest M. Fisher,
with Flint singled out as a poster child.[3]

**IV.–V. Depression and war (1930s–40s).** Detroit shrank less than most cities in the "Dirty
Thirties" (+3.5%) and produced Fr. Charles Coughlin, Mayor/Governor Frank Murphy (who mediated
the 1936–37 UAW sit-down strike at Flint rather than authorizing force against it), and Walter
Reuther, who built the UAW into a major political force; Detroit grew 14% during WWII war
production while many cities shrank.[3]

**VI. 1950–70: shrinkage despite boom conditions.** Detroit's population fell 10% in the 1950s
even as national auto demand, the Interstate Highway System, and the St. Lawrence Seaway were all
expanding — which Gaffney reads as evidence the cause was endogenous (tax policy), not
exogenous. Governor "Soapy" Williams's 1953 Business Activities Tax and Governor George Romney's
income tax (sold as "property tax relief") coincided with land being assessed, per Gaffney citing
Robert Andelson's 2000 volume (quoting Southfield Mayor James Clarkson), "at next to nil," on
Depression-era valuations that no one — not even economists — was contesting.[3] The 1967 12th
Street riot (2,000+ buildings destroyed) is narrated as politically damaging to both Romney and
LBJ; Gaffney does not draw an explicit tax-policy causal line to the riot itself, and this page
does not impute one on his behalf.

**VII. Southfield, 1960–70 — "the success story ignored."** Gaffney's flagship pro-Georgist case
within the essay: Southfield Mayor **James Clarkson** hired Georgist assessor **Ted Gwartney**
(previously trained under Georgist Sacramento County Assessor Irene Hickman) to up-value land and
down-value buildings after finding land assessed at 10% or less of market value; Southfield's
population rose from 19,000 (1950) to 69,000 (1970) and its tax base allegedly rose 20%/year,
before the city "leveled off at 76,000... and has not grown since."[3] Gaffney explicitly records
and rebuts a contemporary counter-explanation: Harvard tax scholar **Oliver Oldman**, at a
meeting Gaffney attended, dismissed Southfield's growth as competitive undertaxation exploiting
Detroit's decline and "white flight" — a "race to the bottom," not a Georgist success — which
Gaffney disputes but does not independently adjudicate with data beyond the tax-base growth
figure.[3] **This is the essay's single most useful case for the wiki's Georgist-policy record,
but it is also its weakest-controlled comparison**: Southfield is a Detroit suburb in the exact
window of accelerating Detroit-area white flight and suburbanization, a confounder Gaffney names
via Oldman's objection but does not resolve.

**VIII.–IX. SBT (1975) and the Engler sales-tax shift (1995).** Michigan's 1975 Single Business
Tax let businesses expense real-estate purchases (including land) as a current cost — "a great
subsidy for holding land," in Gaffney's words — while Detroit lost another 20% of its population
by 1980.[3] The essay's longest set-piece is a **verbatim reproduction of Gaffney's own 1995
testimony** to Engler's staff, comparing Michigan's proposed property-tax cut to California's
post-Prop.-13 record: falling school-funding rank (#5 to #40 nationally by 1985), county road
maintenance stretched to once every 130 years, credit-rating decline, rising unemployment and
poverty rates, 426,000 net outmigrants in 1993 alone, blighted post-Northridge-earthquake
neighborhoods, and economist David Shulman's line that Prop. 13 "breached the social compact."[3]
**This is advocacy testimony, reproduced by its own author, from the losing side of a real policy
fight** — it should be read as a primary record of what Gaffney argued in 1995, not as neutral
retrospective analysis.

**X. Aftermath (2000s).** Post-1995, Gaffney reports Michigan losing 300,000 jobs since 2000,
per-capita income falling below the national average, elevated youth outmigration, and — writing
in November 2008 — the "Big Three" auto bankruptcy crisis then unfolding in Washington.[3] He
closes by noting Detroit's population had by then fallen 50% from its 1950 peak, Flint 40%, and
that vacant urban land was again being farmed by the unemployed — "Pingree's Potato Patches
again, 105 years later."[3]

**Confounders Gaffney does not quantify.** The essay's central weakness, carried forward here
rather than smoothed over: it treats a century of Michigan and Detroit history — Pingree's land
tax, 1920s annexation sprawl, the Depression, WWII mobilization, postwar suburbanization, the
SBT's expensing quirk, and the 1995 sales-tax swap — as segments of one continuous causal story
about land-tax policy, without separating out or quantifying the independent contribution of the
**national auto industry's own trajectory** (post-1970s oil shocks, foreign competition, the
industry's eventual 2008–09 crisis) to Michigan's decline. Gaffney's own account of Southfield
names but does not resolve the suburbanization/white-flight confound; his account of the 1967
riot and the SBT-to-Engler sequence similarly compresses distinct eras and mechanisms into a
single "senility" narrative. None of this makes the individual facts (population figures,
assessment practices, dates, tax-law mechanics) unreliable — they are Gaffney's own citations to
named secondary sources (Holli, Catlin, Lovett, Smith, Andelson, Fine, Hines) and are treated here
as checkable A/B-claims — but the causal thread connecting them across a century is Gaffney's own
argumentative synthesis, not something independently identified.

## Johannesburg vs. Cape Town (WP077)

A short comparative essay, internally datable to roughly 1991–92: Gaffney writes that in
Johannesburg "blacks could not buy land there, or in any 'white' area, until a year or so ago,"
placing composition shortly after South Africa's 1991 land-measure repeals, and it is thematically
paired in the corpus with WP076 ("South African Diary, March 17–30, 1992").[4]

Gaffney frames Johannesburg as an urban-economics puzzle: it has no harbor, no water
transportation, no natural rail hub, poor surrounding farmland, and its founding mines have
played out — "it should now be a ghost town" — yet it remains South Africa's largest city and
economic capital.[4] His explanation is the city's **site-value-only property tax at what he
reports as a 4% rate**: "Give me a better explanation and I'll back off. I haven't heard one
yet."[4] By contrast, Cape Town — endowed with a far superior natural site, climate, and the
national legislative capital — "subsists without that benefit" of a site-value tax and, per
Gaffney, shows urban sprawl, a fractured central business district, blighted downtown land, and
underused rail yards; he reports the city's mayor, Frank van der Velde, as favoring a switch to
site-value taxation on the merits.[4] Gaffney is explicit that Johannesburg's success has not
solved South Africa's "great national problems of race discrimination and black homelessness,"
and frames the city as a refuge rather than a cause of black in-migration and homelessness under
apartheid-era restrictions.[4]

**Relation to existing wiki coverage.** This wiki already has dedicated pages on
[Johannesburg site-value rating](/wiki/johannesburg-site-value-rating/) and
[South Africa](/wiki/south-africa/), both built on different sources (chiefly Fred Harrison's
*The Power in the Land* and the 2009 PDG/Isandla municipal-rates report), which document
Johannesburg's site-value rating under the Transvaal Local Authority Rates Ordinance without
independently confirming a 4% rate or crediting the tax with the city's overall growth
trajectory relative to other South African cities — that specific comparative-growth argument
is new to this workpaper and not corroborated elsewhere in the wiki's sources. There is no
existing Cape Town page; this is the wiki's first documented mention of Cape Town's tax history.
The Transvaal-specific origin of site-value rating that [South
Africa](/wiki/south-africa/) already documents (a *provincial* ordinance, not a national one)
is independent corroboration of why Cape Town — in the separate Cape Province — would not have
shared the same system.

**Confounders Gaffney does not quantify.** A two-city comparison here conflates several
non-tax factors: Johannesburg's historic role as the country's mining-finance capital versus
Cape Town's role as legislative capital and tourist destination; apartheid-era Group Areas Act
spatial planning, which shaped both cities' land use independent of rating policy; and simple
distance from the industrial Witwatersrand economic core. Gaffney names none of these as
alternative explanations for the growth differential; the essay's own rhetorical structure
("give me a better explanation") invites but does not itself supply a test against them. The 4%
rate figure is also unsourced beyond "they tell me" — a first-hand but unverified report, not a
cited statute or valuation roll.

## Standing and Limits

**Claim class.** All four pieces are historical-narrative advocacy by a committed Georgist
economist, self-published (three as undated internal workpapers, one in a Georgist newsletter),
not peer-reviewed, and not independently replicated. Population and assessment figures are
generally **A/B-claims** — traceable to named secondary sources (Bremner, Russell, Cramer, Hoyt,
Holli, Catlin, Lovett, Smith, Andelson, Fine, Hines) or to US Census data, and checkable in
principle — but the **causal attribution of city growth or decline to Georgist tax policy is a
D-claim** (interpretive/argumentative) in every case. None of the four is a matched-control or
panel study; all rely on hand-picked comparison cases (a growing city vs. an implied
counterfactual, or one city vs. one neighbor) rather than a comparison group designed to isolate
the tax variable from confounders.

**What Gaffney himself concedes.** Notably, Gaffney's own text supplies two of this page's
strongest caveats without prompting: his explicit statement that Chicago's case does not "fully
add up... to a definitive showing" and needs "more research," and his verbatim reproduction of
Oliver Oldman's white-flight counter-explanation for Southfield, which he disputes but does not
independently refute with data. Both are preserved above rather than dropped, following the same
"a sequence is not always a consequence" standard [New Life in Old
Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/) already applies to Gaffney's city-growth work.

**What Gaffney does not fully address.** Annexation's contribution to Chicago's 1890s growth
rate (he flags it but does not net it out); the national auto industry's independent trajectory
as a driver of 20th-century Michigan's rise and fall, alongside — not instead of — tax policy;
suburbanization and white flight as an alternative explanation for Southfield's growth relative
to Detroit's decline; and, in the Johannesburg/Cape Town case, apartheid-era spatial planning and
each city's distinct economic role (mining-and-finance capital vs. legislative-and-tourism
capital) as confounds independent of tax policy.

**Why this page exists as a cluster rather than four page-level mentions.** The four pieces
share one thesis (Georgist-era land-tax choices shaped city-growth trajectories), one author,
overlapping subject matter with [New Life in Old Cities](/wiki/gaffney-new-life-in-old-cities/),
and — for Cleveland, Chicago, and Detroit — a Pingree-to-Johnson-to-Baker mentorship lineage that
only becomes visible when the pieces are read together. None of the four alone would clear the
bar for a dedicated page under the wiki's thin-material fold-in convention; together they do.
