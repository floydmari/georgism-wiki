## Overview

Richard Almy's "Valuation and Assessment of Immovable Property" (OECD Working Papers on Fiscal Federalism, No. 19, OECD Publishing, Paris, 2014) surveys property tax valuation policy and administration across 172 countries, addressing what is valued, how it is valued, and who values it, with particular attention to mass-valuation systems used for recurrent property taxes.[1] [Lars Doucet's *Land Is a Big Deal*](/wiki/land-is-a-big-deal/) draws on Almy's paper for a cost figure — roughly €20 per property to assess, or about 1% of typical tax revenue collected, compared with 6–12% total collection cost for federal income tax and 3–13% for sales tax — as part of its Part IV case that unimproved land value can be, and routinely is, accurately and cheaply assessed by government valuation authorities.[2]

As an OECD survey of existing government practice across many countries rather than a study focused on land value taxation specifically, Almy's paper functions as institutional, cross-national evidence that routine land/property valuation is standard administrative practice — bearing on the [land cannot be assessed](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/) objection — rather than as an argument for or against LVT itself.
