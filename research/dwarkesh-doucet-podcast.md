## Summary

Released **9 January 2023** — "**Lars Doucet — Progress, poverty, Georgism, & why rent is too damn high**" (the show was then still called *The Lunar Society*) — this roughly **1h40m** episode has host Dwarkesh Patel interview [Lars Doucet](/wiki/lars-doucet/), author of *[Land Is a Big Deal](/wiki/land-is-a-big-deal/)*, about Georgism, land value taxation, and assessment. Patel calls it "one of my best episodes ever," and it is frequently recommended as the best **single-sitting introduction** to the modern Georgist case.

## Why It Matters

The conversation covers the whole arc — why land is economically distinct, whether values are large enough to matter, the [incidence](/wiki/landlords-cannot-pass-lvt-to-tenants/) and [assessment](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/) objections, and practical implementation. Patel said it "completely changed how I think about who creates value in the world and who leeches off it." As accessible audio, it has been a major on-ramp for newcomers. The episode draws directly on the research Doucet began after his prize-winning [ACX book review of *Progress and Poverty*](/wiki/doucet-acx-book-review/) and his [*Does Georgism Work?*](/wiki/doucet-does-georgism-work/) series.

## What the Episode Covers

The published timestamps map the conversation's full arc: Georgism defined; "Metaverse Housing Crises" (Doucet's virtual-land case that scarcity of *location* reappears even in digital worlds — see [virtual land](/wiki/virtual-land/)); speculation and frontiers; the social value of search; "Will Georgism Destroy The Economy?"; San Francisco's economics; land reform in the Asian Tigers; libertarian Georgism; the transition question; Doucet's assessment startup; and a closing section titled "Someone Is Always Collecting Rents."[1]

Substantive positions Doucet takes on-air (all verifiable in the published transcript):[1]

- **Magnitude.** "There's $44 trillion of land value in America and I believe we can raise about $4 trillion in land rents annually with 100% land value tax. We would probably do less than that in practice" — but even the low end could fully pay for Social Security or defense.
- **The search-theoretic critique.** Doucet answers Bryan Caplan's discovery objection directly: the "critique only applies to a 100% LVT... the most extreme Georgists I know are only advocating for an 85% land value tax," which leaves a return to discovery — and Caplan "doesn't account at all for the negative effects of speculation." (The academic version of this exchange is at [Gochenour & Caplan](/wiki/gochenour-caplan-search-theoretic/) and [Foldvary's reply](/wiki/foldvary-reply-gochenour-caplan/).)
- **Severance taxes as geoism.** He presents Norway's oil severance tax as "essentially a land value tax but of a different kind" and "a key reason that Norway has avoided the resource curse" — the rent-gradient extension covered at [resource rents](/wiki/resource-rents/).
- **Assessment and regressivity.** He argues most analyses finding land taxes regressive are "mostly done on the basis of our current assessments," which "could be massively improved" — the improvement agenda behind his assessment startup ValueBase (whose lead investor, Patel notes, is Sam Altman).

## Context and Reception

Patel's episode page opens: "One of my best episodes ever," and notes Doucet's book had been "praised by Vitalik Buterin, Scott Alexander, and Noah Smith."[1] The episode (1h40m, released while the show was still called *The Lunar Society*) is the audio companion to the Doucet corpus this wiki carries — the ACX review, the *Does Georgism Work?* series, and *Land is a Big Deal* — and is the piece most often recommended to newcomers who prefer listening to reading.
