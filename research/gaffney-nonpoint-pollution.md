## Overview

"Nonpoint Pollution: Tractable Solutions to Intractable Problems" is a Mason
Gaffney essay published in *JBA* (the printed running header gives only this
abbreviation; the full journal title is not stated in the document itself —
`[VERIFY]`), Vol. 18, Nos. 1 and 2 (1988/89), pp. 133–154, while Gaffney was
at the University of California, Riverside.[1] An acknowledgement note dates
an earlier version to a conference on "Political, Institutional and Fiscal
Alternatives to Accelerate Nonpoint Pollution Programs," Milwaukee, December
9, 1987.[1] The masongaffney.org mirror carries two scanned copies of the
identical article — `F7Nonpoint_Pollution.CV.pdf` and
`F8-NonpointPollution.CV.CV.pdf` — from different source scans; F7's OCR
text layer is markedly cleaner and is the version read and re-OCR'd for this
page (see Provenance below). F8 is not separately mined.

The essay fills a genuine, previously undeveloped gap in the wiki's coverage
of environmental economics: [concepts/pigouvian-taxation](/wiki/pigouvian-taxation/)
and [concepts/ecological-georgism](/wiki/ecological-georgism/) both make the
standard case for charging polluters (Boyce, Daly, the IMF), but neither had
carried a Gaffney-specific treatment, still less one that identifies where
the standard Pigouvian toolkit *breaks down*. This essay is that argument,
and it resolves the breakdown by routing back to Gaffney's core subject —
land — rather than to a better-designed emissions tax.

## The Core Argument: Nonpoint Pollution Defeats the Standard Toolkit

Gaffney opens by naming the problem precisely: "nonpoint" pollution — runoff
from agriculture, forestry, mining, construction grading, paved surfaces,
lawns, septic systems, and urban storm drains — cannot be metered at a
single outlet the way a smokestack or an effluent pipe can. Conventional
economists, he argues, are "overtrained towards becoming protagonists of the
price system" and respond to unmeterable pollution by taxing or regulating a
*surrogate* instead — pesticides, fertilizers, salt — on the theory that
this "internalizes the externality" once removed.[1]

He then lists seven specific failure modes of the surrogate-tax approach,
worked out from concrete cases (mostly agricultural pesticides and
fertilizer):

1. **No locational sensitivity.** A tax on a surrogate applies uniformly,
   but damage from runoff varies enormously by site; a tax high enough to
   protect a sensitive watershed reduces yields everywhere, which can
   perversely pull more marginal acreage into production.
2. **Revenue capture.** Once a surrogate tax raises money, the recipients
   develop an interest in the revenue that can override the regulatory
   purpose — Gaffney draws the parallel to 19th-century tariff debates,
   where revenue interests routinely swamped the stated regulatory aim.
3. **Leakage.** Excise taxes are not leakproof; Gaffney cites his own
   experience as "a small fruit grower" tempted by illicit herbicide
   supplies, and a documented pattern of windfall compensation to sellers
   holding stock of a chemical about to be banned or taxed — which he calls
   a second "pesticide treadmill," since it rewards developing new harmful
   products for the payout when they are eventually banned.
4. **Substitution the tax cannot anticipate**, e.g., a nitrogen tax simply
   pushes farmers toward legumes, a benign switch, but the broader point is
   that surrogate taxation invites unpredictable substitution effects that a
   regulator cannot fully model in advance.
5. **No discrimination among applicators.** A uniform surrogate tax "taxes
   the best for the sins of the worst," over-penalizing careful users and
   under-penalizing careless ones regardless of the rate chosen.
6. **Contested regulatory objectivity.** Gaffney cites a 1987 Alameda
   County Superior Court finding that the University of California violated
   the Hatch Act by favoring agribusiness over family farmers, and quotes
   entomologist Robert van den Bosch's "pesticide mafia" charge — used to
   argue that the institutions that would set surrogate tax rates are not
   neutral referees.
7. **Second-best.** Because the land market is already badly distorted
   (see next section), the textbook case that "getting one price right"
   improves welfare does not straightforwardly apply — "when the system is
   balanced wrong anyway, what is one more distortion?"

This is, in substance, an internal critique of Pigouvian taxation from a
sympathetic critic — Gaffney does not reject charging for externalities in
principle (he explicitly separates himself from the Coase/Dales
tradeable-permit school, declining even to engage with how "offset rights"
might apply to nonpoint sources, since he judges permit markets for point
sources already captured by "a few corporations" that "grown rich by
establishing their respective histories of pollution"). His target is
narrower and sharper: nonpoint pollution specifically defeats price-based
correction because there is no meterable point at which to apply the price.

## The Land-Market Diagnosis

The essay's most distinctive move, and its clearest link to the rest of
Gaffney's corpus, is where it goes next. Rather than searching for a better
surrogate, Gaffney relocates the problem: "land use decisions are
superimposed on a settlement pattern based on massive market failure in
land. The phenomena rather imprecisely called 'land speculation' and
'absentee ownership' betray market failure... The result is that the land
market is not efficient; land is not properly priced and allocated to begin
with. This is the thread I will follow."[1]

He works this thesis through three parallel domains, each showing the same
structural pattern — intensive use of good land is discouraged, so activity
sprawls onto marginal, erosion-prone land, generating the runoff:

### Forestry

Extending the argument already on
[research/gaffney-forest-taxation](/wiki/gaffney-forest-taxation/) (Gaffney's
1980 tax-base survey and 1977 National Forests address), this essay adds a
runoff-specific case for why land value taxation of timberland reduces
nonpoint pollution rather than merely raising revenue. Good, low, flat
growing sites are underharvested and marginal, steep, erosion-prone sites
are overharvested, Gaffney argues, because of four tax-code biases: (1)
replanting costs must be capitalized rather than expensed, deferring the
write-off for decades; (2) most states' yield tax (as opposed to a property
tax) removes any carrying cost during the "sterile downtime" between harvest
and replanting, so idle cut-over land costs nothing to hold; (3) states that
have cut the land-value component of the property tax on timber remove the
one incentive that "pricks holders in the most compelling way to restock,"
while a land-value tax base is automatically zero on genuinely marginal
land, so it cannot cause abandonment there; and (4) standing timber's
value-added is largely unrecognized as taxable income (capital-asset
treatment since 1944) while harvested value-added is taxed at full ordinary
rates, which rewards deferring harvest on the best land and pushes cutting
pressure to the margin. The essay explicitly proposes a synthesis rather
than a single doctrine: "Concept A" (conserve for the future) and "Concept
B" (use land now, intensively) are not opposites but apply to *different
places* — "use the good lands intensively... thus relieve human pressure
and help conserve the vulnerable, erosive lands." This is a distinct,
land-value-tax-specific delta on the forestry page's ground: the earlier A3
piece surveys tax bases in the abstract; this essay adds the runoff/erosion
consequence of getting the choice wrong.

### Urban Sprawl

The essay makes explicit an argument only implicit in
[research/gaffney-containment-policies-urban-sprawl](/wiki/gaffney-containment-policies-urban-sprawl/)
(1964) and
[research/gaffney-synergistic-city](/wiki/gaffney-synergistic-city/) (1978):
that sprawl is not just wasteful of land, capital, and fuel but is itself
the mechanism of nonpoint pollution. Gaffney lists roughly a dozen ways
sprawl "maximizes or worsens" runoff and related harms — more car-miles and
smog per unit of urban linkage; more paved area and roadside salt; grading
and denuding of new (typically upstream, sloping) land; more septic tanks
in place of sewers; larger lots, longer driveways; and the filling of
wetlands that would otherwise filter runoff before it reaches shellfish beds
and estuaries. He cites a concrete figure: developing urban areas cover only
2.6% of the Menominee River watershed in Wisconsin but contribute 37% of
suspended solids and 48% of phosphorous loading (Bauman et al. 1980, cited
via Falk 1985). His illustrative case is Milwaukee, which lost 20% of its
population after 1960 even as its suburban counties grew and its own core
decayed — "a fourth of Milwaukee should be renewed forthwith, obviating much
of the random lateral expansion onto new land upstream of it," a compact-city
argument for pollution control rather than aesthetics or transit efficiency,
citing his own 1970 work.[1] The essay closes on Milwaukee's Depression-era
socialist mayor Daniel Hoan as a model of civic will: "Dan Hoan had fun
making Milwaukee work; he is as good a model as we need."

### Agriculture

The essay's briefest domain (the case is truncated relative to forestry and
urban sprawl in the surviving text) restates the same displacement logic for
farmland: tillage-versus-grazing debates and site-specific erosion controls
matter less than whether cultivation pressure concentrates on good land or
spills onto fragile land — "'where' and 'when' are as [important as
technique]... the proper watchword."

## Standing and Limits

- **A Gaffney-authored policy essay in a lower-profile venue, not a
  peer-reviewed empirical study.** The essay's factual claims (the pesticide
  windfall-compensation pattern, the Milwaukee CBD figures, the Menominee
  River loading data) are drawn from secondary sources Gaffney cites but
  this wiki has not independently verified; treat the argument as Gaffney's
  own synthesis (C/D-claim) rather than as newly established fact.
- **The seven surrogate-tax failure modes are argued, not modeled.** No
  formal welfare-loss calculation is offered for any of the seven; they are
  worked examples and citations to others' findings (Hall & Norgaard 1973;
  Lipsey & Lancaster 1956–57 for the second-best point), not a Gaffney
  original econometric contribution.
- **The venue could not be fully verified.** Only the abbreviation "JBA" and
  "Vol. 18, No. 1 and 2, 1988/89" appear in the scanned document; the full
  journal title is unconfirmed (`[VERIFY]`).
- **The land-value-tax solution is asserted, not tested against nonpoint
  pollution outcomes specifically.** No study cited in the essay (or found
  independently for this page) measures runoff or water-quality outcomes
  under a land-value-tax regime versus a conventional property tax; the
  causal chain — LVT discourages sprawl and idle-land holding, which
  reduces grading/denuding, which reduces runoff — is Gaffney's inference
  from the LVT-anti-sprawl mechanism already documented (with its own
  caveats) on the containment-policies and synergistic-city pages, not an
  independent empirical result of this essay.
- **F2Q, a related but distinct 1966 item, was read and declined a page
  this wave.** "Welfare Economics and Environmental Quality" (Gaffney's
  comments on Allen Kneese at the 1966 Resources for the Future Forum on
  Quality of the Environment) is a witty discussant response, not an
  original research contribution — its one durable point, that "increments
  to land rent represent the benefits of pollution control in an area, net
  of the associated costs" (i.e., land-value capitalization as a measure of
  environmental-quality benefits), restates the same land-rent-as-benefit-
  measure logic already covered on this wiki via
  [tax capitalization](/wiki/tax-capitalization/) and
  [Oates (1969)](/wiki/oates-1969-capitalization/), predating both by three
  years but adding no new mechanism beyond what those pages already
  establish. Zero-addition verdict; no registry row (C2/E15 precedent).

## Bears On

- **Concept:** [Pigouvian Taxation](/wiki/pigouvian-taxation/) — supplies
  the wiki's first Gaffney-specific critique of the standard effluent-charge
  and tradeable-permit toolkit, identified as breaking down specifically for
  diffuse, unmeterable pollution.
- **Concept:** [Ecological Georgism](/wiki/ecological-georgism/) — an early
  (1988/89) primary-source Gaffney argument for the page's general thesis
  that environmental problems are land-commons problems, worked out for a
  pollution category (runoff) the page did not previously cover.
- **Research:** [Gaffney: Alternative Ways of Taxing Forests /
  Greater Social Benefits from our National Forests](/wiki/gaffney-forest-taxation/) —
  extended with a runoff/erosion-specific case for land-value taxation of
  timberland.
- **Research:** [Gaffney (1964): Containment Policies for Urban
  Sprawl](/wiki/gaffney-containment-policies-urban-sprawl/) and
  [Gaffney (1978): The Synergistic City](/wiki/gaffney-synergistic-city/) —
  extended with an explicit pollution/runoff rationale for compact
  settlement, not present on either earlier page.
- **Benefit:** [Land value taxation reduces urban sprawl](/wiki/lvt-reduces-sprawl/) —
  cited as a further, later (1988/89) Gaffney restatement connecting the
  anti-sprawl mechanism specifically to water-quality outcomes; historical
  context, not new empirical evidence for that page's claim.
