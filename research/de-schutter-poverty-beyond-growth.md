## Summary

*Eradicating Poverty Beyond Growth* is the report of **Olivier De Schutter**, UN Special Rapporteur on extreme poverty and human rights, presented to the Human Rights Council's fifty-sixth session (18 June–12 July 2024) under document symbol **A/HRC/56/61**. [VERIFY: the source URL provided in this task, basicincome.org's "De-Schutter.2024.A_79_SR-Poverty" PDF, is in fact a *different*, later De Schutter report — "The Burnout Economy: Poverty and Mental Health" (A/79 session, General Assembly) — not this one; this entry was written from the correct A/HRC/56/61 report, retrieved separately from the UN's official document system (docs.un.org) after confirming the mismatch. See the final note to the editor for detail.] De Schutter, a Belgian legal scholar and former UN Special Rapporteur on the right to food, argues that the dominant anti-poverty strategy — grow aggregate output (GDP) and redistribute some of the proceeds through taxes and transfers — is "misguided," because "an increase in gross domestic product is not a precondition for the realization of human rights or for combating poverty and inequalities." This entry reads the 18-page report in full.

The report is explicitly a first installment: it states the *why* (growth is not necessary for poverty eradication and has become ecologically destructive in rich countries) and the *what* (the broad shape of a "post-growth" anti-poverty strategy), with a follow-up "Roadmap for Eradicating Poverty Beyond Growth" (2025) later supplying implementation detail. As a UN Special Rapporteur mandate holder, De Schutter's reports carry institutional standing but are personal expert analysis, not negotiated UN policy — the Human Rights Council receives but does not endorse them.

## The Core Argument / Findings

De Schutter's central claim is that growth and poverty reduction have decoupled in wealthy economies: "the economies of rich countries have grown far beyond what is necessary to allow people to flourish; they have become obese... growth is failing to reduce poverty and inequalities and to create jobs," while remaining ecologically destructive. He rejects the "ideology of 'growthism'" as a distraction from the two things he says actually matter: reducing extractive, exploitative growth's harms, and building direct redistributive and provisioning mechanisms that do not depend on aggregate output rising.

The report's constructive proposals fall into several buckets. First, **protecting and rebuilding "the commons"** — collectively managed resources such as grazing land, water, and fisheries — as a poverty-relevant institution independent of market income; he cites the FAO's Voluntary Guidelines on tenure governance and argues states have a duty to "recognize and protect... publicly-owned land, fisheries and forests and their related systems of collective use and management." Second, **universal basic services**: publicly provided housing, health care, school meals, water, energy, transport, and digital access, financed through "strongly redistributive taxation," which he argues reduces inequality more reliably and with less administrative leakage than income-tested cash transfers. Third, a **financing menu independent of income and payroll taxes**: taxing inheritances and inter-generational gifts (currently "often not taxed, or taxed only at very low levels"); a carbon tax paired with compensation for low-income households; a wealth tax on the ultrarich (he cites an estimate that a 1.5–3% wealth tax on the world's roughly 65,000 centimillionaires would raise $300 billion/year); and closing corporate tax-avoidance and tax-haven leakage (cited losses of $311 billion and $169 billion respectively). Fourth, **labor-market measures** — job guarantees, shorter working hours, and stronger worker bargaining power — framed as reducing dependence on wage income for basic needs.

Notably, the report does **not** center cash universal basic income; where it discusses cash transfers it favors "universal basic services" and job guarantees as ways to let people "access the necessities of life by means other than work," treating UBI as one option among several rather than a preferred instrument. It also does not use "economic rent," "land value," or "unearned increment" as analytic categories anywhere in the text — the closest conceptual overlap is the commons discussion, which is framed around customary and collective tenure in developing-country contexts (grazing land, fisheries) rather than the urban land-rent-and-housing-cost mechanism central to the Georgist case in rich economies.

## Relation to the Georgist Case

This report's link to Geoism runs through the [citizen's-dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) and rent-capture lane only by inference, not by explicit argument — a materially weaker connection than the World Bank or Namibia entries, and it should be cited accordingly. De Schutter's financing menu (inheritance tax, carbon tax, wealth tax, anti-avoidance measures) overlaps with instruments the wiki treats elsewhere as rent-adjacent (a carbon tax-and-dividend is functionally a rent-capture-and-dividend scheme for atmospheric commons, in the wiki's own scope-expansion framing), but De Schutter does not make that connection himself, and he never proposes a land value tax or discusses land rent as a distinct category from other wealth. His commons discussion is the one passage genuinely adjacent to Georgist land theory — "the commons... has been relied upon for access to water, grazing land or food" and states have a duty to protect such collective tenure from "privatization" — but this is a developing-world customary-tenure argument, not the urban land-value-capture argument the wiki's [rising land costs](/wiki/rising-land-costs-drive-poverty/) claim makes about rich-country housing.

Because the report is UN-advocacy-adjacent (a Special Rapporteur mandate is explicitly a human-rights-advocacy role, not a research post) and makes no land-rent-specific argument, the honest use of this source on the wiki is as **corroborating institutional testimony that a leading UN poverty expert rejects growth-dependent anti-poverty strategy and favors direct redistributive/commons-based instruments over trickle-down growth** — a framing compatible with, but not evidence for, the specific Georgist claim that land rent capture is the superior redistributive instrument.

## Nuances and Limits

- **Attribution matters.** This is the analysis and argument of one UN Special Rapporteur, not a peer-reviewed empirical study or a Human-Rights-Council-endorsed position. De Schutter cites a wide secondary literature (Piketty, Hickel, Jackson, Ostrom, Kallis) rather than presenting new data or original empirical analysis.
- **No land-rent-specific argument.** As above — the report's financing menu is broad-based ("beyond growth" redistribution generally) rather than rent-targeted; readers should not present this report as making a Georgist case it does not make.
- **Degrowth framing is itself contested economics.** The claim that rich-country growth is unnecessary for poverty reduction and is "obese" reflects the degrowth school (Hickel, Kallis, Jackson are cited approvingly), a minority position within mainstream economics; the report does not engage counter-arguments from growth economists in comparable depth.
- **Document provenance flag.** The URL supplied for this task's source material pointed to a different, later De Schutter report (the "burnout economy" mental-health report). This entry corrects that and cites the actual A/HRC/56/61 text, retrieved directly from the UN's official document system; anyone updating this page should re-verify the source_url resolves correctly, as UN document URLs migrated systems (undocs.org → docs.un.org) during 2026.

## Bears On

This report bears most directly on the [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) concept page as **context**: institutional testimony (from a human-rights mandate holder, not a rent-capture advocate) that redistribution independent of growth is both feasible and necessary. It does not meet the bar for `supported_by`/`challenged_by` wiring on [Rising land values and housing costs drive poverty](/wiki/rising-land-costs-drive-poverty/), since the report does not address land rent or housing costs as a distinct mechanism — that page's existing evidence base (Knoll/Schularick/Steger, Rognlie, Albouy) remains the stronger citation set. It is a useful concurrent-sibling citation alongside [Exploring Universal Basic Income (World Bank, 2020)](/wiki/worldbank-exploring-ubi/): both are 2024-adjacent institutional documents surveying redistributive-instrument design, from opposite ends of the growth-orthodoxy spectrum (the World Bank report is growth-agnostic but market-conventional; De Schutter explicitly rejects growth-dependence).
