## Summary

In 2021, [Lars Doucet](/wiki/lars-doucet/) — a Norwegian-Texan game designer and self-described "pretty serious Georgist" — won **first place** in Scott Alexander's *Astral Codex Ten* Book Review Contest with a review of Henry George's *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*. The review was published on ACX (anonymously, as a contest finalist) on **16 April 2021**; Alexander announced the results, with Doucet's entry taking **first place**, on **10 July 2021**. It is widely credited as the spark of the **modern Georgism revival** online.

## Why It Matters

Doucet's review reintroduced George's 142-year-old argument to a large, analytically demanding audience — rationalists, effective altruists, and tech readers — who had never taken land seriously as an economic factor. The win was the launch point of a whole series: Alexander invited Doucet back as a guest author, and the resulting *[Does Georgism Work?](/wiki/doucet-does-georgism-work/)* essays began running on ACX on **9 December 2021**. The same body of work fed into his book *[Land Is a Big Deal](/wiki/land-is-a-big-deal/)* and his [Dwarkesh Podcast appearance](/wiki/dwarkesh-doucet-podcast/), and seeded interest among figures like [Vitalik Buterin](/wiki/buterin-on-radical-markets/) and Noah Smith. It is the single most influential piece of recent Georgist popular writing.

## What the Review Argues

The review (published as "Finalist #3 of the book review contest") opens from George's own question — "In 1879, a man asked 'How come all this new economic development and industrialized technology hasn't eliminated poverty and oppression?'" — and compresses the book to one sentence: "Poverty and wealth disparity appear to be perversely linked with progress, The Rent is Too Damn High, and it's all because of land."[1] Doucet gives three stated reasons for writing it: George's land/labor/capital framework as "a fresh alternative to conventional ideas about 'Capitalism' and 'Socialism'"; the book's "timeless advice for navigating modern crises such as ever-rising rents, homelessness, and the NIMBY vs. YIMBY wars"; and the chance to overuse "By George!"[1] It also popularized the now-standard biographical hooks of the revival: George's 1886 New York mayoral run (beating Theodore Roosevelt but losing to Tammany Hall's candidate), his death four days before the 1897 election, and the *Landlord's Game*-to-*Monopoly* lineage.[1]

The essay is a *synthesis and popularization*, not original research — its empirical claims (land-share magnitudes, incidence, assessment feasibility) were developed and sourced in the follow-up [*Does Georgism Work?*](/wiki/doucet-does-georgism-work/) series, which is where this wiki cites them.
