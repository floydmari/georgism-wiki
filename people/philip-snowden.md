## Overview

**Philip Snowden, 1st Viscount Snowden** (18 July 1864 – 15 May 1937) was a British Labour politician and the first Labour Chancellor of the Exchequer, holding the post in 1924 and again from 1929 to 1931.[1] Like many in the early Independent Labour Party, Snowden's economic thinking was shaped in part by the lecture tours and writings of [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/), and as Chancellor he revived the land-value-taxation project that [David Lloyd George](/wiki/david-lloyd-george/)'s 1909 People's Budget had begun and seen collapse.[2][3] Snowden's 1931 Finance Act put a land value tax on the UK statute books for the first time since the Lloyd George duties, but the National Government that replaced Labour's minority administration later that year suspended the tax in 1932 and repealed it outright in the Finance Act 1934, before it ever raised revenue.[1][4]

## The 1931 Land Value Tax and Its Repeal

Snowden's 1931 budget legislated a new land value tax, intended to require valuation of land on an unimproved basis as a step toward taxing site value directly — the same underlying approach the Lloyd George duties had attempted, and failed at administratively, two decades earlier.[3][4] Political conditions immediately turned against it: the financial crisis of 1931 split the Labour government, Snowden left the party to serve as Chancellor in the Conservative-dominated National Government, and the incoming administration moved to unwind the tax. Clause 24 of the 1932 Finance Bill suspended the land value tax before it took effect, and Clause 25 of the Finance Act 1934 formally repealed it.[4] Fred Harrison, in *Boom Bust*, cites the episode as one of a recurring pattern in which UK land taxation reached the statute book only to be reversed by the following government — grouping Snowden's 1931 tax with Lloyd George's 1909 duties as parallel political failures.[3]
