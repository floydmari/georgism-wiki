## Overview

Richard Cantillon (c. 1680s – 1734) was an Irish-French banker and merchant, born in County Kerry, who made a large fortune speculating around John Law's Mississippi Company scheme in Paris before turning that fortune into one of the earliest systematic works of economic theory.[1] His *Essai sur la Nature du Commerce en Général* ("Essay on the Nature of Trade in General"), written around 1730 and published posthumously in French in 1755, is widely regarded as the first attempt at a complete, self-contained treatise on economic theory — later "rediscovered" by economist William Stanley Jevons, who called it "the cradle of political economy."[1] Cantillon died in a fire at his London house in May 1734, under circumstances that led contemporaries to suspect he had been murdered by a servant who then set the house alight.[1]

## Land-Grounded Value Theory and the Landlord's Surplus

Cantillon's *Essai* opens with a land-and-labor theory of value: the "intrinsic value" of any good, he argued, is measured by the quantity of land and labor that entered into its production, though actual market price may diverge from this intrinsic value.[1] Building on this, Cantillon modeled a stylized economy in which farmers pay rent to landlords for the use of land, and argued that this rental surplus — what remained after paying the costs of production — ultimately flowed to and concentrated in the hands of landowners, who in turn drove the spending patterns of the wider economy.[1] This landlord-centered account of economic surplus anticipated the [Physiocrats](/wiki/economic-theory-in-retrospect/)' later doctrine that land rent was the sole source of a society's genuine surplus, a lineage that runs forward through [Adam Smith](/wiki/adam-smith/) and [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/) to Henry George.

## The Cantillon Effect

Cantillon is also credited as the first economist to systematically analyze how an increase in the money supply affects relative prices *unevenly*, depending on who receives the new money first: those closest to the source of new credit benefit before general prices rise, while those who receive it last see their real incomes eroded.[1] This mechanism, now called the "Cantillon effect," has been picked up by later writers examining credit and land cycles; [Phillip Anderson](/wiki/anderson-secret-life-real-estate/), for instance, uses "the Cantillon Effect" as the name for one of his forecasting indicators — the tendency of the world's tallest buildings to be completed near the peak of a credit-driven real estate cycle — in his account of the 18-year land cycle.[2]
