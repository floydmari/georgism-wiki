## Overview

James Gordon Brown (born 20 February 1951) served as UK Chancellor of the Exchequer from 1997 to 2007 under Tony Blair, then as Prime Minister from 2007 to 2010.[1] As Chancellor, "boom and bust" became a recurring theme of his budget speeches and party-conference rhetoric; in his eleventh and final Budget speech as Chancellor, on 21 March 2007, he told the House of Commons "we will not return to the old boom and bust," a claim he had made in various forms since at least 1999.[2] The UK entered recession within about eighteen months of that speech, as the 2008 financial crisis reached Britain.

As Chancellor, Brown commissioned economist Kate Barker to review why UK housing supply was not increasing despite a growing economy; her final report, the [Barker Review of Housing Supply](/wiki/barker-review-housing-supply/), was published by HM Treasury in 2004 and diagnosed the shortage as a land-supply and planning problem, but most of its recommendations were only partially implemented.[3] [Fred Harrison](/wiki/fred-harrison/)'s *[Boom Bust](/wiki/harrison-boom-bust/)* (2005) treats Brown, alongside the US Federal Reserve chairman of the period, as a central figure whose fiscal policy shifted debt onto households and failed to address the land-driven [boom-bust cycle](/wiki/boom-bust-cycle/) that Harrison and other land-cycle forecasters had been warning of since the 1990s.[4] This is Harrison's Georgist critique rather than an uncontested judgment; mainstream retrospectives on the 2008 crisis center on bank leverage and mortgage securitization, and credit Brown's chancellorship with a period of low inflation and low interest rates prior to the crash.[1]
