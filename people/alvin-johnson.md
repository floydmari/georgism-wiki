## Overview

Alvin Saunders Johnson (December 18, 1874 – June 7, 1971) was an American
economist, editor, and educator, best remembered as a **co-founder and the first
director of the New School for Social Research** in New York.[1][2] Born on a farm
near Homer, Nebraska, he took his PhD at Columbia in 1902 under
[Edwin R. A. Seligman](/wiki/era-seligman/) and [John Bates Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)
— two figures who also appear on this wiki as academic antagonists of
[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) — and taught at Columbia, Nebraska, Texas,
Chicago, Stanford, and, from 1913, Cornell before turning to editing and
institution-building.[1][3]

For this wiki Johnson matters in two ways. First, he was **a serious
period critic of the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/):** his essay
*The Case Against the Single Tax*, published in the *Atlantic Monthly* in
January 1914 while he was a professor at Cornell, became one of the most-cited
academic rebuttals of George's program — the wiki carries its
[full public-domain text](/wiki/johnson-case-against-single-tax-1914/), a
[research summary and Foldvary's rebuttal](/wiki/johnson-1914-single-tax-critique/),
and situates him among the [Critics of Henry George](/wiki/critics-of-henry-george/).
Second — and in tension with the caricature of the anti-George academic — Johnson
was no land-blind neoclassicist: his 1909 *Introduction to Economics* developed an
explicit **"natural capital" versus "artificial capital"** taxonomy that kept land
analytically distinct within a capital-theoretic framework, an alternative that
Missemer and Pottier cite as evidence the two-factor collapse of American economics
was a theoretical *choice* rather than a logical necessity
([Missemer & Pottier](/wiki/missemer-pottier-land-labor-capital/)).[4]

## Later career

Johnson helped found the New School in 1918 and became its director in 1922.[1][2]
In the 1930s and 1940s he organized the **"University in Exile,"** a New School
division that rescued scores of central-European scholars — many of them Jewish —
from Nazi persecution and gave them academic posts in New York.[1][2] He was also
associate editor and, in effect, the driving editorial force behind the fifteen-volume
*Encyclopaedia of the Social Sciences* (1930–1935), and earlier served as an editor of
the *New Republic*.[1] His autobiography, *Pioneer's Progress*, appeared in 1952.[1]
