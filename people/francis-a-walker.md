## Overview

Francis Amasa Walker (July 2, 1840 – January 5, 1897) was an American economist, statistician, and former Union Army general who served as the third president of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (1881–1897) and, from 1885 to 1892, as the first president of the American Economic Association.[1] He is separately known in the history of economics for demolishing the classical "wage-fund" doctrine in his *The Wages Question* (1876).[1][2]

Beginning shortly after the 1879 publication of [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s [Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/), Walker became one of the era's most prominent academic critics of George's land-value doctrine, engaging him in public debate over land, rent, and taxation.[1][2] Walker set out his rival theory of rent — treating it as a category continuous with ordinary factor returns rather than George's unique, unearned surplus — in *Land and Its Rent* (1883), a book based on a series of Lowell Institute/Harvard lectures written explicitly against George's argument.[1][2] As his career advanced, Walker moved further toward the economic establishment's conservative wing, becoming a sharper critic of George, socialists, and Gilded Age populists generally.[1]
