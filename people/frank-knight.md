## Overview

Frank Hyneman Knight (November 7, 1885 – April 15, 1972) was an American economist regarded as the principal founder of the Chicago School of economics.[1] After a Cornell PhD (1916) and a decade at the University of Iowa, he joined the University of Chicago faculty in 1927, where he taught until 1952 and shaped a generation of economists — including future Nobel laureates Milton Friedman, George Stigler, and James Buchanan — as their doctoral advisor.[1] His 1921 book *Risk, Uncertainty and Profit* is considered a foundational text in the economics of entrepreneurship and market uncertainty.[1]

Knight was also one of the single tax's most persistent academic critics. His essay "The Fallacies in the Single Tax" (1953) argued against treating land rent as a uniquely unearned or uniquely taxable surplus, contending that on close analysis "land yields no unearned surplus so long as competition keeps the returns to individuals at market levels."[2] Mason Gaffney's [*The Corruption of Economics*](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/) names Knight as a key adversary of Georgist economics, crediting him with attacking the Austrian "period of production" concept used to distinguish capital from land and with denying that rent constitutes a taxable surplus distinct from other returns — arguments Gaffney treats, adversarially, as having "inherited" and extended [J.B. Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)'s earlier campaign against Henry George.[2] Knight's reasoning continues to be cited by opponents of land value taxation.
