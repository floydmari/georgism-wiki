## Overview

Ramin Shokrizade is a virtual-economy and game economist who, while helping CCP Games design EVE Online's player-driven economy in 2003, arrived at a fix for a virtual land bubble that reproduces the logic of a [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/). At launch, players raced to buy the game's "factories" — fixed, scarce production sites tied to particular star systems, functionally identical to land — and by the time Shokrizade could log in, every prime factory had already been grabbed by speculators charging $300–400 apiece for access, with no guarantee of secure title.[1] His proposed fix was to raise the recurring holding fee on factories high enough that only players actively producing goods would want to keep them, creating a "hot potato" effect that pushed speculators out; the change took several months to implement but, once live, broke the speculators' grip on the economy, lowering consumer prices and reducing the wealth concentration the bubble had produced.[1]

Shokrizade has said he had no formal grounding in Georgist theory when he devised the mechanism, arriving at it through game-design trial and error; he later drew an explicit parallel to California's [Proposition 13](/wiki/proposition-13/) (1978), reasoning that when property taxes are lower than a property's rate of appreciation, holding land becomes a profitable speculation in the real world just as it had been for EVE's factory-hoarders.[1] His case is cited by [Lars Doucet](/wiki/lars-doucet/) in [*Land Is a Big Deal*](/wiki/land-is-a-big-deal/) (Ch. 10) as a real-world instance of the land-value-tax mechanism being rediscovered independently of the Georgist tradition, and a secondary write-up on virtual-world game design describes him as having had "no formal knowledge of Georgist theory" when he fixed EVE's land recession.[2]
