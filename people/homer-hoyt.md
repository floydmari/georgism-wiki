## Overview

Homer Hoyt (1895–1984) was an American land economist and real-estate analyst best known for
his 1933 University of Chicago doctoral dissertation, *[One Hundred Years of Land Values in
Chicago](/wiki/hoyt-chicago-land-values/)*, published by the University of Chicago Press.[1] The
study traced Chicago land values from 1830 to 1933 and documented a recurring rhythm of roughly
18 years between successive land-price peaks — alternating booms and busts driven by
speculation and credit — making it the founding empirical work behind what this wiki calls the
**[18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/)**.[1] Hoyt went on to a career in urban land
economics and real-estate appraisal, including developing the "sector model" of urban growth.

## Influence on Georgist Land-Cycle Analysis

Hoyt's empirical chronology became the historical backbone for later Georgist writers who argue
that land speculation, not just credit alone, is a primary driver of the business cycle.
**[Fred Harrison](/wiki/fred-harrison/)** drew directly on Hoyt's Chicago data in *The Power in
the Land* (1983) to argue that the same land-and-credit rhythm Hoyt found in nineteenth-century
Chicago recurs in modern economies, a claim Harrison later used to forecast the 2008 financial
crisis.[2]
