## Overview

William Vickrey (1914–1996) was a Canadian-American economist at Columbia, awarded
the 1996 Nobel Memorial Prize (days before his death, shared with James Mirrlees)
for work on incentives under asymmetric information — including the **Vickrey
auction**.[1] A lifelong public-finance reformer, he was also among the most
distinguished modern economists to identify openly with the Georgist tradition.

## Views on Land

Vickrey argued that the conventional property tax is really **two taxes** — a good
tax on land and a bad tax on buildings — and that the building portion should be
reduced or eliminated in favour of taxing land
([split-rate taxation](/wiki/split-rate-taxation/)). He considered the land tax
close to an ideal revenue source: efficient, hard to evade, and fair. He
contributed to the [Henry George Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/) literature
on financing city public goods from land rent, and championed marginal-cost
pricing of urban services with land rents covering the fixed costs.[2] In 1990 he
signed the [open letter to Gorbachev](/wiki/open-letter-to-gorbachev/) urging the
Soviet Union to retain land rent as public revenue.[3]

## Broader Contributions

Vickrey pioneered **[congestion pricing](/wiki/congestion-pricing/)** — charging
for scarce, commonly-owned road space on the same logic Georgists apply to land,
a theory since borne out by large measured traffic reductions in Singapore (1975),
London (2003) and Stockholm (2006). Posner and Weyl's
[*Radical Markets*](/wiki/posner-weyl-radical-markets/) treats his auction work as
a direct ancestor of their COST proposal (Ch. 1).[4] His standing gave LVT strong
respectability in mainstream public economics.
