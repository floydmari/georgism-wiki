## Overview

William Vickrey (1914–1996) was a Canadian-American economist at Columbia, awarded
the 1996 Nobel Memorial Prize (days before his death, shared with James Mirrlees —
who chaired the UK's later Mirrlees Review of taxation, itself an endorsement of
land taxation)[5] for work on incentives under asymmetric information — including the **Vickrey
auction**.[1] A lifelong public-finance reformer, he was also among the most
distinguished modern economists to identify openly with the Georgist tradition.

## Views on Land

Vickrey argued that the conventional property tax is really **two taxes** — a good
tax on land and a bad tax on buildings — and that the building portion should be
reduced or eliminated in favour of taxing land
([split-rate taxation](/wiki/split-rate-taxation/)). He considered the land tax
close to an ideal revenue source: efficient, hard to evade, and fair. He
contributed to the [Henry George Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/) literature
on financing city public goods from land rent, and championed marginal-cost
pricing of urban services with land rents covering the fixed costs.[2] A 2019 paper
extending the theorem to cities with heterogeneous sites names Vickrey's 1977
contribution alongside Flatters, Howrey & Wilson (1974) and [Arnott & Stiglitz
(1979)](/wiki/arnott-stiglitz-henry-george-theorem/) as the three founding
statements of "the classic 'Henry George Theorem'" — putting Vickrey's HGT work on
a documented par with Stiglitz's better-known formalization, not merely adjacent to
it.[6] In 1990 he
signed the [open letter to Gorbachev](/wiki/open-letter-to-gorbachev/) urging the
Soviet Union to retain land rent as public revenue.[3]

## A Formal Capital-Neutrality Proof with Gaffney

Beyond the Henry George Theorem, Vickrey collaborated directly with [Mason
Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) on the mathematics of capital-theory neutrality.
Gaffney's five-part 1970–71 *American Journal of Economics and Sociology* series
"Tax-Induced Slow Turnover of Capital" carries two mathematical appendices bearing
Vickrey's name: Appendix I (with Matthew P. Gaffney Jr. and Joseph Holzinger)
formally derives that the after-tax return on a point-input, point-output investment rises
with the investment's life under any positive tax rate on the maturity gain; Appendix
II — originally formulated by Michèle Consigny and generalized by Vickrey — establishes,
using continuous-time present-value calculus, the series' central **intertemporal-neutrality
theorem**: an income tax that allows deduction of true (economic) depreciation
collects revenue without distorting an asset's optimal holding period at all.[7] The
same Vickrey appendix was reprinted in Gaffney's 1977 Alaska oil-leasing report to
argue an ad valorem charge on oil-in-place does not bias extraction timing the way a
royalty does, and Gaffney's later "Sales-Tax Bias against Turnover and Jobs" applies
the same identity to VAT and retail sales taxes.[7] This is Type-C theoretical
material — formal derivation from stated assumptions, not an empirical finding — and,
consistent with the wiki's treatment of Gaffney's other capital-theory proofs, it is
kept out of every `supported_by` array on problems/benefits pages.

## Broader Contributions

Vickrey pioneered **[congestion pricing](/wiki/congestion-pricing/)** — charging
for scarce, commonly-owned road space on the same logic Georgists apply to land,
a theory since borne out by large measured traffic reductions in Singapore (1975),
London (2003) and Stockholm (2006). Singapore's own 28-year evolution from the
manual 1975 Area Licensing Scheme to the fully automated 1998 Electronic Road
Pricing system is, per the standard scholarly account, "the automatic, finely-
differentiated, per-passage price that congestion theory (Vickrey) had long pointed
toward" — the design that finally fixed the coarse cordon-charge's "wrong price,
wrong margin" problem.[8] Posner and Weyl's
[*Radical Markets*](/wiki/posner-weyl-radical-markets/) treats his auction work as
a direct ancestor of their COST proposal (Ch. 1), and traces a further lineage
through his "most prominent followers, Roger Myerson... and Mark Satterthwaite,"
whose 1983 impossibility result on efficient bilateral trading is cited against
purely Coasean solutions to land-assembly holdout problems.[4][9] His standing gave
LVT strong respectability in mainstream public economics.
