## Overview

David Lloyd George (1863–1945) was the Liberal Chancellor of the Exchequer who introduced the
**[1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/)**, proposing new land value duties framed
as a recovery of the "[unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/)" created by the community
rather than the landowner.[1] Defending the budget at Limehouse on 30 July 1909, he attacked
landlords who did nothing to earn rising land values and championed a "halfpenny tax on unearned
increment," a speech that became one of the most quoted moments in British land-tax history.[2]
The House of Lords' rejection of the budget triggered a constitutional crisis, two general
elections in 1910, and the Parliament Act 1911, which stripped the Lords of their veto over
money bills.[1] He later served as Prime Minister (1916–1922).
