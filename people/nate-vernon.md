## Overview

Nate Vernon (listed as Nate Vernon-Lin in some IMF publications) is an economist in the International Monetary Fund's Fiscal Affairs Department, whose recent work includes the design and modeling of climate-mitigation fiscal policy.[1] He holds a bachelor's degree in financial economics from the University of Rochester and a master's degree in public policy from the Harvard Kennedy School, and previously worked at IDinsight and the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development.[1] Public biographical detail beyond this is thin; no further claims are made here without a source. At the IMF, Vernon co-authored two of the wiki's sources on rent-targeted taxation of excess profits. With Thomas Baunsgaard, he wrote [Taxing Windfall Profits in the Energy Sector](/wiki/baunsgaard-vernon-windfall-profits/) (IMF Note NOTE/2022/002, August 2022), which argues that 2022's fossil-fuel windfall profits are, in practice, inseparable from ordinary [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/), and recommends a permanent rent-targeting tax over an ad hoc levy.[2] With Shafik Hebous and Dinar Prihardini, he wrote [Excess Profit Taxes: Historical Perspective and Contemporary Relevance](/wiki/hebous-excess-profit-taxes/) (IMF Working Paper WP/22/187, September 2022), which surveys World War I- and World War II-era excess profit taxes and argues a modern equivalent can be built as a non-distortionary rent tax via an allowance for corporate capital, while flagging that cross-border profit shifting — largely absent from the historical episodes — complicates unilateral design today.[3]
