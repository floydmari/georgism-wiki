## Overview

Charles M. Tiebout (1924–1968) was an American economist best known for a single, highly
influential 1956 paper, **"A Pure Theory of Local Expenditures,"** published in the *Journal of
Political Economy*, vol. 64, no. 5, pp. 416–424.[1] The paper proposed what became known as the
**Tiebout model**: households, in choosing where to live among many competing local
jurisdictions, effectively "vote with their feet" by sorting into the jurisdiction whose bundle of
local taxes and public services best matches their preferences. Under idealised conditions
(many jurisdictions, costless mobility, full information), this sorting process was argued to
produce an efficient allocation of local public goods, analogous to how markets allocate private
goods.

## Influence on Property-Tax and Land-Value Research

Tiebout's model became the foundation for later work connecting local public finance to land and
property values. **[Wallace Oates](/wiki/oates-1969-capitalization/)**'s 1969 study of tax and
spending capitalization in New Jersey municipalities was the first empirical test of the Tiebout
hypothesis, finding that both property taxes and local school spending capitalize into house
prices.[2] **Bruce Hamilton**'s 1975 and 1976 papers extended the Tiebout model with zoning and
intrajurisdictional capitalization to argue that, under the right conditions, the local property
tax behaves as an efficient benefit tax rather than a distortionary capital tax — the "benefit
view" discussed on [Hamilton's Benefit View](/wiki/hamilton-benefit-tax/).[3] Tiebout sorting is
also the mechanism underlying later formalizations of the **[Henry George
Theorem](/wiki/henry-george-theorem/)**, which link local public-goods provision to aggregate
land rent.
