## Overview

Sun Yat-sen (1866–1925) was the revolutionary leader regarded as the founding father of the Republic of China. Among his **Three Principles of the People** (*sanmin zhuyi*), the principle of *minsheng* (people's livelihood) included the **"equalization of land rights"** (*pingjun diquan*) — a land policy Sun proclaimed as a plank of his platform as early as **1905** and drew chiefly from [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/), and secondarily from [John Stuart Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/).[2]

## Georgist Influence

Sun encountered George's work and explicitly endorsed capturing land-value increases for the public. The connection is documented in his own words: in **1912** he told an interviewer that "the teaching of your single taxer, Henry George, will be the basis of our program of reform," a statement preserved in the [Lincoln Institute](/wiki/lincoln-institute/)'s case study of Taiwan.[1]

His mechanism prefigured modern self-assessment designs: landowners would **self-declare** their land values, the state would tax those declared values, and the **increase** in value over time would be captured publicly — with the state entitled to **buy the land at the owner's declared price**. This scheme closely mirrors George's [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) and is treated by [Posner & Weyl's *Radical Markets*](/wiki/posner-weyl-radical-markets/) as a precursor of their [Harberger tax / COST](/wiki/harberger-tax/) proposal — though they note the Taiwan implementation largely failed to enforce the buyout threat (Ch. 1, p. 46 per the wiki's book scan).[3]

## The Kiautschou Lineage — Real but Not Overclaimed

A concrete intellectual-transmission channel connects George's ideas to Sun through the German colonial land experiment at **[Kiautschou](/wiki/wertzuwachssteuer-germany/)** (Kiaochow / Jiaozhou, centred on Tsingtau/Qingdao). The 1898 Kiaochow Land Statute — a Georgist single-tax scheme taxing one-third of the realized land increment on resale plus an annual ~6% land charge — was drafted by the colonial official **Wilhelm Schrameier** (1859–1926). Michael Silagi & Susan Faulkner's peer-reviewed history calls Kiaochow "the only State-like territory to introduce a Single Tax on land in response to [Henry] George's campaign," and argues that Sun, who had already proclaimed "equalization of land rights" in 1905, found in "the administration of Kiaochow" a demonstration of "how that theory could be put into practice."[4] The biographical link is documented: Schrameier returned to China in **early 1924 at Sun Yat-sen's personal invitation** to advise on land reform and taxation, and after his death in Canton in **1926** Sun's son **Sun Fo** erected a monument to him.[4]

**Graded honestly.** This lineage is real and citable but is sometimes overstated. Schrameier did **not** author Sun's doctrine: Sun's "equalization of land rights" predates his 1924 collaboration with Schrameier and derives primarily from George directly. The hard facts — the 1905 platform, the 1924 invitation, Schrameier's role as Sun's emissary, the 1926 monument — are independently solid; the stronger interpretive claim that Kiaochow *showed Sun how* Georgism could work in practice comes from Silagi, a historian writing within the Georgist tradition. Treated as a well-attested transmission channel rather than sole causation, the Kiautschou → Sun → [Taiwan LVIT](/wiki/land-value-increment-tax/) lineage holds.[4]

## Legacy

Sun's land principle became constitutional doctrine in the Republic of China (Section 142 of the ROC Constitution) and was institutionalized in **[Taiwan](/wiki/taiwan/)** through the **1954 Statute for the Equalization of Land Rights** — which codified Sun's four principles (fair assessment of land value; taxation according to declared value; government optional purchase at declared value; public enjoyment of the future land-value increment) — and, in operative detail, the 1977 Land Tax Law that gave rise to the [land value increment tax](/wiki/land-value-increment-tax/).[1] This makes Sun the channel by which Georgist ideas reached East Asian land policy at national scale, and the intellectual ancestor of a self-assessment mechanism that [Posner & Weyl](/wiki/posner-weyl-radical-markets/) treat as a forerunner of their COST design.[3]
