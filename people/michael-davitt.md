## Overview

Michael Davitt founded the **Irish National Land League** in 1879 and, alongside Charles Stewart Parnell, led the mass agitation of the **[Irish Land War](/wiki/irish-land-war/)** (1879–82) against evictions and rack-renting by landlords, centred on the "Three Fs" — fair rent, fixity of tenure, and free sale.[1] Davitt engaged seriously with **[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)**'s ideas during George's 1881–82 tour of Ireland and Britain, and the two corresponded and collaborated for a period.[1][2] However, Davitt's own preferred remedy leaned toward **state land nationalization**, at times combined with peasant-proprietorship politics, diverging from George's tax-based **[single tax](/wiki/single-tax/)** approach set out in *[The Land Question](/wiki/the-land-question/)* (1881).[1][2]

## The Land League and the Land War

Davitt (born Straide, County Mayo, 25 March 1846; his family was evicted when he was four and emigrated to Lancashire) was a former Fenian: an organiser of the **Irish Republican Brotherhood** who took part in the failed 1867 raid on Chester Castle and was convicted of treason-felony for arms trafficking in 1870, sentenced to fifteen years' penal servitude and held at Millbank, Dartmoor, and Portsmouth before his release on ticket-of-leave in 1877. On his release he pioneered the "New Departure" — cooperation between the physical-force and constitutional wings of Irish nationalism on the land question — and founded the Irish National Land League in 1879 with Parnell as its president.[1][4] The League organised tenant farmers against evictions and rack-renting by mostly absentee landlords, demanding the "Three Fs": fair rent (judicially determined), fixity of tenure (security from eviction), and free sale (the tenant's right to sell their interest).[1] The British government's response, Gladstone's **Land Law (Ireland) Act 1881**, partially codified the Three Fs and opened state-assisted land purchase.[1]

The Land War also pushed the League's programme increasingly toward **peasant proprietorship** — buying out landlords and transferring titles to tenant farmers — a direction that Henry George would criticise sharply in *The Land Question*.[2]

## Relationship with Henry George

George wrote *The Land Question* (originally *The Irish Land Question*) at the height of the Land War in early 1881, directly addressing the Land Leagues in its subtitle.[2] Later that year George sailed for Ireland as a correspondent for Patrick Ford's New York paper *The Irish World*, reporting on the Land War for roughly a year and lecturing across Ireland and Britain.[2][3] He corresponded and worked with Davitt during this period, and in early August 1882, on a research trip through western Ireland with the Eton College master James Leigh Joynes, was arrested twice within two days — first at Loughrea, County Galway, where both men were detained under the Crimes Act as "suspicious strangers" ("the police seemed to start from the houses on each side and follow us"), and again the next day at Athenry, a few miles on, where police seized George alone (not Joynes) at the railway station as he prepared to leave for Galway. George treated the Loughrea arrest as farce — "I lost all sense of annoyance at my own arrest" at seeing "an Eton master lugged through the town as too suspicious a stranger to be left at large" — but formally protested both as "needlessly annoying and insulting"; he was discharged after several hours each time, the Athenry magistrate citing questions raised in the House of Commons by Chief Secretary George Trevelyan. The episode drew press attention on both sides of the Atlantic and George unsuccessfully sought a formal US protest through Minister James Russell Lowell.[2][5]

Even as this Irish sojourn proceeded, George's most intimate exchange with Davitt on the movement's direction came the following May, immediately after the Phoenix Park assassinations of the new Chief Secretary Lord Frederick Cavendish and Under-Secretary T.H. Burke — a crisis both men lived through in London together. George had spent the previous evening with Davitt, freshly released from Portland prison, and was the one who woke him with the news; Davitt's exclamation, as George recorded it, was "My God! have I got out of Portland for this!... For the first time in my life I despair."[5]

Davitt engaged seriously with George's analysis of rent and land monopoly, and the two maintained a correspondence and periods of collaboration.[1][2] However, their preferred **remedies diverged**. Davitt leaned toward state land nationalisation — the public ownership of land — and at times accommodated peasant-proprietorship politics, whereas George argued that the essential step was not changing *who* held title but capturing land **[economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/)** through taxation.[1][2] George contended that peasant proprietorship would merely reconstitute [land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) in new hands, as rising land values would eventually reproduce the same dynamic of rent extraction.[2]

The historian Andrew Phemister's *Land and Liberalism: Henry George and the Irish Land War* (Cambridge University Press, 2023) is the fullest modern academic treatment of Davitt's relationship with George and the Irish Land War's place in Anglo-American land reform politics.[1] The divergence between them is best read as one of programme rather than a single dramatic rupture: Davitt increasingly and publicly advocated outright **land nationalisation** (he set it out at length in his 1904 memoir *The Fall of Feudalism in Ireland*), while George held to rent-capture through the single tax; the two collaborated closely during George's 1881–82 Irish period even as their preferred remedies pulled apart.[1][4]

## Legacy

Davitt's Land War and the Land League movement contributed to a series of late-nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century land reforms that gradually transferred ownership from landlords to Irish tenant farmers through successive **land purchase acts**: the Purchase of Land (Ireland) Act 1885 (the Ashbourne Act, providing state loans for tenant purchase), the Land Law (Ireland) Act 1887 and the Purchase of Land (Ireland) Act 1891 (the Balfour acts), and above all the **Land Purchase (Ireland) Act 1903 (the Wyndham Land Act)**, whose generous bonus terms accelerated the wholesale buyout of Irish estates in the years around Davitt's death.[4] The divergence between Davitt's nationalisation route and George's single-tax route exemplified a broader split in the nineteenth-century land-reform movement between state-ownership advocates (such as [Alfred Russel Wallace](/wiki/alfred-russel-wallace/)'s Land Nationalisation Society) and tax-based reformers — a distinction discussed on this wiki's [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) and [land nationalization](/wiki/land-as-commons/) pages.
