## Overview

François Quesnay (1694–1774) was a French physician and economist, court surgeon to Louis XV, and the founder and intellectual leader of the **Physiocrats**, generally regarded as the first systematic school of economic thought.[1][2] In 1758 he published the *Tableau économique* (Economic Table), a diagrammatic model of the circulation of income among three classes of society — a "productive" agricultural class, a land-owning "proprietary" class, and a "sterile" manufacturing and commercial class — often cited as one of the first attempts to model an economy analytically.[2]

Central to Physiocratic doctrine was Quesnay's claim that agriculture alone produced a genuine surplus (the *produit net*, or net product) beyond the cost of production, and that this surplus ultimately accrued to landowners as rent. From this followed the **impôt unique**: a single tax levied directly on that net rental income — which Quesnay put at roughly a third of the net product — intended to minimize collection costs by taxing directly the one income source the Physiocrats believed ultimately bore the incidence of taxation, however it was formally levied.[1]

## Adam Smith on Quesnay (Wealth of Nations, Book IV, Ch. IX)

Adam Smith gives Quesnay the most direct, personal notice he affords to any rival economist in *[The Wealth of Nations](/wiki/wealth-of-nations/)* (1776) — naming him outright and describing his temperament, not merely his doctrine. Introducing the *Tableau économique*, Smith writes that the distribution of produce among the three classes "is represented by Mr Quesnai, the very ingenious and profound author of this system, in some arithmetical formularies. The first of these formularies, which, by way of eminence, he peculiarly distinguishes by the name of the Economical Table" (Book IV, Ch. IX).

Smith then offers a biographical aside, reading Quesnay's whole system as an extension of his training as a court physician:

> "Mr Quesnai, who was himself a physician, and a very speculative physician, seems to have entertained a notion of the same kind concerning the political body, and to have imagined that it would thrive and prosper only under a certain precise regimen, the exact regimen of perfect liberty and perfect justice." (Book IV, Ch. IX)

Smith immediately qualifies the compliment with his central objection to the system — that Quesnay, unlike a physician who allows for a body's own self-healing tendencies, "seems not to have considered, that in the political body, the natural effort which every man is continually making to better his own condition, is a principle of preservation" capable of offsetting "the bad effects of a political economy, in some degree both partial and oppressive" (Book IV, Ch. IX). Elsewhere in the same chapter Smith records the wider school's reverence for its founder — quoting the elder Mirabeau's judgment that Quesnay's Économical Table ranked with the invention of writing and of money among "three great inventions which have principally given stability to political societies" (Book IV, Ch. IX, quoting Mirabeau) — and notes that Quesnay "was himself a man of the greatest modesty and simplicity" (Book IV, Ch. IX). (A-claims for the quotations, public domain text, verified verbatim 2026-07-18.)

For Smith's fuller critique of the Physiocratic system this chapter mounts — including his "with all its imperfections... nearest approximation to the truth" verdict and his five-point rebuttal of the "barren and unproductive" doctrine — see [Physiocrats](/wiki/physiocrats/).

## Precedent for George's Single Tax

Economist Mark Blaug, in [*Economic Theory in Retrospect*](/wiki/economic-theory-in-retrospect/), traces the lineage of [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) back to Quesnay and the Physiocrats, while distinguishing the two carefully: "the physiocratic single tax is not a tax on 'the unearned increment' of rental values in response to a rising population à la James and John Stuart Mill but a species of land value taxation in the form of a levy on pure rent."[1] Both proposals share a core logic — that a tax on land rent, being a payment for a fixed, non-reproducible resource, does not distort production the way taxes on labor or capital do — but Quesnay's version, aimed at the whole agricultural "net product" of the economy, predates and differs in scope from George's narrower focus on site value alone.
