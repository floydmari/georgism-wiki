## Overview

**Adair Turner**, Baron Turner of Ecchinswell (born 5 October 1955), is a British businessman, academic, and life peer who chaired the UK's Financial Services Authority (FSA) from September 2008 — just as the [2008 financial crisis](/wiki/2008-financial-crisis/) broke — until the FSA's abolition in March 2013.[1] After leaving the FSA he joined George Soros's Institute for New Economic Thinking as a senior research fellow and wrote *Between Debt and the Devil: Money, Credit, and Fixing Global Finance* (Princeton University Press, 2016), which argues that most bank credit is not needed to finance productive investment but instead flows into pre-existing real estate — generating self-reinforcing cycles of credit supply, asset prices, and further credit demand.[2] Turner's earlier CBI leadership (1995–1999) and later climate-policy and pensions roles are not central to his relevance here; his significance for Geoism is that a prominent financial-regulation insider, writing without any Georgist framing, reached conclusions that dovetail with the argument that land and mortgage credit — not general "capital" — drive financial instability.[2]

Ryan-Collins, Lloyd and Macfarlane's *[Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing](/wiki/ryan-collins-rethinking-land-housing/)* cites Turner directly on this point, quoting his observation that "lending against real estate generates self-reinforcing cycles of credit supply, credit demand and asset prices."[3] This is one strand of evidence behind the broader claim that [finance's growth is largely mortgage credit against land](/wiki/finance-growth-is-land-credit/). Turner has since become a prominent advocate of "helicopter money" and other monetary-financing reforms, positions that go beyond the land-credit argument and are not endorsed here as part of the Geoist case.
