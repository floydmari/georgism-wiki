## Overview

Thorstein Bunde Veblen (1857–1929) was an American economist and sociologist,
best known for *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899) and as the founder of
**institutional economics**. His later book *[Absentee Ownership and Business
Enterprise in Recent Times](https://www.panarchy.org/veblen/absentee.html)*
(1923) extended his critique of unproductive wealth to real estate and land
speculation specifically. Veblen argued that the American "country town" —
he named Spoon River, Gopher Prairie, Emporia — was "basically a project to
puff up real estate prices," with municipal affairs and civic boosterism
converging on speculative land values that, he wrote, "seldom" exceeded the
land's actual use-value — as measured by tax assessors — "by less than 100
per cent."[1] Economist [Michael Hudson](/wiki/michael-hudson/), in a 2012
Levy Economics Institute study of Veblen's rent theory, summarizes this
strand of Veblen's work as treating real estate as "the great popular arena
in which to seek speculative gains," organized through **absentee
ownership** — control of land and productive assets by owners detached from
their operation, extracting income without corresponding productive
contribution.[2]

Veblen's critique parallels core Georgist concerns — unearned income from
[land speculation](/wiki/land-speculation/), the social rather than
individual origin of land value, and skepticism of a rentier class living off
site values it did not create — but it arrives at them through institutional
and sociological analysis (conspicuous consumption, "trained incapacity,"
the leisure class) rather than through Ricardian [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/)
theory. Hudson notes Veblen's vision of finance "hijacking" the economy's
technological potential was "as materialist and culturally broad as that of
the Marxists," making Veblen a distinct third strand — alongside Georgism and
Marxism — in the broader American tradition of critiquing rentier income.[2]
