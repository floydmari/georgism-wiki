## Overview

Robert Torrens (1780–1864) was a Royal Marines officer, newspaper proprietor, Member of Parliament, and one of the more prolific political economists of the Ricardian era. In 1815 he published *An Essay on the External Corn Trade*, one of four pamphlets — the others by Thomas Malthus, Edward West, and [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/) — that appeared within weeks of one another and independently set out the classical theory of differential rent amid the debate over Britain's Corn Laws.[1][2] Mark Blaug's standard history of economic thought credits Torrens, alongside West, as a "co-discoverer" of this rent theory and of the associated law of diminishing returns.[2]

## The 1815 Rent Pamphlets

The Corn Law controversy of 1815 — over tariffs protecting British landowners from cheaper imported grain — prompted a burst of near-simultaneous theorizing about why rent rises as cultivation is forced onto poorer land. Torrens's *Essay on the External Corn Trade* argued that rent is a differential surplus tied to the margin of cultivation, the same core mechanism [Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/) formalized in his own 1815 pamphlet and later systematized in the *Principles* (1817).[1][2] This episode is often treated by historians of economic thought as the first clear statement of the [margin of production](/wiki/margin-of-production/) principle, applied here to the [law of rent](/wiki/law-of-rent/) — the analytical engine [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) would generalize six decades later.[2]

## Other Contributions

Torrens is also credited by some historians with an early — and disputed — anticipation of the theory of comparative advantage in international trade, a distinction more commonly attributed to Ricardo's 1817 *Principles*. A recent scholarly reassessment (Maneschi-style history-of-thought work published in the *European Journal of the History of Economic Thought*, 2020) traces the evolution of Torrens's trade theory across editions of his *Essay on the External Corn Trade* and argues that as late as the early 1820s Torrens still adhered to an *absolute*-advantage framework — complicating the simple "Torrens anticipated comparative advantage" claim.[3] Beyond economics, Torrens was a founding member of the Political Economy Club, sat as an MP, part-owned the newspaper *The Globe*, and was an active promoter of the colonization of South Australia; his name survives in Adelaide's River Torrens.[1]

**Identity note.** This page is about **Robert Torrens the economist (1780–1864)**, the Royal Marines officer, MP, and rent-theory pamphleteer — *not* his son **Sir Robert Richard Torrens (1814–1884)**, the South Australian premier who gave his name to the **Torrens title** system of land registration. The two are frequently conflated; every claim on this page (the 1815 *Essay on the External Corn Trade*, the Corn Law debate, differential rent, comparative advantage, the *Globe*, the South Australian Colonisation Commission) belongs to the father, the economist, and matches his dates and career.[1]

## Significance for Georgism

Torrens belongs to the founding generation of classical economists — alongside Malthus, West, and Ricardo — whose independent 1815 formulations of differential rent theory gave [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) the analytical raw material for *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* six decades later. His work is a reminder that the [law of rent](/wiki/law-of-rent/) was not a single economist's discovery but the near-simultaneous product of a specific policy controversy — the Corn Laws — that forced rent theory to the center of British political economy.[2]
