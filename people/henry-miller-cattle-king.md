## Overview

**Henry Miller** (born Heinrich Kreiser, July 21, 1827 – October 14, 1916) was a German-American rancher known as the "Cattle King of California," who at the height of his career was among the largest private landowners in the United States.[1] Born in Brackenheim, Württemberg, he emigrated to New York in 1846, worked as a butcher, and moved to San Francisco in 1850 — adopting the name "Henry Miller" from a non-transferable steamship ticket bought secondhand from a friend.[1] After building a butcher business, he partnered with fellow German immigrant Charles Lux in 1858, and the firm of Miller & Lux expanded from meatpacking into cattle ranching and land acquisition across California, Nevada, and Oregon. The standard scholarly history — David Igler's *Industrial Cowboys: Miller & Lux and the Transformation of the Far West, 1850–1920* (University of California Press, 2001) — establishes the firm as having "consolidated the West's most extensive land and water rights"; scholarly and archival accounts put its outright holdings at **over 1.25 million acres** across California, Nevada, and Oregon (Waldschmidt-Nelson: "close to 1.3 million"; Oregon Historical Society: "over 1.25 million"), with popular sources citing figures up to ~1.4 million.[1][4][5] Through leases, riparian claims, and water rights, Miller & Lux controlled far more — by widely repeated accounts on the order of some 22,000 square miles of cattle and farm land — though these control-not-ownership figures vary by source and era.[1]

Much of that land was acquired cheaply through aggressive use of loosely administered federal and state land laws, including the Homestead Act of 1862, the Desert Land Act of 1877, and — most notoriously — the **Swamp and Overflow Lands Act of 1850**, which let claimants obtain land nearly free if it was underwater and traversable only by boat.[2] According to a widely repeated account, Miller had a rowboat mounted on a wagon and was driven across dry San Joaquin Valley land so that he and a witness could truthfully swear, by affidavit, that the tract had been traversed by boat — securing title to it as "swampland."[3] Miller & Lux also bought up former Spanish and Mexican land grants from indebted owners squeezed by the California Land Law of 1851, consolidating vast holdings around the Bay Area and San Joaquin Valley.[2] Contemporaries in similar businesses — the "Wheat King" Hugh Glenn, the "Sugar King" Claus Spreckels, the railroad "Big Four" — used comparable tactics, and Miller & Lux's methods, including dummy buyers and favorable relationships with county assessors, were not exceptional for the era.[1][2]

At his death in 1916, Miller's estate was appraised at roughly $40 million (well over $1 billion in 2025 dollars), though smaller than at his peak.[1] For Georgist purposes, Miller's career is a paradigm case of **land monopoly** built not through market competition but through exploitation of gaps and loopholes in public-land administration — a 19th-century instance of the broader pattern in which control over land, rather than production, was the primary source of a great American fortune.
