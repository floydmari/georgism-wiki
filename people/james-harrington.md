## Overview

James Harrington (1611–1677) was an English political theorist whose utopian
treatise *The Commonwealth of Oceana* (1656) argued that a state's form of
government is fixed by its underlying distribution of property, above all
**land**: where one ruler owns most of the land, absolute monarchy results;
where a narrow aristocracy holds it, mixed monarchy follows; but where land
is widely distributed, only a republic can remain stable — the maxim
historians summarize as *"empire follows the balance of property."*[1] To keep
land distribution from re-concentrating, Harrington proposed an **agrarian
law** capping individual land holdings (at an income of roughly £2,000 a
year) — an early proposal to regulate land ownership by statute, over two
centuries before [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) proposed capturing land's
rent directly through taxation rather than capping holdings by size.[1][2]
Oceana was seized by order of Oliver Cromwell while in press and released
only after the intercession of Cromwell's daughter Elizabeth Claypole; it
appeared later in 1656.[1] [Fred Harrison's *Ricardo's Law*](/wiki/harrison-ricardos-law/)
cites Harrington's land-power thesis in its prologue and in discussing the
historical link between land concentration and political power.[2]

Harrington's argument is a structural, pre-Georgist ancestor of the claim
that [land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) is a political as well as an
economic problem: control of land is control of the state, not merely a
source of private income. Where Harrington's remedy was a legal ceiling on
how much land any one person could hold, the Georgist tradition instead
targets the *rent* land produces, leaving ownership and holding size
unregulated — a difference in mechanism that later land-and-power theorists,
including [John Locke](/wiki/john-locke/), [Thomas Paine](/wiki/thomas-paine/),
and [Herbert Spencer](/wiki/herbert-spencer/), inherit and revise in their
own ways.[1]
