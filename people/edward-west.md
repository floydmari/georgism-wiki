## Overview

Sir Edward West (1 March 1782 – 18 August 1828) was a British economist and judge who served in colonial India.[1] Educated at Harrow and University College, Oxford (BA 1804, MA 1807), he was called to the Bar in 1814, then appointed Recorder and later Chief Justice of the King's Court of Bombay, where he died and was buried.[1] He is remembered in economics for a single, sharply timed contribution: his 1815 pamphlet, *Essay on the Application of Capital to Land*, stated the law of diminishing returns and a theory of differential rent almost identical in substance — historians note "in virtually the same form and language" — to the argument [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/) published independently within the same few weeks.[1][2]

West's essay argued against further restriction of corn imports and observed that "in the progress of the improvement of cultivation the raising of rude produce becomes progressively more expensive... the ratio of the net produce of land to its gross produce is constantly diminishing" — an early statement of the [law of diminishing returns](/wiki/law-of-diminishing-returns/).[1] His pamphlet was one of four independent statements of differential rent theory that appeared within about three weeks of each other in February 1815, alongside those of Thomas Malthus, Robert Torrens, and Ricardo, an episode covered on the wiki as [the 1815 rent theory pamphlets](/wiki/1815-rent-theory-pamphlets/).[1][2] Ricardo himself credited Malthus and West for priority on the diminishing-returns principle, though it was Ricardo's own fuller 1817 treatment that became the version economics remembers.[2]
