## Overview

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913), the naturalist who independently conceived natural selection alongside Darwin, was also a committed social reformer — and one of Britain's most prominent advocates of **land nationalisation**.

## Land Reform

Wallace's 1880 essay "How to Nationalize the Land: A Radical Solution of the Irish Land Problem" (*Contemporary Review*, November 1880) was instrumental in the founding of the **Land Nationalisation Society in 1881**, of which Wallace was elected the **first president** — a post he held for the rest of his life. The Society's aim, in its own words, was "to restore the Land to the People and the People to the Land." He set out the full argument in ***Land Nationalisation: Its Necessity and Its Aims*** (1882), holding that private monopoly of land was a fundamental social injustice and that the state should take ownership of the land.

His position was close to, though distinct from, Henry George's. Where George proposed taxing away land rent while leaving legal title private (the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/)), Wallace favoured the state acquiring the land itself — buying out landlords, in his scheme, at a valuation below full market price and separating the "inherent" value of land from the value of tenants' improvements. This state-purchase route, rather than George's tax method, was the model [Michael Davitt](/wiki/michael-davitt/) adopted when he declared for "land nationalisation" in his Liverpool speech of 6 June 1882. The two approaches were part of the same late-19th-century ferment over the land question that *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* helped ignite. Wallace was also among the contemporaries who publicly criticised [Herbert Spencer](/wiki/herbert-spencer/)'s retreat from the equal-rights-to-land position of his early *Social Statics*.

Mason Gaffney's 1997 account of Wallace's campaign (*American Journal of Economics and Sociology* 56(4)) supplies the compensation mechanism's specifics: Wallace's proposed annuity to bought-out landowners was deliberately narrow, "limited to the duration of lives in being" and based "only on the net income actually being derived from the land before nationalization — i.e. not from the highest and best use, and not from future higher uses" — a design Gaffney reads as evidence Wallace "knew the nuts and bolts of his subject."[5]

## Wallace, John Stuart Mill, and the Land Tenure Reform Association

Wallace's turn to land reform ran through [John Stuart Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/), not around him. Mill, in his last years (1871–73), formed and led the [Land Tenure Reform Association](/wiki/land-tenure-reform-association/) and personally sought Wallace out to join it — Wallace deferred to Mill as "the veteran economist and social leader" and, per Gaffney, "had no problem playing second fiddle, as he did earlier for Darwin, and later for Henry George."[5] Mill's own program was cautious — taxing away only *future* increments of land value — and it was only after Mill's 1873 death that Wallace grew more assertive. The 1880 Irish land agitation moved him to public criticism of Charles Stewart Parnell's peasant-proprietorship program as not abolishing land privilege but merely "reshuffling some land titles from a smaller to a larger minority"; in 1881 Wallace founded and led the Land Nationalisation Society on his own, more thoroughgoing lines.[5]

Once George toured Britain, Liberal Prime Minister Herbert Asquith — a friend to both men — treated Wallace's nationalisation and George's single tax as complementary levers rather than rivals, coining the slogan "Tax or Buy": "If valuation was too low, Buy! If too high, Tax!"[5]

## Wallace versus the Social Darwinists

Gaffney's account places Wallace at "the other pole" from contemporaries who used Darwinian language to defend concentrated landownership — Thomas Henry Huxley (who "devoted chapters in several books to defending the concentrated control of land in England" and attacking George), Herbert Spencer (who coined "survival of the fittest" and held that poverty marked the unfit), and William Graham Sumner. Where they treated existing land-tenure arrangements as a fixed backdrop to natural selection among humans, Wallace held that ending land inheritance and privilege would let individual merit and, in his own framework, female choice of mates — rather than inherited land title — drive human progress. Gaffney notes Huxley and Wallace nonetheless "remained personal friends and mutual fans" despite the dispute.[5] Gaffney, who also wrote the Wallace entry in *The New Palgrave Dictionary of Political Economy* (1987), closes by observing that Britain's eventual land-use reform, the Town and Country Planning Act (1947), "evinced more Wallace than George" — George's followers, he suggests, would not have owned it.[5]

## Significance

As a scientist of the first rank lending his name and energy to land reform, Wallace gave the nationalisation wing of the British land movement unusual visibility. His Society was one of the routes by which the "land question" reached British reformers, and it defined — alongside George's tax route — the two poles of land-reform strategy that ran through the 1880s and into the [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/) era.
