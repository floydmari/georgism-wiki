## Overview

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913), the naturalist who independently conceived natural selection alongside Darwin, was also a committed social reformer — and one of Britain's most prominent advocates of **land nationalisation**.

## Land Reform

Wallace's 1880 essay "How to Nationalize the Land: A Radical Solution of the Irish Land Problem" (*Contemporary Review*, November 1880) was instrumental in the founding of the **Land Nationalisation Society in 1881**, of which Wallace was elected the **first president** — a post he held for the rest of his life. The Society's aim, in its own words, was "to restore the Land to the People and the People to the Land." He set out the full argument in ***Land Nationalisation: Its Necessity and Its Aims*** (1882), holding that private monopoly of land was a fundamental social injustice and that the state should take ownership of the land.

His position was close to, though distinct from, Henry George's. Where George proposed taxing away land rent while leaving legal title private (the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/)), Wallace favoured the state acquiring the land itself — buying out landlords, in his scheme, at a valuation below full market price and separating the "inherent" value of land from the value of tenants' improvements. This state-purchase route, rather than George's tax method, was the model [Michael Davitt](/wiki/michael-davitt/) adopted when he declared for "land nationalisation" in his Liverpool speech of 6 June 1882. The two approaches were part of the same late-19th-century ferment over the land question that *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* helped ignite. Wallace was also among the contemporaries who publicly criticised [Herbert Spencer](/wiki/herbert-spencer/)'s retreat from the equal-rights-to-land position of his early *Social Statics*.

## Significance

As a scientist of the first rank lending his name and energy to land reform, Wallace gave the nationalisation wing of the British land movement unusual visibility. His Society was one of the routes by which the "land question" reached British reformers, and it defined — alongside George's tax route — the two poles of land-reform strategy that ran through the 1880s and into the [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/) era.
