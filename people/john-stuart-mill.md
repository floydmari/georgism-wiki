## Overview

**John Stuart Mill** (1806–1873) was the leading British philosopher and political
economist of the mid-nineteenth century — utilitarian, logician, champion of liberty and
of women's suffrage, and Member of Parliament for Westminster (1865–1868). Educated with
famous intensity by his father James Mill, he became the age's most influential public
intellectual. His *Principles of Political Economy* (1848) was the standard economics
textbook for a generation, and its treatment of land rent — set out in Book V, and examined
in depth on the wiki at [Mill on Land and Taxation](/wiki/mill-principles-land/) — directly
anticipated [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/).

## The Unearned Increment

Mill's most consequential contribution to the land question was to name and diagnose the
**[unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/)**. Landlords, he observed, "grow richer,
as it were in their sleep, without working, risking, or economizing": the rent of their land
rises through the growth and effort of the surrounding society, not through anything the
owner does.[1] Because that gain is socially created, Mill argued it was a uniquely fit
subject for taxation — a tax on land rent, unlike a tax on wages or capital, discourages no
production, since land's supply is fixed. This is the classical case the whole wiki rests on
([ground rent](/wiki/ground-rent/); [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/)).

## Why He Stopped Short of George

Mill matters to Georgism as both forerunner and foil, and the difference is worth stating
honestly. Mill's remedy was **gradualist and compensatory**: the state should tax only the
*future* increase in land values above their present level, leaving current values (and thus
current owners' expectations) largely intact — a landowner should not be dispossessed of
what the law had led him to expect. [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/), a generation later,
rejected this restraint: in *Progress and Poverty* (1879) he argued for capturing
essentially the *whole* of land rent, present as well as future, and without compensation,
on the ground that the community had never owed the landlord the rent in the first place.
Mill supplied the principle and the vocabulary; George pushed it to its radical conclusion.
The gap between "tax the future increment, with compensation" and "capture the rent, without"
is the central fault line between moderate and thoroughgoing land reform to this day.

## The Land Tenure Reform Association

In 1869 Mill lent his prestige to founding the **[Land Tenure Reform
Association](/wiki/land-tenure-reform-association/)** (LTRA), and in 1871 he wrote its
*Programme* with an explanatory statement — his last major public campaign before his death
in 1873. Mill personally recruited [Alfred Russel Wallace](/wiki/alfred-russel-wallace/) to
the LTRA in these years; Wallace, newly turned to land reform, deferred to Mill as its
"veteran economist and social leader" and only struck out on his own more radical
nationalisation program after Mill's 1873 death.[4] The programme's most striking planks were **heavy taxation of the unearned
increment** of land values and the promotion of **cooperative agriculture**, alongside the
abolition of primogeniture and entail and the reclamation of waste lands for the public. The
LTRA was small and short-lived, but its programme carried the unearned-increment idea into
organised Liberal politics, where it inspired later generations down to [David Lloyd
George](/wiki/david-lloyd-george/) and the land-value provisions of the
[1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/). Through the LTRA, Mill turned a passage
in a textbook into a live political demand.

## A Skeptical Read of the Gradualism

Mason Gaffney's 1982 historiographical survey treats Mill's gradualism more
critically than most Georgist accounts. Gaffney notes that Mill "recognizes
fleetingly that present land values include discounted future increments,
but dances calisthenically around this," before concluding it is fine to
tax only future increments — a position Gaffney finds does not hold up,
since in a rising market "future increments for all future time are
already reflected in land prices."[3] Yet Gaffney also credits Mill with
stronger language than the future-increments qualification suggests
Mill intended to concede: "among the very few kinds of income which are
fit subjects for peculiar taxation, these (urban) ground-rents hold the
principal place," and a house-tax, so far as it falls on the
ground-landlord, "is liable to no valid objection" (Mill 1872, Book V, Ch.
II) — "for Mill the moralist and logician, that is strong language."[3]
Gaffney is also more pointed than this wiki's account above about the
timing of Mill's Land Tenure Reform Association activism, calling it "almost
a posthumous activity" that came "only after his autobiography is finished
and his main energies spent."[3]

## Legacy

Mill gave the Georgist movement something it could not have manufactured for itself:
**mainstream intellectual respectability** and a ready-made vocabulary. When George argued
that land rent was unearned and socially created, he was building on the most respected
economist of the previous generation, not inventing a fringe doctrine. That lineage — Smith
to Ricardo to Mill to George — is why the efficiency case for taxing land sits inside the
classical tradition rather than outside it.
