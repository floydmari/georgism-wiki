## Overview

Mariana Mazzucato (b. 1968) is an Italian-American-British economist and **Professor in the Economics of Innovation and Public Value at University College London**, where she is the **Founding Director of the UCL Institute for Innovation and Public Purpose (IIPP)**; she previously held the RM Phillips Professorial Chair at the Science Policy Research Unit (SPRU) at the University of Sussex.[3] She is known for *The Entrepreneurial State* (2013), *The Value of Everything* (2018), and *Mission Economy* (2021). Her central theme — the difference between **creating** value and **extracting** it — directly revives the classical and Georgist concern with economic rent.

## Relevance to Georgism

Mazzucato argues that modern economies increasingly reward **value extraction** — rent-seeking through finance, intellectual property, and land — over genuine value creation, and that national accounting wrongly counts much extraction as production. Her organizing device is the [**production boundary**](/wiki/production-boundary/): the shifting line national accounts draw between activity counted as productive and activity treated as merely redistributive. *The Value of Everything* argues the modern "comprehensive" boundary erased the classical distinction by defining value as whatever commands a market price — so that, for instance, imputed rent on owner-occupied housing is booked as roughly 6% of US GDP even though, as she writes, "capital gains from holding property arise out of increases in land value, which itself are determined by collective investment (in roads, schools, etc.) — little to do with the effort of the property owner."[2]

**An important limit, kept honest.** *The Value of Everything* makes **no land-value-tax or land-value-capture proposal and never mentions Henry George** — its land-rent content is one instance of a general pattern, not a dedicated case study.[2] The explicit link to land rent comes in the 2023 paper below.

## Role in the Wiki's Rent-Gradient Frame

With Josh Ryan-Collins and Giorgos Gouzoulis, Mazzucato authored **["Mapping Modern Economic Rents"](/wiki/mazzucato-mapping-rents/)** (2023, *Cambridge Journal of Economics*), which extends the classical analysis of [land rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) to three domains — **land and natural resources, finance, and digital platforms** (network and data rents) — arguing that much of what is recorded as "profit" or "capital income" is in fact rent. This paper is the wiki's **bridge between the classical land story and the modern rents debate**: it treats land rent as the *template* case and asks which contemporary income flows share its extractive structure, organizing several of the wiki's clusters — the [FIRE-sector](/wiki/fire-sector/) financial rents, the [platform/data rents](/wiki/data-rents/) dispute, and the land-and-housing base.[1]

**How the wiki weights it.** The paper takes the rent reading as its **premise**, so the wiki reports it as a *framework, not a finding*. Whether these contemporary flows genuinely *are* rents (versus the [quasi-rent](/wiki/quasi-rent/) that rewards real innovation and [intangible capital](/wiki/crouzet-eberly-intangibles/)) is [genuinely contested](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/), and the wiki carries that dispute at full strength on its own pages — never letting the airtight land case lend its certainty to the platform-rent frontier.

## Significance

Mazzucato brings the language of [rent-seeking](/wiki/rent-seeking/) into mainstream policy discourse, providing contemporary intellectual support for taxing rent rather than production — even where she does not frame it as Georgism, and even though her own policy prescriptions center on mission-oriented public investment and value-measurement reform rather than land taxation.
