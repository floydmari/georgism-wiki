## Overview

Alan Greenspan chaired the US Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, appointed by President Reagan and reappointed through four terms — the second-longest tenure of any Fed chair.[1] In 2002 he received an honorary UK knighthood (KBE) from Queen Elizabeth II "for his contribution to global economic stability."[5] He died on June 22, 2026, at his Washington, D.C. home from complications of Parkinson's disease, at age 100.[2] Through the 1990s and 2000s Greenspan repeatedly argued that asset bubbles cannot reliably be identified before they burst; in June 2005, with US house prices near their peak, he conceded only "signs of froth in some local markets" rather than a bubble, a position he maintained until after the 2008 crash, when he acknowledged the Fed had failed to gauge the housing bubble's scale.[3][4]

Georgist writers, notably [Fred Harrison](/wiki/fred-harrison/) in *Boom Bust* (2005), treat Greenspan's tenure as a case study for the claim that monetary policy cannot manage a [land-driven boom-bust cycle](/wiki/boom-bust-cycle/): Harrison had forecast a downturn "around 2008" years in advance using the [18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/), while Greenspan's Fed, on the credit-cycle reading, denied a bubble was underway until it had already burst.[5] This is Harrison's interpretation of events, not an uncontested finding; mainstream accounts of the crisis center on bank leverage and mortgage securitization rather than a predictable land cycle.
