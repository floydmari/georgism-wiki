## Overview

**William Simon U'Ren** (January 10, 1859 – March 8, 1949) was an American lawyer and political reformer, born in Lancaster, Wisconsin, who settled in Oregon in 1889 and became known as the "Father of the Oregon System" — the initiative, referendum, and recall.[1][2] On his way to Oregon, U'Ren read [Henry George's](/wiki/henry-george/) *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*, and later, encountering James W. Sullivan's *Direct Legislation by the Citizenship through the Initiative and Referendum* (1892), concluded that direct democracy was the political tool needed to enact a [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) on land value over the resistance of Oregon's legislature.[1]

U'Ren and the Direct Legislation League won voter approval of an initiative-and-referendum constitutional amendment in 1902 (following legislative passage in 1899 and 1901), making Oregon the first U.S. state to adopt statewide direct legislation; he went on to campaign successfully for the recall (1908) and for direct election of U.S. senators.[1][2] The "Oregon System" was subsequently copied by many other states. U'Ren's own ultimate goal — a statewide single tax on land values — was put to Oregon voters multiple times via the initiative process he had built, and was defeated each time, a striking case of a reformer succeeding brilliantly at building the machinery of reform while failing at the substantive policy it was built to deliver.[1]
