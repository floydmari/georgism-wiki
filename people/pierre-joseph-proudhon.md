## Overview

Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (January 15, 1809 – January 19, 1865) was a French printer, journalist, and self-taught political theorist, born in Besançon. His first major work, *Qu'est-ce que la propriété?* (*What Is Property?*, 1840), opened with the declaration "Property is theft!" — an attack on property income (rent, interest, profit) derived from ownership rather than labor.[1] Proudhon distinguished this exploitative "property" from mere "possession" — the right to use and hold what one occupies and works — which he regarded as legitimate and essential to liberty; he is widely credited as the first person to call himself an anarchist, and became a leading theorist of mutualism.[1]

Proudhon's slogan places him at one pole of the property-rights argument that runs from [John Locke's](/wiki/john-locke/) natural-rights defense of private ownership at one extreme to full common ownership at the other. Georgism is often read as occupying a middle position on land specifically: it rejects Proudhon's blanket condemnation of property as such, but agrees that *unearned* income from exclusive control of a fixed natural resource is illegitimate and should be publicly reclaimed — a narrower, land-specific version of his complaint against unproductive property income.[2] Ryan-Collins, Lloyd, and Macfarlane discuss Proudhon alongside Locke, Paine, Marx, and Mill as one of the pre-Georgist voices in the long European debate over the moral status of land ownership.[2]
