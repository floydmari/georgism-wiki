## Overview

Michael Hudson (b. 1939) is an American economist known for his analysis of debt, finance, and economic rent. He frames the modern economy as a contest between productive activity and **rent extraction** by the **FIRE sector** (finance, insurance, and real estate), reviving classical and Georgist concerns about unearned income.

## Key Work

- ***[Killing the Host](/wiki/hudson-killing-the-host/)*** (2015) argues that the financial sector increasingly extracts rents — much of it ultimately **land rent** via mortgage credit — rather than funding productive investment.
- **["Finance Is Not the Economy"](/wiki/bezemer-hudson-finance-is-not-the-economy/)** (with Dirk Bezemer, 2016, *Journal of Economic Issues*) is the peer-reviewed statement of the thesis: bank credit decoupled from GDP growth after the mid-1980s, and the extra credit flowed chiefly into household mortgages rather than productive lending — on a 14-country OECD panel, mortgage credit's share of all bank lending rose from about 20% to 50% between 1990 and 2011.
- **"Finance Capitalism versus Industrial Capitalism"** (2021, *Review of Radical Political Economics*) restates the thesis for a political-economy audience.
- He co-authored the 2021 [super-stimulus paper](/wiki/goodhart-stimulus/) with Goodhart, Kumhof, and Tideman, linking his rent analysis to a concrete land-tax policy proposal.
- **["Where Did All the Land Go?"](/wiki/hudson-where-did-all-the-land-go/)** (2001) is his accounting critique of the Federal Reserve's land-value statistics: because the Fed derives land value as a residual after subtracting buildings' replacement cost, it reported nonfinancial corporations' land as worth **negative $4 billion** by 1993 — a result even Hudson calls absurd — and stopped publishing the series in 1995. The mechanical flaw he identifies is independently corroborated by mainstream researchers (Albouy, Ehrlich & Shin 2018; Kuminoff & Pope 2013), even though his own back-of-envelope revaluation is a rough estimate, not a formal measurement.
- **["Henry George's Political Critics"](/wiki/hudson-georges-political-critics/)** (2008, *American Journal of Economics and Sociology*) is a reception-history article, distinct from his FIRE-sector work: it catalogues twelve political (not economic) criticisms — George's refusal to merge the single-tax campaign with other reform movements, his narrow focus on ground rent over other monopoly income, his treatment of interest-bearing debt as separate from ground rent — that isolated George from socialist, labor, and academic coalitions after 1887.

## Significance

Hudson connects Georgist land-rent theory to the dynamics of debt and banking, arguing that rising land prices and the credit that funds them are central to financial instability — a view aligned with the [18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/). His own scholarship on George is not only inheritance but direct historiography: his 2008 article on George's political isolation argues the single-tax movement's coalition failures were political rather than a refutation of the underlying economics, foreshadowing his later extension of rent theory from land to finance.
