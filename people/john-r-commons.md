## Overview

John R. Commons (1862–1945) was an American institutional economist, labor historian, and progressive reformer who spent most of his career at the **University of Wisconsin–Madison**, where he helped originate the "Wisconsin Idea" of university faculty advising state government and later served as president of the American Economic Association.[1] Wikipedia's own category labels for Commons include "Georgist economists," reflecting that he explicitly carried forward [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s "single tax" economics — with a focus on land and monopoly rents — as a "Georgist" or "Ricardian" strand within his broader institutionalist framework.[1]

Mason Gaffney and Fred Harrison's *[The Corruption of Economics](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/)* credits Commons with drafting Wisconsin's **Grimstad Bill**, a state land-value tax proposal, with the young assemblyman (and later professor) Harold Groves as a supporting sponsor.[2] The same source attributes to Commons the claim, drawn from his 1922 article "A Progressive Tax on Bare Land Values" (*Political Science Quarterly* 37(1), 41–68), that roughly **95% of American millionaire fortunes** traced to land and franchise (monopoly) values rather than to productive enterprise — a figure used in the wiki's broader narrative about land's role in fortune-building.[2][3]
