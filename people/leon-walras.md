## Overview

Marie-Esprit-Léon Walras (16 December 1834 – 5 January 1910) was a French
mathematical economist and, alongside William Stanley Jevons and Carl Menger,
one of the three independent founders of the marginal-utility revolution.[1]
As professor of political economy at the University of Lausanne he founded
the **Lausanne School**, and his *Éléments d'économie politique pure* (1874)
pioneered **general equilibrium theory** — the mathematical demonstration
that prices across all markets can simultaneously clear. [Joseph Schumpeter](/wiki/joseph-schumpeter/)
called the achievement so significant that he considered Walras "the greatest
of all economists," and the general-equilibrium framework was carried forward
by Vilfredo Pareto, Knut Wicksell, and later formalized rigorously by Kenneth
Arrow and Gérard Debreu.[1] Wikipedia's own classification places him in the
same category as "Georgist economists" — one of the few founding marginalists
for whom that label is applied without qualification.[1]

## Land Nationalization and the Social Dividend

Walras inherited his father Auguste Walras's interest in social reform, and —
much like the Fabians in Britain — called for the **nationalization of land**.
His reasoning was that land's productivity would keep rising over time, so
the rent collected from nationalized land would eventually be sufficient to
fund the state entirely, letting **all other taxes** on goods, labor, and
capital be abolished; Walras held those taxes "eventually realize effects
exactly identical to a consumption tax" and so damage the economy in a way a
tax on land rent does not.[1] In *Radical Markets*, Eric Posner and E. Glen
Weyl quote Walras's justification directly: "Declaring individual land
ownership … means … thwarting the beneficial effects of free competition by
preventing the land from being used as is most advantageous for society."[2]
Posner and Weyl describe Walras as advocating **state ownership of land with
its rents returned to citizens as a "social dividend"** — a proposal they
treat, alongside [Henry George's](/wiki/henry-george/) land value tax, as a
direct ancestor of their own Common Ownership Self-Assessed Tax.[2]

Mark Blaug's standard history notes the specific mechanism Walras proposed
for the transition: **full compensation**. Existing landowners would be
bought out with government bonds, with the future rents collected by the
state used to pay the bonds' interest — a scheme resting on Ricardo's belief
that rents tend to rise as an economy grows, so the state's obligation would
shrink in relative terms over time. Blaug groups Walras with
[Philip Wicksteed](/wiki/philip-wicksteed/) as marginalists who, unlike
[John Bates Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/) in the United States, advocated
land nationalization rather than folding land analytically into capital.[3]

## Significance for Georgism

Walras matters to the Georgist case for a reason distinct from George's own
American contemporaries: he reached broadly similar land-policy conclusions
from the opposite theoretical direction, using the same mathematical
marginalist apparatus that, in Clark's hands, is often credited (or blamed)
with erasing land as a distinct category of economics. That a founder of
general equilibrium theory — the intellectual ancestor of modern mainstream
microeconomics — treated land rent as a fit and sufficient basis for public
revenue undercuts the notion that taxing land specially is a classical
relic incompatible with rigorous marginal analysis.
