## Overview

Alanna Hartzok is an American author, activist, and co-founder of Earth Rights Institute. Active in the Georgist movement since 1980, she has been a leading voice connecting Georgist land economics with **environmental justice, peace, and human rights**.

## Roles and Advocacy

Hartzok serves as **Administrative Director of the International Union for Land Value Taxation (the IU)** and coordinates the IU's team of representatives to the United Nations in consultation with **ECOSOC**, the UN Economic and Social Council — the channel through which she has carried land-value-capture and resource-rent-sharing arguments into international development and UN forums. She co-founded **Earth Rights Institute** to advance these ideas globally, and has lectured on Georgist economics in many countries. She is a recipient of the International Earth Day Award and the Radical Middle Book Award, and twice stood for the US Congress (Green Party, 2001; Democratic Party, 2014). She represents the values-driven, internationalist wing of the movement, and the earth-rights strand of the [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) debate that grounds a per-person distribution in common ownership of the commons.

## Written Work

Her book ***The Earth Belongs to Everyone*** (2008) collects roughly thirty essays arguing that capturing land and resource rent for the common good underpins a just and ecological economy — a foundational text of [ecological Georgism](/wiki/ecological-georgism/) and the [land-as-commons](/wiki/land-as-commons/) tradition. Separately, her scholarly paper **"Pennsylvania's Success with Local Property Tax Reform: The Split Rate Tax"** (*American Journal of Economics and Sociology* 56(2), 1997, pp. 205–213) is a widely cited empirical account of Pennsylvania's two-rate ([split-rate](/wiki/split-rate-taxation/)) property tax — documenting, among other findings, that Scranton and Pittsburgh maintained a land-to-building tax ratio of roughly 2-to-1 from 1913 until 1979, and tracing the extension of the two-rate option to third-class cities and school districts. That paper is cited on this wiki's [Pennsylvania](/wiki/pennsylvania/) and [Pittsburgh](/wiki/pittsburgh/) pages.
