## Overview

Thomas Robert Malthus (13 February 1766 – 29 December 1834) was an English cleric
and political economist, from 1805 professor of political economy at the East
India Company's college at Haileybury — reportedly the first such academic post in
Britain.[1] He is best known for *An Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798),
which argued that population tends to grow faster than the food supply, so that
poverty, famine, and vice act as inevitable checks on human numbers.[1] Henry
George devoted much of Book II of [*Progress and Poverty*](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)
(1879) to refuting this claim, arguing that rising population expands the division
of labor and productive power rather than simply outrunning subsistence, and that
Malthusian theory wrongly let economic and political causes of poverty off the
hook.[2]

Less well known in the population debate is Malthus's separate, foundational
contribution to **rent theory**: his 1815 pamphlet *An Inquiry into the Nature and
Progress of Rent* set out a differential theory of rent — that rent is a surplus
enabled by the scarcity of fertile land, not a cost added to prices. Malthus
there defines rent as "that portion of the value of the whole produce which
remains to the owner of the land, after all the outgoings belonging to its
cultivation, of whatever kind, have been paid, including the profits of the
capital employed," and locates its "immediate cause" in "the excess of price
above the cost of production at which raw produce sells in the market."[3] While
fertile land remains abundant, he writes, "this surplus produce, this bountiful
gift of Providence, shews itself chiefly in extraordinary high profits, and
extraordinary high wages, and appears but little in the shape of rent... nobody
of course will pay a rent to a landlord" — rent emerging only once the
"diversities of soil and situation" force cultivation onto poorer land.[3] The
pamphlet was published essentially simultaneously with (by some accounts slightly
ahead of) similar pamphlets by [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/), Robert
Torrens, and Edward West, making the four of them independent co-discoverers of
what became the classical [Law of Rent](/wiki/law-of-rent/).[3][4] Ricardo's
fuller development of the theory eclipsed Malthus's priority in most later
histories of economics.[4]

## George's Engagement with Malthus

George's quarrel with Malthus is not only over population. In Book II of
*Progress and Poverty* George shows how Malthus's population doctrine was fused
with the Ricardian rent theory Malthus himself had helped found, into what George
calls a "triple combination": "Ricardo... furnished the Malthusian theory an
additional support by calling attention to the fact that rent would increase as
the necessities of increasing population forced cultivation to less and less
productive lands... In this way was formed a triple combination, by which the
Malthusian theory has been buttressed on both sides — the previously received
doctrine of wages and the subsequently received doctrine of rent."[2] George's
target is precisely this mutually reinforcing structure: he accepts the
differential law of rent (Malthus's 1815 contribution) while rejecting the
Malthusian population premise that, joined to it, made poverty appear a law of
nature rather than a remediable effect of land monopoly.[2]
