## Overview

John Bates Clark (1847–1938) was an American economist, a founder and early president
of the American Economic Association, and the principal architect of American
**marginal-productivity theory** — the account of factor payments most fully stated in
*The Distribution of Wealth: A Theory of Wages, Interest and Profits* (Macmillan,
1899).[1] The theory holds that under competition each factor of production earns its
marginal product; in Clark's hands it unified the returns to labour and capital in a
single framework — and treated **land as a form of capital** rather than as the
categorically distinct third factor of classical economics.

For this wiki, Clark matters twice over: he is the era's most consequential academic
respondent to [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/), and he is the central figure in
the contested historiography of *why* land disappeared from economics — the question
mapped on [The Corruption of Economics narrative](/wiki/the-corruption-of-economics/).

## The George Connection — In Clark's Own Words

Clark's engagement with George was direct, public, and acknowledged. The theory's
development runs from the paper "Capital and Its Earnings" (1888) through the 1899
book,[1] and Clark's own preface credits George's wage doctrine as the stimulus:

> "It was the claim advanced by Mr. Henry George, that wages are fixed by the product
> which a man can create by tilling rentless land, that first led me to seek a method
> by which the product of labor everywhere may be disentangled..."[1]

The marginal-productivity answer generalized George's frontier-wage logic: every
factor's return, not just wages at the rentless margin, is set by marginal
contribution. In the same construction Clark absorbed land into the general capital
fund — the analytic step with the largest consequences for Georgist economics, since
the classical earned/unearned distinction (and with it the special case for taxing
[land rent](/wiki/economic-rent/)) presupposes land's separateness.

## The Two Historiographies

**Gaffney's reading.** [Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) argues the merger was
not innocent: early American economics departments were funded by landed and
corporate interests, and Clark's reframing — developed across the very years George
was a live political threat — served to dissolve the category on which George's case
rested. The full argument is in
["Neo-classical Economics as a Stratagem Against Henry George"](/wiki/gaffney-neoclassical-stratagem/)
(1994).[2] On this reading Clark is exhibit A for a discipline reshaped against a
reformer.

**The mainstream reading.** Historians of economic thought generally attribute the
two-factor consolidation to analytic development, not patronage.
[Blaug (2000)](/wiki/blaug-henry-george-rebel/) documents that the leading economists
of the day rejected the single tax on stated theoretical grounds;[3] Irving Fisher's
stock-flow capital theory soon subsumed land into the capital stock on definitional
grounds independent of George. Thomas Leonard's *History of Political Economy* study
of Clark rejects the hired-apologist reading on biographical evidence: Clark was
neither a laissez-faire apologist nor a bought man — an original theorist who
publicly rejected laissez-faire.[4] On this reading, Clark's preface is the point:
he answered George *analytically, in print, under his own name* — engagement, not
conspiracy.

The wiki takes no position between them; both are carried, attributed, on the
[narrative page](/wiki/the-corruption-of-economics/). What neither side disputes:
George mattered to the theory's construction, land vanished as a category for most
of a century, and its modern empirical return
([Land is Back](/wiki/bonnet-land-is-back/);
[Rognlie](/wiki/rognlie-capital-share/)) has made the stakes of Clark's move visible
again.

## Legacy

Clark's marginal-productivity framework became the backbone of twentieth-century
distribution theory, and his name marks the profession's most prestigious early-career
honor: the American Economic Association's **John Bates Clark Medal**, awarded to the
outstanding American economist under forty.[5] Within the Georgist literature his
legacy is precisely the controversy above — the economist whose answer to George
set the terms on which George was forgotten, whatever the motive.
