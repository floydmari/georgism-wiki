## Overview

John Bates Clark (1847–1938) was an American economist, a founder and long-serving president of
the American Economic Association, and one of the principal architects of American neoclassical
**marginal-productivity theory**, most fully set out in his book *The Distribution of Wealth*
(1899). Clark's framework analysed the returns to labour, capital, and land within a single
unified theory of factor payments determined by marginal productivity.

## Gaffney's Argument: Merging Land into Capital

According to economist **[Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/)**, Clark's treatment of land as
simply one form of "capital" — rather than as a categorically distinct factor of production, as
the classical economists and Henry George had held — was the pivotal analytical move by which
late-19th-century neoclassical economics undercut the intellectual basis for George's
[single tax](/wiki/single-tax/). In Gaffney's account, once land was no longer treated as a
separate factor, the classical distinction between "earned" income (from labour and capital) and
"unearned" income (land rent) lost its analytical grounding, and with it the case for taxing land
rent specifically.[1] Gaffney frames this as part of a broader argument that early American
economics departments, funded in part by landed and corporate interests, reshaped the discipline
partly in response to the political threat posed by George's single-tax movement — a historical
thesis that remains contested among historians of economic thought even as the underlying facts
about land's disappearance from the standard factors of production are widely acknowledged.[1]
