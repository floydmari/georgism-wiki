## Overview

Arthur Cecil Pigou was an English economist who succeeded [Alfred Marshall](/wiki/alfred-marshall/) as Professor of Political Economy at the University of Cambridge in 1908, holding the chair until 1943. His most influential book, *The Economics of Welfare* (1920, an expansion of the 1912 *Wealth and Welfare*), formalized the idea that private and social costs can diverge and proposed correcting that gap with a tax — now called the "Pigouvian tax" — the framework underlying most modern environmental and carbon-pricing policy (see [Pigouvian Taxation](/wiki/pigouvian-taxation/)). In 1909 Pigou also wrote an essay favoring land value taxation, read at the time as support for [David Lloyd George](/wiki/david-lloyd-george/)'s [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/); biographers credit Marshall's own writing on land taxation with shaping Pigou's later, more general theory of taxing negative externalities.

Pigou's welfare-economics program — distinguishing "marginal private net product" from "marginal social net product" — gave 20th-century economics its standard vocabulary for market failure and correction, though Ronald Coase later challenged the tax-based remedy by arguing that well-defined property rights and bargaining could resolve many externality problems without government intervention.
