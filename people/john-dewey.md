## Overview

**John Dewey** (October 20, 1859 – June 1, 1952) was one of the most influential American philosophers, psychologists, and educational reformers of the first half of the twentieth century, a founder of pragmatism who taught at the University of Michigan, the University of Chicago, and, from 1905 to 1929, Columbia University.[1] Beyond philosophy and education, Dewey was a lifelong, outspoken admirer of **[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)**, serving as honorary president of the **[Henry George School](/wiki/henry-george-school/)** of Social Science in New York and writing an appreciation of George that was later used as the introduction to the Robert Schalkenbach Foundation's edition of *Significant Paragraphs from Progress and Poverty*, in which Dewey called George "one of the great names among the world's social philosophers."[2] Dewey credited *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* (1879) with an unusually wide readership for a work of political economy.

Historian Christopher England's 2018 study situates this admiration within Dewey's broader democratic theory, arguing that Dewey treated the "socialization" of land values along Georgist lines as a prerequisite for a genuinely democratic public.[3]
