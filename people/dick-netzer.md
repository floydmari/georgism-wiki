## Overview

Dick Netzer (May 14, 1928 – May 7, 2008) was an American public-finance economist who spent most of his career at [New York University](/wiki/new-york-city/), where he served as dean of the Graduate School of Public Administration (now the Robert F. Wagner Graduate School of Public Service).[1][2] He earned his Ph.D. in economics from Harvard and became one of the leading American specialists on state and local government finance, publishing widely on property taxation and municipal fiscal policy; he also helped New York City navigate its 1970s fiscal crisis.[1][2] His best-known work, *Economics of the Property Tax* (Brookings Institution, 1966), is the canonical statement of the **traditional (excise-tax) view** of property-tax incidence — that a local property tax is borne by housing consumers through higher prices, since capital can migrate out of a high-tax jurisdiction until after-tax returns equalize.[3]

Netzer's traditional view is one of the three rival incidence theories that [George R. Zodrow](/wiki/zodrow-three-views/)'s 2001 survey "A Room with Three Views" organizes the property-tax-incidence literature around, alongside the "new"/capital-tax view and the benefit view — Zodrow dates the traditional view's modern statement to Herbert Simon (1943) and Netzer (1966).[3] Andrew Haughwout's 2001 computable general-equilibrium study of land taxation in New York City, part of the wiki's cluster on [site-value CGE simulations](/wiki/site-value-ge-simulations/), was prepared for a conference held in Netzer's honor, reflecting his standing in the property-tax-economics field.[4]
