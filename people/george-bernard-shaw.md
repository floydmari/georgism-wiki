## Overview

**George Bernard Shaw** (1856–1950), the Irish playwright and critic, dated his conversion to economics and socialism to September 5, 1882, when he attended a London meeting addressed by **[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)** and then read *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*, an encounter he later said "changed the whole current of my life."[1] Shaw went on to study Marx before joining the **Fabian Society** in 1884, on whose executive committee he served for 27 years.[1]

Historian of economic thought Mark Blaug credits Shaw, alongside **Sidney Webb**, with fusing Ricardo's and George's rent theory — reworked through the marginal-utility revolution — into the distinctive analytical framework of Fabian socialism, notably in the landmark essay collection *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (1889).[2]

## Significance

Shaw's trajectory illustrates one of the clearest cultural conduits by which George's land-value ideas entered British political life: George's American critique of land monopoly, carried by his London speaking tours, helped radicalize a generation of British socialists who then recast it within a home-grown Fabian, gradualist program rather than George's own single-tax movement.
