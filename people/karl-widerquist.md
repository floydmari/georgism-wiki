## Overview

Karl Widerquist is a political theorist and one of the basic-income movement's most
prolific scholars — then at Georgetown University Qatar, and one of the most prominent
academic voices in the Basic Income Earth Network (BIEN).[1] For this wiki he matters
chiefly as co-editor (with philosophy professor Michael W. Howard of the University of
Maine) of *Alaska's Permanent Fund Dividend: Examining Its Suitability as a Model*
(Palgrave Macmillan, 2012) — the standard scholarly examination of the
[Alaska Permanent Fund](/wiki/alaska-permanent-fund/) dividend as a template for a
[citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/), and a key source for the
political-durability lesson carried on
[resource-rent dividends work](/wiki/resource-rent-dividends-work/).[1] He has also
written for popular Georgist outlets on exporting the Alaska model.[2]

## The Volume He Edited

Widerquist and Howard's book is, per the wiki's own research summary of it, "the
closest thing to a peer-reviewed academic audit" of the PFD, gathering economists,
historians, and philosophers with direct access to Alaska's fiscal and legislative
record — including economist Scott Goldsmith, whose chapter finds Alaska moved from the
US state with the *most* unequal income distribution in the early 1980s to one of the
*most equal* over the dividend's first two decades.[1] The volume is candid about the
limits of generalizing from Alaska: its oil-royalty revenue is a depleting windfall
requiring a permanent invested fund, a structure a genuine land-value-based dividend
would not need since site rent does not deplete; and the dividend itself, while
politically durable for decades, is set annually by ordinary statute rather than
constitutionally guaranteed — a limit tested in 2016, when Governor Bill Walker's
line-item veto cut that year's payout to roughly half the statutory formula, a cut the
Alaska Supreme Court upheld.[1] A companion volume the same two editors produced,
*Exporting the Alaska Model: Adapting the Permanent Fund Dividend for Reform around the
World* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2012), extends the analysis to other jurisdictions,
including a chapter on Vermont by Gary Flomenhoft.[1]
