## Overview

**John Muellbauer** (born 17 July 1944) is a British economist, a Senior Research Fellow of Nuffield College and Professor of Economics at the University of Oxford, and a Senior Fellow of the Institute for New Economic Thinking (INET) at the Oxford Martin School.[1] He is a Fellow of the British Academy, the Econometric Society, and the European Economic Association. Trained at Cambridge and UC Berkeley, he held posts at Birkbeck College London and Warwick before joining Nuffield College in 1981.[1] His most cited work is the 1980 paper with Angus Deaton, "An Almost Ideal Demand System" (*American Economic Review*), a foundational contribution to applied consumer-demand theory later recognized among the journal's top-twenty papers of its first century.[1]

From the late 1980s Muellbauer became one of the first economists to study systematically how housing markets, household balance sheets, and credit constraints interact with the wider macroeconomy — work that eventually brought him into direct engagement with land and property taxation.[1] He wrote the foreword to Ryan-Collins, Lloyd, and Macfarlane's *[Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing](/wiki/rethinking-economics-land-housing/)* (2017), and the book cites his research (with Blöchliger) on the finding that land/property taxes reduce house-price volatility.[2] In a 2023 INET Oxford working paper, Muellbauer proposed a **"green land value tax"** for the UK, arguing that over 70% of the value of the average UK home is land value — a higher share, and one that has risen more since the 1980s, than in any other G7 country — and in 2025 he put forward a two-part reform combining a wealth-style surcharge on the top Council Tax bands with a land value tax on farmland, forestry, and unoccupied land above £40,000 per hectare.[3]
