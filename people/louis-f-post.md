## Overview

**Louis Freeland Post** (1849–1928) was the single-tax movement's leading
journalist-organiser after [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) himself, and the most
senior public official the movement ever produced. A New Jersey-born lawyer and
journalist, he was converted to George's ideas in the 1880s and was active in George's
1886 [United Labor Party](/wiki/single-tax/) campaign for Mayor of New York. For the next
four decades he was George's ablest defender in print and on the platform — and, late in
life, the federal officer who single-handedly stopped the mass deportations of the first
Red Scare.

## The Single-Tax Journalist

Post's main instrument was **The Public**, a national weekly he edited from **1898 to
1913**, first in Chicago — the movement's paper of record, mixing single-tax advocacy with
progressive politics, free trade, and civil-liberties causes. He was George's stand-in in
set-piece debates, most famously the **1903 Cooper Union debate against
[John Bates Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)** on the single tax, and he worked the
[Joseph Fels](/wiki/joseph-fels/) Lecture Bureau circuit that carried the argument across
the country.[1] Through *The Public* Post kept George's programme in front of a
progressive-era audience long after George's death in 1897.

## The Palmer-Raid Stand

Post's historical significance rests on what he did as **Assistant Secretary of Labor**
under Woodrow Wilson. In the winter of 1919–20, Attorney General **A. Mitchell Palmer** and
a young **J. Edgar Hoover** rounded up thousands of suspected radicals for deportation in
the **Palmer Raids**. Because deportation was an administrative
matter run through the Labor Department's Bureau of Immigration, the cases landed on Post's
desk — and in **March 1920**, with Secretary William B. Wilson absent, Post became **acting
Secretary** and the official responsible for them.

Applying ordinary standards of evidence and due process, Post reviewed the backlog of
roughly **1,600 cases and dismissed about 71% of them**, cancelling well over 1,500
deportation warrants as unlawfully obtained — arrests without warrants, coerced
confessions, denial of counsel.[2] It was the decisive act that broke the Raids' momentum.
Palmer's congressional allies retaliated: on **15 April 1920**, Representative **Homer Hoch**
of Kansas moved to **impeach** Post for abuse of power. The move backfired. Testifying
before the House Rules Committee on **7–8 May 1920**, Post — by all accounts a formidable
and caustic speaker — so thoroughly demolished the case against him, and so effectively
exposed Palmer's abuses, that the impeachment collapsed and Palmer's own credibility began
to unravel.[2] A single-taxer's insistence on due process had checked one of the worst
civil-liberties episodes in American history.

## *The Deportations Delirium of Nineteen-Twenty*

Post told the story himself in **_The Deportations Delirium of Nineteen-Twenty_** (1923),
part memoir and part indictment of the Raids — a first-hand account by the official at their
centre. Published before 1931, it is in the public domain and is a priority for the wiki's
[reading room](/wiki/agrarian-justice/) as a `texts/` transcription.

## Legacy

Post is the clearest case of Georgist principle carried into high office. He held that the
same commitment to justice that made land monopoly intolerable made the arbitrary
deportation of immigrants intolerable too — and he had the nerve to act on it against a
popular Attorney General and the threat of impeachment. For a movement often caricatured as
a single-issue tax crusade, Post is evidence of its broader progressive and civil-libertarian
character.
