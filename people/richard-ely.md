## Overview

Richard T. Ely (1854–1943) was a co-founder of the American Economic Association
(1885, its secretary until 1892 and president 1899–1901) and, at Wisconsin, founder
of the **Institute for Research in Land Economics and Public Utilities** (1920) and
its journal, today's *Land Economics*.[1] He engaged
[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) critically from the 1880s — George published a
direct reply to Ely on compensation of landowners in 1887[2] — arguing that land
values do not reliably rise everywhere and that separating land from improvement
value is impractical, an early statement of the
[assessment objection](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/).[1]

In [Gaffney and Harrison's *The Corruption of Economics*](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/),
Ely is the institutional centerpiece of the argument that patron-funded academic
economics was reshaped to bury the land question (Ch. 5 discusses Ely's institute
and its funding); the wiki carries that as **Gaffney's documented argument, not
settled history** — see [the corruption narrative](/wiki/the-corruption-of-economics/)
for the counter-reading.
