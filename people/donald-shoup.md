## Overview

Donald C. Shoup (1938 – February 6, 2025) was a distinguished professor of urban
planning at the **University of California, Los Angeles**, where he taught from
1974 until his death.[1][2] He is best known for *The High Cost of Free Parking*
(2005, revised 2011), which argued that municipal minimum-parking requirements and
zero-priced curb parking are effectively a hidden subsidy that inflates the cost of
land and housing and encourages excess driving — an argument that treats parking
space as a form of underpriced **land rent** rather than a free amenity.[3][4] His
proposed remedy, **parking benefit districts**, charges market rates for curb
parking and dedicates the revenue to visible local improvements in the same
metered area — a value-capture mechanism analogous to Georgist land rent
capture.[4]

Separately, and earlier, Shoup's 1980 paper "Financing Public Investment by
Deferred Special Assessment" proposed that neighborhood public investments (and,
by extension, land value increases) be recovered through a special assessment
whose **payment is deferred**, accruing as a lien against the property, until the
property is sold or transferred — an answer to the standard objection that a land
value tax would force asset-rich, cash-poor owners (the "little old lady on a
high-value site") to sell.[5] Fred Harrison's *The Power in the Land* cites this
deferred-assessment model directly in its own discussion of that objection.[5]
