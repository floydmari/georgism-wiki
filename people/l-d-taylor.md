## Overview

Louis Denison ("L.D.") Taylor was a "Single Tax" advocate elected mayor of [Vancouver](/wiki/vancouver/) in multiple non-consecutive terms between 1910 and the mid-1930s, presiding over the city's shift to taxing land value alone while exempting buildings from municipal property tax.[1] Interviewed in New York in September 1911, Taylor summarized the policy's logic: "My theory of a prosperous community is one in which nothing that a man creates through his own energy alone is taxed, but all those things are taxed which are made by nature for the use of the people as a whole."[1] Under his tenure, Vancouver drew international attention as one of the clearest city-scale applications of single-tax ideas by an English-speaking government, and by 1911 land value reportedly supplied close to four-fifths of the city's municipal tax revenue.[1]
