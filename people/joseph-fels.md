## Overview

Joseph Fels (December 16, 1853 – February 22, 1914) was a Philadelphia soap
manufacturer who became the single-tax movement's great financier. Born in Halifax
County, Virginia, to German-Jewish immigrants and raised in Baltimore, he took
control of a Philadelphia soap firm by 1876 and made his fortune with the
**Fels-Naptha** brand he developed in 1894.[2] He had become an adherent of
[Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) by around 1890, and thereafter devoted much of
his wealth to putting George's program into practice.[1][2]

Fels's giving ran on two tracks. First, he **bankrolled the movement's
organization**: the **Joseph Fels Fund** (the Fels Fund Commission of America,
organized in 1909) financed state single-tax campaigns and the Philadelphia
lecture bureau that employed Louis F. Post, Scott Nearing, and Frederic Howe
([Gaffney's account](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/), essay Ch. 3) —
the reason the movement's 1909–1916 peak, from the [People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/)
era through the western US initiative fights, had professional organization behind
it.[1] Second, he **helped finance the practical experiments**: the American
[single-tax colonies](/wiki/single-tax-colonies/) at Fairhope, Alabama (1894) and
Arden, Delaware (1900), and a series of English land colonies — notably at Mayland
in Essex (1906), whose cottages were designed by the architect Charles Holden.[2]
He also backed the Jewish Territorialist Organization's search for a homeland to be
founded on Georgist land principles.[2] After his death from influenza in 1914 his
widow, Mary Fels, continued the work.[2]
