## Overview

Christopher William England is a historian of 19th- and 20th-century U.S. political economy, intellectual history, and social movements whose work provides key historical and political-economy scholarship on the [single-tax](/wiki/single-tax/) and [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) movements. He earned his Ph.D. in history from Georgetown University in 2015, has taught at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, Stanford University, and Georgetown University, and joined Towson University's history department in 2023, where he is an Assistant Teaching Professor.[7][8] He is the author of *Land and Liberty: Henry George and the Crafting of Modern Liberalism* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2023), a comprehensive modern history of the U.S. single-tax movement,[1] and of "Land Value Taxation in Vancouver: Rent-Seeking and the Tax Revolt" (*American Journal of Economics and Sociology*, 2018), a study of how organized property-owner resistance reversed Vancouver's seven-decade land-only municipal tax regime.[4] He should not be confused with **Richard W. England**, the University of New Hampshire economist who co-authored the 2005 Dover, New Hampshire LVT distribution study cited elsewhere on this wiki (see below). Together, Christopher England's works anchor the wiki's historical coverage of single-tax movements in both American and Canadian contexts.

## The 2023 Book: *Land and Liberty*

England's *Land and Liberty: Henry George and the Crafting of Modern Liberalism* (Johns Hopkins University Press, published February 14, 2023) is cited across this wiki as the definitive modern history of the U.S. single-tax movement — the publisher's own tagline describes it as "a comprehensive history of Henry George and the single tax movement."[1] Note on the subtitle: the book is sometimes cited (including elsewhere on this wiki) as *Henry George and the Single Tax Movement*, but the published subtitle is *Henry George and the Crafting of Modern Liberalism*; the single-tax-movement phrasing comes from England's 2015 Georgetown doctoral dissertation, *Land and Liberty: Henry George, The Single Tax Movement, and the Origins of 20th Century Liberalism*.[1][9]

According to the publisher's description, the book is "the first comprehensive account of this important movement to nationalize land and expropriate rent," drawing on the private papers of a network of single-tax activists. Its narrative runs from concerns about rising urban rents in the 1870s to the New Deal policies that extended public control over land, natural resources, and housing, arguing that Georgism served as a catalyst for democratizing reforms and that George's calls for collective rights to land and natural resources "helped push liberalism leftward."[1] The book is used on this wiki for:

- The context of the [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/) and the constitutional crisis it triggered in Britain, situating the British land-taxation episode within the broader transatlantic single-tax movement.[2]
- The history of the [single-tax colonies](/wiki/single-tax-colonies/) — Fairhope, Alabama and Arden, Delaware — as intentional communities founded around 1900 to demonstrate Henry George's ideas in practice.[3]

## The 2018 Vancouver Study

England's 2018 paper in the *American Journal of Economics and Sociology* — "Land Value Taxation in Vancouver: Rent-Seeking and the Tax Revolt" (77(1): 59–94) — reconstructs [Vancouver](/wiki/vancouver/)'s tax rolls across the full arc of its land-only municipal taxation regime, from its 1910 introduction under mayor [L.D. Taylor](/wiki/l-d-taylor/) to the complete phasing-out of the improvement exemption in 1984.[4]

### The Phase-Out Timeline

England documents that Vancouver's exemption of improvements was eroded in stages: buildings were assessed at zero percent of the land rate through the policy's early years, taxed at 50 percent of the land rate from 1919 to 1969, raised to 75 percent from 1969 to 1984, and finally brought to full parity with land after 1984.[4] This gradual rollback — spanning roughly six and a half decades — is a central empirical contribution of the paper, providing the detailed timeline cited on this wiki's [Vancouver](/wiki/vancouver/) page.

### The Collective-Action Explanation

England argues the retreat is best explained by Mancur Olson's logic of collective action: property owners, as a comparatively small and well-organized group with a direct financial stake in tax policy, were able to out-organize the more diffuse group of renters and prospective buyers who benefited from land-only taxation. Proponents of restoring the improvement tax initially framed their case in "ability to pay" terms, but England contends they ultimately revealed more narrowly self-interested motives, producing a decades-long "tax revolt" that shifted the burden back onto buildings — and, over time, onto tenants — as home-ownership rates rose.[4]

### The Transitional-Gains-Trap Framing

This wiki's [transitional gains trap](/wiki/transitional-gains-trap/) page connects England's Vancouver case to Gordon Tullock's concept, suggesting that land-value tax benefits and burdens became embedded in property values in ways that shaped political resistance to the policy.[5] That link should be read as an interpretive bridge drawn on this wiki rather than an established feature of England's paper: England's **published abstract frames the rollback entirely through Mancur Olson's model of collective action and rent-seeking**, and does not mention Tullock or the transitional-gains-trap concept. Whether England explicitly invokes the Tullockian framework in the full (paywalled) text could not be confirmed from a primary source this session (see the verification note below). The underlying connection to [tax capitalization](/wiki/tax-capitalization/) — LVT benefits and burdens capitalizing into property values — is conceptually sound in its own right regardless of England's precise wording.

The published abstract (verified this session against the Wiley and IDEAS/RePEc listings) directly confirms the core claims above: that early-20th-century Vancouver "obtained international acclaim as a 'single tax' city," that the system "was scaled back and ultimately ended in 1984," that England explains the property tax revolt "with Mancur Olson's model of collective action" in which better-organized property owners prevailed "over the interests of tenants and prospective buyers," and that revolt rhetoric "articulated in the progressive language of ability to pay... collapsed into explicit arguments for rent-seeking" when challenged.[4]

## Not the Author of the 2005 Dover Study

An earlier version of this page attributed to Christopher England the 2005 *National Tax Journal* study of a revenue-neutral shift from a uniform property tax to a two-rate (land-favoring) tax in Dover, New Hampshire. That attribution was an error of author identity: the study — "Assessing the Distributive Impact of a Revenue-Neutral Shift from a Uniform Property Tax to a Two-Rate Property Tax with a Uniform Credit," *National Tax Journal* 58(2): 247–260 — is by **Richard W. England**, an economist at the University of New Hampshire, and Min Qiang Zhao.[6] Richard W. England is a different, unrelated scholar; Christopher England is a historian and did not co-author it. The Dover study, and its contestation by Bowman & Bell (2008) on Roanoke, VA data, are documented on this wiki's [England & Zhao — LVT Distribution](/wiki/england-zhao-lvt-distribution/) and [Bowman & Bell — LVT Distribution](/wiki/bowman-bell-lvt-distribution/) research pages, which attribute the paper correctly.

## Significance for the Wiki

England's work contributes to the wiki's coverage in two distinct ways:

1. **Historical narrative**: *Land and Liberty* (2023) supplies the authoritative modern history of the U.S. single-tax movement, grounding the wiki's accounts of [single-tax colonies](/wiki/single-tax-colonies/) and the transatlantic context of the [1909 People's Budget](/wiki/1909-peoples-budget/).[1][2][3]
2. **Political economy of LVT rollback**: The 2018 Vancouver study provides the detailed phase-out timeline and the Olsonian collective-action explanation for why land-only taxation was politically reversed — a key case study for understanding the political sustainability of LVT, not just its economic effects.[4]
