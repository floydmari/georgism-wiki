## Overview

Lawson Purdy (c. 1863 - 1959) was an American tax administrator and Georgist reformer, converted, like many civic reformers of his generation, by [Henry George's](/wiki/henry-george/) single-tax doctrine. He served as secretary of the New York Tax Reform Association (1896-1906) and then as president of the New York City Department of Taxes and Assessments (1906-1917), where his signature achievement was securing legislation requiring public assessment rolls (1902) and, from 1906, the separate listing of land value and total (land-plus-improvement) value for every assessed property in the city — a practical administrative step toward taxing land value distinctly, short of the full [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) itself. He was also a leading figure in the National Tax Association in its founding decade, and later directed the New York Charity Organization Society (1918-1933).

## Georgist Context

Gaffney and Harrison's ***[The Corruption of Economics](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/)*** names Purdy among the wave of Georgist-influenced officials and campaigners active around the turn of the twentieth century, alongside figures such as [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/) and [Louis F. Post](/wiki/louis-f-post/) — part of the movement's push to embed land-value assessment inside mainstream US tax administration even as academic economists such as [E.R.A. Seligman](/wiki/era-seligman/) worked to blunt the single-tax movement's proposals.
