## Overview

Ronald Banks was a British Georgist writer and analyst best known as editor of *Costing the Earth* (Shepheard-Walwyn, 1989), a study attempting to value the land and natural resources of Britain — described by its publisher as the first such comprehensive assessment since the Domesday Book.[1] Beyond editing the volume he contributed its Chapter 2, "Terra Incognita" (the opening chapter, "Ecology, Politics and the Nature of Rent," is by [Fred Harrison](/wiki/fred-harrison/)).[1] Banks remained an active figure in UK land-reform circles for decades: he served as **Chair of Trustees of the Land Research Trust**, under whose imprint he authored a series of policy papers around 2015–2016 (e.g. "Would Exiting the EU be Disastrous for Britain?", "Debt and the Fiscal Crisis: the Rentenmark lesson," and "Transforming the Nation's Finances").[3] Fred Harrison, in the preface to *[Boom Bust](/wiki/harrison-boom-bust/)* (2005), acknowledges Banks and credits him with calculating the estimate that shifting UK public revenue from conventional taxes onto land rent could unlock roughly **£880 billion** in additional national output (around £15,000 per person).[2] [CITATION NEEDED: Banks's birth/death dates could not be verified in this research pass — no obituary or biographical record with dates was located; his active-career span (1989 *Costing the Earth* through ~2016 Land Research Trust papers) is documented, but exact dates remain **(dates unverified)**.]

## *Costing the Earth* (1989)

*Costing the Earth*, edited by Banks, argued that a wide range of economic and environmental problems — from urban decay to resource depletion — trace back to the mispricing of land and natural resources, and presented an attempt to place a systematic value on Britain's land and natural-resource base.[1] The book sits within the same UK Georgist tradition as [Fred Harrison](/wiki/fred-harrison/)'s work and the broader case that conventional taxation obscures a large pool of collectible economic rent.

## The £880bn Estimate

Harrison cites Banks's calculation alongside [Tideman and Plassmann's](/wiki/tideman-plassmann-losses-of-nations/) independent estimate of roughly $7 trillion in deadweight losses from conventional taxation across the G7, using both figures to argue that the [deadweight loss](/wiki/deadweight-loss/) of taxing labour and capital instead of land rent is large enough to be a first-order policy question rather than a marginal efficiency footnote.[2] This estimate is one data point in the broader Georgist argument that land rent could fund a substantial share of public revenue; as with any such macro-level fiscal estimate, the specific methodology and assumptions behind Banks's £880bn figure have not been independently verified for this page.
