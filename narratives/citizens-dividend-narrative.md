## Core Claim

The rent of land and natural resources is income that no individual produced — so it
belongs to everyone. Collected publicly and paid out as an equal
[citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/), it becomes the one basic income that
requires taxing nobody's work: "your share of what we own together." The narrative's
power is that it converts an abstract tax argument into a **check with your name on it**.

## Who Promotes It

- [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) argued rent should be the common fund (he favored
  public spending of it; the per-capita dividend is a later refinement of the same
  premise).[1]
- [Alanna Hartzok](/wiki/alanna-hartzok/) and the earth-rights strand frame it as each
  person's birthright share; [geolibertarians](/wiki/geolibertarianism/) frame it as the
  only redistribution consistent with self-ownership.
- [Karl Widerquist](/wiki/karl-widerquist/) and Michael Howard connected it to the
  modern basic-income movement through their Alaska volumes.[2]
- In Canada, [Common Wealth Canada](/wiki/common-wealth-canada/) (founded by
  [Floyd Marinescu](/wiki/floyd-marinescu/)) proposes a national
  [Common Wealth Fund](/wiki/common-wealth-fund/) paying dividends from
  [Canada's](/wiki/canada/) land and resource rents.[3]

## Research That Supports It

- **The Alaska proof of concept** — the strongest evidence base in this narrative's
  portfolio ([resource-rent dividends work](/wiki/resource-rent-dividends-work/), 5
  supporting studies): the [Permanent Fund Dividend](/wiki/alaska-permanent-fund/) has
  paid every resident since 1982, survived every administration, and remains fiercely
  defended across party lines.
- [Jones & Marinescu (2022)](/wiki/jones-marinescu-alaska-pfd/): no detectable fall in
  aggregate employment — the canonical answer to "people will stop working."[4]
- [Goldsmith (2002)](/wiki/goldsmith-alaska-pfd-wealth-distribution/) documents the
  poverty and distribution effects two decades in.[5]
- [Widerquist & Howard (2012)](/wiki/widerquist-howard-pfd/) assess exportability;
  [Segal (2011)](/wiki/segal-resource-dividend/) estimates a global resource dividend
  could substantially cut world poverty; [Hartwick's rule](/wiki/hartwick-rule/) supplies
  the intergenerational logic for converting resource rents into permanent income.
- The ceiling question — how big could a dividend be — rests on the
  [land-rent scale evidence](/wiki/land-rent-could-fund-government/) (Larson's ~$23T US
  land stock; [CWC's](/wiki/natural-common-wealth-economic-rent-canada/) ~$241B/yr
  collectible Canadian rents).

## Research That Challenges It — or Is Missing

- **The flagship case is oil rent, not location rent.** No jurisdiction has run a
  *land*-rent dividend at scale; extrapolating from Alaska's oil to urban land involves
  a genuine leap the page must not blur.
- **Rent windfalls can corrode governance.**
  [Martinez's Colombia evidence](/wiki/martinez-colombia-resource-rents/) — wired as
  challenged_by on the dividends outcome — shows resource windfalls weakening local tax
  effort and accountability; a dividend design has to answer it (Alaska's answer:
  pay the money out, don't let governments spend it quietly).
- **Norway is not a dividend.** The leading resource-fund state
  ([Norway](/wiki/norway/)) deliberately budgets its fund rather than paying citizens —
  the save-vs-share design choice the narrative must argue for, not assume.

## Counter-Arguments and Georgist Responses

- **"People will work less."** Response: the best causal study of a permanent universal
  dividend ([Jones & Marinescu](/wiki/jones-marinescu-alaska-pfd/)) found no aggregate
  employment decline — a dividend from rent is not a wage tax, so it does not penalize
  the next hour of work.
- **"There isn't enough rent."** Response: partly conceded — the wiki grades
  [revenue sufficiency](/wiki/land-rent-could-fund-government/) as contested and the
  [objection](/wiki/lvt-not-enough-revenue/) is steelmanned; but a *meaningful* dividend
  needs only a fraction of measured rents, and Alaska pays real money from one resource
  in one state.
- **"It's welfare by another name."** Response: the geolibertarian framing answers on
  the right ("compensation for exclusion from the commons, not a transfer") while the
  common-wealth framing answers on the left ("a property right, not charity") — the
  narrative's unusual breadth is that both work.

## Historical Examples

- **[Alaska](/wiki/alaska/)** — four decades of universal dividends from oil rent; the
  cross-partisan durability is the anchor fact.
- **[Norway](/wiki/norway/)** — the fund-without-dividend contrast case.
- **[Canada](/wiki/canada/)** — the live proposal: CWC's fund design over provincial
  resource and land rents, in the tradition of Alberta's heritage fund.

## How to Deploy It

Strongest with UBI and tech audiences, and in resource provinces. Lead with Alaska —
concrete, durable, popular, and empirically studied — then generalize to land. Use the
"check with your name on it" frame to make rent capture *visible* (the political-economy
lesson of Alaska: dividends create their own constituency, which answers the
transitional-gains politics that killed earlier land taxes). Concede the oil-to-land
leap explicitly; it reads as honesty, and the scale numbers carry the rest.
