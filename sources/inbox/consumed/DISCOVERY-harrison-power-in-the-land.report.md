## Summary
Harrison's central thesis: land speculation is the primary cause of the 18-year business cycle. The "scissors mechanism" — diverging returns to land (rising) vs. capital (falling) — periodically destabilizes industrial economies. The book marshals evidence from the UK, USA, Japan, and Australia, and advocates a 100% tax on the annual rental value of land (the Single Tax) as the remedy. It critiques Adam Smith for shielding landlords, Marx for misdiagnosing the problem, and modern economists (Keynes, Samuelson, Friedman) for neglecting land as a distinct factor of production.

### CONCEPTS

- **18-year land value cycle** · concept · Central thesis: land values peak approximately every 18 years, triggering recessions · Ch. 5–12, throughout
- **Scissors mechanism** · concept · Diverging returns to land (rising) vs. capital (falling) that periodically cuts economic growth · Ch. 6, pp.72–3
- **Land speculation** · concept · Withholding land from use to capture future capital gains; primary cause of recessions per Harrison · Ch. 5, pp.63–9
- **Economic rent** · concept · Surplus above returns to labour and capital; the fund that should be taxed for public revenue · Ch. 2, p.27; throughout
- **Land value taxation / Single Tax** · concept · 100% tax on annual rental value of all land, offset by reduction in other taxes · Ch. 1, p.17; Ch. 15, throughout
- **Land monopoly** · concept · Private appropriation of land value; "the mother of all monopolies" (Churchill) · Ch. 1, pp.11–14; throughout
- **Site value rating (SVR)** · concept · Property tax based on unimproved land value only; contrasted with net annual value (NAV) · Ch. 15, pp.195–7
- **Net annual value (NAV)** · concept · Property tax on both land and buildings; discourages construction · Ch. 15, pp.195–7
- **Kondratieff long waves** · concept · 50-year cycles in capitalist economies; critiqued as lacking causal explanation · Ch. 1, p.19
- **Kuznets cycles** · concept · 20-year cycles in population, migration, house building · Ch. 5, p.64
- **Under-consumption thesis** · concept · Robert Owen's theory that capitalism creates gap between consumption and production · Ch. 4, p.49; Ch. 5, p.61
- **Over-production thesis** · concept · Marxist theory that excess goods relative to demand causes recessions · Ch. 5, p.62
- **Say's Law** · concept · Supply creates its own demand; cited by Reaganomics proponents · Ch. 9, p.127
- **Supply-side economics / Reaganomics** · concept · Tax cuts to stimulate investment; Harrison argues benefits captured by land monopolists · Ch. 1, p.17; Ch. 9, pp.125–7
- **Laffer Curve** · concept · Based on principle that Henry George's tax canons were "the essence" of supply-side · Ch. 1, p.17

### OBJECTIONS

- **"Single Tax cannot raise enough revenue"** · objection · Samuelson claimed rent was only ~5% of GNP; Harrison argues it exceeds 30% · Ch. 15, pp.200–7
- **"Land tax would destroy land value and eliminate price signals"** · objection · Hallett claimed tax would reduce land value to zero; Harrison responds rental value persists · Ch. 16, p.211
- **"Isolating economic rent is impossible"** · objection · Lipsey's claim; rebutted by Denmark, Johannesburg, Pittsburgh evidence · Ch. 16, p.213
- **"Land tax would eliminate incentive for development"** · objection · Hoyt argued developers need land value gains; rebutted by competitive market analysis · Ch. 15, pp.197–8
- **"Little old lady on high-value site"** · objection · Classic objection to land tax; solved by deferred assessments (Shoup model) · Ch. 17, pp.224–6
- **"Land speculation is not a significant problem"** · objection · RICS official position; contradicted by extensive evidence throughout book · Ch. 22, p.298
- **"Land is no different from other capital goods"** · objection · Friedman/IEA position; Harrison argues land is fixed in supply, imperishable, non-reproducible · Ch. 22, p.297
- **"OPEC caused the 1970s recession"** · objection · Harrison shows downturns preceded oil price rises in both 1973 and 1979 · Ch. 1, p.13; Ch. 12, p.167; Ch. 19, pp.247–8
- **"Wages/unions caused profit decline"** · objection · Burns/Thatcher thesis; Harrison shows post-tax wage share was actually declining · Ch. 6, Table 6:V; Ch. 21, p.274
- **"18-year cycles have ceased" (Hoyt)** · objection · Hoyt declared cycles ended post-1933; Harrison shows 1955–73 cycle operated · Ch. 8, p.109
- **"Socialism/nationalization solves the land problem"** · objection · Examined via Yugoslavia, Israel, Sweden, UK Labour; all failed · Ch. 13–14
- **"Land tax is socialism/communism"** · objection · Both left and right accused George; Harrison shows it's a third model · Ch. 15, pp.193–4; Ch. 22, pp.294–5
- **"Marx's theory of revolution is the answer"** · objection · Marx acknowledged land monopoly as basis of capital monopoly but prescribed wrong solution · Ch. 22, pp.302–3
- **"Interest rates determine building cycles"** · objection · UK/US evidence shows housing cycles independent of interest rate movements · Ch. 7, pp.91–2
- **"Over-production causes recessions"** · objection · Marxist thesis; Harrison argues people were hungry and under-consuming, not over-producing · Ch. 5, p.62
- **"Under-consumption causes recessions"** · objection · Owen/Engels thesis; Harrison argues demand existed but was blocked by land costs · Ch. 5, p.61
- **"Keynesian demand management prevents recessions"** · objection · Failed in 1970s; didn't address land speculation as root cause · Ch. 22, p.299
- **"Monetarism is the answer"** · objection · Thatcher/Reagan approach; failed because it didn't address land monopoly · Ch. 20, pp.260–2

- **"George advocated physical land redistribution"** · objection · Rothbard's mischaracterization; George proposed fiscal redistribution of rent only · Ch. 16, p.210

## Key Themes for Wiki Integration

1. **18-year land cycle theory**: Harrison's primary contribution — systematic documentation across four countries (UK, USA, Japan, Australia)
2. **Scissors mechanism**: The diverging returns to land vs. capital as the transmission mechanism for recessions
3. **Australian SVR vs. NAV evidence**: Victoria state provides natural experiment — SVR cities had 12.9% dwelling growth vs. 2.8% for NAV cities
4. **Japan land prices**: Urban land increased 2,200% (1955–1973); 30x income growth gap
5. **UK land speculation**: Centre Point, 1974 crash, "lifeboat" rescue preventing necessary land value correction
6. **Critique of Adam Smith**: Smith shielded landlords from competition, betraying his own free market principles
7. **Critique of Marx**: Marx acknowledged rent but misdiagnosed the problem as capital rather than land monopoly
8. **Critique of Keynes**: Relegated land problem to agricultural era; promoted demand management that didn't address root cause
9. **Land value tax as revenue source**: Cord estimated US rent could exceed all government revenue; Australian data showed 35% capture rate
10. **Veil of secrecy**: Systematic suppression of land ownership and value data by vested interests
