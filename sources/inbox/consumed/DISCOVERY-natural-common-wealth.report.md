## FINDING FIRST: the version discrepancy is now resolved (editor action suggested)

`research/natural-common-wealth-economic-rent-canada.md` (Sources §2 and the Correction note) flags an unreconciled "$194 billion" figure from a CWC blog post and says "A future editor could reconcile which scenario the blog's $194B refers to." **Reconciled this session:** there is a **July 2023 "Final" revision** of this same paper —
https://static1.squarespace.com/static/6323904e211cd97c7d32a788/t/64f2272306efb178b0560521/1693591332842/Natural+Common+Wealth+and+Economic+Rent+in+Canada_July+2023+Final.pdf (fetched 2026-07-09, sha256: c7545963f6c4a3965540a7cf7f1d696e4eb39e360a905b077e40919a151a340e) — which **replaces the Jan-2023 land-rent methodology**. Verbatim from the July 2023 text: land value "$5.824 trillion" (2022, a revised StatCan measurement vs Jan's $6.423T); "the capitalization rate in Canada fluctuates between 3% and 8%"; "the total available taxable land rents for 2022 in Canada equals $320 billion (=$5.824 [trillion × 5.5%])"; 75% capture → LVT "proposal at $194 billion" net new. So: **Jan 2023 v.3 = 8.32%/yr land-price-growth proxy → $362.5B net LVT; July 2023 Final = 5.5% capitalization-rate method → $194B net LVT.** The CWC blog's $194B and the distributional note's $194B envelope both cite the July 2023 revision. The wiki page for this paper (and the "$421B total" headline, which July 2023 revises to ~$253B by implication of the smaller land figure — editor should verify the July total before citing) should be updated to note the two versions explicitly. This also materially affects `outcomes/land-rent-could-fund-government.md` and `objections/lvt-not-enough-revenue.md`, which currently inherit the higher Jan-2023 figure: **the publisher itself cut its headline land estimate by ~46% between versions** — an honest-limits point worth stating on those pages.

## Headline claims of the paper (Jan 2023 v.3), with locators

- Total potential NEW economic rent collectible: **"$421 billion / year"**, "largely driven by the possible rent collection that could come from a national land value tax ($362.5 billion)" — Executive Summary p. 3, Table A; Table 1 p. 11.
- Per-category "Proposed Additional Rent Collection" (Table 1, p. 11): Land $362.5B (of $401B possible; $38.5B current property-tax-on-land); Minerals $8.4B (of $11.3B; $2.9B current); Energy (oil & gas) $15.3B (of $19.2B; $3.9B current); Forestry $1.7B (of $3.8B; $2.1B current); Fisheries $213M (of $253M; $40M current); Air (carbon) $32.9B (of $38.8B; $5.9B current). Totals: $474.4B possible / $53.3B current / $421.01B additional.
- Land method (pp. 15–17): total land value $6.423T at Q2 2022 (StatCan Table 36-10-0580-01, fn 57); "Since 1990 (the date of first available data), land has increased in value in Canada on average 8.32% per year" (p. 16); capture rate 75% → 6.24% of land value → $401B (Table 4, p. 17).
- Redistribution scenarios (Tables 3/3.1, p. 14; Vivic Research, SPSM 29.0, 2021 tax year): 50% of LVT ($181.3B) → no PIT on first $71,900 (69.4% of Canadians pay no PIT) + $6,817/adult dividend; 50% of all rents ($210.5B) → no PIT on first $111,500 (73% pay none) + $7,917/adult; 100% of all new rents as dividend → **$15,834/adult/year** (26.6M adults).
- Scale claim: new rents "would exceed the income tax revenues of the federal, provincial and territorial governments combined" (p. 3; 2021 PIT = $289B, Table 2 p. 12).
- Water (pp. 31–33): costs catalogued (IISD $1.04B/yr pollution costs; Lake Erie algae >$8.5B) but **no rent estimated** — "we have not estimated a rent that can be applied on water" (p. 30).
- Social common wealth (pp. 34–36): telecom/EM spectrum, IP, banking, AI flagged qualitatively only, no dollar rent estimates.

All of the above (except the water/social sections' detail) is already on the consumed wiki page; logged here for the discovery-protocol record.

### objections

- **"LVT would strand mortgaged owners / speculation-unwinding problem"** — the paper itself concedes (fn 28, p. 9) that mortgaged landowners "do require some unearned income in order to service the debt" and that an LVT "would involve/require winding down existing speculation in land"; and fn 69 (p. 17) concedes LVT would reduce land prices and therefore its own revenue base (self-eroding-base point). Both are candid-limits material for `objections/lvt-not-enough-revenue.md` and `objections/lvt-hurts-asset-rich-cash-poor.md` (both exist)
- **"Price-growth is not rent"** — the Jan-2023 method equates average land-price *growth* (8.32%/yr) with annual *rent*; the July 2023 revision's abandonment of this method is the strongest internal evidence for the objection. Already partially flagged on the consumed page's "Limits" section; the version-change fact strengthens it
