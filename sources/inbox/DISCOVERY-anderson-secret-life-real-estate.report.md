# Discovery Report: The Secret Life of Real Estate and Banking

**Author:** Phillip J. Anderson  
**Year:** 2008 (first published as *The Secret Life of Real Estate*; reprinted 2009 under new title)  
**Publisher:** Shepheard-Walwyn (Publishers) Ltd, London  
**ISBN:** 978-0-85683-263-5  
**Source file:** `/Users/OpenClaw/wiki-books/extracted/secret-life.txt` (~16,384 lines)

## Overview

This book traces US real estate cycles from 1800 to 2008, arguing that an ~18-year land-price cycle repeats because the economic rent of land is permitted to capitalise into a tradable commodity, financed by bank credit creation (fractional reserve banking). Anderson uses Homer Hoyt's Chicago land-value data, Roy Wenzlick's real estate transfer indices, Fred Harrison's cycle profiles, W.D. Gann's time-analysis methods, and Ricardo's Law of Rent as the theoretical framework. The book is structured in three parts: Part I (the 2008 subprime crisis), Part II (historical cycles 1792–2006), and Part III (theory, forecasting, and the Real Estate Clock).

## Key Themes for Georgism Wiki

1. **18-year cycle thesis**: Land prices peak 12–36 months before recession; building peaks follow land-value peaks but precede recession; pattern holds since 1800.
2. **Ricardo's Law of Rent as root cause**: The cycle exists because economic rent capitalises into a tradable price; without this, no violent cycle.
3. **Banking as transmission mechanism**: Fractional reserve banking creates credit against land value; credit contraction at peak causes bank failures; each cycle marked by a major bank collapse.
4. **Canberra leasehold and Danish land tax experiments**: Demonstrated that collecting economic rent prevents capitalisation and eliminates the cycle.
5. **Forecasting toolkit**: Land price/GDP barometer, yield curve inversion, Cantillon Effect (skyscrapers), copper prices, W.D. Gann time analysis.
6. **War as credit expansion**: Every war expanded national debt via fractional reserve banking; war follows economic downturns; military-industrial complex.
7. **Government-granted licences as broader category**: Anderson generalises from land to all government privileges (broadcast spectrum, taxi medallions, banking charters) that capitalise rent.
8. **Frontier closure (1890)**: Eliminated the safety valve of free land; made subsequent depressions more severe (especially 1930s).
9. **"New era" thinking**: Declared at every peak; always wrong; cognitive dissonance drives trend persistence.
10. **Georgist policy prescriptions implied but not fully advocated**: Anderson implies but does not explicitly campaign for land value taxation as the solution.
