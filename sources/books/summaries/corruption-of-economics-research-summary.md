## Core Thesis

Gaffney's central argument is that **neoclassical economics (NCE) was not a natural evolution of classical economics but a deliberate stratagem to neutralize Henry George and the single-tax movement**. The key method was **semantic**: redefining economic terms to destroy the conceptual vocabulary in which George expressed his arguments. The most critical move was **merging land into capital**, erasing the classical (Ricardian) distinction between land as a non-produced, fixed natural factor and capital as a produced, reproducible factor.

Gaffney writes: "Few people realize to what degree the founders of neo-classical economics changed the discipline for the express purpose of deflecting George and frustrating future students seeking to follow his arguments" (Ch. 1). He quotes Simon Patten (1908): "Nothing pleases a … single taxer better than … to use the well-known economic theories … [therefore] economic doctrine must be recast" (Ch. 1, citing Patten 1908: 219; Collier 1979: 270).

George himself suspected this, stating in his posthumous *The Science of Political Economy* (1898: 200–209) that economists were recasting the discipline to refute him. Gaffney initially found this "paranoid" but changed his view after deeper research: "I have changed my view, however, after learning more about the period, the literature, and later events" (Ch. 1).

## Gaffney's "Canon" of Neoclassical Sins Against Land Theory

Gaffney does not present a formal numbered "canon" of sins, but his essay systematically documents specific intellectual moves by named economists that collectively constitute the corruption. These can be organized as follows:

### 1. Merging Land into Capital (J.B. Clark)
J.B. Clark's primary contribution was endowing capital with a "Platonic essence" — a deathless, immaterial substance that could "transmigrate" into land, making land just another form of capital. Gaffney calls this "jelly capital" or "putty capital." Clark's *The Distribution of Wealth* (1899) systematically merges rent with interest throughout. "Clark's capital theory '… gives the appearance of being specially tailored to lead to arguments for use against George'" (Ch. 3, citing Collier 1979: 270). Clark's bibliography includes at least 24 works directed against George over 28 years (1886–1914).

### 2. Redefining Rent as Opportunity Cost (Pareto)
Pareto redefined rent as "the gain from reallocating a resource — any resource" — the excess of current return over opportunity cost — and belittled Ricardian rent as "just a particular case" (Ch. 7, citing Pareto 1906: 247). Gaffney argues this was "calculated to divert attention from land rent as a taxable surplus." Modern NCEists gradually dropped "Paretian" and call it simply "rent," as though no other meaning exists.

### 3. Eliminating Time and Sequence from Microeconomics (Clark/Knight)
Clark's static-state methodology assumed away capital formation, depreciation, and obsolescence. Knight continued this by attacking the Austrian concept of a "period of production" (which distinguishes capital, having one, from land, not having one). Gaffney: "Micro-economic theory has been largely lacking a a time-dimension: a curious lack for a discipline using calculus and aping the methods of physics" (Ch. 3).

### 4. Denying Rent as a Taxable Surplus (Clark/Knight/Pareto)
Clark argued wages are rent too, implying wages are equally taxable. Knight fused individual and social viewpoints: "A cost to one firm is a cost to society" — denying any aggregation problem or fallacy of composition. Knight: "land yields no unearned surplus so long as competition keeps the returns to individuals at market levels" (Ch. 8, citing Knight 1924, rpt. 1952: 167–69).

### 5. Compartmentalization
NCE's defense against discordant data: Keynesian macroeconomics was "hived off into a separate compartment" so it "did not disturb basic tenets of NCE." Business cycle theory, Wesley Mitchell's work, and other inconvenient lines of inquiry were segregated. "Compartmentalization … is the common NCE defense against discordant data and reasoning" (Ch. 1).

### 6. Redefining "Land" Out of the Lexicon (Ely)
Ely, while founding "land economics" as a discipline, systematically denied land's unique qualities: "land value 'is governed by the same laws that govern the values of other requisites of production'" (Ch. 5, citing Ely 1922, II: 78). His Institute for Research in Land Economics and Public Utility Economics was funded by railroads, utilities, and real estate interests to preempt and redirect applied land economics away from taxation.

### 7. The Doctrine of Uniformity (Seligman)
Seligman used Clark's capital theory to argue that taxing land drives capital out of land, misallocating resources. Therefore "the essential for neutrality in taxation is uniformity, including uniformity between land and capital" (Ch. 3). This doctrine "informed the architects of the Tax Reform Act of 1986" and remains influential.

### 8. Defining Away Unearned Increment (Plehn/King)
Plehn referred to unearned increments of land value as "capital gains," a camouflage that "has of course become standard," and argued they should be exempt from income taxation (Ch. 9, citing Plehn 1926: 272). King argued land value rises are part of the return to capital, hence an incentive payment for saving (Ch. 9).

### 9. Pareto Optimality as Status Quo Entrenchment (Edgeworth/Pareto)
Edgeworth's "Pareto Optimality" concept firms up and freezes existing entitlements to property: "all existing entitlements to property, whatever their origins, should be firmed up and frozen" (Ch. 6). Gaffney: "The operational part, of course, is what you do first: firm up and freeze existing entitlements. The rest is mostly moonshine, a promise made to be broken" (Ch. 6).

### 10. Indifference Curves and Ordinal Welfare (Pareto/Edgeworth)
Pareto's indifference curve technique makes "it technically more difficult to explain and perceive simple points, thus excluding more people from understanding" (Ch. 7). The shift from cardinal to ordinal rankings eliminates interpersonal welfare comparisons, dismisses egalitarian ideals, and makes land rent and taxable surplus harder to express.

## The ATCOR Concept

**The term "ATCOR" (All Taxes Come Out of Rent) does not appear in this book.** Gaffney developed the ATCOR acronym in later work (cited as Gaffney 2005 in other sources). However, the **conceptual foundation** is present in several passages:

1. **Gaffney's nine-point summary of George's program** (Ch. 2): "George's proposed land tax might be called 'true fiscal stimulus'. It stimulates demand for labor by promoting employment; it precludes inflation as the labor produces goods to match the new demand. It precludes deficits because it raises revenue" (Ch. 2, point 6).

2. **The "negative shifting" concept** (Ch. 2, point 3): "The only shifting of a land tax is negative. By negative shifting I mean that the supply-side effects of taxing the rent of land will raise supplies of goods and services, and raise the demand for labor, thus raising the bargaining power of median people in the marketplace" (Ch. 2).

3. **Feder's postscript** articulates the mechanism most explicitly: "In an economy like that of South Africa, where real wages approach subsistence and can be forced no lower, both labour and capital are highly elastically supplied — so, most kinds of taxes on production are ultimately shifted largely to immobile land, which can neither starve nor flee. The converse of this is that when these taxes are reduced, the primary result of tax un-shifting is an increase in the gross rent of land" (Postscript, discussing tax capitalization and incidence).

4. **Harrison's prologue** references the revenue sufficiency question: "at the end of the 19th century there was little doubt that the Single Tax afforded a sufficient tax base to finance the needs of the state" (Prologue). He notes modern economists claim rent is "a derisory sum, of anywhere between 2% to 8% of national income" without evidence (Prologue).

The book thus presents the **pre-acronym conceptual basis** for ATCOR: that taxes on labor and capital are ultimately incident on land rent, and that removing them would expand the rent base, but does not formally name or model the theorem.

## Key Findings by Chapter/Section

### Gaffney Ch. 1–2: George as Problem-Solver
Gaffney identifies **nine constructive reconciliations** in George's reform proposal that explain its political threat:
1. Reconciles common land rights with private tenure and free markets
2. Lowers taxes on both labor and capital without reducing public revenues
3. Reconciles equity and efficiency (progressive AND pro-incentive)
4. Lets governments finance generous public services without driving away business
5. Contains urban sprawl through market mechanisms
6. Creates jobs without inflation or deficits ("true fiscal stimulus")
7. Attracts people and capital without diluting the resource base (synergy)
8. Encourages ecological conservation while making jobs
9. Strengthens public revenues while promoting economy in government

Gaffney argues these nine features "should be enough to persuade one that it had the potential to become very popular. Its premise was socializing land rents. Its very strengths were its undoing, however, for they evoke a powerful, intransigent, wealthy counterforce" (Ch. 2).

### Gaffney Ch. 3: The Empire Strikes Back
Documents personal confrontations between George and the founders of NCE:
- **J.B. Clark** debated George at Saratoga (c. 1890) and George's lieutenant Louis F. Post at Cooper Union (1903). Clark was recruited to Columbia by Seth Low, who was preparing to run for Mayor against George. Clark's concept of capital was "almost as though he borrowed from Marx" but was "aimed against Henry George" (Ch. 3).
- **E.R.A. Seligman**, scion of a wealthy banking family, "created the field of public finance in America" and devoted Chapter III of *Essays in Taxation* (1895) to savaging the single tax. "The spirit of invective is not the spirit of science nor philosophy" (Ch. 3).
- **Philip Wicksteed** was friendly to George, formally anticipating marginal productivity theory in *An Essay on the Coordination of the Laws of Distribution* (1894), paraphrased from *Progress and Poverty* Book III, Ch. VII. He "remained always loyal to Progress and Poverty's central idea" (Ch. 3).
- **Alfred Marshall** debated George at Oxford in 1883 but was "cautious to a fault" and actually supported Lloyd George's land-tax budget of 1909. "George could hardly ask for a more useful, constructive critic than Marshall" (Ch. 3).
- **Francis A. Walker** confronted George in the press (1883), wrote he "would not insult my readers by discussing a project so steeped in infamy," but later engaged more substantively (Ch. 3).

### Gaffney Ch. 4–5: Institutional Capture
- **Andrew Dickson White** (Cornell) and **Daniel Coit Gilman** (Hopkins) controlled American graduate economics education. Gilman was "hounded from Berkeley in 1874–75 by a crusading populist journalist, Henry George" (Ch. 5), creating a personal enmity that shaped the profession.
- **Richard T. Ely** founded the American Economic Association in 1885 with Walker, Clark, and Seligman — all personal adversaries of George. The AEA's undemocratic "Council" was designed "to prevent our organization from being captured by some economic sect or group of reformers" — code for single-taxers (Ch. 5).
- Ely later founded the Institute for Research in Land Economics, funded by railroads, utilities, and NAREB (National Association of Real Estate Boards), to preempt applied land economics and attack the Ralston-Nolan Bill (a federal land tax proposal, 1919–24).
- Academics were fired for Georgist sympathies: Allen Eaton (Oregon), Elisha Andrews (Brown), Scott Nearing (Pennsylvania/Wharton).

### Gaffney Ch. 6–7: Edgeworth and Pareto
- **F.Y. Edgeworth** was heir to Irish landlord estates (Edgeworthtown), creating personal motive against George, who attacked absentee Irish landlords. Edgeworth introduced "Pareto Optimality" in *Mathematical Psychics* (1881), which firms up existing property entitlements. He held eugenicist views: "there appears a deeper sentiment in favour of aristocratical privilege" (Ch. 6, citing Edgeworth 1879: 77).
- **Vilfredo Pareto** expressed contempt for egalitarianism and humanitarianism: "the error of the humanitarians … is not in having a religion … but in having chosen a religion which is appropriate only to weak beings lacking in all energy and courage" (Ch. 7, citing Pareto 1906: 364). His redefinition of rent, indifference curves, and ordinal welfare all serve to obscure land rent as a taxable surplus.

### Gaffney Ch. 8: The Chicago School
- **Frank Knight** inherited Clark's anti-Georgism via Alvin Johnson at Cornell. "In terms of numbers, and intensity of feeling generated, Knight probably produced more NCEists and NCEism than anyone in history" (Ch. 8). His "Fallacies in the Single Tax" (1953) continued the campaign. Knight argued slave-owners had just title and that "summary liberation" was unethical, and that land value is a human product created by "killing off previous claimants" (Ch. 8, citing Knight 1924: 167–69).
- The chain: Rockefeller → Harper → Laughlin → J.B. Clark → J.M. Clark → Knight → Stigler, Friedman, Harberger. "Chicago is still the lengthened shadow of John D. Rockefeller" (Ch. 8, citing Barber 1988d).

### Gaffney Ch. 9: The Bafflegabbers
- **W.I. King** "believed that land is equally distributed" based on anecdote about one village (Ch. 9). 
- **Carl C. Plehn** (Berkeley, Ely product) held "an important regional command" for 35 years, publishing five editions of *Introduction to Public Finance* (1896–1926) that systematically misrepresent George, call the single tax a "scheme" with "ulterior" purpose, and contradict himself on tax incidence. "Multiply Plehn by 100 or so … and you have the tragedy of American higher education in economics" (Ch. 9).

### Gaffney Ch. 10: The Bitter Harvest
Documents the consequences of NCE policy dominance:
- **Labor**: Real wages falling since ~1975; wages of men with high school education fell 21% (1973–91), less than high school fell 26%, young urban black workers fell 50%. Unemployment normalized at ever-higher "natural" rates.
- **Capital**: Low returns on savings, low domestic saving rate, decaying infrastructure, financial system "a shambles."
- **Concentration**: Number of American farms fell from 6 million to 1 million (1920–90); Gini coefficient among remaining farms rose from .57 (1910) to .76 (1987), or .92 when adjusted for lost farms.
- **Social**: Homelessness, hunger, beggary, crime, civil disturbances (LA riots 1992), underground economy, loss of community.
- **National stature**: Falling behind in public health, infant survival, longevity, literacy, etc.
- Gaffney's verdict: "NCE, forged as a strategem to discomfort Henry George and Georgists, is intellectually, morally, and practically bankrupt" (Ch. 10).

### Harrison's Prologue: Who's Afraid of Henry George?
Harrison frames the historical context: George was perceived as more dangerous than Marx. Palgrave's *Dictionary of Political Economy* (1909) described George's proposals as dangerous, while treating Marx's style as "singularly brilliant." "Marx was evidently no threat to the class interests of those who controlled society; he could be left alone to wade through his manuscripts in the British Museum" (Prologue). Harrison critiques Galbraith as an exemplar of how even sympathetic economists remain trapped in NCE's bilinear (capital vs. labor) framework, conceptually smothering land.

### Harrison: The Georgist Paradigm
Harrison argues both capitalism and communism are discredited paradigms. The Georgist paradigm: (1) identifies fatal flaws in both systems, (2) synchronizes with human aspirations for liberty, (3) specifies a non-coercive transition mechanism, (4) is scientifically testable (falsifiable), (5) is grounded in morality and anthropology. He cites Stiglitz's "Henry George Theorem" and an Open Letter to Gorbachev signed by three Nobel laureates (Solow, Modigliani, Tobin).

### Feder & Harrison: Postscript on South Africa
Documents the 1994 opportunity for single-tax reform in post-apartheid South Africa, where most municipalities already levied site-value taxes. Meintjes and Jacques submitted proposals to the Tax Commission. The Free Market Foundation (patronized by diamond cartel interests) commissioned Richard Grant to attack the proposal using Frank Knight's arguments. Feder systematically demolishes Grant's objections, showing that "of Dr. Grant's many objections to the use of rent as the primary source of public revenue, not one withstands inspection" (Postscript).

### Harrison: Corrupting the Body Politic (c. 2022)
A retrospective chapter examining South Africa, Russia, and China since 1994. South Africa cancelled site-value revenue policy in 2004; Russia privatized land under "shock therapy" (Sachs), creating oligarchs; China privatized rent through lease sales, fueling corruption and ghost towns. Harrison argues all three cases demonstrate that **privatizing rent corrupts governance**, while the rent-as-public-revenue model would have produced accountable, democratic politics.

## Historical Suppression of Henry George

Gaffney documents suppression through multiple mechanisms:

### Political Threat Assessment
George's *Progress and Poverty* (1879) sold in the millions. Seven years later he nearly became Mayor of New York City. By 1889 he became "adviser and field-general in land reform strategy" to the Radical wing of the British Liberal Party. His ideas influenced Tolstoy, Kerensky, Sun Yat-sen, Lloyd George, Churchill, Billy Hughes, Chaim Weizmann, Francisco Madero, and others worldwide (Ch. 1).

### Institutional Mechanisms
1. **Academic firings**: Eaton (Oregon), Andrews (Brown), Nearing (Pennsylvania) — all fired for Georgist sympathies (Ch. 3).
2. **AEA capture**: Founded by George's adversaries (Walker, Clark, Seligman, Ely), with an undemocratic Council to exclude single-taxers (Ch. 5).
3. **University patronage**: Clark recruited to Columbia by Seth Low (George's political opponent); Seligman as Chair of Economics for decades; Gilman's network controlling Hopkins and graduate education (Ch. 3, 5).
4. **Funded institutes**: Ely's Institute, funded by railroads and NAREB, produced anti-Georgist "research" and attacked federal land tax legislation (Ch. 5).
5. **Textbook dominance**: Ely's *Outlines of Economics* was "the bread-and-butter text from 1893 to about 1930"; Clark's theory "has provided the basis for virtually all empirical work on wealth and income" (Ch. 3, citing Dewey 1987: 429).
6. **The Great Red Scare (1919–89)**: "George's ideas and the allied Progressive Movement fell, not from failure to deliver, but to the Great Marathon Red Scare that has dominated much of the world from 1919 to 1989" (Ch. 1).

### Minimizing George's Historical Significance
Gaffney documents that modified Georgism "helped run the USA for 17 years (1902–19) working through both major political parties" (Ch. 1). The single-tax movement's influence on the Progressive Era, direct democracy (initiative/referendum), the income tax, and municipal reform is "a great dereliction of American historians" to have neglected (Ch. 1). Eric Goldman (1956) "found George to have inspired most of the major reformers of the early 20th Century" (Ch. 1).

## Policy Recommendations

### Primary Policy: The Single Tax / Land Value Taxation
The book advocates collecting the economic rent of land as the primary source of public revenue, while abolishing or reducing taxes on wages, profits, and consumption.

**Specific mechanisms:**
- Tax the rental value of land (excluding improvements) at high rates (approaching 100%)
- Exempt buildings, capital, labor, and exchange from taxation
- Include natural resources and public franchises in the tax base
- Use market mechanisms (auctions) to determine rental values
- Phase in gradually to allow adjustment and minimize transition costs
- Maintain transparent public information on land values

**Claimed outcomes:**
- Full employment without inflation or deficits
- Elimination of land speculation and the business cycle
- Containment of urban sprawl
- Ecological conservation
- Reduced crime, welfare costs, and social unrest
- Equitable distribution of natural resource benefits
- Strengthened democratic accountability

### Constitutional/Structural Reform
Harrison argues the rent-as-public-revenue principle should be enshrined in constitutional law, filling the "void" in the American Constitution's treatment of property rights. He traces the Founding Fathers' omission of "estate" (Locke's term for land) from the Declaration of Independence to the landowning interests of the drafters (Ch. 10 / "From Chaos to Cosmos").

### International Strategy
Harrison proposes that if China and Russia adopted the rent-as-public-revenue policy, "the economic consequences … for the exporting nations, would be traumatic. Russia and China … would come to dominate the global markets within a decade" because "by not having to carry the burden of taxes on labour and capital, the prices of their manufactured products would be too low" for Western nations to compete (Ch. 10). This competitive pressure would force other nations to adopt the policy.

## Relevance to Wiki Pages

This source is directly relevant to:

- **ATCOR**: The book provides the pre-acronym conceptual foundation. ATCOR itself was formalized by Gaffney in 2005, but the mechanism (taxes on labor/capital are ultimately incident on rent) is articulated, particularly in Feder's postscript discussion of tax incidence and the "negative shifting" concept in Ch. 2.
- **Economic rent**: Gaffney's entire essay is a defense of the classical/Ricardian concept of rent as a surplus, against the neoclassical redefinition. Pareto's redefinition (Ch. 7) and Joan Robinson's counter ("From the point of view of society, land … is provided free, and the whole rent is a surplus") are directly relevant.
- **Land value tax**: The book is one of the most detailed historical and theoretical defenses of LVT/the single tax. Gaffney's nine-point case (Ch. 2) and Feder's postscript provide comprehensive policy analysis.
- **Henry George**: The book is a foundational text for understanding the historical suppression of George's ideas, documenting specific economists, institutions, and mechanisms over a century.
- **Neoclassical economics**: The book offers a critical historiography of NCE's founding, tracing specific intellectual moves to specific individuals with documented conflicts of interest.

## Limitations and Caveats

1. **Gaffney's tone is polemical**, not neutral. He explicitly acknowledges this: "This work is addressed and dedicated to" "the young, the brave, the energetic, the sincere and the sceptical" (Ch. 1). The essay is an argument, not a detached survey.
2. **Some claims about individual motivations are inferential**, based on circumstantial evidence (landholdings, family background, funding sources) rather than documented statements of intent. Gaffney generally acknowledges this, using qualifiers like "it seems entirely believable" and "one surmises."
3. **ATCOR is not named or formally modeled** in this book. The concept appears in outline but was developed by Gaffney in later work (2005, 2009).
4. **The "Corrupting the Body Politic" chapter is from a 2022 revised edition**, not the original 1994 publication. It references events through 2021 (Biden, Xi, Zuma) and should be cited as from the revised edition.
5. **The book predates the modern empirical LVT literature** (e.g., Tideman, Oates, Fischel) and does not engage with computable general equilibrium models or modern public finance econometrics.
6. **Gaffney's claims about the California irrigation districts** (Wright Act) as a Georgist success story, while well-documented, represent a selective case study rather than systematic comparative evidence.
7. **The charge of deliberate conspiracy** is central to the book's thesis. While Gaffney documents extensive conflicts of interest and institutional capture, the degree of intentional coordination versus emergent institutional bias remains debatable. The book presents evidence consistent with both interpretations.
