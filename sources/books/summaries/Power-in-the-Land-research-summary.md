## 2. Core Thesis and Argument Structure

**Thesis (Ch. 1, "The Fatal Mistake"):** Industrial society was built on a "corrupt foundation" — the institutionalisation of land monopoly at the dawn of the Industrial Revolution (1780s). Land speculation disrupts the industrial economy by (a) distorting income distribution in favour of rent, and (b) contracting the supply of land for homes/factories/offices/farms. The "fatal mistake" of the founding fathers of industrialism was to accept and institutionalise land monopoly while preaching free-market laissez faire (lines 154-162, 207-213).

**Argument structure (22 chapters in 7 parts):**
1. **The Unfree Market (Ch. 1-4):** Classical economics erred by collapsing land into capital; land monopoly is inconsistent with laissez faire (134-2179).
2. **A Theory of Recessions (Ch. 5-7):** Henry George's hypothesis that land speculation is "the great initiatory cause" of depressions, formalised via Homer Hoyt's 18-year real-estate cycle, tested against UK evidence (2179-3610).
3. **The United States Economy (Ch. 8-10):** Hoyt's own speculative career as evidence the cycle persisted post-1933; REIT bubble 1969-74; US land policy as "policies of pillage" (3920-5099).
4. **The Japanese 'Miracle' (Ch. 11-12):** Meiji land tax failures and the postwar 1955→1973 cycle that brought down the "miracle" economy (5680-6079).
5. **The Socialist Models (Ch. 13-14):** Marxist theory and Soviet experiment; nationalisation vs. LVT (referenced in TOC, lines 112-114).
6. **Land Value Taxation (Ch. 15-18):** The Single Tax, academic critiques, equity/financing, and Australia as case study (6961-8950).
7. **The Poverty of Politics (Ch. 19-21):** 1974-78 policy failures, Reagan-Thatcher myth, and a concrete LVT-based recovery programme (10112-10500).
8. **Capitalism (Ch. 22):** Requiem or revival? — reform rather than revolution is sufficient (11100-11283).

## 4. The Land Cycle Theory as Presented

**Name:** 18-year cycle in land values (Harrison uses "18-year cycle" throughout; Hoyt used "urban real estate cycle").

**Origin:** Homer Hoyt, *One Hundred Years of Land Values in Chicago* (Univ. Chicago Press, 1933); updated in ULI Technical Bulletin No. 38 (1950). Harrison extends Hoyt with UK, Japanese, Australian and US postwar data (lines 2323-2328, 2350-2356).

**Mechanism (Ch. 6, lines 2618-2755):**
1. **Bifurcation/scissors:** Over ~two decades the return to capital trends down (classical tendency, verified by Phelps Brown & Weber) while the return to land trends up. Capital investment is starved; land hoarding becomes attractive.
2. **Speculative build-up:** Land can be held idle indefinitely (non-perishable, refinanciable); capital cannot. Speculators buy at the trough, hold 15-16 years, sell to "mugs" in the last 12-18 months.
3. **Peak:** Land-value peak arrives 12-24 months before the general recession. Building-cycle peak follows land-value peak and precedes the business-cycle downturn.
4. **Collapse:** Over-valued land → rents/land prices cannot be serviced out of current output → bankruptcies, bank failures, unemployment. Recession forces rents back to a realistic level; only then can growth restart.
5. **Reset:** The trough creates low land prices and high capital yields → the next cycle begins.

**Periodicity evidence (Table 5:I, lines 2347-2356):** US land-value peaks 1818 → 1836 → 1854-56 → 1872 → 1892 → 1907 → 1925 → (war gap) → 1973. Recessions follow 12-24 months later. The 1940s peak is missing owing to WWII (line 2330-2332).

**Postwar dating:** Harrison dates the modern cycle from 1955 (UK: Nov 2 1954 lifting of building licences; US: 1954-56 farmland-value trough; Japan: 1955 "high postwar growth" start; Australia: 1955 turning point in years-of-earnings-to-buy-a-house). Peak 1973, recession 1974 — "dead on target, 18 years" (lines 2867-2869, 4467, 5985-5988, 8605).

**Speculator time-horizon (Ch. 5):** Botha's "intermediate" speculator holds ~10 years; Harrison argues the optimum is **15-16 years** (buy trough, sell before peak) — synchronised with the 18-year cycle (lines 2436-2444). Empirically corroborated by the 700-owner N. American survey (transition begins >15 years before development) and the South Wales vacant-site study (modal 15-19 yrs) (F5, F6).

**Why land ≠ capital (Ch. 5, lines 2478-2535):**
- Land is non-perishable → can be held idle without depreciation.
- Banks continue to lend against land in uncertain times; credit for inventory/stock is restricted → speculators can refinance and wait.
- Land hoarding contracts supply of a fixed factor → forces up rents → bankrupts firms. Goods hoarding does not destabilise aggregate demand (substitutable supply).
- Speculation in shares lowers cost of capital; speculation in land raises interest rates and crowds out productive investment.

## 6. Policy Recommendations (LVT, Land Reform)

Harrison's programme (Ch. 21, "1980s: Policies for Recovery," lines 10112-10500):

1. **Shift the tax burden onto land values.** A 100% tax on annual land values is the theoretical ideal; an 80% rate is used in his Diagram 21:I illustration (line 10310). This:
   - Loosens labour/product-market rigidities
   - Raises wages' share (boosts consumption) and interest share (stimulates investment)
   - Permits reduction of taxes on wages and capital (line 10274-10280)
2. **Phase-in over two parliamentary terms (8-10 years).** Stage 1: convert PAYE income tax into a corporate liability (reducing UK assessments from 24m → 1m), simultaneously valuing land (line 10346-10352).
3. **House-building as leading sector.** Construction = 11% of UK GDP, 2m jobs, £22bn output, £3bn exports (1980). LVT unlocks vacant land → revives construction → pulls economy out of recession (line 10472-10490).
4. **Self-assessment land valuation** with later corrective adjustments — a complete transformation can be done within 5 years (line 10344-10345).
5. **No incomes policy.** Wage share is roughly constant (~50% in manufacturing) across all major economies; suppressing wages deflates demand and fails to raise profits (line 10244-10258).
6. **Free-market, not nationalisation.** LVT works best with a free market in land; socialist nationalisation (Jamaica, USSR) fails because central planning cannot price land. Henry George "saw a continuing but benign role for government" (line 6986-6988).
7. **Tax-rate must be deterrent.** Tax rates "too low to neutralise the malign effects of the 18-year cycle" (e.g. Australia's ~5% of site value) merely prove misadministration, not LVT failure (line 7967, 8826-8828).
8. **No betterment levy.** Partial levies (UK development charges 1947, NSW 30% betterment 1970) are sidestepped by owners withholding land → shrinking supply → raising prices; they lapse as unworkable (line 8930-8934, 3603-3609).
9. **Annual revaluations** are essential; lagged valuations cause politically destabilising step-jumps in tax bills (as Victoria 1974 valuations → 1977-78 anti-land-tax lobby) (line 8817-8822).
10. **Reduce taxes on wages/capital in lock-step** with rising LVT yield. Land values themselves rise when other taxes fall, expanding the tax base endogenously (line 7343-7355).

## 7. Relation to Georgist Economics and Henry George

Harrison self-identifies as a Georgist and treats *Progress and Poverty* (1879) as the foundational text of his own theory (lines 405, 2257, 4930).

**Where Harrison extends George:**
- George offered "largely impressionistic evidence" for the speculation theory of depressions; Harrison claims to supply "statisticians [who] have produced data in anything like sufficient quantity" (line 2303-2306).
- George did not specify a periodicity; Harrison adopts and extends Hoyt's **18-year** dating (George wrote of long swings but not a fixed cycle length) (line 2307-2322).
- George treated the moral case; Harrison adds the **scissors mechanism** (rising rent vs falling profit) and a formal transmission path through the building cycle (Ch. 6-7).
- Harrison adds **four modern case studies** (USA, UK, Japan, Australia) plus the Jamaica socialist-LVT experiment and the Canberra leasehold model (lines 246-249, 7170-7230, 8693-8727).

**Where Harrison differs from / interprets George:**
- "Henry George did not fit neatly into any of the established categories of political philosophy" — neither capitalist nor socialist (line 6975-6982).
- George "was closer to the capitalist" because he advocated the free market; but the land-tenure system was the obstacle (line 6996-7000).
- Harrison cites Churchill (1909) and Marx (Capital Vol. II, pp. 623-4; Critique of the Gotha Programme) as corroborating that rent absorbs a rising share of surplus value (lines 3140-3155, 11194-11201).
- Harrison explicitly rejects both Marxist nationalisation and conventional income-tax redistribution as inadequate; LVT + free market is "both a necessary and sufficient step" (line 11187-11189).

**Hoyt as foil:** Hoyt himself dismissed his own cycle as obsolete post-1933 (line 3943-3952) and labelled George's Single Tax "communism" (line 7154). Harrison devotes Ch. 8 ("The Hoyt Heist") to refuting Hoyt, using Hoyt's own multi-million-dollar land dealings (1953-72 Fairfax, Treasure Beach) as evidence the cycle persisted (line 3997, 4010-4012, 4056-4057).

**Academic critics addressed (Ch. 16):** Samuelson (rent is a declining fraction of GNP), Rothbard, Friedman, Feldstein — Harrison argues they conflate land with capital, use incomplete data, and ignore imputed rent of idle land (lines 3098-3119, 7661-7760).

## 9. Limitations and Critiques

**Internal concessions:**
- "We have to grapple with piece-meal evidence to show the trends and relationships in this first major cycle because statistics were not systematically compiled" (line 2689-2691).
- "Even today the deficiencies in the quantitative evidence present us with a serious difficulty. Economic rent... is not published as a category in its own right" (line 2692-2697).
- Hoyt's post-1973 US land values did **not collapse** as historical pattern would predict (Treasure Beach $3m → $6m → $9.5m, 1978-82) — Harrison defers explanation to Ch. 19 but acknowledges the anomaly (line 4017-4022, 4256-4261).
- "The element of arbitrariness in the selection of dates for turning points is something against which we have to guard" (line 4382-4383).
- Table 6:I appears to contradict the scissors thesis (profits rising at cycle end); Harrison argues exceptional factors (motor/electricity boom, tariff protection, rearmament) and accepts the cycle would have overridden them (line 2829-2837).
- Cord's US rental-income estimate includes one over-estimate (property-tax capitalisation) but Harrison argues it is "more than offset by the extremely conservative assumptions" (line 7279-7287).
- "We cannot do so satisfactorily, with statistics, for the land has not been valued separately from all capital improvements upon it" — the disaggregation of land from capital returns is inferential, not measured (line 4409-4411).

**External / structural critiques (implicit):**
- The 18-year periodicity relies on selected peak dates; cycle length varies (Harrison cites 20-year Kuznets swings, 18-year Hoyt, and admits 1815-1938 = 20-year gap with war distortions).
- The theory is tested on only four developed economies plus Jamaica; no formal econometric identification strategy is offered (correlation + narrative causal mechanism).
- The claim that OPEC was "secondary" (line 2945-2954) is asserted by timing argument, not by a counterfactual model.
- LVT-revenue sufficiency relies on Cord's and Hutchinson's contested estimates; Harrison concedes "we do not know — within a tolerable margin of error — how much is enjoyed by the recipients of the economic rent of land" (line 7256-7261).
- No formal model of the 18-year mechanism (e.g. difference/differential equations) is presented; the theory is verbal and diagrammatic (Diagram 21:I).
