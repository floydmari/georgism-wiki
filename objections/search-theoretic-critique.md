## The Objection

The search-theoretic critique of Georgism, associated with Zachary Gochenour and Bryan Caplan, argues that a full land value tax undermines the incentive to *search for* and discover the best uses (and hidden qualities) of land: if all site value is taxed away, nobody profits from finding out what a site is really worth, so valuable information goes unproduced. ## The Response

On the Dwarkesh Podcast, [Lars Doucet](/wiki/lars-doucet/) discussed the critique at length and offered rebuttals: taxing less than 100% of land rent (he proposes ~85%) preserves a discovery margin, and explicit discovery incentives — such as a Norway-style subsidy for resource exploration — can reward search directly without leaving all land rent private.

## Net Assessment

One of the more sophisticated contemporary academic challenges to Georgist policy. It argues against a 100% rent capture rather than against land value taxation as such, and standard responses (sub-100% rates, discovery subsidies) address it at the cost of conceding that some rent must remain private.
