## Overview

The 1886 New York City mayoral election was the most significant electoral moment in the history of the Georgist movement. **Henry George**, by then internationally famous as the author of *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*, ran as the candidate of the United Labor Party on a platform centred on his land-value-tax ideas.

## The Campaign

George was drafted by a coalition of labour unions and reformers and campaigned on capturing land rent for public benefit and breaking the grip of land monopoly on the city. A committee from the city's Trade and Labour Conference approached him three times before he agreed to run; he made his acceptance conditional on **at least thirty thousand citizens** pledging in writing to vote for him, "a guarantee that there should be no ignominious failure, and a mandate that I could not refuse" (letter to J. P. Archibald, late August 1886) — more than 34,000 signatures were ultimately delivered to his Cooper Union nomination meeting. His friend [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/) and Father [Edward McGlynn](/wiki/edward-mcglynn/) — who counselled him to run and later called his acceptance letter the "master stroke" — were among his closest advisers throughout (Henry George Jr., *Life of Henry George*, 1900, Third Period, Ch. VIII).

Before the nomination, Tammany and County Democracy emissary William M. Ivins privately offered George a safe Congressional seat if he would withdraw. George's own account of the exchange: Ivins told him "You cannot possibly be counted in... You cannot be elected, but your running will raise hell!" George replied: "You have relieved me of embarrassment. I do not want the responsibility and the work of the office of the Mayor of New York, but I do want to raise hell! I am decided and will run" (George's account, published 1897, quoted in *Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII).

The campaign mobilised an unusually broad working-class coalition and alarmed the city's political establishment. Accepting the nomination at Cooper Union on 5 October, George told the crowd: "Why should there be such abject poverty in this city?... It is held by dogs in the manger who will not use it themselves nor allow anybody else to use it, unless he pays an enormous price for it.... We propose to put that tax on land exclusive of improvements, so that a man who is holding land vacant will have to pay as much for it as if he was using it" (*Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII).

## The Result

George finished **second**, defeated by the Tammany Hall Democrat Abram S. Hewitt, but **ahead of the third-place Republican candidate, the 28-year-old Theodore Roosevelt** — a future US president. The official canvass as the biography reports it: **Hewitt 90,552; George 68,110; Roosevelt 60,135** (*Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII, pp. 480–483; modern reference works commonly give Roosevelt's total as 60,435 — the two figures differ in one digit and the discrepancy between the 1900 biography and later compilations has not been adjudicated here). The biography also preserves the campaign's closing scene — a rain-soaked election-eve parade of labour-union marchers past Union Square. Georgists have long argued the count was affected by Tammany's machine — George's labour party had no poll watchers under the election law and had to print and distribute its own ballots — and George himself "believed at the time, and many circumstances afterwards confirmed his belief, that he had really been elected, but had been 'counted out'" (*Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII) — a belief widely shared in the movement, though it is George's camp's own claim, not an independently established fact. Regardless, polling ahead of a future president demonstrated the movement's genuine electoral reach. On election night George told his supporters: "This is the Bunker Hill. We have been driven back... but thank God, men of New York, we in this fight have won a victory... We have lit a fire that will never go out" (*Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII). Two nights after the vote, at Cooper Union, George gave the movement its lasting frame for the defeat: "It is not the end of the campaign; it is the beginning. We have fought the first skirmish."

Hewitt's campaign leaned on fear of the "anarchists, nihilists, communists, socialists" he claimed George's candidacy represented, invoking "the horrors of the French Revolution" (Hewitt's letter of acceptance, quoted in *Life of Henry George*, Third Period, Ch. VIII); George countered by naming Tammany boss Richard Croker as the real threat to the city.

## Aftermath

The campaign's Catholic dimension carried past election day: Father Edward McGlynn's public support for George — defying his Archbishop's order not to speak at a pre-election rally — led to his suspension within days of the vote and his excommunication the following year, a controversy George fought in his new weekly *The Standard* and which produced the [Anti-Poverty Society](/wiki/anti-poverty-society/), the movement's most visible vehicle through 1887. See [Edward McGlynn](/wiki/edward-mcglynn/) for the full episode.

## Significance

The 1886 race marked the peak of Georgism as a mass political force in the United States. George ran again in 1897 for mayor of the newly consolidated Greater New York but died four days before that election — see [The Last Campaign, 1897](/wiki/1897-nyc-mayoral-campaign/). The 1886 campaign remains the clearest historical case of land-value-tax ideas contending seriously for executive power in a major city.
