## Overview

Between the 1910s and 1930s, Georgists in California mounted a series of statewide ballot initiatives to enact a **single tax** on land values. The most consequential was a 1916 initiative led by **Luke North**, which proposed a "pure single tax" and won **31% of the statewide vote** — a result Georgist historians treat as the movement's high-water mark at the ballot box in the United States.[1] Even in defeat, the campaign kept the issue prominent, and Georgist assessment practices had real influence at the local level in California in the years around it: land's share of the property-tax assessment roll reportedly reached roughly 72% by 1917, well above the national norm.[1]

Later campaigns continued into the 1920s and 1930s. The lawyer and Stanford lecturer **Jackson H. Ralston**, drafter of the federal Ralston-Nolan land-tax bill (1919), led further California single-tax initiative efforts in this period, though public support for the single tax nationally waned as American politics turned more conservative after 1920.[1] [CITATION NEEDED: a precise date and vote total for Ralston's later California campaign(s)].
