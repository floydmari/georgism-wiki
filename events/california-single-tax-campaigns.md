## Overview

Between the 1910s and 1930s, Georgists in California mounted a series of statewide ballot initiatives to enact a **single tax** on land values. The most consequential was a 1916 initiative led by **Luke North**, which proposed a "pure single tax" and won **31% of the statewide vote** — a result Georgist historians treat as the movement's high-water mark at the ballot box in the United States.[1] Even in defeat, the campaign kept the issue prominent, and Georgist assessment practices had real influence at the local level in California in the years around it: land's share of the property-tax assessment roll reportedly reached roughly 72% by 1917, well above the national norm.[1]

Later campaigns continued into the 1920s and 1930s. The lawyer and Stanford lecturer **Jackson H. Ralston**, drafter of the federal Ralston-Nolan land-tax bill (1919), led further California single-tax initiative efforts in this period, though public support for the single tax nationally waned as American politics turned more conservative after 1920.[1] Ralston's culminating effort was the "California Plan of 1938," which reached the ballot as **Proposition 20** at the general election of **November 8, 1938** — an initiative to exempt homes valued up to $1,000, repeal the sales tax, and phase out taxes on improvements and personal property. It was decisively defeated, receiving **372,386 "Yes" votes (16.86%) against 1,836,585 "No" votes (83.14%)**, marking the last major single-tax ballot campaign in the United States.[3]
