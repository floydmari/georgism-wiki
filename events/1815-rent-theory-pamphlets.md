## Overview

In February 1815, four British economists — **Thomas Malthus**, **Edward West**, **Robert Torrens**, and **[David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/)** — independently published pamphlets within about three weeks of one another, each setting out essentially the same theory of **differential rent**.[1][2] The occasion was a Parliamentary inquiry into why grain prices had collapsed after the Napoleonic Wars, feeding directly into that year's Corn Law debate ([repeal of the Corn Laws](/wiki/repeal-of-the-corn-laws/)).[2] Malthus's *An Inquiry into the Nature and Progress of Rent* appeared first; Ricardo's own contribution, *An Essay on the Influence of a Low Price of Corn on the Profits of Stock*, was written partly as a response to Malthus.[1][2]

## The Shared Argument

All four writers converged on the same mechanism, later canonized as the [Law of Rent](/wiki/law-of-rent/): wartime disruption of grain imports had forced English farmers onto progressively less fertile land to meet demand, and the rent commanded by superior land was the differential surplus between its output and the output of that marginal, rent-free land.[1][2] The economist James Anderson had anticipated the idea as early as 1777, but his work went largely unnoticed at the time — the 1815 pamphlets are what fixed differential rent theory in the discipline.[2]

## Significance for Georgism

Ricardo's fuller elaboration of the argument two years later, in Chapter 2 of *[On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation](/wiki/law-of-rent/)* (1817), became the canonical statement historians credit as "the first appearance of the marginal principle in economic theory" (Blaug, [*Economic Theory in Retrospect*](/wiki/economic-theory-in-retrospect/), Ch. 3, §8).[3] It is this 1817 restatement — built on the 1815 pamphlet debate — that [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) generalized six decades later into the argument at the heart of *Progress and Poverty*.
