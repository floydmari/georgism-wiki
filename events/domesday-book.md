## Overview

The **Domesday Book** is the manuscript record of a nationwide survey of landholdings and their annual value across most of England, commissioned by King **William I** at his Council of Gloucester at Christmas 1085 and carried out in 1086.[1] Royal commissioners were sent into the localities to record, manor by manor, who held each piece of land, its resources (ploughs, labourers, livestock, mills), and — critically for a Georgist reading — its **annual value**, both at the time of the 1066 conquest and in 1086.[1] The survey's chief purpose was fiscal: to give the Crown a systematic basis for assessing tax (geld) and other dues, and to settle disputes over landholding after two decades of Norman resettlement.[1] The results survive in two volumes, Great Domesday (covering most of southern and central England) and the more detailed Little Domesday (East Anglia), together recording over 13,000 places.[1]

Fred Harrison's *Ricardo's Law* cites Domesday as evidence that the Ricardian rent gradient — higher land value concentrated near centres of population and trade, falling off toward the periphery — held even in an 11th-century agrarian economy: of roughly £73,000 in total annual rental revenue recorded, Wessex alone contributed about £32,000, while Yorkshire and Lancashire together contributed only around £1,200.[2] Harrison treats the survey primarily as evidence for this spatial pattern rather than as a land-value-tax precedent as such, but the underlying administrative fact — that an 11th-century state could assess the annual rental value of virtually every parcel of land in the kingdom without modern statistical or computing tools — is also cited by Georgists as a rebuttal to the claim that land assessment is impractical.[2]
