## Overview

The **Housing Act 1980**, passed by the Conservative government led by Margaret Thatcher, created a statutory "Right to Buy" allowing most public-sector ("council") housing tenants in England and Wales to purchase their homes from local authorities at a discount to market value — initially 33% to 50% of value (up to 70% for flats), rising with years of tenancy.[1] Environment Secretary Michael Heseltine, who steered the legislation, later said "no single piece of legislation has enabled the transfer of so much capital wealth from the state to the people."[1] Uptake was rapid: roughly **1.5 million council homes had been sold by 1990**, and one recent estimate puts the number of council homes sold in England since 1980 at around 1.9 million, with a combined current market value of about £430 billion.[1]

## A Land-Wealth Transfer

Because a large share of a council home's value is the land underneath it rather than the depreciating structure, Right to Buy is a case of the state transferring, at a large discount, a stock of publicly held land-backed housing wealth into private hands — the reverse motion of [land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/), which aims to route land-value gains toward the public rather than away from it. *[Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing](/wiki/ryan-collins-rethinking-land-housing/)* cites Right to Buy, alongside financial deregulation and tightening planning constraints, as one of the post-1970 policy shifts that reduced the UK's low-cost housing stock and moved subsidy from supply-side ("bricks and mortar") housebuilding toward demand-side subsidies such as Housing Benefit (Ch. 4, §4.4, Box 4.5).[2]

## Honest Limits

Right to Buy remains politically popular and expanded homeownership for many purchasing tenants. The main dispute concerns long-run supply effects — for decades local authorities faced restrictions on using sale receipts to fund replacement council housing — and the resulting reduction in the social-housing stock, not the underlying transfer-of-wealth facts.
