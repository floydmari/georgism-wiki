## Overview

Around the turn of the 20th century, Georgist reformers founded **single-tax colonies** — intentional communities organised to demonstrate Henry George's ideas in miniature. Rather than wait for national reform, they held land in common and charged residents a single levy on land value.

## Fairhope and Arden

- **Fairhope, Alabama** (founded **1894**) was established by the [Fairhope Single Tax Corporation](/wiki/fairhope-single-tax-corporation/). It began as the **Fairhope Industrial Association**, formed by members of a single-tax club in **Des Moines, Iowa** led by **Ernest B. Gaston**, who "wanted to do more than just discuss the single tax theory."[1][2] A site committee examined locations across five states before the group settled on the eastern shore of Mobile Bay, and in November 1894 twenty-eight colonists assembled there to begin the community; the name records a founder's remark that there was "a fair hope that the experiment would succeed."[2] The Association incorporated in Alabama under its present name in 1904.[2] The Corporation owns the land and leases it to residents on 99-year renewable leases, charging an annual rent based on land value in lieu of the land being privately owned — and uses the proceeds for community purposes, including bayfront parks, a library, and a history museum.[2] Fairhope grew into a thriving town, and the Corporation still operates today as a 501(c)(4) non-profit holding roughly 4,500 acres, about a quarter of the modern city.[2] Notably, [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) himself never endorsed the Fairhope experiment and declined every invitation to visit it.[2]
- **Arden, Delaware** (founded **1900**) by Frank Stephens and Will Price as an arts-and-crafts single-tax community; it too retains its land-rent system and remains an incorporated village today.

## An In-Movement Failure: Fairhope Was Whites-Only

The colonies' longevity should not obscure a documented failure in the movement's flagship American experiment. The Fairhope Single Tax Corporation held its land for **whites only** for most of its history. Historian Paul M. Gaston — a civil-rights scholar and grandson of Fairhope's founder E.B. Gaston — records that the colonists "wanted equality for all in their 'model community', but feared that doing so 'would likely mean destruction of the colony' and therefore determined that Fairhope Single Tax Colony property was for whites only," rationalised by the belief that "at bottom, racial prejudice was a function of economic injustice," so exclusion was framed as "hastening the day when racism might disappear."[3] The full episode, including the Nancy Lewis eviction, is documented on this wiki's [Land and the Black–white Wealth Gap](/wiki/land-and-the-black-white-wealth-gap/) narrative, which argues that a page on the Georgist movement's community-building tradition is not honest about that tradition if it omits this record.

## Significance

The colonies are living, century-long demonstrations that community land ownership with a [land value](/wiki/land-value-tax/) charge is workable. They are the most tangible legacy of the [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) movement, surviving long after the movement's national political peak faded. Their founding was helped along by the movement's great financier, the soap magnate [Joseph Fels](/wiki/joseph-fels/), who contributed to both Fairhope and Arden and funded a parallel English land colony at **Mayland, Essex** (1906), whose cottages were designed by architect Charles Holden.[4]
