## Overview

**Enclosure** (also spelled "inclosure") is the process, spanning roughly six centuries of English history, by which land historically farmed in open, unfenced strips or held as common pasture and waste — subject to shared customary rights of grazing, gleaning, and fuel-gathering — was consolidated into fenced or hedged plots reserved for the exclusive use of a single owner.[1] Early enclosure by informal agreement or unilateral landlord action dates at least to the 12th century, and accelerated sharply under the Tudors as rising wool prices made converting arable land to sheep pasture profitable, provoking royal "tillage acts" (1489–1597) intended to slow the resulting depopulation.[1] From the early 17th century, enclosure increasingly proceeded through **Acts of Parliament**: the first Parliamentary enclosure act was passed in 1604, for Radipole, Dorset, and between 1604 and 1914 Parliament passed more than 5,200 such acts, enclosing roughly 6.8 million acres — about one-fifth of the total land area of England.[1] The process is a central historical reference point for the Georgist account of **[land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/)**: it supplies a documented, non-hypothetical case in which private land titles were created and expanded through a political and sometimes coercive process rather than solely through voluntary exchange or productive desert.[2]

## From Custom to Statute: Medieval and Tudor Enclosure

Under the medieval **open-field system**, which dominated a broad band of lowland England from Yorkshire and Lincolnshire down through the Midlands to south-central England, arable land was divided into large common fields farmed in narrow strips under a communally managed rotation, with hay meadows, "waste," and common pasture available to tenants under customary rights such as pasture, pannage (feeding pigs on woodland mast), and estovers (gathering wood).[1] The Statute of Merton (1235) first gave manorial lords the legal power to consolidate and enclose their own strips, provided sufficient common land remained for tenants — an opening that widened considerably under the Tudors, when the profitability of wool drove landlords to convert arable land to sheep pasture, evicting tenants in the process.[1] Alarmed at the resulting vagrancy and the weakening of the rural workforce, Parliament passed a series of "tillage acts" between 1489 and 1597 attempting to restrain conversion to pasture — enforced, inconveniently, by the same landowning class that benefited from evasion — and Sir Thomas More's *Utopia* (1516) satirised the process in a famous passage describing sheep "that were wont to be so meek and tame, and so small eaters, now, as I heard say, be become so great devourers and so wild, that they eat up, and swallow down the very men themselves" (Ralph Robinson's 1551 translation).[1][3] The Tillage Act 1533 capped flock sizes at 2,400 sheep and the Taxation Act 1549 taxed sheep and cloth, but it was rising grain prices after 1550, not statute, that eventually made arable farming attractive again and slowed pasture conversion.[1]

## Parliamentary Enclosure, c.1604–1914

From the 1750s, enclosure by individual Act of Parliament became the dominant method, culminating in the general **Inclosure Act 1773** and the **Inclosure Act 1845**, which created a standing Board of Inclosure Commissioners empowered to approve enclosures without a fresh Act for each parish.[1] Parliamentary enclosure both consolidated existing open-field strips and privatised common "wastes" — fens, marshes, heathland, downland, and moor — that had previously supported grazing, fuel-gathering, and subsistence use by cottagers and the landless poor.[1] Compensation was typically offered to those with a recognised legal interest (as an allotment of enclosed land or a cash payment), but usually amounted to a smaller and poorer-quality holding than what was lost, and — this is the historically contested point discussed below — those without a formal legal claim to common rights, however long-standing their customary use, generally received nothing at all.[1][4] The process peaked between roughly 1760 and 1832, a period some historians treat as substantially completing the dissolution of the English open-field peasant community and pushing surplus rural labour toward the growing industrial towns.[1]

The financial stakes were large and are documented in surviving rent records: the economic historian D. N. McCloskey's tabulation of villages enclosed between 1765 and 1805 shows rents roughly doubling or more at Coney Weston (Suffolk), Riseley (Bedfordshire, 90–157%), and Queensborough (Leicestershire, 92–130%), among others — direct evidence of the value transfer enclosure created for whoever held the resulting title.[1]

## Resistance and Enclosure Riots

Enclosure provoked recurring, sometimes armed, popular resistance across four centuries. Land rights featured in **Jack Cade's Rebellion** (1450) and became a central grievance in **Kett's Rebellion** (Norfolk, 1549), in which rebels under Robert Kett threw down enclosure hedges before their eventual defeat.[1] The **Midland Revolt** of 1607 — centred on villages in Leicestershire, Warwickshire, and Northamptonshire and led by "Captain Pouch" (John Reynolds), who claimed royal authority to level enclosures and promised protesters supernatural protection from the contents of a pouch that, on his capture, proved to contain only a piece of mouldy cheese — gave rise to the terms "leveller" and "digger" for those who tore down enclosing fences and ditches, terms later claimed by the Civil War–era political movements of the same names.[1] An anonymous eighteenth-century verse, still quoted as a summary of the underlying grievance, captured the asymmetry commoners perceived between petty theft and the legal seizure of the commons itself: "The law locks up the man or woman / Who steals the goose from off the common, / But lets the greater felon loose / Who steals the common from the goose."[1]

## The Historiographical Debate: Dispossession or Efficiency?

Professional historians disagree sharply over how to weigh enclosure's costs and benefits, and this wiki's companion research page on Simon Fairlie's *[A Short History of Enclosure in Britain](/wiki/fairlie-short-history-enclosure/)* discusses the debate at length; only the outline is repeated here. The Fabian historians **J. L. and Barbara Hammond**, writing in 1911, framed enclosure as ruinous to the rural poor, concluding that it was "fatal to three classes: the small farmer, the cottager and the squatter" and that "before enclosure the cottager was a labourer with land; after enclosure [he] was a labourer without land."[1] Later economic historians **J. D. Chambers and G. E. Mingay** challenged this reading, arguing the Hammonds overstated the human costs and that enclosure delivered genuine productivity gains — more food for a growing population and, on balance, more rural employment — a view with roots in Arnold Toynbee's account of enclosure enabling more efficient crop rotation and ending "constant quarrels over boundaries."[1] Since the late twentieth century a further wave of scholarship, including **Gregory Clark and Anthony Clark's** 2001 study of English charity land records, has complicated both sides: using a large sample of parish charity land as a proxy for the wider landscape, they find common land was already a minority of English land by 1600 (about 27%, and perhaps only a third even in 1500), that most of it was "stinted" (restricted to those already holding formal property rights, not open to the landless), and that common land genuinely accessible to the landless poor was only about 4% of England's land, much of it marginal — implying both the productivity case *for* enclosure and the dispossession case *against* it were, in aggregate national terms, smaller than either the Hammond or Toynbee traditions suggest, though the concentration of loss in specific communities could still be severe.[4] The dispute is over magnitude and distribution, not over whether Parliamentary enclosure occurred or whether the process was shaped disproportionately by those with existing political power: Fairlie's essay reports that committees reviewing Oxfordshire enclosure bills were dominated by local, mostly landowning MPs, and that allocations of enclosed land to labourers and cottagers were minimal.[2]

## Georgist Significance

Enclosure is not, by itself, a Georgist argument — none of the historians above write from a land-value-taxation standpoint, and the historical record of dispossession does not on its own establish that rent capture is the appropriate remedy going forward.[2] Its relevance to this wiki is as **historical backdrop**: it documents that a large share of the concentrated landownership Georgists describe as [land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) has a traceable institutional and political history — acts of a Parliament substantially composed of landowners, backed by force and eviction in the Tudor period, formalised by statute from 1604 to 1914 — rather than an origin story of purely voluntary exchange among equals.[1][2] Henry George himself, writing after the main wave of Parliamentary enclosure had concluded, argued in works such as *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* that whatever the historical process by which land titles arose, the ongoing economic case for taxing land value rests on the nature of land as a fixed, unproduced factor rather than on any particular claim about how existing titles were acquired — a distinction worth holding onto when enclosure is invoked rhetorically, since the strength of the land-value-tax argument does not depend on every historical land transfer having been unjust.[5]
