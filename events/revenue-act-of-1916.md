## Overview

The **Revenue Act of 1916** was a United States federal law, signed on September 8, 1916, that raised income and estate tax rates to fund military preparedness ahead of US entry into the First World War. It raised the bottom income-tax rate from 1% to 2% and the top marginal rate to 15% on incomes above $2 million (up from a 7% top rate under the 1913 act), and it created the first federal **estate tax**, with a $50,000 exemption and rates from 1% to 10%.[1] According to Gaffney and Harrison's *The Corruption of Economics*, the 1916 act — and the broader early federal income-tax push of that decade — had a drafting hand from **Warren Worth Bailey**, a single-tax Congressman from Pennsylvania, who saw a progressive income tax as a way to shift federal revenue away from wage income and toward unearned income; the corpus's summary of the book describes it as "the first Federal income tax, drafted by Georgist Congressman Bailey," which exempted most wage income.[2]

Bailey's own single-tax sympathies were not the mainstream motivation for the act, which was primarily a wartime revenue and defense-preparedness measure supported by the Wilson administration; independent tax-history sources make no mention of Bailey in connection with the 1916 act specifically, crediting him instead with an earlier flanking maneuver in the 1913 tariff-and-income-tax fight.[1][3] The episode nonetheless illustrates a broader point in the Georgist historiography: that the graduated federal income tax, rather than a tax on land rent, became the vehicle through which "unearned income" language entered American fiscal policy — a substitution Gaffney and Harrison treat as part of the single-tax movement's absorption into, and dilution by, the broader progressive income-tax cause. ## Sources

1. *Revenue Act of 1916* (Act of September 8, 1916, ch. 463), 39 Stat. 756 — the primary law text (Statutes at Large, 64th Congress, Sess. I). The income-tax schedule (Title I) confirms the graduated "additional tax" rising to "thirteen per centum … upon the amount by which such total net income exceeds $2,000,000," which combined with the 2% normal tax yields the 15% top marginal rate (39 Stat. 756–757). The estate tax (Title I-A, Sec. 201, 39 Stat. 777) is "imposed upon the transfer of the net estate of every decedent dying after the passage of this Act," at "One per centum of the amount of such net estate not in excess of $50,000" graduating to "Ten per centum of the amount by which such net estate exceeds $5,000,000" — confirming the 1%–10% estate-tax schedule and the $50,000 first bracket.
