## Overview

In 1897, eleven years after his famous [1886 run](/wiki/1886-nyc-mayoral-election/), [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) ran a second time for Mayor of New York — now the newly consolidated **Greater New York**, formed that year by the merger of Manhattan, Brooklyn, and the outer boroughs into the world's second-largest city. He entered the race against his physicians' explicit warning that the strain would likely kill him, and died of a stroke four days before the election.[1]

## Decision to Run

George's health had been declining through 1897; his married daughter Jennie died suddenly on 2 May, a blow from which those close to him said he never fully recovered.[1] When labour and reform elements pressed him to run against Tammany's Robert Van Wyck, independent Republican Seth Low, and Republican machine candidate Benjamin Tracy, his doctor, M. R. Leverson, told him directly that running would "most probably prove fatal." George's reply, as recorded by his son: "I have got to die. How can I die better than serving humanity? Besides, so dying will do more for the cause than anything I am likely to be able to do in the rest of my life."[1] His wife Annie, asked by friends to dissuade him, refused: "He must live his life in his own way and at whatever sacrifice his sense of duty requires; and I shall give him all I can — devotion." George invoked her own words to Michael Davitt fifteen years earlier during the 1882 Phoenix Park crisis — that a man in danger "should go to Ireland by the first train, and be a leader to the people" — asking her now to hold him to the same standard.[1]

## The Campaign

George accepted the nomination of "The Party of Thomas Jefferson" at Cooper Union on 5 October 1897, arriving "with thin body and ashen face" and nearly fainting on the way to the hall, but spoke with his old defiance: "Whether it be success or failure matters nothing to me... To make the fight is honour, whether it be for success or failure."[1] He went on to speak at three to five meetings nightly for three weeks — more than the other three candidates combined, by his son's account — supported financially chiefly by [Tom L. Johnson](/wiki/tom-l-johnson/) and August Lewis, the two men to whom George dedicated his unfinished last book.[1]

## Death

On the night of 28–29 October 1897, after his last full night of campaign speeches, George died of a stroke (apoplexy) in his suite at the Union Square Hotel in the early hours of the morning, four days before the election.[1] His son Henry George Jr. was put forward to carry the ticket in his place but drew little support.[1] An estimated 100,000 people passed his casket lying in state at the Grand Central Palace, with another 100,000 turned away; the funeral procession that evening passed City Hall — where he might have presided had he lived — before crossing to Brooklyn, where he was buried at Green-Wood Cemetery beside his daughter Jennie.[1] Father [Edward McGlynn](/wiki/edward-mcglynn/), who had officiated at Jennie's wedding and funeral, spoke at George's funeral service alongside clergy of several denominations.[1]

## Significance

George's death mid-campaign, four days before a contested municipal election, is without close parallel in American mayoral politics; contemporary newspapers across the political spectrum treated it as a genuinely national and international event, with tributes arriving from Britain, France, Germany, Denmark, Australia, Japan, and China.[1] The 1897 campaign closes the Georgist movement's period of direct electoral contention for New York's highest office; George's death did not end the single-tax movement, but it removed the one figure capable of holding its factions together as a unified political force in the city.
