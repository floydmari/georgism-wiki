## Overview

In *The Corruption of Economics* (1994), Mason Gaffney and Fred Harrison list three early-20th-century American academics — Scott Nearing (University of Pennsylvania, 1915), Elisha Benjamin Andrews (Brown University, 1897), and Allen H. Eaton (University of Oregon, 1917) — as casualties of institutional hostility toward land-value-tax and single-tax sympathizers, part of a broader argument that neoclassical economics was reshaped to marginalize Henry George.[1] Independent historical accounts of each case, however, cite different proximate causes — Nearing's outspoken opposition to child labor before a trustee board dominated by bankers and executives, Andrews's advocacy of "free silver" monetary policy, and Eaton's pacifism during World War I — without independently confirming a land-tax-specific motive, making Gaffney and Harrison's "purged for Georgism" framing a contested interpretation rather than an established consensus (see [Narrative: The Corruption of Economics](/wiki/the-corruption-of-economics/) for the wider evidentiary debate over the book's intentionality thesis).

## The Three Cases

**Scott Nearing** was dismissed from the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania in June 1915 when trustees declined to renew his contract despite a favorable faculty recommendation; contemporary and modern accounts attribute the decision to his radical economic views and prominent opposition to child labor, and the episode became a landmark case in the founding of the American tenure system.[2]

**Elisha Benjamin Andrews**, president of Brown University, resigned in 1897 after members of the university's governing Corporation objected to his public advocacy of "free silver" monetary policy, a controversy that reportedly cost the university donor support. Twenty-four Brown professors and hundreds of alumni petitioned for his reinstatement on academic-freedom grounds, and Andrews withdrew his resignation.[3]

**Allen H. Eaton** was expelled from the University of Oregon faculty in 1917 for his pacifism during World War I, a dismissal publicly criticized by the novelist Upton Sinclair.[4]

## Significance and Caveats

Gaffney and Harrison present these three cases as part of a wider pattern of "institutional mechanisms" — alongside the founding of the American Economic Association by George's personal adversaries and [Richard T. Ely's](/wiki/richard-t-ely/) railroad-funded land-economics institute — that they argue marginalized George's ideas within academic economics.[1] The book is explicitly polemical in structure and intent, and its specific claim that these three dismissals turned on Georgist sympathy — rather than on the child-labor, free-silver, and pacifism controversies documented in independent sources — is not corroborated by those sources. Readers should treat the "purged for Georgism" framing as [Mason Gaffney's](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) and Harrison's interpretation of a broader climate of hostility toward heterodox economists, not as a settled finding about the specific cause of any one dismissal.
