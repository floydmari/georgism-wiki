## Overview

The **Dissolution of the Monasteries** was a set of administrative and legal processes, beginning in 1535–36 and largely complete by 1541, by which King Henry VIII disbanded all Catholic monasteries, priories, convents, and friaries in England and Wales, seized their assets, and — critically for the land market — sold most of the resulting land off rapidly to fund his 1540s military campaigns, rather than retaining it as a permanent Crown estate.[1] Monastic houses had held roughly one-third of all land in England, and that land had previously been legally unsellable (bound by mortmain and perpetual tenure), so its sudden, wholesale conversion into ordinary marketable freehold property was economically unprecedented rather than a marginal change at the margin of an existing market.[2]

Historian R.H. Tawney's classic thesis — that the Dissolution's resale of monastic land drove a "rise of the Gentry," a new class of commercially minded landowners who bought, consolidated, and actively managed estates for profit rather than holding them under static feudal custom — has since been tested econometrically. Heldring, Robinson & Vollmer (NBER Working Paper 21450, 2015, revised 2021) find that English parishes more exposed to the Dissolution subsequently show a documented rise of Gentry landownership, higher agricultural innovation and yields, a larger non-agricultural workforce, and, ultimately, higher levels of industrialization — evidence consistent with land's shift from feudal custom to market allocation being a real driver of English commercialization.[2]
