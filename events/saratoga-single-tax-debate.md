## Overview

On **September 5, 1890**, at Saratoga, New York, the single tax got its formal hearing
before the organized American social-science establishment: a set-piece debate hosted
by the **American Social Science Association** in which [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)
faced his academic critics in person. [John Bates Clark](/wiki/john-bates-clark/)
confronted George on "The Moral Basis of Property in Land"
([Gaffney's account](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/), essay Ch. 3),
and [E. R. A. Seligman](/wiki/era-seligman/) delivered an address for the academic
opposition.[1][2] The papers were published as *The Single Tax Discussion, Held at
Saratoga, Sept. 5, 1890* (American Social Science Association; F. B. Sanborn, ed.;
Concord, Mass.).[2] The session crystallized the George-versus-academy antagonism that
[Gaffney's corruption thesis](/wiki/the-corruption-of-economics/) treats as formative
for American economics — an interpretation to read as Gaffney's, though the debate
itself is documented record. Gaffney's account adds one further biographical detail
to that framing: Clark had been recruited to Columbia by Seth Low, who was at the time
preparing his own run for Mayor of New York against George — placing an academic
appointment inside the same 1886–90 political rivalry the debate grew out of.[1]

## The Session and Its Wider Cast

Thirteen pieces were read and debated that day — five speeches for the single tax
(S. B. Clarke, Louis F. Post, William Lloyd Garrison, James R. Carret, and George
himself) and six against, including Seligman, Clark, Edward Atkinson, W. T. Harris, and
Brown University's President E. Benjamin Andrews.[2] The published proceedings run to
roughly 55,000 words across all thirteen; the wiki's primary-text page carries only the
~18,000-word George–Seligman core, described below.

## The George–Seligman Core

The confrontation the event is best remembered for ran on two exchanges the same day.
In his opening address, Seligman conceded the narrow economic point — that a tax on
land values "will fall wholly on the owner," agreeing there with Adam Smith, Ricardo,
and Mill — before building his case against the single tax on the canons of
universality and equality and a compensation dilemma he pressed at length. George's
floor reply denied that compensation was owed, arguing the single tax only reaches
value accruing in the future rather than "settling old scores," and sharpened the
"landless man" objection in a direct exchange with Edward Atkinson. Seligman's
same-evening rejoinder pushed back point by point, extending the unearned-increment
argument beyond land to railroad bonds and appreciating art, and predicted the tax
would burden farmers while doing nothing for crowded, already-competitive urban rents.
The wiki's [George–Seligman primary-text page](/wiki/saratoga-single-tax-debate-1890/)
transcribes all three pieces in full; a fuller account of the exchange's content is on
[Seligman's own page](/wiki/era-seligman/).[2]
