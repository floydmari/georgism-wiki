<h2>The Claim</h2>
<p>An increasing share of corporate profit in the United States (and, more weakly, other developed economies) is <strong>economic rent</strong> — returns above what is needed to compensate capital and effort in a competitive market — rather than the ordinary reward for investment. This matters for the Georgist framework because it extends the classical rent analysis from land to other non-reproducible positions: market power, network position, regulatory protection, and control of scarce intangibles.</p>
<h2>The Case, Guided</h2>
<p><strong>Start with the three independent measurements.</strong> The claim's backbone is that different methods on different data agree. <a href="/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/">De Loecker, Eeckhout &amp; Unger</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-1">№1</a> estimate <strong>markups</strong> from firm microdata: from ~21% above marginal cost in 1980 to ~61% by 2016, concentrated in the upper tail. <a href="/wiki/barkai-declining-shares/">Barkai</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-2">№2</a> decomposes <strong>factor shares</strong>: labor <em>and</em> required-return capital shares both fell while the residual pure-profit share rose sharply. <a href="/wiki/power-frerick-excess-returns/">Power &amp; Frerick</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-3">№3</a> find the same in the government's own <strong>tax data</strong>: the normal-return share of the corporate tax base fell from ~40% to ~25%. A fourth angle — <a href="/wiki/furman-orszag-firm-rents/">Furman &amp; Orszag's</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-4">№4</a> firm-level <strong>return dispersion</strong> (90th-percentile returns more than 5× the median, winners persistent) — points the same way.</p>
<p><strong>Then the corroboration from institutions and models.</strong> <a href="/wiki/akcigit-ates-business-dynamism/">Akcigit &amp; Ates</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-5">№5</a> show the trends cohere in a single growth model driven by slowing knowledge diffusion. The IMF's cross-country excess-profit estimates <a class="evref" href="#ev-6">№6</a> corroborate from an entirely separate dataset, and the <a href="/wiki/cea-2016-market-power/">2016 CEA brief</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-7">№7</a> is the official-institution confirmation. The remaining supporters explain <em>why</em> rents persist and where they now live: political entrenchment <a class="evref" href="#ev-8">№8</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-9">№9</a>, the wage side <a class="evref" href="#ev-10">№10</a>, and the intangible/platform frontier <a class="evref" href="#ev-11">№11</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-12">№12</a>.</p>
<p>A rare admission-against-interest rides alongside: OpenAI's own 2026 <a href="/wiki/openai-industrial-policy-intelligence-age/">policy paper</a> concedes "a risk that the economic gains concentrate within a small number of firms like OpenAI" — corroborating context for the concentration premise, though it never argues those gains are rents (context, so not a rail card).</p>
<h2>The Counter-Case</h2>
<p>The main rival reading sits in the rail in red. <a href="/wiki/crouzet-eberly-intangibles/">Crouzet &amp; Eberly</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-13">№13</a> show concentration and high measured profits partly reflect <strong>intangible capital</strong> that standard accounts undercount — productivity-driven concentration in consumer sectors, markup-driven in healthcare, both in high-tech. The <a href="/wiki/superstar-firms/">superstar-firms</a> literature (<a href="/wiki/autor-superstar-firms/">Autor et al. 2020</a> — wired as a challenger on <a href="/wiki/capital-share-rise-is-land/">the capital-share page</a>, cited here as context) similarly attributes rising concentration partly to winner-take-most competition among unusually productive firms. These are not fully exculpatory — rents on intangibles and network positions are still returns to scarcity and position, which is why <a href="/wiki/mazzucato-mapping-rents/">Mazzucato et al.</a> <a class="evref" href="#ev-11">№11</a> treat them as the modern frontier of rent analysis — but they mean the <em>size</em> of the pure-rent component is genuinely contested. The second challenger <a class="evref" href="#ev-14">№14</a> is a scope cap, not a rebuttal: the Bank of Canada's timing test warns against stretching this long-run claim into a "greedflation" story about any particular price surge.</p>
<h2>Strength of Evidence</h2>
<p><strong>Moderate–strong that the profit rise is real and abnormal</strong> — the markup, pure-profit, and dispersion methodologies <a class="evref" href="#ev-1">№1</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-2">№2</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-3">№3</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-4">№4</a> are independent and agree on direction, with official-institution corroboration <a class="evref" href="#ev-6">№6</a><a class="evref" href="#ev-7">№7</a>. <strong>Contested on interpretation:</strong> how much is extraction versus mismeasured returns to intangible investment <a class="evref" href="#ev-13">№13</a> remains an active dispute; the honest reading is "a substantial but not precisely known share is rent." The rail also makes the composition visible: roughly half the supporting cards are direct empirical measurements; the rest are mechanism, synthesis, and framework — corroboration, not independent confirmation.</p>
<h2>Why It Matters</h2>
<p>Henry George's analysis located unearned income in land; the modern rent literature finds structurally similar unearned income in corporate position. The policy translation is the same in spirit — tax the rent, not the production — though instruments differ (competition policy, spectrum/resource auctions, platform regulation, and rent-focused taxation — see <a href="/wiki/allowance-for-corporate-equity/">allowance for corporate equity</a> and <a href="/wiki/cash-flow-tax/">cash-flow tax</a> — rather than a land value tax). See <a href="/wiki/economic-rent/">economic rent</a>, <a href="/wiki/rent-seeking/">rent-seeking</a>, and the narrative <a href="/wiki/the-rentier-economy/">The Rentier Economy</a>.</p>
<h2>Context — Not in the Rail</h2>
<p>Adjacent evidence, deliberately excluded from the rail because it is not wired as direct support or challenge: <a href="/wiki/azar-schmalz-tecu-common-ownership/">Azar, Schmalz &amp; Tecu on common ownership</a> (a concrete, contested rent-extraction channel), <a href="/wiki/rent-seeking-drags-growth/">rent-seeking drags growth</a> (the growth cost of the rents documented here), <a href="/wiki/philippon-finance-efficiency/">Philippon's finance-efficiency puzzle</a> (the parallel evidence in the financial sector), and <a href="/wiki/kerspien-madsen-strulik-buildings-labor-share/">Kerspien et al.</a> (a non-market-power account of the same labour-share decline — a reminder it is over-determined).</p>
