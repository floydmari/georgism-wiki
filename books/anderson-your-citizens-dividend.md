## What It Is

*Your Citizen's Dividend: How to double your wage, dramatically reduce
financial stress and improve living standards for everyone* is a free 16-page
advocacy eBook by [Phillip J. Anderson](/wiki/phillip-j-anderson/) (©2024
[Property Sharemarket Economics](/wiki/property-sharemarket-economics/)), distributed via his
[citdiv.org](https://citdiv.org/) microsite in six languages. It is the
program statement behind the site: collect society's
[economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) through a **special-purpose vehicle
(SPV)** modeled on the UK **Crown Estate**, pay it out as a per-capita
[citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/), and phase out
"all taxes... Every single one" (p. 8). **Advocacy source:** cited on this wiki to
represent Anderson's own position (EDITORIAL source-hierarchy class 6), not
as evidence.

## The Argument, Chapter by Chapter

1. **Government as wealth-extractor** (pp. 3–4): a catalogue of state
   corruption and repression (1MDB, Saudi arrests, Chinese censorship) framing
   the pitch:
   "eliminating the need for governments, all taxes and all of our paid politicians is really simple to do" (p. 3).
2. **The Domesday lineage** (pp. 4–6): William the Conqueror's 1086 survey as
   history's model rent assessment; London's aristocratic estates (the
   Grosvenor 500 acres → Mayfair/Belgravia) as the private-capture
   counterfactual.
3. **The Crown Estate as the working model** (pp. 7–9): since George III's
   1760 surrender of the royal lands, "all of the Crown Estate's Rents are
   returned to the UK Treasury" (p. 8), with the monarch's ~15% stipend recast
   by Anderson as history's first "citizen's dividend"; the eBook cites
   ~£330m collected in 2018.
4. **The mechanism claim** (pp. 10–12): "All of society's economic and social
   gains ultimately end up in land value" (p. 10) — an
   [ATCOR](/wiki/atcor/)-family claim, stated as fact where the wiki's own
   [ATCOR page](/wiki/atcor/) carries it as contested theory. Illustrations:
   Churchill's 1909 toll-bridge story (rents on the south bank rose by the
   amount of the abolished toll — see
   [Churchill's land speeches](/wiki/churchill-peoples-rights/)); *Big Issue*
   sellers bidding for high-traffic corners; and the claim that a UBI funded
   from ordinary taxes "will... simply make the non-landowners and workers
   even poorer" because it capitalizes into rents (p. 11).
5. **Q&A** (pp. 13–16): Alaska's PFD (given as US$1,022/yr — a stale figure;
   see note below), Norway's GPF, Singapore/Temasek as "the special purpose
   vehicle created specifically for the task of collecting the entire
   Singapore Rent" (p. 13), Australian arithmetic (ABS total land value
   ~A$10.7T at March 2024, rent ≈ 5% ≈ A$535B/yr, plus
   [Fitzgerald's resource-rents estimate](/wiki/geoism/)), and Swiss-style
   semi-professional parliament as the end-state governance (with the eBook's
   own correction that Swiss parliamentarians are in fact compensated, p. 16).

## Accuracy Notes (T1 scan, 2026-07-06)

- **Stale Alaska figure:** the PFD is given as "US$1022" per year (p. 13) in a
  ©2024 edition — verified against the PFD Division's historical payment table
  (pfd.alaska.gov, "Summary of Dividend Applications & Payments," retrieved
  2026-07-10): $1,022.00 is exactly and uniquely the **2016** dividend (2015:
  $2,072; 2017: $1,100), so the eBook's figure was eight years stale at
  publication. Recent dividends have been substantially higher (2022: $3,284;
  2023: $1,312; 2024: $1,702). The wiki's evidence on dividends lives at
  [resource-rent dividends work](/wiki/resource-rent-dividends-work/).
- **The Great Smoky Mountains study** (pp. 11–12): the eBook's account of the
  Cherokee casino-dividend natural experiment (better schooling, less crime,
  fewer mental-health problems, poorest children benefiting most) matches the
  direction of the published literature. The Great Smoky Mountains Study of
  Youth tracked the Eastern Band of Cherokee before and after 1996 casino
  per-capita distributions began: the foundational finding is Costello,
  Compton, Keeler & Angold, "Relationships Between Poverty and Psychopathology:
  A Natural Experiment," *JAMA* 290(15) (2003): 2023–2029, and the economics
  quasi-experiment on income and child outcomes is Akee, Copeland, Keeler,
  Angold & Costello, "Parents' Incomes and Children's Outcomes: A
  Quasi-experiment Using Transfer Payments from Casino Profits," *American
  Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 2(1) (2010): 86–115. A dedicated
  research page for the dividends outcome remains queued in BACKLOG.
- **"No paid politicians" (Switzerland):** the eBook corrects its own claim in
  a footnote — Swiss parliamentarians are compensated (p. 16). Reported here
  as the eBook states it.
- The housing-shortage aside ("It isn't true," p. 6) contradicts the supply
  literature the wiki carries ([Hilber & Vermeulen](/wiki/hilber-vermeulen-england-supply/);
  [Saiz](/wiki/saiz-housing-supply-elasticity/)) — noted as the author's
  position, unsourced in the eBook.

## Why the Wiki Carries It

It is the most explicit popular statement of the **SPV/Crown-Estate capture
design** — rent collection institutionally insulated from politics (Temasek
as the exemplar) — which is a genuine design idea in the
[Geoism](/wiki/geoism/) instrument space, distinct from tax-code instruments
like [LVT](/wiki/land-value-tax/) or the
[ACE](/wiki/allowance-for-corporate-equity/). It also states plainly the
advocacy version of claims the wiki carries with evidence gradings elsewhere
(ATCOR; UBI-capitalization; the [Alaska](/wiki/alaska/) and Singapore
models) — a useful, honest specimen of the persuasive case.
