## Summary

*The People's Budget 1909/10: Lloyd George and Liberal Fiscal Policy* is a monograph by **Bruce K. Murray**, published by Oxford University Press (originally, with a 2019 digitization by the Internet Archive). Murray's research was conducted at the University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg, with support from the British Council. The book examines the fiscal strategy of the 1909/10 Budget through the prism of Liberal party politics in the Edwardian era.

Murray's thesis is that the People's Budget was essentially the personal initiative of Lloyd George, who had to fight against Treasury orthodoxy and a cautious Cabinet to implement a fiscal strategy that incorporated virtually the entire progressive taxation programme toward which Liberalism had been moving. The Budget was designed to raise revenue for social reform (old-age pensions) and increased naval expenditure through progressive direct taxation, including supertax, land-value duties, and increased licence duties, while avoiding direct taxes that would substantially burden the majority of middle-class income tax payers (Murray 1980, pp. 17–24).

The book is a study of a UK government fiscal policy document — the Budget of 1909/10 presented by Chancellor Lloyd George to the House of Commons on 29 April 1909 — and the political crisis it precipitated, including its rejection by the House of Lords, the resulting general elections of January and December 1910, and the eventual passage of the Budget.

## Core Findings

### The Fiscal Strategy (Ch. I–II, pp. 17–75)

Murray documents that the Edwardian period witnessed a marked upswing in government expenditure for social reform and national defence. The question of how to finance this expenditure became, in P. F. Clarke's formulation, "the core of the argument" between Liberal and Unionist parties by 1909/10. The Unionists looked to tariff duties; the Liberals to increases in direct taxation. Winston Churchill declared the difference was "the difference between the taxation of wages and the taxation of wealth" (Murray 1980, p. 17, quoting Clarke and Churchill).

### Preparation of the Budget (Ch. IV–V, pp. 76–147)

Murray shows that the Budget was not a sudden innovation: much groundwork was prepared by H. H. Asquith as Chancellor (1905–1908), and almost all its direct taxes — supertax, land-value duties, increased licence duties — had been actively campaigned for within the Liberal party. Lloyd George had to fight "long and hard" to get his proposals past a Treasury "still strongly attached to Gladstonian orthodoxy" and through a Cabinet "alarmed by the boldness of the Budget" (Murray 1980, p. 21).

### The Budget's Design (Ch. V–VI, pp. 112–171)

The Budget was carefully designed to avoid directly antagonizing the bulk of the middle classes. The increases in direct taxation were aimed mainly at the wealthy and at unearned sources of income. The *Liberal Magazine* for May 1909 noted: "the middle class who work for the incomes which are taxed are most gently treated; the 'rentier' middle class rather less gently" (Murray 1980, p. 21). The land-value duties sought to tax "property values created by the community" — a Georgist principle applied through incremental taxation rather than full rent collection.

### The Budget in the Commons and the Country (Ch. VII, pp. 172–235)

Murray documents how the Budget was "enormously popular" among a large segment of the Liberal rank and file, seen by "advanced" Liberals as a proclamation that the Liberal party was to be "the party of social-democratic reform and progressive taxation." Right-wing Liberals were disturbed by the fiscal strategy, fearing it would "promote rather than postpone the arrival of class-based politics" and lead to "the ultimate demise of the Liberal party" (Murray 1980, p. 24).

### The Constitutional Crisis (Ch. VIII–X, pp. 236–290)

The Budget's rejection by the Unionist-dominated House of Lords triggered a constitutional crisis. Two general elections followed (January and December 1910). The Budget was eventually passed in April 1910 after the Lords' veto was overridden following the January 1910 election.

### The Land-Value Duties

Murray documents that the land-value duties — including increment value duty, reversion duty, undeveloped land duty, and mineral rights duty — were among the most contested elements. These duties sought to capture the "unearned increment" of land value, echoing Henry George's principles, though at modest rates rather than the full collection George advocated. ## Policy Recommendations

Murray does not offer explicit policy recommendations; the book is a historical monograph. However, its findings bear on:

- The political feasibility of progressive taxation and land-value taxation
- The role of fiscal policy in coalition-building across class lines
- The institutional obstacles to land-value taxation (Treasury orthodoxy, Cabinet caution, House of Lords obstruction)

## Nuances and Limits

- Murray's analysis is based primarily on British political archives (Bodleian Library, Beaverbrook Library, British Library, Nuffield College, Public Record Office); the perspective is that of high politics rather than grass-roots reaction.
- The land-value duties were modest in their rates and were largely evaded in practice; they were repealed in 1920. [VERIFY: implementation outcomes — Ch. XI]
- Murray focuses on the Liberal party's internal politics; the Unionist and Labour perspectives are treated more briefly.
- The book was published in 1980 and does not engage with more recent historiography on Edwardian fiscal policy.
