## What It Is

*The Deportations Delirium of Nineteen-Twenty* is the memoir and documentary indictment
that [Louis F. Post](/wiki/louis-f-post/) — [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/)'s ablest
lieutenant and the single-tax movement's most senior federal officeholder — wrote about the
first American Red Scare. Post edited the Georgist weekly *The Public* (1898–1913); by 1920
he was **Assistant Secretary of Labor**, and because deportation was an administrative matter
run through the Labor Department's Bureau of Immigration, the flood of Palmer-raid cases
landed on his desk. The book is his first-hand account of what he found there and what he did
about it, published in 1923 by the co-operative labor press Charles H. Kerr & Company with an
Introduction by the civil-libertarian lawyer **Moorfield Storey**, first president of the
NAACP.

Its thirty-seven chapters run from the December 1919 sailing of the "Soviet Ark" (the army
transport *Buford*, which deported 249 Russian residents) through the nationwide January
raids, Post's case-by-case review of the deportation backlog, the impeachment attempt his
review provoked, and a closing summary; an appendix collects the constitutional texts he
invokes throughout. Applying ordinary standards of evidence and due process, Post cancelled
well over 1,500 warrants as unlawfully obtained — arrests without warrants, coerced
confessions, denial of counsel — and then, testifying before the House Rules Committee on
7–8 May 1920, dismantled the impeachment case against him.

## Why It Matters Here

- It is the primary record of a **Georgist statesman carrying single-tax principle into high
  office**. Post held that the same commitment to justice that made land monopoly intolerable
  made the arbitrary deportation of immigrants intolerable too — and he acted on it against a
  popular Attorney General and the threat of impeachment. For a movement often caricatured as
  a single-issue tax crusade, the book is documentary evidence of its broader progressive and
  civil-libertarian character (see the [Single Tax narrative](/wiki/single-tax-narrative/)).
- It is a **contemporaneous insider account** of the Palmer Raids by the official at their
  administrative center — the counter-narrative to A. Mitchell Palmer's and a young
  J. Edgar Hoover's, written while the events were fresh and while Post's own conduct was
  still contested.
- It supplies the primary-source spine for the biography of [Louis F. Post](/wiki/louis-f-post/):
  the acting-secretary role, the roughly 1,600-case review with about 71% dismissed, and the
  failed Hoch impeachment.

## Reception and Limits

The book is at once history and self-defense: as Storey notes in the Introduction, it is "in
part a defence of his own course in the crisis." Post writes as an advocate and a participant,
not a detached historian, and the reader should weigh his account of his adversaries'
motives accordingly. Its facts, however — the warrant cancellations, the raid methods, the
impeachment testimony — are drawn from the official record he administered, and the work has
long been treated as a standard first-hand source on the deportations phase of the Red Scare
(it was reissued in a 1970 Da Capo Press scholarly reprint).
