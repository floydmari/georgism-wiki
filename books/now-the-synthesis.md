## Summary

*Now the Synthesis: Capitalism, Socialism and the New Social Contract* (1991) is an edited volume published by the Centre for Incentive Taxation Ltd, London (Shepheard-Walwyn) and New York (Holmes & Meier). The book brings together essays by Nicolaus Tideman, Francis M. Smith, Fred Harrison, James L. Busey, Jerome F. Heavey, David Richards, Jürgen Backhaus, Jacob Jan Krabbe, Richard Body, and C. Lowell Harriss, with an Introduction (pp. 1–11) and a keynote chapter titled "Dialectics and the Millennium: emergence of the Synthesis" (pp. 13–46) by editor Richard Noyes. The volume argues that land value taxation offers a "synthesis" between capitalism and socialism — preserving market freedom while restoring the community's claim on economic rent (Noyes 1991, p.13).

Noyes frames the book as articulating an "applied philosophy" for the post-Cold War era, arguing that the old orders of Western individualism and Eastern statism alike "exaggerated the potential for conflict by over-simplifying, through their institutions and social processes, the rights and obligations of the individual and of the State" (Noyes 1991, p.1). The volume's thesis is that Henry George's principle — that land value is created by community and should be returned to community — represents the emerging social synthesis, observable in diverse phenomena from UK "planning gain" to ecological tax reform and post-socialist transitions in Eastern Europe.

## Core Findings

### The Constitutional Case for 100% LVT (Tideman, Ch. 2)

Nicolaus Tideman argues that 100% land value taxation, introduced without compensation, would not be barred by the US constitutional prohibition of uncompensated takings if introduced as a constitutional rather than legislative matter. Tideman draws an analogy to the abolition of slavery: the Fourteenth Amendment explicitly denied compensation for emancipated slaves, establishing that "those who held slaves should have known that it is not possible for one human being to own another" (Tideman 1991, p.56). Similarly, Tideman argues, "no one can have a claim to own land, because no one made the land" (Tideman 1991, p.56).

Tideman distinguishes between 100% LVT (requiring constitutional consensus) and incremental property tax reform (shifting taxes from improvements to land), which "probably causes land prices to rise on average" since land becomes more valuable when improvements carry no additional tax liability (Tideman 1991, p.58). It is this "absence of anything like a taking" that makes the latter "fair game for ordinary politics" (Tideman 1991, p.58).

### Planning Gain as Implicit Land Value Taxation (Smith, Ch. 3)

Francis M. Smith analyzes UK "planning gain" — the practice of local authorities extracting benefits from developers in exchange for planning permission. Smith argues this is "a partial demonstration of the principle that Henry George established, i.e., that the value of land is created by the community and should justly be returned to the community" (Smith 1991, p.60). When farmland is converted to housing or commercial use, the increase in land values "can be a thousandfold" (Smith 1991, p.61).

Smith documents the evolution from the 1947 Town and Country Planning Act's "betterment" concept (later abandoned), through the Development Land Tax of 1976 ("it was ineffective and was abolished in 1985"), to the informal Planning Gain system of the 1980s (Smith 1991, p.63). He cites the Canary Wharf agreement (1987), where Tower Hamlets Borough Council secured a commitment of 2,000 jobs for local people (with a £7,000 penalty for each job short) and £2.5m over eight years for training from the developers of the 71-acre site (Smith 1991, p.64).

### Post-Socialism and the Single Tax (Harrison, Ch. 4)

Fred Harrison argues that post-socialist societies — above all Gorbachev's USSR — were attempting transformation "without the aid of the holistic philosophy that was a pre-condition of success" (Harrison 1991, p.77). Henry George's philosophy, he contends, "addresses the immediate needs of hungry people while also addressing Gorbachev's larger vision. It is a holistic program, readily available to guide the emergence of post-socialist society" (Harrison 1991, p.78). The chapter develops a land-value-taxation-based development model for the transition, integrating "social, economic and ecological objectives into a single thrust" (Harrison 1991, p.96).

### Geocracy in Latin America (Busey, Ch. 5)

James L. Busey asks whether classical social theory, as refined by Henry George into what he calls "geocracy," can fill the doctrinal gap left by the collapse of Marxism (Busey 1991, p.113). Using Central America as his case study, he attributes Costa Rica's relative exception from the region's "pattern of unremitting misery" to "a wider distribution of land and therefore of other property than is the norm in Central America" (Busey 1991, p.113). Geocracy, he argues, rejects state ownership of the means of production while joining Marx and Engels only in part of the first plank of the *Communist Manifesto*: the "application of all rents of land to public purposes" (Busey 1991, p.126).

### The Greens and the Tax on Rent (Richards, Ch. 7)

David Richards examines why politically active ecologists have failed to adopt land value taxation despite its ecological fit. He restates George's "most controversial prescription... that the community should recover all the land rents that the creation of freehold tenancies had alienated into private ownership," and extends it: users of land and natural resources already in the public domain should be charged full market rent, and any new property rights in natural resources created by government decree — such as marketable permits to emit waste — should be designed to return the resource's rent to the community (Richards 1991, p.158). He documents the UK Green (Ecology) Party's 1981 conferences on land tenure policy, whose agreed aims he calls "a perfect statement of the Georgist position," even though the party could not agree on land value taxation ("Community Ground Rent") as the mechanism (Richards 1991, p.160).

### Incentive Taxation and the Environment (Backhaus & Krabbe, Ch. 8)

Jürgen Backhaus and Jacob Jan Krabbe argue that incentive taxation (taxing land and resource rents rather than labor and capital) is environmentally beneficial — "complex — yet feasible," per their subtitle (Backhaus & Krabbe 1991, p.174). They read *Progress and Poverty* (1879) as containing "the blueprint of a comprehensive policy towards the environment" (Backhaus & Krabbe 1991, p.174). Their conclusions: a substantial number of natural resources are used inefficiently, in part because "grandfathering" of established property rights acts as an entry barrier to new industry; the remedy is "an alternative system in the spirit of Henry George" covering all natural resources, dealing with resource damage "along the lines of Henry George's fiscal policy" (Backhaus & Krabbe 1991, p.202).

### International Trade and Rent (Part V, Chs. 9–10)

The volume's international trade section comprises Richard Body's "Protectionism, Rent and the Dynamics of Agricultural Degradation" (Ch. 9) and C. Lowell Harriss's "Trade and Investment: preserving the fruits of liberty" (Ch. 10). Body, a British parliamentarian and farmer, argues that "the ecological argument for free trade was no less strong than either the moral or economic one" (Body 1991, p.207), and that the economic value of agricultural subsidies and protection "is capitalised into higher land prices," making the landowner — not the farmer, laborer, or consumer — the primary beneficiary (Noyes 1991, Introduction, pp. 8–9). Harriss restates George's *Protection or Free Trade* case that free trade principles are consistent with rent capture (Noyes 1991, Introduction, pp. 8–9).

## Policy Recommendations

- **100% Land Value Taxation**: Tideman argues for full rent capture without compensation, pursued through constitutional consensus rather than narrow coalition politics (Tideman 1991, p.57)
- **Property Tax Reform**: Shifting taxes from improvements to land as an incremental step appropriate for "ordinary politics" (Tideman 1991, p.58)
- **Planning Gain as a Step to Land Value Capture**: Smith argues planning gain reveals a growing recognition that land value benefits "should accrue directly to society as a whole, which has the moral right to them," rather than being "drawn off by private individuals as the fruits of privilege" (Smith 1991, p.73)
- **Ecological Tax Reform**: Backhaus & Krabbe advocate shifting taxation from production to land and resource rents, covering all natural resources (Backhaus & Krabbe 1991, p.202)

## Nuances and Limits

- The book is an edited volume with varying levels of analytical rigor across chapters; some claims are normative rather than empirical
- Tideman's constitutional argument for uncompensated 100% LVT is explicitly a normative position about justice, not a tested legal doctrine: he grounds it in the logic that "one constitutional amendment cannot preclude another" and in the Fourteenth Amendment precedent (Tideman 1991, p.56), and conditions its justice on a prior "change in society's consensus regarding the requirements of justice" (Tideman 1991, p.57); no such amendment has ever been attempted
- The post-socialist chapters were written in 1991, before the outcomes of post-Soviet transitions were known
- Planning gain data is UK-specific; generalizability to other jurisdictions is not directly addressed

## Key Quotes

> "George's most controversial prescription, therefore, was that the community should recover all the land rents that the creation of freehold tenancies had alienated into private ownership." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), p. 158

> "The rules by which land is occupied dictate the way people relate to each other and determine the success with which they interact. The system of land tenure may be used to divide people, or to integrate them into healthy communities. Examples of the former case are the apartheid laws of South Africa, which created the Bantustans to segregate races, and the large rural estates of Latin America which also vouchsafe the best land to a few." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), p. 158

> "That every person in every generation has an equal right to the use of land was Henry George's basic moral axiom. It is one with which few people would disagree, and it is certainly common ground in the Green movement. Collection of land rent by the community is not commonly perceived as its necessary logical outcome, however." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), pp. 158–59

> "Socialists see the connection, but go on to make the fatal mistake of proscribing private ownership of land altogether, thereby missing another connection — between private decision-making and prosperity." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), p. 159

> "Henry George's message appears destined to fall between political stools. To Greens it offers equitable sharing of the world's natural resources; to non-Greens, economic efficiency and, hence, growth. Within both groups, it offers thoroughgoing private ownership of producible goods and services to those of Rightward persuasion, and untrammelled social ownership of natural resources to those of Leftward persuasion." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), p. 159

> "Is it conceivable that classical social theory, especially that of an almost forgotten eighteenth century French physiocracy as further refined at the hands of Henry George (which I shall call geocracy), might fill a doctrinal gap and be suitable both for the preservation of freedom and for the resolution of human travail?" — James L. Busey, "Conflict, Ideology and Hope in Central America" (Ch. 5), p. 113

> "The aims of land tenure policy were quickly established. They were to (a) re-establish land as a common heritage and community asset, no longer subject to monopoly or speculative pressures; (b) establish for all equal right to occupy land, so providing a proper framework for the ecological use of land in small units; (c) guarantee security of tenure to occupiers of land on this new basis; (d) ensure that returns from land which are in no way due to the efforts of individuals shall accrue to the community (Ecology Party 1981: 9). This is a perfect statement of the Georgist position." — David Richards, "The Greens and the Tax on Rent" (Ch. 7), p. 160
