## Bibliographic Information

*Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* is a textbook by **Robert J. Gloudemans** and **Richard Almy**, published by the International Association of Assessing Officers (IAAO) in Kansas City, Missouri, in 2011 (ISBN 978-0-88329-198-6; a 2024 Kindle reissue lists 604 pages).[1][2] It is a paywalled IAAO Store item ($50 as of a 2022 Idaho course listing) with no free full text available online; this page is built from IAAO's own course materials and professional standards that describe and cite the book page-by-page, not from a direct read of the text.[3][4]

## What the Book Is

*Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* is the assessment profession's standard introductory textbook on **mass appraisal** — valuing many properties simultaneously, on a common date, using standardized statistical methods rather than individual expert appraisals — as opposed to single-property appraisal. IAAO markets it as covering "everything you need to know to develop a mass appraisal system, build and calibrate models, and conduct a revaluation."[2] It is the required text for **IAAO Course 300: Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal**, the entry-level course in IAAO's 300-series and a prerequisite for further mass-appraisal coursework; students informally call it "the gray book."[3][4] IAAO's own Body of Knowledge framework maps the book to two of its core knowledge areas: "Collecting and Maintaining Property Data" and "Appraising Property."[3]

It succeeded an earlier, shorter Gloudemans-authored IAAO textbook, *Mass Appraisal of Real Property* (1999), and both authors had already collaborated on IAAO's *Property Appraisal and Assessment Administration* (Eckert, Gloudemans & Almy, eds., 1990).[5] Almy also co-authored the companion volume *Fundamentals of Tax Policy* (Almy, Dornfest & Kenyon, 2008) — a distinct book on tax policy design, already cited on this wiki via the [IAAO Standards](/wiki/iaao-standards/) page, and not to be confused with this one.[6]

## Structure and Contents

The book's contents are not independently reproduced here, but IAAO's own **Course 300** syllabus — built directly on this textbook — outlines eight chapters' worth of learning objectives, which give a reliable proxy for the book's structure:[3]

1. **Mass appraisal fundamentals** — how mass appraisal differs from single-property appraisal, its historical evolution, and the basic structures of cost, sales-comparison, and income models; the distinction between model *specification* and *calibration*.
2. **Components of a mass-appraisal system** — the property tax system, computer-assisted mass appraisal (CAMA) system components, the ten steps of a reappraisal, and property-characteristics data collection.
3. **Data accuracy and collection** — evaluating data accuracy, property-record-card design, field-collection protocol, and staffing/time estimation for a reappraisal.
4. **Introductory statistics** — frequency distributions, measures of central tendency and dispersion, cross-tabulations, and scatter diagrams.
5. **Ratio studies** — the purpose and mechanics of sales-ratio studies, including the coefficient of dispersion (COD) and coefficient of variation (COV), horizontal vs. vertical equity, and IAAO's *Standard on Ratio Studies* performance benchmarks.
6. **The cost approach** — reproduction vs. replacement cost, quantity-survey, unit-in-place, comparative-unit, and trended-original-cost estimation methods, and cost-manual design.
7. **The sales comparison approach** — additive, multiplicative, and hybrid model structures; treatment of location; multiple regression analysis (MRA); adaptive estimation procedure (AEP); and automated comparable-sales analysis.
8. **The income approach** — gross income multipliers, overall capitalization rates, vacancy and expense-ratio estimation, and stratified-model reliability.

Citations in IAAO's own *Standard on Mass Appraisal of Real Property* (approved 2017) indicate the published book runs to at least a ninth chapter (on commercial-property characteristics and income modeling) and an appendix beyond the eight-chapter course outline — so the course is a compressed subset of the full text, not an exact chapter-for-chapter map.[7]

## Land Valuation Treatment

IAAO's *Standard on Mass Appraisal of Real Property* — the profession's binding methodological standard, read in full for the wiki's [IAAO Standards](/wiki/iaao-standards/) page — directs readers to specific pages of *Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* for land-valuation guidance. The standard's own land-valuation section states: "The sales comparison approach is the primary approach to land valuation and is always preferred when sufficient sales are available. In the absence of adequate sales, other techniques that can be used in land appraisal include allocation, abstraction, anticipated use, capitalization of ground rents, and land residual capitalization," citing *Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal*, pages 178–180, for elaboration.[7] The standard separately notes that land value is "the most difficult" aspect of the cost approach to estimate, "often... extracted from sales of improved property because sales of vacant land are scarce," and again points to the book (pages 189–192) for depreciation-schedule extraction methods used in that process.[7] It also cites the book (pages 139–143) for neighborhood/subarea stratification guidance relevant to land-value tables.[7]

This matches the technique inventory the wiki's [Mass Appraisal Methods](/wiki/mass-appraisal-methods/) concept page already documents via [Lars Doucet](/wiki/doucet-does-georgism-work/)'s account — comparable vacant-lot sales, teardown sales, and residual estimation — and confirms that these are standard professional techniques codified in the field's core textbook, not Georgist-specific workarounds.

## Ratio-Study Statistics: COD, PRD, PRB

The same IAAO standard cites *Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* (pages 385–392 and Appendix B) alongside the *Standard on Ratio Studies* as the source of "guidance and relevant measures" for **price-related differential (PRD)** and **price-related bias (PRB)** — the statistics that test whether low-value and high-value properties are assessed at consistent fractions of market value.[7] These are the same metrics documented first-hand on the wiki's [IAAO Standards](/wiki/iaao-standards/) research page (PRD target 0.98–1.03; PRB target ±0.05, unacceptable outside ±0.10) and used to characterize [Berry (2021)](/wiki/mass-appraisal-methods/)'s finding that US property assessments are "typically regressive."[8] The book is, in other words, the textbook explanation behind statistics the wiki already cites from the standards that formalize them.

## The Authors

**Robert J. Gloudemans** is a consultant and partner in Almy, Gloudemans, Jacobs & Denne (AGJD), a Phoenix-based firm working exclusively in property-tax and assessment administration for governments. He was formerly a research associate for IAAO and administrator of computer-assisted appraisal for the Arizona Department of Revenue, and has advised assessment jurisdictions across North America and in countries transitioning to market-value-based property tax systems.[9] IAAO named him a Most Valuable Member in 2018 and an IAAO Fellow in 2019, and he served three terms on the IAAO Standards Committee.[10]

**Richard Almy** is also a partner at AGJD. He began his career as an appraiser with the Detroit, Michigan, Board of Assessors, later serving as research director and then executive director of IAAO. He co-authored several IAAO texts including this one and *Fundamentals of Tax Policy* (2008), and his consulting work spans "more than 100 projects in twenty-three countries."[1] Almy's later solo work, the 2014 OECD paper [*Valuation and Assessment of Immovable Property*](/wiki/almy-oecd-valuation-assessment/), is already a wiki source for the assessability objection's cost evidence.[1]

Gloudemans has a separate, more directly Georgist-relevant research record: a 2000 Lincoln Institute of Land Policy working paper (funded by a David C. Lincoln Fellowship in Land Value Taxation), *Implementing a Land Value Tax in Urban Residential Communities*, and a 2002 Lincoln Institute follow-up, *An Empirical Analysis of the Incidence of Location on Land and Building Values*. The 2002 paper reports that CAMA models combining vacant and improved sales "can be used to predict land values with acceptable reliability, even when some neighborhoods lack vacant land sales altogether," and concludes that "modern mass appraisal methods are capable of producing reasonable estimates of the value of land as if vacant even in neighborhoods with no or few vacant land sales, provided there are other neighborhoods in the model with adequate vacant land sales to provide reality checks."[11] These are separate working papers, not part of *Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* itself, but they show that one of the textbook's authors has directly researched land-value-tax assessment feasibility — the general textbook doesn't argue for LVT, but its co-author has separately produced land-value-tax-specific evidence the wiki could draw on further.

## Standing in the Profession

The book's status as the field's reference text is corroborated from multiple angles already verified for other wiki pages: IAAO's *Standard on Property Tax Policy* (2020) directs assessors researching "automated mass valuation methods" to the *Standard on Mass Appraisal of Real Property* and to *Fundamentals of Mass Appraisal* specifically.[12] The *Standard on Mass Appraisal of Real Property* itself cites the book more than a dozen times across its guidance on data collection, cost schedules, depreciation, stratification, sales-comparison modeling, income modeling, staffing, and ratio-study statistics.[7] IAAO continues to run in-person and self-study sections of Course 300 built on the book as recently as 2025–2026, and several US state revenue departments (e.g., Idaho) list it as the recommended or required text for certifying property-tax appraisers.[3][4]

## Relevance to Georgist Assessability Debates

The book is a **general mass-appraisal textbook, not a land-value-taxation advocacy source** — a distinction worth stating plainly given how often it is name-checked in wiki pages defending the [land-cannot-be-assessed](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/) objection's rebuttal. Nothing found in this research indicates the book argues for or against site-value or split-rate taxation as policy; land valuation is treated as one routine sub-topic of the cost approach, alongside dozens of others (data collection, income modeling, staffing).

What it does establish, filtered through IAAO's own citing standard, is that professionally standardized techniques for estimating land value **separately from improvements** — sales comparison, allocation, abstraction, anticipated use, ground-rent capitalization, and land residual capitalization — are treated as ordinary, textbook-level mass-appraisal practice, not a specialized or speculative Georgist workaround. That is consistent with, and reinforces, the framing already documented on the wiki's [Mass Appraisal Methods](/wiki/mass-appraisal-methods/) and [IAAO Standards](/wiki/iaao-standards/) pages. It should be cited for that narrow, well-supported point — the existence and standardization of land-valuation technique — not stretched into an endorsement the book does not make.

## Limits and What Wasn't Verified

- **No direct read of the primary text.** The book is not freely available; this page is built entirely from IAAO's course materials, its own standards (which cite the book at specific pages), and bookseller/bio pages — not a first-hand reading of the book's prose. No claim above should be read as a direct quotation from the book itself; quoted language is either IAAO's own standard's text (citing the book for elaboration) or from the authors' separate, freely available papers.
- **Chapter numbering is approximate.** The Course 300 syllabus's eight-chapter structure is IAAO's training compression of the book, not confirmed as an exact match to the book's own table of contents; the *Standard on Mass Appraisal*'s citations to "chapter 9" and pages beyond 400 show the full book extends further than the course outline suggests.
- **No independent review or reception literature was located.** Searches did not surface academic book reviews or critical assessments of the text (unsurprising for a professional training textbook rather than a trade or academic monograph); its "reception" evidence here is institutional adoption (Course 300, cross-referencing standards), not critical commentary.
- **Page count is uncertain for the original 2011 print edition.** The 604-page figure comes from a 2024 Kindle relisting of the same title and authors; it was not independently confirmed against the original paperback.
