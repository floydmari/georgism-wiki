## The Claim

The rising share of national income flowing to "capital" in developed economies — popularised by Thomas Piketty — is, when decomposed, **almost entirely a rise in the housing sector**, and therefore largely a rise in **land rent**. Reproducible capital (machines, equipment, structures) shows little long-run increase in its income share; land does.

## The Evidence

- [Rognlie (2015)](/wiki/rognlie-capital-share/) decomposed the capital share across developed economies and found the long-run rise is concentrated in housing.
- Bonnet, Chapelle, Trannoy & Wasmer (2021), *"Land is Back, It Should Be Taxed, It Can Be Taxed"* (European Economic Review), independently confirmed the result with European data.

## Why It Matters

If inequality's capital dimension is really a **land** dimension, then a tax on land values targets the actual driver — without the efficiency cost of taxing productive capital. This connects 21st-century inequality research directly to Henry George's 19th-century diagnosis.

## Strength of Evidence

**Strong** — independently replicated across US and European datasets by separate research teams.
