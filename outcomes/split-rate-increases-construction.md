## The Claim

When a jurisdiction taxes **land** at a higher rate than **buildings** (a "split-rate" or "two-rate" property tax), construction activity tends to **increase** relative to what comparable jurisdictions experience. The mechanism is straightforward: taxing improvements less removes a penalty on building, while taxing land more makes holding a site idle or underdeveloped costly.

## The Evidence

| Study | Setting | Finding |
|-------|---------|---------|
| [Oates & Schwab (1997)](/wiki/oates-schwab-pittsburgh/) | Pittsburgh vs. 15 comparison cities | ~70% rise in building-permit value after the 1979–80 land-tax increase, far exceeding comparison cities |
| [Plassmann & Tideman (2000)](/wiki/plassmann-tideman-construction/) | 15 Pennsylvania municipalities, 1972–1994 | Higher land-to-building tax ratio significantly raises permits |

## Strength of Evidence

**Moderate to strong.** The direction of the effect is consistent across studies and matches theory. Limitations: most rigorous evidence comes from Pennsylvania (the main jurisdiction allowing split rates), and case studies cannot fully rule out confounding local factors. The result is best read as "robustly suggestive," with the multi-municipality panel evidence stronger than any single city.
