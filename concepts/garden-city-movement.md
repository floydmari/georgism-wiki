## Overview

The **Garden City movement** was an urban-reform and town-planning movement founded by the English reformer **Ebenezer Howard** (1850–1928). Howard set out the idea in his book *To-morrow: A Peaceful Path to Real Reform* (1898), reissued in 1902 as ***Garden Cities of To-morrow*** — the title by which it is remembered.[1][2] The proposal was for planned, self-contained new towns of limited size, ringed by a permanent agricultural greenbelt, combining the economic opportunity of the city with the health and space of the country (Howard's "Town-Country magnet").

The movement's distinctive economic feature — the reason it matters to Georgism — is its treatment of land. In Howard's scheme the entire estate is bought at agricultural prices and **legally vested in trustees who hold it in trust for the people of the town**, so that all ground rents are paid to the community rather than to private landlords.[3] As the town grows and land becomes more valuable, that rising value returns to the residents. Howard gave this combined payment a new name — **"rate-rent"** — the single sum a Garden City occupier pays, which covers interest on the founding debentures ("landlord's rent"), repayment of the purchase money ("sinking fund"), and the local taxes that fund public works ("rates").[3] Because the community both captures the rising land value *and* spends it on the roads, schools, and services that make the location valuable, Howard argued the rate-rent an occupier would willingly pay would exceed the ordinary rent paid to a private landlord.[3]

The **Garden City Association** (later the Town and Country Planning Association) was founded in 1899 to promote the idea, holding its first meeting on 10 June 1899.[2] It led to two real garden cities north of London in Hertfordshire: **Letchworth**, the world's first, where First Garden City Ltd began construction in **1903** with a founding commitment "to repatriate all profits back into the Estate," and **Welwyn Garden City**, begun in **1920**.[1][2] Letchworth's community-ownership model persists: the estate passed by Act of Parliament to the **[Letchworth Garden City](/wiki/letchworth/) Heritage Foundation**, which still manages the property portfolio and reinvests the income in the town.[1]

## Relevance to Georgism

The Garden City movement is one of the clearest historical instances of **land-value capture** actually built and sustained, and it drew directly on Georgist ideas:

- **It captures the unearned increment for the community.** The whole design turns on the community, not private owners, receiving the rising value its own growth creates — precisely the [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/) that Georgist [land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/) aims to recover. Howard's "rate-rent" is a practical mechanism for doing so through communal freehold rather than through a tax.
- **It is a land-value-capture precedent, not a full LVT.** Howard captured land rent by **owning the land and leasing it** — the [public land leasing](/wiki/public-land-leasing/) / community-freehold route — rather than by taxing privately owned sites, so it sits alongside, rather than inside, the [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) family. The land case is the clean one here: the value being recaptured is site value created by community presence, exactly the surplus Georgists identify as capturable without deterring production.
- **A shared lineage with the community land trust.** The Garden City's trustee-held, permanently-communal land is a recognized forerunner of the modern [community land trust](/wiki/community-land-trust/), which likewise separates ownership of land (held in trust for the community) from the use and improvement of it. Howard himself was influenced by Henry George and by earlier land-reform and cooperative thinking.

## Letchworth: the empirical record

Letchworth is the movement's one long-running natural experiment in community land-value capture, and its record is mixed in an instructive way.

**The land-capture machinery survived; the wider utopian programme did not.** The founding social and economic package — integrated industry and agriculture, cooperative enterprise, the fully operationalised "rate-rent" — eroded within a few decades, leaving the town's architectural and planning legacy more intact than its economic one. In the standard scholarly assessment, Letchworth's "social and economic elements of integrated industry, agriculture, and cooperative land ownership eroded fairly quickly, leaving architectural and aesthetic concerns to dominate the Garden City's legacy," so that the town "demonstrates the tenacity of structural market and economic forces in guiding the implementation of planning projects."[4] First Garden City Ltd paid dividends to shareholders (capped, but private), and the pure trust-for-the-people design was compromised from the start.

**But the community's ownership of the estate — and the recapture of its ground rent — did persist, because the freehold was never sold.** The rising land value the town's own growth created made the undervalued estate a takeover target in the early 1960s; Parliament responded by converting the private company into a statutory public body, and the freehold has stayed in community hands ever since. Today the estate is held by the **Letchworth Garden City Heritage Foundation**, established under the **Letchworth Garden City Heritage Foundation Act 1995** as successor to First Garden City Ltd and the interim Letchworth Garden City Corporation.[5] The Foundation is a charitable **Community Benefit Society** (registered no. 28211R) that owns the freehold of the estate and reinvests the surplus from its property portfolio into the town — the same own-and-lease logic Howard designed, now a century old.[5][6]

**The modern figures show what the mechanism yields — and its limits.** In 2024 the Foundation reported total income of about **£14.4 million**, "largely derived from commercial and residential rental income," and spent roughly **£4 million** on its charitable objectives, including a **£600,000 community grants programme** it describes as the largest run by any garden city.[6] Its own accounting is candid about the ratio: "For every £1 received from our investments, we are now spending 65p on managing the estate, and our community reinvestment is up to 34p."[6] So the estate captures a real, recurring land-rent stream for the community, but management and upkeep of an ageing property portfolio absorb most of it — an honest picture of land-value capture through freehold ownership rather than through a tax, where the community also bears the landlord's costs and risks.
