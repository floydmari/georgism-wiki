## Overview

**Nikolai D. Kondratiev** (1892–1938) was a Soviet economist and statistician who, in his 1925 study *The Major Economic Cycles*, presented empirical evidence for **long waves of roughly 40–60 years** in prices, wages, and production across major capitalist economies, driven by successive clusters of technological innovation (steam and textiles; railways and steel; electricity and chemicals; and later, automobiles and mass production).[1] Kondratiev's finding that capitalism exhibited self-renewing long cycles rather than terminal Marxist crisis contributed to his fall from favor under Stalin: he was imprisoned in 1930 and executed during the Great Purge in 1938.[1]

Georgist land-cycle writers treat the Kondratiev long wave as a companion cycle operating on a different timescale from the [18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/). Fred Harrison, in *The Power in the Land* (1983, Ch. 1), cites Kondratiev's long waves as a popular long-cycle theory but — per the wiki's discovery notes on that book — critiques it as lacking a clear causal mechanism, in contrast to Harrison's land-speculation account of the shorter cycle. Akhil Patel (2023) integrates the two more explicitly, arguing each 55–60 year Long Cycle spans roughly three 18-year real-estate cycles, and that land booms coinciding with the final years of a Long Cycle upswing — as in the 1920s and (Patel argues) potentially the mid-2020s — produce the largest speculative peaks (Patel, *The Secret Wealth Advantage*, Ch. 11, pp. 217–232).
