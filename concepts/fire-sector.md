## Definition

**FIRE** (Finance, Insurance, and Real Estate) is a sectoral grouping used in national accounts
that economist [Michael Hudson](/wiki/michael-hudson/) treats as the analytical core of his
critique of modern capitalism. In *Killing the Host: How Financial Parasites and Debt Bondage
Destroy the Global Economy* (2015), Hudson argues the FIRE sector has displaced industrial
capitalism as the dominant force in the economy by extracting **economic rent** rather than
funding productive investment.[1] His central mechanism is that banks lend against **land and
real estate as collateral**, and that this credit is capitalized directly into land prices:
rising mortgage debt bids up land values, and rising land values in turn support larger mortgage
loans, in a self-reinforcing cycle — so that much of what national income statistics record as
financial-sector "output" is, in his account, actually a claim on land rent dressed up as a
return to capital.[1]

## Why It Matters to the Rent Debate

Hudson frames the FIRE sector as a "parasite" that disguises itself as part of the productive
host economy, consuming surplus that would otherwise fund production and public investment.[1]
This connects Hudson's argument directly to classical and Georgist [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/)
theory: both hold that land rent is unearned because it reflects the value of a fixed,
non-produced asset rather than any effort by its owner. Hudson's distinctive contribution, as the
wiki's [narrative on the rentier economy](/wiki/the-rentier-economy/) frames it, is to connect
this classical rent theory to modern **banking and credit** specifically, extending the "rent"
framing beyond land itself to the financial mechanisms that capitalize and extract it — an
extension the wiki treats as "analytically coherent but empirically thinner" than the direct,
land-specific case, since there is no equivalent of the Rognlie/Bonnet land-share decomposition
for financial-sector rent specifically.[2]
