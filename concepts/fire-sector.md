## Definition

**FIRE** (Finance, Insurance, and Real Estate) is a sectoral grouping used in national-accounts reporting that economist [Michael Hudson](/wiki/michael-hudson/) treats as the analytical core of his critique of modern capitalism. In *Killing the Host: How Financial Parasites and Debt Bondage Destroy the Global Economy* (2015), Hudson argues the FIRE sector has displaced industrial capitalism as the dominant force in the economy by extracting [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) rather than funding productive investment.[1] His central mechanism is that banks lend against **land and real estate as collateral**, and that this credit is capitalized directly into land prices: rising mortgage debt bids up land values, and rising land values in turn support larger mortgage loans, in a self-reinforcing cycle — so that much of what national income statistics record as financial-sector "output" is, in his account, actually a claim on land rent dressed up as a return to capital.[1][2]

The acronym itself predates Hudson; it appears in standard economic classifications (e.g., NAICS, SIC) as a grouping of finance, insurance, and real-estate activities. Hudson's distinctive contribution is not the label but the analytical claim that these three sectors function together as a **rent-extraction complex** rather than as productive contributors to output. ## Hudson's FIRE-Sector Thesis

Hudson frames the FIRE sector adversarially, describing it as a "parasite" that disguises itself as part of the productive host economy — attaching itself to production, disabling the host's own understanding of what is happening to it, and ultimately consuming the surplus that would otherwise fund productive growth and public investment.[1] The publisher's own description of *Killing the Host* characterizes the FIRE sector's growth as coming "at the expense of industrial capitalism and governments," worsening economic polarization between asset owners and wage earners.[2]

A substantial part of Hudson's argument is historical: he traces what he presents as a deliberate intellectual retreat from classical rent theory, arguing that the classical distinction between earned income and unearned rent was progressively erased from mainstream ("neoclassical") economics from the late nineteenth century onward — in part because it was analytically inconvenient for a finance sector whose income increasingly took a rentier form. According to a review by Dwight D. Murphey in the *Journal of Social, Political, and Economic Studies* (Summer 2016), Hudson "reviews the long history of the insight that a significant part of wealth accrues to its owners as a windfall," tracing the concept through the Physiocrats, [Adam Smith](/wiki/adam-smith/), [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/), [John Stuart Mill](/wiki/john-stuart-mill/), and [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/).[3] This is a historiographic and interpretive claim about the discipline's development; it should be read as "Hudson argues," not as an established account of the history of economic thought.[2]

## The Mortgage-Credit Mechanism

The specific mechanism Hudson identifies — the credit-land-price feedback loop — is the analytical bridge between classical rent theory and modern banking. Banks lend against land as collateral; the credit is capitalized into land prices; rising land values support larger loans; and the cycle repeats. In this account, the FIRE sector does not merely *facilitate* productive investment but actively **converts** land rent into financial claims, extracting income that classical economists would have classified as unearned rent.[1][2]

This mechanism parallels, and is likely one of the sources for, the land-credit feedback loop described independently by [Josh Ryan-Collins](/wiki/josh-ryan-collins/), Toby Lloyd, and Laurie Macfarlane in *Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing* (2017), which argues that bank credit secured against a fixed supply of land is the engine that turns housing into a rent-extracting asset class rather than a place to live.[4] It also connects to the [18-year land cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/) tradition, in which credit-fuelled land-price inflation drives recurrent boom-bust dynamics.[2]

## Relation to Classical and Georgist Rent Theory

Hudson's framework is explicitly continuous with classical and Georgist rent theory. Both hold that land rent is unearned in the sense that it reflects the value of a fixed, non-produced asset rather than any effort by its owner, and both conclude that taxing this rent is efficient and fair where taxing labor or produced capital is not (see [Economic Rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) and [Land Value Tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/)).[1][2]

Hudson's distinctive contribution, as the wiki's [narrative on the rentier economy](/wiki/the-rentier-economy/) frames it, is to connect this classical rent theory to modern **banking and credit** specifically — arguing that mortgage lending is the primary channel through which land rent is currently capitalized, extracted, and concentrated, rather than treating land rent purely as a static public-finance question. He extends the "rent" framing beyond land to finance and monopoly income more broadly.[5]

Consistent with this diagnosis, Hudson co-authored the 2021 CEPR paper with Goodhart, Kumhof, and [Nicolaus Tideman](/wiki/nicolaus-tideman/) modelling a shift of taxation from income onto land as a source of both fairness and macroeconomic stimulus — an applied policy version of the rent-capture argument.[6]

## Mazzucato and the Value-Extraction Framework

[Mariana Mazzucato](/wiki/mariana-mazzucato/) generalizes the value/rent distinction that Hudson applies to the FIRE sector across the whole economy. In *The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy* (2018), she argues that modern economies increasingly reward value **extraction** over value **creation**, and that GDP accounting fails to distinguish the two — a problem she traces to the shifting [production boundary](/wiki/production-boundary/) in national accounts.[7] With Ryan-Collins and Giorgos Gouzoulis, she extended this into a formal framework spanning land, finance, and digital-platform rents in *Mapping Modern Economic Rents* (2023), arguing that much of what national accounts record as profit is economically rent.[8]

Where Hudson focuses on the FIRE sector as the specific institutional vehicle for rent extraction, Mazzucato broadens the analytical lens to any activity that captures existing wealth rather than creating new value. Both converge on the classical insight that the [production boundary](/wiki/production-boundary/) — the line national accounts draw between productive output and mere transfers — has been drawn too generously, allowing rentier income to be misclassified as productive output.[5][7]

## The Contested Boundary: Extensions Beyond Land

The extension of the FIRE/rentier framing beyond land to finance, intellectual property, and digital platforms is analytically coherent but **empirically thinner** than the land-specific case. As the wiki's [rentier economy narrative](/wiki/the-rentier-economy/) notes, there is no equivalent of the [Rognlie](/wiki/rognlie-capital-share/)/[Bonnet](/wiki/bonnet-land-is-back/) land-share decomposition for financial-sector rent specifically — no widely accepted method decomposes a firm's profit margin or a bank's income into "rent" versus productive return outside the land case.[5]

A competing explanation for the same aggregate trends is offered by the [superstar firms](/wiki/superstar-firms/) literature: [Autor et al. (2020)](/wiki/autor-superstar-firms/) argue that the fall in labor's share and the rise in measured profit margins are substantially explained by the rise of highly productive firms that win larger market shares through technology and efficiency — a story of legitimate scale economies, not rent extraction.[9] The [Austrian critique](/wiki/lvt-austrian-critique/) poses a more fundamental challenge: if there is no principled line between land and other capital, the entire "rentier vs. producer" framing loses its cleanest empirical case.[10]

The wiki's editorial position, as represented on the [rentier economy narrative](/wiki/the-rentier-economy/) and [rentier](/wiki/rentier/) pages, is that the land-specific version of the FIRE critique is the best-evidenced and should be deployed first; the extensions to finance and platforms are advocated by Hudson and Mazzucato but should be marked as "advocates argue" rather than settled fact.[5]

## Independent Evidence: Krippner on Financialization

Sociologist Greta Krippner's empirical study of the postwar U.S. economy provides an independent, non-Georgist record of the trend Hudson's narrative describes. Krippner documents the growing importance of financial activities as a source of corporate profits from the 1970s onward — a shift she terms "financialization" — offering quantitative evidence that the FIRE sector's share of the economy has indeed grown substantially, though her study does not itself adopt Hudson's rent-extraction framing.[5]

## Significance for Georgism

The FIRE sector concept matters for Georgism because it provides a diagnostic frame connecting the classical concern with [land rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) to contemporary debates about financialization, inequality, and market power. The concept shows that the Georgist claim — that a substantial share of income flows to those who own scarce assets rather than those who produce — is not merely a historical argument about agrarian landlords but speaks directly to the dominant financial structures of the modern economy.[1][5]

The [2008 financial crisis](/wiki/2008-financial-crisis/) is frequently read by Georgist analysts as a paradigm case of credit-fuelled land-price inflation feeding a rentier boom that then collapsed — the mechanism Hudson identifies and that [Goodhart, Hudson, Kumhof & Tideman](/wiki/goodhart-stimulus/) build their land-tax stimulus model to address.[5][6] This connects the FIRE-sector analysis to the broader [boom-bust cycle](/wiki/boom-bust-cycle/) and [land speculation](/wiki/land-speculation/) literature.

However, the step from diagnosis to land taxation specifically needs an explicit bridge: that much FIRE-sector income traces back to land rent does not by itself establish that a [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) alone — rather than financial regulation, antitrust, or debt write-downs — is the right instrument for the finance and platform portions of the claim. Proponents like Mazzucato in fact call for a mix of tools, not land taxation alone.[8]
