Imputed rent is the rental-equivalence method by which national accounts measure the housing services consumed by owner-occupiers, assigning a notional market rent value to owner-occupied housing even though no actual rent transaction occurs. La Cava (2016) demonstrates that the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis measures owner-occupier housing services via rental-equivalence imputation, and shows that imputed rent—rather than landlord market rent—drives much of the secular rise in housing's share of capital income. This measurement concept is Georgist-adjacent because it reveals how the rising capital income share is fundamentally a housing and land phenomenon, even when no explicit rental transaction takes place.
