## Definition

**Kuznets cycles** (or "Kuznets swings") are medium-range economic waves, with an estimated period of roughly **15–25 years**, identified by economist and future Nobel laureate **Simon Kuznets** in 1930.[1] Kuznets connected the waves to demographic processes — particularly immigration inflows and outflows — and the resulting swings in **construction intensity**, which is why he termed them "demographic" or "building" cycles.[1] They have also been interpreted more broadly as infrastructural-investment cycles.[1]

## Relation to the Georgist Land Cycle

Fred Harrison's *[The Power in the Land](/wiki/harrison-power-in-the-land/)* (1983) cites Kuznets's roughly 20-year cycles in US population, migration, and house-building (Ch. 5, p.64) as one strand of evidence for a recurring land-and-construction cycle, alongside [Homer Hoyt](/wiki/homer-hoyt/)'s ~18-year periodicity in Chicago land values.[2] Wikipedia notes more directly that "some modern economic commentators argue the Kuznets swing reflects an 18-year cycle in land values," citing Harrison's argument that the cycle of boom and bust could be smoothed or avoided through an annual land value tax.[1] Because Kuznets's cycle was derived independently, from demographic and construction data rather than from land-price series, Georgist writers treat the overlap as corroboration that a real, mainstream-recognized periodicity underlies the land-cycle model documented on this wiki's [18-Year Land Cycle](/wiki/18-year-land-cycle/) page.

## Caveats

The Kuznets cycle is not accepted without dispute even within mainstream economics: Howrey (1968) argued the apparent cycle Kuznets found was largely an artifact of the statistical filter he applied, and that similar spurious cyclical patterns could be produced by running the same filter over random (white-noise) data.[1] Harrison himself notes the cycle length is not perfectly consistent — citing 20-year Kuznets swings alongside an 18-year Hoyt periodicity, and acknowledging a roughly 20-year gap (1815–1938) distorted by wartime disruption.[2] The concept should be read as one contested strand of evidence within the broader, still largely practitioner- rather than peer-reviewed, land/business-cycle literature (see [Boom-Bust Cycle](/wiki/boom-bust-cycle/) for the fuller evidence assessment).
