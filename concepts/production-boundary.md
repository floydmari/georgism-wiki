## Definition

The **production boundary** is [Mariana Mazzucato](/wiki/mariana-mazzucato/)'s term, developed
in *The Value of Everything: Making and Taking in the Global Economy* (2018), for the
historically shifting line that economic theory and national-accounting convention draw between
activity counted as productive (inside the boundary, adding to national output) and activity
treated as unproductive or merely redistributive (outside it).[1] Reviewers describe this as the
book's central analytical device: understanding how the production boundary has been drawn and
redrawn over roughly 300 years is, in Mazzucato's account, the key to understanding how theories
of value have changed. Classical political economists — Adam Smith, David Ricardo, and Karl
Marx — drew an explicit line between productive and unproductive labour, with income from
landownership generally classed as a redistributive claim on value created elsewhere rather than
value creation itself; the 20th-century "marginalist" turn redefined value as subjective and
equivalent to whatever price a transaction commands, which Mazzucato argues erased that classical
distinction and let almost any priced transaction count as value creation by definition.[1]

## Why It Matters to the Rent Debate

The production-boundary concept matters to Georgist arguments because it names the mechanism by
which [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) gets recorded as productive output rather than as a
redistributive claim. Mazzucato and reviewers point to concrete cases consistent with this: owner-occupied
housing's imputed rent is counted as part of U.S. GDP, and banks' interest-spread income is
counted as financial-sector output via the FISIM convention formalized in the 1993 System of
National Accounts — both cases where an income stream that classical economists, including
Ricardo, would have analyzed as accruing to a fixed or scarce asset rather than to production is
nonetheless recorded as value added.[1] This is the same logic later formalized for land
specifically, alongside finance and digital platforms, in Mazzucato, Ryan-Collins & Gouzoulis,
"Mapping Modern Economic Rents" (2023).[1] The concept is not, however, uncontested: Tony
Aspromourgos's review for the *History of Economics Review* (2018) specifically challenges
Mazzucato's account of the productive/unproductive labour distinction in Marx and other classical
economists, identifying what he characterizes as terminological imprecision and factual and
bibliographic errors in her treatment of the history of value theory.[2]
