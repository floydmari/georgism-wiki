Agglomeration economies are the productivity benefits and spillovers that arise from the spatial concentration of economic activity in cities, increasing output per worker as city size grows. These economies are central to the model of Duranton and Puga (2023), where productivity spillovers from city size make land valuable and explain why planning restrictions that limit growth in productive cities have aggregate costs. Kanemoto, Ohkawara, and Suzuki (1996) estimated aggregate production functions to measure agglomeration economies across 17 Japanese metropolitan areas, finding that they vary by city-size band. The concept is closely linked to optimal city size, as agglomeration benefits must be balanced against commuting and crowding costs to determine efficient urban population levels.
