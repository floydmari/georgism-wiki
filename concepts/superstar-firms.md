## Definition

**Superstar firms** are dominant, high-markup, low-labor-share firms that win a disproportionate and growing share of sales within their industries as globalization or technological change disproportionately advantages the most productive competitors. The term comes from [Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson & Van Reenen (2020)](/wiki/autor-superstar-firms/), "The Fall of the Labor Share and the Rise of Superstar Firms," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 135(2), 645–709, who use it to explain the aggregate decline in labor's share of national income as a **between-firm composition shift** — industries becoming increasingly "winner-take-most" as sales reallocate toward a small number of highly profitable, low-labor-share firms — rather than a general fall in the labor share within every firm.[1]

The concept is closely related to the finding in [De Loecker, Eeckhout & Unger (2020)](/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/) that average U.S. markups rose from roughly 21% above marginal cost in 1980 to roughly 61% by 2016, with the increase concentrated in a rising upper tail of firms and accompanied by a reallocation of market share toward those high-markup firms.[2] Both papers document the same broad pattern — a shrinking set of dominant firms pulling up aggregate markups and profits — but they differ in interpretation: Autor et al. lean toward an **efficiency** reading (genuine productivity divergence amplified by scale economies), while De Loecker et al. remain **agnostic** on whether the cause is efficiency or market power.[1][2]

## The Efficiency-vs-Rent Debate

The superstar-firms concept is significant on this wiki precisely because it is the most credible **non-rent, non-land** rival explanation of a trend Georgists read as evidence of growing rent extraction: the shift of national income away from labor. The debate turns on whether the high markups and profits of superstar firms represent:

- **Genuine efficiency** — returns to real productivity advantages from technology, scale, and intangible capital, in which case the falling labor share is a composition shift toward more productive firms, not a story of extraction.[1]
- **Market power / rent extraction** — returns to scarce, non-produced positions (patents, network effects, regulatory barriers, brand) that raise prices above marginal cost without corresponding productivity gains, which would be a form of [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) generalized beyond land.[2]

This is not a binary either/or across the economy. [Crouzet & Eberly (2019)](/wiki/crouzet-eberly-intangibles/) provide the most granular sector-by-sector decomposition, finding that concentration driven by intangible capital reflects **productivity gains** in the Consumer sector (retail, distribution), **rising markups without productivity gains** in Healthcare (patentable pharmaceuticals, devices), and **both mechanisms simultaneously** in High-tech.[3] Their paper is the strongest mainstream steelman of the efficiency reading, but it explicitly warns against a "one size fits all" interpretation — the answer depends on the sector.[3]

[Akcigit & Ates (2021)](/wiki/akcigit-ates-business-dynamism/) survey ten stylized facts on declining U.S. business dynamism — rising concentration, markups, profits, and a falling labor share alongside widening frontier-laggard productivity gaps — and propose slowing knowledge diffusion from frontier to laggard firms as a unifying driver. Their model generates six of the ten facts jointly, and they note that the superstar-firms mechanism (Autor et al.) is consistent with their model's composition effect.[4] They also acknowledge the Traina (2018) critique that including SG&A expenses in variable costs substantially attenuates the measured markup rise, placing the paper within the contested empirical landscape.[4]

## Relation to the Land-Rent Explanation

The superstar-firms account bears directly on [the claim that the modern rise in the capital share is land, not capital](/wiki/capital-share-rise-is-land/). That finding, drawn from [Rognlie's decomposition](/wiki/rognlie-capital-share/), operates at the level of national accounts and the housing/land component specifically, while the superstar-firms story operates at the level of firm microdata and industry concentration. The two are **partially overlapping rather than strictly competing**:[1]

- **Different levels and data.** Rognlie works at the macro/national-accounts level, splitting the rise in capital's share into housing and non-housing components. Autor et al. work at the micro/firm level, explaining the fall in labor's share through changing firm composition. "Capital share" and "labor share" are roughly complementary, but a macro housing/land decomposition and a micro firm-concentration decomposition answer different questions with different data.[1]
- **Where they are complementary.** Both reject the older "generic capital deepening" story. Rognlie's answer is *land*; Autor et al.'s answer is *market concentration and markups*. Nothing in either paper rules out the other: some superstar-firm advantages could include land-proximate benefits (urban locations, real estate portfolios), but Autor et al. do not decompose profits by asset type, so their paper is **silent** on land.[1]
- **Where they compete.** Both compete for explanatory share of the broader shift of income away from labor. Rognlie's story is a **rent** story in the classical, land-specific sense. Autor et al. present theirs primarily as an **efficiency** story. To the extent the efficiency reading is correct, a meaningful share of the shift is *not* a land-rent story — a real limit on how far the land-rent explanation can be pushed as a complete account.[1]

The outcome page on [corporate profits increasingly reflecting rents](/wiki/corporate-profits-increasingly-rents/) assesses the broader evidence as "moderate–strong that the profit rise is real and abnormal; contested on how much is rent vs. efficiency." The superstar-firms literature is the primary source of that contestation.[5]

## Measurement Controversy

The empirical foundation of the superstar-firms/markups literature is itself contested. The key dispute concerns the De Loecker–Eeckhout markup methodology, which uses **cost of goods sold (COGS)** as the variable input while excluding Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A) expenses. [Traina (2018)](/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/) argues that including SG&A — which has grown as a share of firm cost structures — substantially attenuates the measured markup rise, finding markups increased only modestly and remained within their historical range.[2] [Basu (2019)](/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/) surveys the main approaches and concludes that different reasonable assumptions produce substantially different conclusions, and that existing methods cannot yet determine with confidence whether U.S. markups have been roughly stable or risen only modestly.[2] A 2025 comment by Benkard, Miller & Yurukoglu disputes the results on sample-construction grounds, to which De Loecker, Eeckhout & Unger have replied — showing the measurement debate remains active.[2]

These critiques affect the superstar-firms debate because the concentration-and-markup pattern is the shared empirical foundation. If the markup rise is substantially smaller than headline figures suggest, the scope of the efficiency-vs-rent contest narrows accordingly.

## Implications for the Georgist Case

For the wiki's [rentier-economy narrative](/wiki/the-rentier-economy/), the superstar-firms literature is the strongest available counterweight and should be represented as such rather than downplayed. It is a serious, peer-reviewed, widely-cited account showing that at least part of the aggregate shift away from labor's income share has a plausible non-land, non-rent-extraction explanation: legitimate firm-level productivity divergence amplified by concentration.[1]

At the same time, several limits bound how far this displaces the land-rent explanation specifically:

- **It is not a rebuttal of Rognlie.** The superstar-firms literature does not analyze housing, land, or the capital share directly. Rognlie's finding about the housing composition of the rising capital share is untouched by it.[1]
- **The efficiency-vs-market-power question is unresolved.** The papers' own interpretations are contested by other economists who read similar facts as evidence of weakening competition. This is a live empirical debate, not a settled finding.[1][2]
- **Even the "efficiency" sectors do not rule out land-linked advantage.** Crouzet & Eberly treat intangible capital and physical capital/location as separate categories and do not test for interaction between them.[3]
- **Scope is bounded by data and period.** The core findings rest on U.S. Economic Census data through 2012 and Compustat publicly traded firms; more recent developments (e.g., large digital platforms post-2012) are not covered by the original analyses.[1][2]
