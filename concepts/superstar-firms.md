## Definition

**Superstar firms** are dominant, high-markup, low-labor-share firms that win a
disproportionate and growing share of sales within their industries as globalization or
technological change disproportionately advantages the most productive competitors. The term
comes from [Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson & Van Reenen (2020)](/wiki/autor-superstar-firms/),
"The Fall of the Labor Share and the Rise of Superstar Firms," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*
135(2), 645–709, who use it to explain the aggregate decline in labor's share of national
income as a **between-firm composition shift** — industries becoming increasingly
"winner-take-most" as sales reallocate toward a small number of highly profitable, low-labor-share
firms — rather than a general fall in the labor share within every firm.[1]

## Why It Matters to the Rent Debate

The superstar-firms account is significant on this wiki precisely because it is the most
credible **non-rent, non-land** rival explanation of a trend Georgists read as evidence of
growing rent extraction: the shift of national income away from labor. [De Loecker, Eeckhout
& Unger (2020)](/wiki/de-loecker-eeckhout-unger-markups/) document the same underlying pattern
— a shrinking upper tail of firms pulling up aggregate markups — using firm-accounting data,
but their paper stays agnostic on whether the cause is genuine efficiency or market power,
while Autor et al. argue substantially for the **efficiency** reading: superstar firms'
high markups are, in significant part, a return to real productivity and scale advantages, not
extraction.[1][2] This bears directly on [the claim that the modern rise in the capital share
is land, not capital](/wiki/capital-share-rise-is-land/): that finding operates at the level of
national accounts and the housing/land component specifically, while the superstar-firms story
operates at the level of firm microdata and industry concentration — the two are not strictly
competing, but superstar firms supply a serious efficiency-side counterweight to any claim that
the broader shift away from labor is *entirely* a [rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) story.
