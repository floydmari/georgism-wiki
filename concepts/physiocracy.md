## Overview

**Physiocracy** was a school of economic thought that flourished in France from roughly the 1750s to the 1770s, centered on the court physician **François Quesnay** and including the marquis de Mirabeau (Victor de Riqueti) and, later, **Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot**.[1] Its central claim was that agriculture alone produces a true economic surplus — the *produit net*, or net product — while manufacturing and commerce merely rearrange value that already exists without adding to it; Quesnay diagrammed the annual circulation of this surplus among landowners, farmers, and merchants in his 1758 *Tableau économique*.[1][2][8] (The school took its name from the title of the 1767 collection of Quesnay's writings edited by Du Pont de Nemours, *Physiocratie, ou constitution naturelle du gouvernement le plus avantageux au genre humain* — "the rule of nature."[8]) From this productivity theory the Physiocrats derived a striking policy conclusion: because they held that land alone yielded genuine net revenue, and that any tax on labor or trade would ultimately be shifted onto land rent anyway, they argued the state should replace its patchwork of levies with a single tax directly on the net product of land — the ***impôt unique***.[1][4] More than a century later, [Henry George](/wiki/henry-george/) singled out Quesnay and Turgot for praise in [*Progress and Poverty*](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/) (1879), writing that "the French economists of the last century, headed by Quesnay and Turgot, proposed just what I have proposed, that all taxation should be abolished save a tax upon the value of land."[4]

## The Physiocratic System: Land as the Sole Source of Surplus

Quesnay's *Tableau économique* modeled the French economy as a circulation of income among three classes: a "productive" class of farmers who generate the *produit net* through agriculture; a "proprietary" class of landowners (including the crown and the church) who receive that surplus as rent; and a "sterile" class of artisans and merchants whose activity, in the Physiocratic accounting, merely reproduces the value of its own inputs without creating new net wealth.[1][2] The *Tableau* is now generally credited as a founding ancestor of the input-output and circular-flow modeling later formalized by economists such as Wassily Leontief.[2] From this framework the Physiocrats also derived their advocacy of *laissez-faire* — freedom of internal and external trade, above all in grain — on the theory that the "natural order" of markets, left undisturbed, would maximize the net product available for taxation and general welfare; the phrase "laissez-faire, laissez-passer" is associated with the school but its exact coinage is disputed between Quesnay and his contemporary Vincent de Gournay.[1][3]

## The Impôt Unique

The Physiocrats' tax doctrine followed directly from their productivity theory: because they held that land alone yielded a genuine surplus, any tax nominally levied on wages, trade, or manufactured goods would, they argued, eventually be passed backward through the economy and absorbed out of land rent — so a web of indirect taxes, tolls, and the hated *corvée* (forced unpaid labor on royal roads) achieved nothing but administrative waste and economic distortion compared with taxing the net product directly.[1][3] As Controller-General of Finances under Louis XVI from 1774 to 1776, Turgot attempted to put Physiocratic principles into practice, abolishing the *corvée* in favor of a money tax and liberalizing the internal grain trade, before entrenched opposition from the nobility and clergy — who stood to lose their tax exemptions under a land-focused system — forced his dismissal in 1776.[3] The full *impôt unique* was never adopted by the French monarchy, and the school itself declined rapidly after the 1770s, eclipsed by Adam Smith's broader theory of value in *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), which extended the concept of productive labor beyond agriculture to manufacturing and trade.[1] Smith engaged the Physiocrats directly and closely: their doctrine is the subject of Book IV, Ch. IX ("Of the Agricultural Systems"), where he judged that "this system, however, with all its imperfections, is perhaps the nearest approximation to the truth that has yet been published upon the subject of political economy," even as he rejected their claim that only agricultural labor is productive.[7] In Book V he noted, without endorsing, that a tax varying with the rent of land "is recommended by that sect of men of letters in France, who call themselves the economists, as the most equitable of all taxes" — granting that "all taxes ought to fall as equally as possible upon the fund which must finally pay them" while declining "the disagreeable discussion of the metaphysical arguments by which they support their very ingenious theory."[7] Both passages are reproduced verbatim in this wiki's full text of the work and summarised in the book page's honest-limits discussion.

## From Physiocracy to Georgism

The Physiocrats are widely treated as the direct intellectual ancestors of the single-tax and [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) tradition that runs through [Adam Smith](/wiki/adam-smith/), [David Ricardo](/wiki/david-ricardo/), and Henry George.[5] George read the Physiocrats' *impôt unique* as an anticipation of his own [single tax](/wiki/single-tax/) proposal, and this wiki's summary of Ryan-Collins, Lloyd, and Macfarlane's *[Rethinking the Economics of Land and Housing](/wiki/rethinking-economics-land-housing/)* (2017) traces an explicit lineage from the Physiocrats through Smith and Ricardo to George and on to modern Georgist economists.[5] The Physiocrats are likewise noted, in passing, in Gaffney and Harrison's *[The Corruption of Economics](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/)* (1994) as originators of the impôt unique that George's neoclassical opponents later worked to obscure.[6]

## Differences Between Physiocracy and Georgism

The two doctrines share a policy conclusion — a single tax falling on land — but rest on different foundations, and it would overstate the case to treat them as identical. The Physiocrats grounded the *impôt unique* in a theory of agricultural productivity specific to 18th-century France: only farming, they held, produced a physical net surplus, so the tax base was in practice the *rural* net product.[1] George's argument, worked out a century later against the backdrop of American urban and industrial growth, rested instead on [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) as an ethical and distributional category — the unearned increment in land value created by community growth rather than by any individual's effort — and applied as much to urban [ground rent](/wiki/ground-rent/) as to farmland.[4][5] The Physiocrats' reform was also framed as restoring a "natural order" congenial to an enlightened monarchy, whereas George's was framed as a remedy for the paradox of poverty amid material progress.[3][4]
