## Definition

**Radical Markets: Uprooting Capitalism and Democracy for a Just Society** (2018) is a book by
legal scholar Eric Posner and economist Glen Weyl, published by Princeton University Press,
proposing a set of market-design reforms intended to increase both efficiency and equality by
redesigning the rules of property, voting, immigration, and data ownership.[1] Its
best-known and most Georgist-adjacent proposal is the **Common Ownership Self-Assessed Tax
(COST)**, popularized on this wiki as the [Harberger tax](/wiki/harberger-tax/): owners
self-assess the value of an asset, pay a tax on that declared value, and must stand ready to sell
at the declared price to any buyer.[1] Posner and Weyl present COST as a generalization of the
Georgist single-tax insight — that value arising from a fixed or scarce holding, rather than
from productive effort, should be captured through taxation — extended from land specifically to
property and quasi-property more broadly.

## Why It Matters to the Rent Debate

Radical Markets matters to the Georgist case because it is a modern, non-Georgist-branded
academic program that arrives at essentially the same mechanism-design conclusion as land value
taxation — that self-assessed, continuously-taxed common ownership solves both the assessment
problem (owners reveal true values under threat of a forced sale) and the holdout problem (assets
are always available for purchase) — and extends it beyond land to any asset whose supply is
functionally fixed or hard to reallocate.[1] The program was carried into technology and
cryptocurrency circles when Ethereum co-founder [Vitalik Buterin endorsed it](/wiki/buterin-on-radical-markets/)
in a 2018 essay, applying Harberger-style taxation to domain names and other digital assets — a
significant channel through which Georgist-adjacent ideas reached an audience outside traditional
land-tax advocacy.[2]
