## Definition

A **community land trust (CLT)** is a nonprofit organisation that owns **land** and leases it
long-term to residents or businesses, who own the **buildings** on it. By separating ownership
of the land from ownership of the improvements, a CLT keeps the rising value of the land in
common hands rather than letting it accrue to individual owners — applying the Georgist
land/building distinction at neighbourhood scale, one parcel at a time.

## How It Works

The classic CLT design, codified in the movement's founding manual (below), has three
load-bearing features:

- **A ground lease, not a sale.** The trust retains title to the land and grants residents a
  long-term — typically 99-year, renewable and inheritable — **ground lease**. The household
  buys and owns the house; it never owns the land beneath it.
- **A resale formula.** When a homeowner sells, a formula caps the price so that most of the
  land's appreciation stays with the trust and the home remains affordable to the next
  buyer. This is the central trade-off: the owner gets an affordable entry and a modest,
  capped gain instead of full market appreciation.
- **Tripartite governance.** The board is split in thirds among **leaseholders**, **other
  community residents**, and **public-interest representatives**, so no single group captures
  the trust.

The net effect is that the [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/) of rising land
value is captured collectively and recycled into permanent affordability — a voluntary, local
form of [land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/) that works through property title
rather than taxation.

## Origins

The CLT is usually dated to **New Communities Inc.**, founded in **1969** near Albany, Georgia,
by civil-rights organisers including **Charles Sherrod** and **Slater King** — a
5,700-acre effort to give Black farmers secure land tenure, incorporated to "hold land in
perpetual trust for the permanent use of rural communities." The model was developed and named
by **Ralph Borsodi** and **Robert Swann**, whose **International Independence Institute**
(founded 1967, later the Institute for Community Economics) drew explicitly on India's
**Gramdan** ("village-gift") movement, in which villages held land in common trust. In **1972**
the Institute published *The Community Land Trust: A Guide to a New Model for Land Tenure in
America*, which set out the ground-lease, resale-restriction, and tripartite-board features
that define the "classic" CLT.[1][2] The deeper lineage reaches back through [Ebenezer
Howard's](/wiki/ebenezer-howard/) garden cities and the [single-tax
colonies](/wiki/single-tax-colonies/) like [Fairhope](/wiki/fairhope-single-tax-corporation/),
which pioneered community land ownership funded by ground rents.

## Relation to Georgism

The CLT is a **cousin of Georgism, not a copy of it**, and the difference is worth stating.
Both rest on the same insight — that the value of land is created by the community and should
be captured for it rather than pocketed by an owner. But the instruments differ:

- **A land value tax leaves land in the market** and captures its rent through taxation,
  system-wide, on every parcel at once.
- **A CLT removes its parcels from the market**, capturing the increment through ownership and
  a lease, voluntarily and one site at a time.

That makes the CLT a *decommodifying* approach where LVT is a *tax* approach. The CLT's
strength is that it needs no legislation — a community can start one tomorrow — and it delivers
permanent, place-based affordability. Its honest limits are the mirror image: it is
parcel-by-parcel rather than universal, it depends on acquiring land (often with subsidy), and
the resale formula that preserves affordability does so by **limiting the homeowner's
wealth-building**, a real cost that trades individual asset appreciation for collective
affordability. A Georgist reads the CLT as a voluntary, retail demonstration of the principle
that a land value tax would apply wholesale.

## Scale Today

The model spread from a handful of experiments to a substantial movement: hundreds of CLTs now
operate across the United States — the **Champlain Housing Trust** in Burlington, Vermont
(founded 1984, one of the largest) is the standard model — coordinated nationally by the
Grounded Solutions Network, with a growing parallel movement in England and Wales. CLTs remain
a niche within total housing stock, but they are the most widely replicated real-world
institution built directly on the land/building distinction.
