## Overview

The **stationary state** is the end-point classical economists — chiefly David Ricardo and, after him, John Stuart Mill — predicted for a growing economy operating under diminishing returns to land. As population and capital accumulate, cultivation is forced onto less fertile or less well-situated land (the [margin of production](/wiki/margin-of-production/) falls), food prices rise, and under Ricardo's [law of rent](/wiki/law-of-rent/), rent absorbs a growing share of output while profits are squeezed toward the minimum needed to induce further saving.[1] Ricardo treated the stationary state as a bleak, if distant, terminus of growth in his *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817).[1]

Mill's own account, in Book IV, Chapter VI ("Of the Stationary State") of his *Principles of Political Economy* (1848), reversed the emotional valence of the same mechanism: rather than dreading the stationary state as stagnation, Mill argued it could be a desirable end to *quantitative* growth — an economy that turned instead to the distribution of wealth, the quality of life, and the preservation of nature.[2] Both versions of the argument share the same analytical engine: without some check on land's claim on the surplus, diminishing returns to a fixed factor eventually convert the whole of society's growth dividend into rent.

## Georgist Significance

Henry George's *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)* (1879) engaged this classical prediction directly, accepting the underlying mechanism — that rent tends to absorb the surplus of growth — while rejecting the classical economists' fatalism about poverty or stagnation as its necessary consequence. George's proposed remedy, taxing away land rent rather than letting it accumulate as unearned private income, was framed as an escape from the stationary-state trap: capturing the very rent that Ricardo and Mill predicted would choke off growth, and redirecting it to public purposes, rather than treating rent's rise as an inevitable economic destiny.
