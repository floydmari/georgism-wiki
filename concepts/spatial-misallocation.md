Spatial misallocation refers to the loss of aggregate output and total factor productivity that results when housing constraints and land-use restrictions in high-productivity cities prevent labor and capital from flowing to their most productive locations. Hsieh and Moretti (2019) formalize this mechanism, showing that housing supply constraints in high-wage cities like New York and San Francisco distort the spatial allocation of labor and lower aggregate U.S. output. Bakker (2023) extends the concept by demonstrating that urban land rents misallocate labor and capital across cities, lowering aggregate total factor productivity. Duranton and Puga (2023) and Herkenhoff, Ohanian, and Prescott (2018) further link planning regulations and land-use restrictions to spatially misallocated population and reduced economic growth.
