## Definition

**Geolibertarianism** (geo-libertarianism) fuses **libertarian** commitments — strong rights to self-ownership and to the fruits of one's labour — with the **Georgist** position that land and natural resources are common property whose rent belongs to all. Geolibertarians typically argue that a [land value tax](/wiki/land-value-tax/) is the **one legitimate source of public revenue**, because it collects value that belongs to no individual, while taxes on labour and capital are unjust takings of earned income.

## The Core Argument

Self-ownership implies you own what you produce — so taxing wages or capital is a violation. But no one produced land; exclusive title to a location excludes everyone else from a natural opportunity. Geolibertarians resolve this by saying you may hold land exclusively **provided** you pay its rental value to the community you exclude. This reconciles private tenure with equal rights to the earth.

## Lineage

The view descends from classical liberals and from Henry George himself, who saw his program as pro-market and anti-monopoly. It overlaps with the "left-libertarian" tradition and informs modern proposals like the [Harberger tax](/wiki/harberger-tax/).

The term itself was coined by [Fred Foldvary](/wiki/fred-foldvary/) in *Land and Liberty* in 1981, naming the fusion of libertarian property-rights theory with the Georgist claim that land rent — unearned and communally created — is the one legitimate basis for public revenue (see [*Public Goods and Private Communities*](/wiki/foldvary-public-revenue/)). The deeper roots run through [John Locke](/wiki/john-locke/), whose proviso that appropriation leave "enough and as good" for others the geolibertarian position is precisely an attempt to satisfy, and through [Herbert Spencer](/wiki/herbert-spencer/), whose *Social Statics* chapter on [the right to the use of the earth](/wiki/social-statics-right-to-use-earth/) is the classic rights-based statement the position descends from. George's own fullest natural-rights argument — that exclusive private title to land violates the equal right of all to the earth — is [*A Perplexed Philosopher*](/wiki/a-perplexed-philosopher/), his reply to Spencer's later recantation.

## Applications and Rivals

- **Applied governance theory.** Foldvary's [*Public Goods and Private Communities*](/wiki/foldvary-public-revenue/) extends geolibertarianism into practice: because land-rent capture can fund collective goods voluntarily, much of what coercive government does could be organized through consensual, land-rent-funded private communities. His later "geo-rent" work estimated land rent could fund roughly half of all-level US government revenue — an advocacy-side, non-consensus figure.
- **The rival libertarian position.** [Murray Rothbard](/wiki/murray-rothbard/) and the broader [Austrian critique](/wiki/lvt-austrian-critique/) accept the libertarian framework but explicitly reject the geolibertarian claim that land is categorically distinct from produced capital goods; see [Rothbard's "The Single Tax: Economic and Moral Implications"](/wiki/rothbard-single-tax/). Geolibertarians answer that exclusive land title is itself a grant from the community, so capturing its rent restores rather than violates equal rights.
- **Distribution.** Within the [citizen's dividend](/wiki/citizens-dividend/) family of proposals, geolibertarianism carries the property-rights version of the case: rent is distributed because it belongs equally to everyone excluded from the land.
