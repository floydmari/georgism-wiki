## Overview

**Clean Slate proclamations** were periodic royal decrees issued by rulers across Bronze Age Mesopotamia, from roughly 2400 to 1400 BC, that cancelled outstanding agrarian debts, freed people held in debt bondage, and restored land that had been forfeited to creditors back to its original smallholders.[1] Historians and economist [Michael Hudson](/wiki/michael-hudson/) have identified around thirty such general debt cancellations across this period, known by different names in different city-states and periods — *amargi* in Lagash (Sumer), *nig-sisa* in Ur, *andurarum* in Ashur, and *misharum* in Babylon — with the Sumerian *amargi* literally meaning a "return to the mother [state]," i.e., a restoration of the original social order.[1][2]

Hudson's account, developed across decades of research culminating in his book *…and forgive them their debts: Lending, Foreclosure and Redemption From Bronze Age Finance to the Jubilee Year* (2018), argues these proclamations served a specific economic function: interest-bearing agrarian debt in these societies tended to compound faster than smallholders' capacity to repay it, so left unchecked it would have concentrated land in creditors' hands and reduced a growing share of the population to bondage, undermining the crown's tax base and its supply of free labor for corvée duty and military service.[1] Periodic clean slates reset this process before it went too far, restoring the mixed independent-smallholder economy the state relied on. Hudson connects this tradition to the Biblical Jubilee year (Leviticus 25) and presents it as the deep-historical precedent for treating unconstrained rent and debt claims on land as a recurring threat to social and fiscal stability, a theme he develops elsewhere in the more contemporary financial-rent argument of *[Killing the Host](/wiki/hudson-killing-the-host/)*.[1]
