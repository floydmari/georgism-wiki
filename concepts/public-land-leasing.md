## Overview

**Public land leasing** captures land value for public benefit through state ownership and long-term leasing rather than through recurrent [land value taxation](/wiki/land-value-tax/).[1] Under this model, government retains title to land and grants users long-term leases — often auctioned — so that increases in land value accrue substantially to the public landowner rather than to private speculators.[1] [Hong Kong](/wiki/hong-kong/) and [Singapore](/wiki/singapore/) are the leading examples: both fund significant shares of public spending from land-lease revenue, and Canberra, Australia was planned on a similar leasehold system.[1] Yu-Hung Hong's 1996 Lincoln Institute analysis frames leasing as an alternative source of local public finance, distinct from but economically related to recurrent taxation of land value.[1]
