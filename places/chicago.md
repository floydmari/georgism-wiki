Chicago is the subject of Homer Hoyt's *One Hundred Years of Land Values in Chicago* (1933), a century-long case study documenting urban land value patterns from 1830 to 1933. The work traces the cycles of land speculation, boom, and bust that characterized Chicago's growth, providing one of the most detailed empirical records of urban land value dynamics in any city. Hoyt's analysis of Chicago's land market remains a foundational reference in land economics and is frequently cited in Georgist discourse on land speculation and cycles.
