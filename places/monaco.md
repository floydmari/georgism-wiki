## Overview

**Monaco** is a roughly 2.1 km² sovereign city-state on the French Riviera and one of the world's best-known tax havens: it levies no personal income tax on most residents (French citizens are an exception under a bilateral treaty) and has low business taxes, alongside no annual property tax on individually owned real estate.[1] Land in Monaco is also extremely scarce — the state cannot expand except through high-rise building and land reclamation into the Mediterranean — and it has correspondingly become one of the most expensive real-estate markets on earth, with average residential prices reaching roughly €100,000 per square metre by 2018 and newer reclaimed districts pricing considerably higher still.[1][2]

Georgist writers point to Monaco as a vivid, if extreme, illustration of [ATCOR](/wiki/atcor/) and [tax capitalization](/wiki/tax-capitalization/): abolishing taxes on income and capital does not, on its own, make a place cheap to live in. Where land is fixed in supply and highly desired, the money residents save in tax gets bid into land and property prices instead, landing as [economic rent](/wiki/economic-rent/) captured by whoever already owns the land — not as a lower cost of living for newcomers. Fred Harrison discusses low-tax, land-scarce jurisdictions of this kind in *[Boom Bust](/wiki/harrison-boom-bust/)* (2005) as part of his wider argument that untaxed land rent is what asset booms are made of (Ch. 9, §3).[3]
