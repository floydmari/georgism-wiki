## Overview

**British Columbia**, Canada, holds one of the richest historical records of [land value taxation](/wiki/land-value-tax/) anywhere in the world. Inspired directly by Henry George's *[Progress and Poverty](/wiki/progress-and-poverty/)*, B.C. cities adopted site-value taxation on a remarkable scale in the early 20th century, retreated from it after the World Wars, and have repeatedly revisited it since.

## Early Adoption (1890s–1914)

By the 1890s B.C. municipalities had the legal power to assess land separately from improvements — the precondition for an LVT. Adoption then accelerated sharply: a 1994 CMHC study found that **by 1914 about two-thirds of B.C. municipalities** taxed on land value, including Vancouver, Victoria, South Vancouver, North Vancouver, Nanaimo, Prince Rupert, New Westminster, and Kelowna. Victoria and New Westminster **fully exempted improvements in 1911**. The movement was driven by labour activists and reformers reacting to rapid urban growth and land speculation.

## Vancouver's System (1910–1984)

Vancouver became the most significant North American example. By 1911 roughly **79% of municipal revenue** came from land taxation. The system strained as the city grew: by 1913 assessment growth lagged spending, and Vancouver reintroduced a tax on buildings rather than push land rates ever higher. After World War I the broader property-tax base won out as cities needed stable, diversified revenue — an instance of the [revenue-adequacy objection](/wiki/lvt-not-enough-revenue/) playing out in practice.

## The Assessment Foundation (1974)

In 1974 B.C. created the independent **BC Assessment Authority**, consolidating 140 inconsistent assessment bodies into one — under Lands Minister **Bob Williams**, who championed it through a Georgist lens. Land economist [Mason Gaffney](/wiki/mason-gaffney/) advised on the reform, and **Ted Gwartney** — a student of the [Henry George School](/wiki/henry-george-school/) — became BC Assessment's first head. The result is that, to this day, B.C. produces separate land and improvement values, making it the one Canadian jurisdiction with the assessment infrastructure a real LVT requires.

## Habitat I (1976)

Vancouver hosted the UN's first Conference on Human Settlements. Recommendation D.3 of the resulting Vancouver Action Plan endorsed [land value capture](/wiki/land-value-capture/): increases in land value caused by public investment or community growth *"should be recaptured by public bodies."*

## Modern Era (1984–Present)

Since 1984 B.C. property tax has applied equally to land and buildings. But the question keeps returning: Victoria passed UBCM resolutions (2017, 2020) seeking restored local authority to tax land separately, and Vancouver examined land value capture through development levies. Vancouver's 2019 roll showed why — land valued at ~$392.8 billion versus only ~$85.7 billion of improvements.

## Lessons

B.C.'s experience shows LVT as a practical municipal tool rather than a "magic switch": its success depends on assessment quality, political framing, and revenue stability. The record is the empirical backbone of [Common Wealth Canada's](/wiki/common-wealth-canada/) B.C. Land Value Tax campaign.
