## Overview

Alberta is a Canadian province whose economy is heavily built on oil and gas extraction, and which has produced two of the wiki's clearest real-world rent-capture case studies. Its oil-sands royalty — a net-revenue-after-payout regime allowing full expensing of costs — is identified by [Mintz & Chen (2012)](/wiki/mintz-chen-capturing-resource-rents/) as "the closest to the rent-based royalty regime" among twelve jurisdictions they compared, and recommended (with modification to remove its price-sensitivity) as "a rent-tax model for other countries to follow."[1] Alberta also created the **Alberta Heritage Savings Trust Fund** in 1976 under Premier Peter Lougheed — Canada's oldest sovereign wealth fund, seeded with a $1.5 billion transfer from general revenue and originally capitalized by 30% of the province's annual non-renewable resource revenue (a share since reduced, then eliminated in 1987 in favour of general-revenue transfers).[2]

Less well known is Alberta's place in the early-twentieth-century Western Canadian single-tax movement. The wiki's existing coverage of [British Columbia](/wiki/british-columbia/) and [L.D. Taylor](/wiki/l-d-taylor/) cites the IMF's 1967 *Staff Papers* survey "The Taxation of Land Value" for the finding that by 1914, two-thirds of British Columbia's municipalities, **all of Alberta's**, and about a quarter of Saskatchewan's had fully exempted improvements from property tax — making Alberta, on this account, the most completely converted of the three provinces during that era.[3] This wiki has not yet independently sourced the province-specific chronology (e.g., which Alberta cities led the shift, or how the exemption was later rolled back); see the British Columbia and L.D. Taylor pages for the fuller documented history of the same period in the neighbouring province.
