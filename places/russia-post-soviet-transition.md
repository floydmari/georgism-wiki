## Overview

Post-Soviet Russia is treated in Georgist literature as a large-scale missed opportunity: a state that, briefly, already held nearly all land and natural-resource wealth in public hands and could in principle have raised public revenue by charging rent for their use, rather than by privatizing them and then taxing labor and enterprise instead.[1][2] The 1990 [Open Letter to Gorbachev](/wiki/open-letter-to-gorbachev/), signed by Nobel laureates including [William Vickrey](/wiki/william-vickrey/) and organized by [Nicolaus Tideman](/wiki/nicolaus-tideman/), made this case to the Soviet leadership before the USSR's dissolution.[3] Within Russia itself, economist **Dmitry Lvov** (1930–2007) pursued a similar rent-based proposal through the 1990s — an effort that lost out to the "shock therapy" privatization program associated with Yegor Gaidar, Anatoly Chubais, and Boris Yeltsin, whose most consequential episode was the 1995 "loans for shares" scheme that transferred Russia's most valuable oil, mining, and metals enterprises to a small group of private owners who became known as the oligarchs.[4]

## The Road Not Taken: Lvov's Rent-Based Proposal

Dmitry Lvov, an economist associated with the Russian Academy of Sciences, argued through the 1990s that Russia's natural-resource wealth — oil, gas, minerals, and land — should remain formally on the state's books and be charged for as rent, rather than transferred into private ownership.[1] Fred Harrison has described working with Lvov's team on a rent-as-revenue alternative for Russia during this period.[1] In a State Duma address (translated and archived by a third-party outlet), Lvov argued for treating natural-resource wealth as a public asset to be placed "on the books of the state" and its income directed into the treasury, rather than redistributed through privatization.[5] [VERIFY: the Duma speech is available only via a secondary reposting (a Christian Council for Monetary Justice blog quoting a Fred Harrison tweet, itself linking a third-party archive); this wiki has not independently verified the speech's date, full context, or an authoritative primary transcript.]

## Shock Therapy and the Rise of the Oligarchs

Russia's government took the opposite path. After the Soviet collapse in December 1991, Yeltsin's administration pursued rapid market liberalization — freeing prices, then privatizing state enterprises — on the advice of Russian reformers and Western advisers; the International Monetary Fund and other Western creditors were, per NPR's account, notable advocates of the speed and shape of this "shock therapy" program.[4] An initial 1992–94 voucher privatization distributed ownership of roughly 15,000 state enterprises (about 70% of the economy) relatively broadly, but the largest and most valuable state assets — oil, mining, and metals companies — remained in government hands into 1995.[4]

## The Loans-for-Shares Scheme

Facing a 1996 re-election Yeltsin was expected to lose, his government adopted the "loans for shares" scheme: a small group of bankers loaned the state money against collateral in Russia's most valuable enterprises, at auctions widely reported to have been pre-arranged, on the understanding that the government would default and the lenders would keep the assets.[4] Twelve major enterprises — including oil, mining, and shipping companies — changed hands this way between November and December 1995; NPR reports that Boris Berezovsky and Roman Abramovich acquired a large stake in the oil company Sibneft for roughly $200 million, a stake Abramovich later sold back to the Russian state in 2009 for $11.9 billion.[4] The recipients of these transfers subsequently financed Yeltsin's 1996 re-election campaign.[4] Gaffney and Harrison's *[Corruption of Economics](/wiki/gaffney-harrison-corruption-of-economics/)* (2022 revised edition) states flatly that "Russia privatized land under 'shock therapy,' creating oligarchs" — one of three post-1994 cases (with South Africa and China) the book cites as evidence that privatizing rent, rather than collecting it publicly, corrupts governance.[2]

## Georgist Assessment

The Russian case is presented in Georgist sources as the clearest large-scale instance of the "missed moment" theme also found in the [Open Letter to Gorbachev](/wiki/open-letter-to-gorbachev/): a state starting from public ownership of land and resource rents chose privatization-plus-taxation over rent-as-revenue, and the transition produced a small, politically entrenched ownership class rather than the broad-based public revenue Lvov and the Gorbachev letter's signatories argued for.[1][2][3] Harrison's claim (via a 2022 social-media post, not a peer-reviewed source) that the IMF was "the institution most determined to frustrate and demolish" Lvov's efforts is a strong, single-source assertion this wiki cannot independently corroborate; NPR's independent account confirms the IMF's advisory role in shock therapy generally but does not corroborate a specific campaign against Lvov's proposal.[1][4] Whether a Lvov-style rent-based fiscal system was administratively feasible at 1990s-Russia's state capacity — as opposed to normatively preferable — is not addressed in the sources reviewed for this page and would need separate evaluation.
