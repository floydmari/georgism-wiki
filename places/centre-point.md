## Overview

**Centre Point** is a 34-storey, 117-metre office tower at St Giles Circus in central London, built between 1963 and 1966 for property developer **Harry Hyams** by Wimpey Construction, to a design by George Marsh of R. Seifert and Partners.[1] Hyams, who had leased the site for 150 years, intended the whole building to be let to a single tenant at his asking rent and refused to divide it into smaller lettings; as a result the tower stood completely empty from completion until 1975 — about nine years — while property prices around it rose, earning it the nickname "London's Empty Skyscraper."[1] The vacancy, amid an acute London housing shortage, made Centre Point a public symbol of landlords profiting from withholding valuable land from use; the homelessness charity **Centrepoint**, founded in 1969, took its name partly in protest at the building being left empty, and housing campaigners occupied the tower for three days in January 1974.[1]

Georgist writers have used Centre Point as an illustration of [speculative vacancy](/wiki/speculative-vacancy/): an owner of a scarce, well-located site can rationally leave it idle rather than let it below the price they expect the market — and the site's rising capital value — to eventually bear, since holding costs on land are low and the option to wait can be worth more than developing or letting now. Fred Harrison discusses the case in *[The Power in the Land](/wiki/harrison-power-in-the-land/)* (1983) as part of his broader argument about [land speculation](/wiki/land-speculation/) and the 18-year land cycle (Ch. 6, Ch. 7).[2] After decades of office use — including 34 years as the Confederation of British Industry's headquarters — Centre Point was converted to residential flats between 2015 and 2018; even after conversion, observers have noted many units standing dark and unsold, prompting comparisons to earlier patterns of [land monopoly](/wiki/land-monopoly/) and capital being parked in property rather than put to use.[1]
