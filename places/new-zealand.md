New Zealand historically employed land-value (LV) rating as a primary local government funding mechanism, making it one of the most significant real-world cases of pure LVT at the local level. The country's gradual shift from land-value to capital-value rating bases, and the resulting variation across municipalities, has provided researchers with a quasi-natural experimental setting for studying how property tax base choices affect new building development. Gemmell, Grimes, and Skidmore exploited this variation in Auckland to examine whether local property taxes affect new building development, finding little effect on new building consents (only on alterations) in the short window after Auckland's 2010 amalgamation forced a shift away from land-value rating — a null result this wiki records as an honest caveat on the construction evidence.
