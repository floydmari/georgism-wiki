## Overview

**Letchworth Garden City**, in Hertfordshire, England, was the first town built to [Ebenezer Howard's](/wiki/ebenezer-howard/) garden-city model, founded in 1903 after First Garden City Ltd purchased roughly 3,800 acres of land for about £160,000.[1] Architects Barry Parker and Raymond Unwin designed the masterplan, combining planned residential neighbourhoods, industry, and agricultural greenbelt around Howard's central idea from *Garden Cities of To-Morrow* (1902): that a new town's land should be held in common, so that the [unearned increment](/wiki/unearned-increment/) of rising ground rents the town's own growth creates is captured for community benefit rather than private landlords.[2]

## The Georgist Financing Model in Practice

Letchworth is the built proof-of-concept for Howard's land-value-capture design. Rather than selling plots freehold, First Garden City Ltd retained ownership of the land and leased sites to residents and businesses, with the intent that appreciating ground rents would repay development costs and fund local infrastructure and amenities as the town grew.[1] The scheme has persisted institutionally: in 1962 an Act of Parliament transferred the estate to the public-sector Letchworth Garden City Corporation, and in 1995 a further Act passed the roughly £56 million estate to the charitable **Letchworth Garden City Heritage Foundation**, which still owns the town's land and reinvests the landlord's profits — over £1 million a year in community grants, by the Foundation's own account — into local services.[1]

## Significance

Letchworth directly inspired [Welwyn Garden City](/wiki/ebenezer-howard/) (1920) and, more broadly, the garden-city movement's influence on 20th-century UK town planning and the later [community land trust](/wiki/community-land-trust/) movement, which adapted the same land/building separation to smaller urban sites. As a still-functioning, century-old example of land held in trust to fund public goods from the rent it generates, Letchworth is one of the wiki's clearest real-world illustrations of Georgist land economics operating at the scale of an entire town.
