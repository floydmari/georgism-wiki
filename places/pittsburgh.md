## Overview

Pittsburgh has one of the longest histories of split-rate property taxation in the United States, having taxed land at a higher rate than buildings from **1913** — under Pennsylvania's [Graded Tax Law](/wiki/pennsylvania/) for cities of the second class — continuously until **2001**.[1][3] It is best known as the setting for [Oates & Schwab's landmark 1997 study](/wiki/oates-schwab-pittsburgh/) of land value taxation, and, in its abrupt 2001 repeal, as the clearest demonstration that the policy's practical survival depends on sound [land assessment](/wiki/land-cannot-be-assessed/).

## The Split-Rate Era, 1913–2001

Pittsburgh (with Scranton) adopted the graded tax in 1913 and phased it in over five increments between 1915 and 1925, reaching a building rate one-half the land rate.[3] The city then held a **2:1 land-to-building ratio from 1913 until 1979**.[2]

### The 1979–80 reform

Facing fiscal pressure, Pittsburgh **sharply increased its land-tax rate around 1979–80**, reaching roughly **five times** the rate on buildings by the 1980s — the most aggressive split-rate regime in the country at the time.[4] By 1989 the ratio stood at almost **6:1** (31.37 mills on land against 5.44 mills on improvements), and it held there until the 2001 repeal.[1]

### Oates & Schwab's evidence

The 1980s that followed the reform saw a marked rise in building activity even as the city's steel economy collapsed. Comparing the average annual value of building permits in the 1980s against the prior decade for Pittsburgh and **15 comparison cities**, [Oates & Schwab (1997)](/wiki/oates-schwab-pittsburgh/) found Pittsburgh's real building-permit value rose roughly **70%**, placing it **at or near the top** of the sample while most comparators were flat or declining — and this despite Pittsburgh **losing population and shedding steel jobs**, so a general demand surge cannot explain the boom.[4] The authors are careful: the city also ran an aggressive downtown redevelopment push, so they claim only a "meaningful, plausibly significant role" for the tax, not sole causation. The single Pittsburgh case was later generalized by the multi-municipality panels of [Plassmann & Tideman (2000)](/wiki/plassmann-tideman-construction/) and [Banzhaf & Lavery (2010)](/wiki/banzhaf-lavery-pa-sprawl/).

## The 2001 Repeal — a Reassessment Debacle, Not a Tax Failure

Pittsburgh **abandoned** the split-rate system in **2001**, after nearly 90 years — but the cause was a collapse in the *assessment* system, not a failure of the tax principle.[1] The sequence is well documented by Mark Alan Hughes's Lincoln Institute case study:[1]

- **The assessment freeze and the court order.** Allegheny County had chronically under-assessed property on frozen, fractional values. In **1997**, Common Pleas Judge **R. Stanton Wettick Jr.** ruled the freeze violated the state constitution's equal-protection and uniformity requirements, and ordered that all **550,000 properties** in the county be reassigned values by an outside contractor.[1]
- **The Sabre reassessment.** In **March 1998** the county awarded **Sabre Systems and Service of Ohio** a **$23.9 million** contract; over 27 months Sabre reassessed every property and moved the county from a fractional system (assessed values calibrated to 25% of market value) to **full-market-value assessment**.[1]
- **The 2000 rate-setting.** In fall 2000 Sabre reported the overall land share at about **20% of total assessed value**; using that, the city set 2001 rates of 31.37 mills on land and 5.44 on improvements (the ~6:1 ratio in force since 1989), adopted by City Council on December 19, 2000.[1]
- **The January 2001 crisis.** Final reassessment notices — the first to break out **separate land and improvement values** and the two-tier tax owed — landed in January 2001. The **land component rose far more than expected** (assessed land value jumped **81% between 2000 and 2001**), and Sabre's land-valuation method drew severe criticism for **differential impacts by neighborhood**.[1]
- **The switch to a single rate.** On **January 19, 2001** — one week after the story broke — Mayor **Tom Murphy** moved the city to a **single (flat) rate**; City Council adopted a 10.8-mill uniform rate, described as a "temporary measure" to protect homeowners while the assessment system was fixed. It was never reversed. An analysis of the shift found that **54% of homeowners paid more** under the single rate than under the two-tier tax.[1]

The episode is a cautionary tale about administration: the split-rate structure "made a difference" in whose bills rose when the long-frozen assessments were finally corrected, and the political backlash fell on the *tax structure* rather than on the botched *reassessment* that actually drove the changes.[1] As the [split-rate construction page](/wiki/split-rate-increases-construction/) notes, the flagship case's causal story is not undone by the repeal.

## Post-Repeal Record

After 2001 Pittsburgh remained on a single-rate property tax, and Allegheny County's assessment crisis continued (the 2001–2002 assessments were litigated further, under a new contractor, Cole Layer Trumble).[1] The subsequent split-rate literature has turned to the **tax base**: [Yang & Hawley (2022)](/wiki/yang-split-rate-tax-base/), studying Pennsylvania municipalities, find that adopting split-rate taxation raised aggregate property market values by roughly 21.5% (concentrated in the adoption event), with land values falling only slightly — reinforcing, from the revenue angle, the construction story the Pittsburgh case first put on the map.
