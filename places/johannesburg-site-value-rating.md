## Overview

Johannesburg adopted **site value rating** — taxing land value only, exempting the value of buildings — around 1918-1919, becoming the first Transvaal municipality to abandon the conventional property tax as it sought to reinvent itself from a declining mining town into a diversified financial center.[1][2] By 1979, site-value or two-rate taxation had spread to essentially all major towns in the Transvaal province; a comparison summarized by economist John M. Polimeni found that the cities taxing site value alone had **doubled their capital investment and site value** relative to the last two Transvaal holdouts still using the conventional land-plus-buildings tax as of the mid-1980s.[2]

Fred Harrison's *The Power in the Land* (1983) uses Johannesburg alongside Denmark and [Pittsburgh](/wiki/pittsburgh/) as case studies rebutting economist Richard Lipsey's claim that "isolating economic rent is impossible" — citing the city's cheap office rents (£3.85 per square foot, among the lowest of any major world city at the time Harrison wrote) and the ongoing valuation-updating work of City Valuer John McCulloch as evidence that a jurisdiction can run [site-value](/wiki/site-value/) assessment as routine municipal administration for decades.[1]

## Gaffney's Growth-Comparison Argument

Mason Gaffney's workpaper "Enterprising Johannesburg and Sleepy Cape Town: A Contrast"
(undated, internally datable to c. 1991–92) makes a stronger, more contestable claim than the
administrative history above: that the site-value tax *caused* Johannesburg's rise. He frames
the city as an urban-economics puzzle — no harbor, no water transportation, exhausted mines,
poor surrounding farmland — that "should now be a ghost town" but instead became South Africa's
economic capital, and credits a **4% site-value-only property tax** (a first-hand, unverified
figure — "they tell me it is 4%" — distinct from the administrative record in this page's
Sources 1–5 above), contrasted with Cape Town's superior natural site and lack of the same tax,
which Gaffney associates with urban sprawl, a fractured CBD, and underused rail yards.[6] This
comparative-growth argument, and its confounders (apartheid-era Group Areas Act spatial
planning, Johannesburg's distinct role as mining-finance capital vs. Cape Town's as legislative
capital, neither of which Gaffney addresses), are detailed on this wiki's [Gaffney's Georgist
City-Growth Case Studies](/wiki/gaffney-georgist-city-growth-cases/) page — carried there as
attributed historical argument, not as independently corroborated evidence.

## What Happened to It

Site-value rating did not survive as South Africa's default system. The **Municipal Property Rates Act of 2004** (effective 1 July 2005) replaced the old provincial patchwork of site-value, flat, and composite rating with a single national system taxing the market value of land plus buildings, ending Johannesburg's pure land-value base as a matter of national law.[3] The city's rates administration today still shows the imprint of that history: its General Valuation Roll is reissued roughly every four years and is publicly inspectable and open to objection, a modern descendant of the same administrative machinery that once ran a land-only tax.[4] The site-value-rating tradition itself is independently attested by Riël Franzsen's 2003 Lincoln Institute survey, which records that as of the early 2000s site value was still the rating base in roughly a third of South African municipalities, Johannesburg among them, until the framework legislation behind the Municipal Property Rates Act moved the country to a land-plus-improvements base.[5]
